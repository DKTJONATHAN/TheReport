Leave it to KBC, the "Great Grandma" of Kenyan broadcasting, to try and convince us that a new coat of paint makes a crumbling building modern. This Friday, Club 1 Extra is returning with a "fresh start," which is just industry-speak for firing the old people and hiring someone who once went viral. They have replaced the trio of DJ Liza DVA, Wanjiku Wanjiku, and DJ Colejax with Kalondu Musyimi and DJ Brito, hoping we won't notice that the channel still feels like a 1980s government office.

Kalondu Musyimi is the big draw here, apparently because she did a quickfire interview with Stevo Simple Boy that the internet obsessed over for five minutes. In Nairobi, that is all the "award-winning" pedigree you need to be called a heavy hitter. She has sat down with Diamond Platnumz and Khaligraph Jones, which is basically a requirement for anyone with a microphone and a ring light these days. It is the same recycled cycle of celebrity worship that keeps our media landscape as shallow as a puddle in Eastleigh after a light drizzle.

The move is supposedly a signal that KBC is "paying attention" to where entertainment is heading. If they were actually paying attention, they would know that young Kenyans are on TikTok and YouTube, not waiting for a 4:30 PM slot on a Friday to see what is trending. This desperate grab for relevance is just another symptom of the [Clout, Chaos, and Corpses: Why Kenya’s Social Media Feed Is a Crime Scene](/posts/clout--chaos--and-corpses--why-kenya-s-social-media-feed-is-a-crime-scene) era we are living in, where personality is measured by clicks rather than actual substance.

DJ Brito is being brought in for "musical instinct" and "on-air charisma," which is usually code for "he has a good playlist and does not stutter on air." While the duo promises a fast-paced mix of music and celebrity interviews, we all know the drill. It will be the same five artists being interviewed about the same five scandals, all while trying to maintain that "clean" KBC image that nobody actually relates to.

At the end of the day, a rebrand is just a distraction. You can change the hosts, you can change the graphics, and you can promise "fresh energy," but if the foundation is still a state-owned relic, the content will always feel a decade late. We will see if Kalondu and Brito can actually spark a conversation, or if they will just become more background noise for people waiting for the 7:00 PM news. Don't hold your breath; Nairobi has a way of turning "fresh energy" into exhaustion real fast.
