World Bank Tries to Mend Nairobi's Broken IMF Promise

The World Bank is apparently playing peacemaker, trying to nudge Kenya back into the IMF's good books. It’s a rare move, this multilateral lender acting like a marriage counselor for countries. Meanwhile, our Treasury, in their infinite wisdom, decided to pretend this whole IMF funding thing wasn't happening, conveniently leaving it out of budgets until 2030. Why bother with those pesky "tough conditions," right? It’s all about getting multi-billion shilling loans without the fuss, apparently.

But the World Bank, bless their cotton socks, insists the IMF’s involvement is more than just about cash. They’re talking about the IMF’s "policing" and "reform agenda." Basically, they think the IMF can whip us into shape, making sure we don't fritter away every shilling we get. The World Bank is even holding "Article IV consultations" with the IMF, which sounds suspiciously like them comparing notes on our economic health - or lack thereof. And to top it off, the IMF has already sent us a draft assessment highlighting all our "governance weaknesses and corruption vulnerabilities." Lovely.

Never mind that Kenya is now swimming in a Sh97 billion ($750 million) loan from the World Bank, disbursed yesterday. They’re saying the IMF is crucial because our economic outlook is looking decidedly shaky. We’ve been flying solo from the IMF since March 2025, when they pulled the plug on a previous arrangement, leaving us Sh110 billion ($850 million) short. These fresh talks are dragging on, and the World Bank warns that any more delays could make our fiscal framework look like a joke. It’s like they’re saying, "We'll keep lending you money, but maybe the IMF should keep you honest."

The real reason we’re supposedly pushing for this new IMF arrangement, according to the World Bank’s subtle hints, isn't just the money. It's about instilling some much-needed discipline in how we spend and how we collect taxes. We’ve been dodging those painful IMF conditions - higher taxes, job freezes, spending cuts - by not even including their cash in the budget. After the last deal went south due to breached conditions, we’re approaching these talks with all the enthusiasm of a donkey being led to water it doesn't want to drink. It’s no surprise we've been relying more heavily on the World Bank, which, let's be honest, tends to have a softer touch and longer repayment terms than the IMF’s short-to-medium-term fire-fighting. This whole situation is starting to sound a bit like that time the government kept power tariffs unchanged, then the KPLC tried to review them - a back-and-forth that never quite resolves anything.

The World Bank is also wringing its hands about external factors. A prolonged conflict in the Middle East could jack up fuel and fertilizer prices, while also hitting those diaspora remittances we love so much. And let's not forget the August 2027 General Election, which is guaranteed to inject a healthy dose of political risk and derail any feeble attempts at fiscal sanity. They're practically saying that if borrowing gets more expensive, or if we can't refinance our debts, the private sector will get squeezed, investors will pack their bags, and any hope of domestic demand picking up will go out the window.

The IMF had previously approved a Sh310.8 billion ($2.4 billion) program, with an additional Sh71.4 billion ($551.4 million) for resilience. We managed to get a good chunk of that, Sh404 billion ($3.12 billion) and Sh23.3 billion ($180.4 million) respectively, before things went pear-shaped in March 2025. The IMF cited breaches, leaving a cool Sh110 billion ($850 million) on the table. Now, Kenya is leaning on the World Bank for increasingly large sums, a forecast of Sh170.5 billion annually for the next four years. It’s all a bit of a mess, isn’t it?
