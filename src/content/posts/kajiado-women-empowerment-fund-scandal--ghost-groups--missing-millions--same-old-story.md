Kajiado's "Women Empowerment" Fund: Ghost Groups, Missing Millions, Same Old Story

A probe into Kajiado County's Women Economic Empowerment Fund has unearthed the usual suspects: ghost beneficiaries, funds mysteriously allocated elsewhere, and a management system that's more of a sieve than a safeguard. Millions meant to uplift women are, shocker, nowhere to be found.

The nine-member committee, led by MCA Naisiae Karia, has apparently stumbled upon "critical gaps" in how this fund is being run. It's almost as if giving large sums of money to be "managed" by committees is a recipe for disaster. We've seen this play out before. You'd think by now, with all the talk about closing financial and digital gaps to empower businesses, someone would actually, you know, empower them instead of siphoning off the cash. It's a stark reminder that talk is cheap, and these empowerment projects, while sounding nice, often end up lining someone else's pockets.

This isn't just about Kajiado, is it? It's the same old song and dance we hear across the country. Promises of financial inclusion and market access for women's businesses are dangled, but then the reality hits: corrupt officials and poorly managed funds. It’s a cynical, but accurate, reflection of how things often work. We’re told investors and policymakers need to rethink capital allocation, viewing diversity as a strategic move, not charity. Yet, here we are, with a fund supposedly designed for empowerment turning into another avenue for… well, whatever this is. It’s a far cry from the dreams of women making cassava cakes to support their families. It’s a betrayal of that very aspiration.

Perhaps if our leaders spent less time making grand pronouncements about national economic growth and more time ensuring the money actually reaches the intended recipients, we might see some progress. But I wouldn't hold your breath. This kind of scandal just highlights how deeply entrenched the problems are, much like the way global financial structures are shifting, a topic that’s as complex as it is concerning. One wonders if any of this money would have been better off invested in something as tangible as Bitcoin, which at least has a visible market, rather than vanishing into thin air through these so called "empowerment" schemes.
