So, the man of the people, President Ruto, decided it was time for a little getaway. While Kenya was teetering on the edge of collapse thanks to a transporters' strike over insane fuel prices, what does our fearless leader do? He hops on a chartered luxury jet to Azerbaijan. Not a word, not a peep about the impending chaos. Just a quick vanishing act while the country braced for a standstill. Typical.

The strike, as predicted, did exactly what it promised: grinded the country to a halt. Think about it, no trucks means no goods, no supplies, and prices for everything else shooting up faster than a poorly managed pyramid scheme. And while the average Kenyan is worrying about how to get to work or put food on the table, our esteemed leader is likely sipping champagne somewhere. It's a classic Nairobi story, isn't it? The rich get richer, and the rest of us get left to pick up the pieces, or in this case, the fuel receipts.

Then came the "ruthless security response" that Murkomen so eloquently confirmed. Four Kenyans dead, over 30 injured, and nearly 350 arrested. All for the crime of protesting exorbitant fuel prices. Because clearly, the best way to solve a crisis is to shoot and arrest your own citizens. It’s a tactic that’s as old as time, and sadly, as effective as ever in silencing dissent. It reminds me of the way official pronouncements often paper over the real issues, much like the recent scandals in the [Kenya Energy Sector](%2Fposts%2Fkenya-energy-sector-on-edge--substandard-fuel-scandal-sparks-political-circus-and-market-shockwaves).

Of course, after the bloodshed and the country being brought to its knees, the "talks" happen. And surprise, surprise, the strike gets suspended. Funny how quickly solutions appear once the pressure is on and lives have been lost. The government probably wheeled out some empty promises and a few platitudes, and the transporters, tired of the struggle and the risk, agreed to a temporary truce. Don't expect any real change, though. This is just a breather before the next inevitable price hike.

And while all this drama unfolds, the President is busy with his foreign visits. One can only imagine the pressing matters of state that require a private jet and a hefty bill. It’s a stark reminder of where the priorities lie. It’s not about the common man struggling to survive; it’s about maintaining the image and the comforts of power. This whole charade is just another episode in the ongoing circus of Kenyan politics.
