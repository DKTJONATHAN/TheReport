Ruto Urges Compassion and Unity in Eid Message - For Now

WARNING: President Ruto's call for unity and compassion during Eid ul-Fitr is a familiar tune, but don't expect the song to last beyond the festivities.

President William Ruto, in his wisdom, has decided it's time for a bit of national togetherness as the Muslim faithful celebrate Eid ul-Fitr. He’s out here urging Kenyans to embrace generosity and social cohesion, you know, the usual platitudes thrown around during religious holidays. Apparently, this season is crucial for strengthening faith and fostering national harmony. Because, of course, a few days of good vibes will magically fix all the cracks in this country.

Ruto, bless his heart, waxed lyrical about how Eid celebrates faith, unity, and gratitude. He wants us to remember the less fortunate and share kindness. It’s a nice sentiment, but let's be real, how much of that "kindness" actually trickles down from the top? It’s easy to talk about sharing blessings when you’re not the one struggling to make ends meet, worrying about whether your next meal will come from the kindness of strangers or the government’s latest, likely insufficient, handout. This whole song and dance feels a lot like the "big titles for small change" we’ve seen before, like the Sh70 Million for West Pokot.

He then goes on to mention that this occasion is an "opportunity" to reflect on shared values and support the vulnerable, emphasizing unity as the "key to national progress." It’s the same script, different holiday. Every leader says this. Unity is always the buzzword, especially when they want us to stop looking too closely at where the money's going or why things aren't improving. It’s a classic distraction technique, and it works wonders, especially when you've got a whole nation looking for some hope.

The president expressed hope that these celebrations would inspire peace, strengthen community bonds, and renew optimism. Oh, the optimism! It’s almost palpable, isn't it? This is what they want us to believe. The "Festival of Breaking the Fast" is a symbol of unity and compassion, tracing its origins back to the 7th century. Fascinating. Meanwhile, back in the 21st century Nairobi, we're still dealing with the same old issues of inequality and broken promises. It’s a nice story, this notion of historical unity, but reality often has a more cynical way of unfolding, much like the "war games and the 24-hour casino of global chaos" we've seen brewing.

Sure, Eid is traditionally about charity and communal gatherings. The Zakat al-Fitr is meant to ensure everyone can join in. And yes, in Kenya and globally, it's a symbol of unity. But let's not get carried away. This call for unity and compassion, while commendable on the surface, often serves as a temporary balm on deeper wounds. Once the feasting is done and the new clothes are put away, the real struggle for survival for many Kenyans will continue, and these lofty pronouncements from the head of state will likely fade into the background until the next religious observance rolls around.
