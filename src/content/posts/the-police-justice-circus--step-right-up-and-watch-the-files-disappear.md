In Nairobi, we have a special word for justice. We call it a dream. The Director of Public Prosecutions is busy lecturing us about the delicate balance of rights, but if you live in Mathare or Kibera, you know that balance always tips toward the man with the rungu. It is a tired script: a rogue cop kills someone, the public screams, and then the legal system swallows the case whole. These systemic delays are not accidents; they are the architecture of a country that prefers its victims silent and its officers protected.

While we are busy being shocked by [The Kitengela Bloodbath: New Year, Same Old Rungus](/posts/the-kitengela-bloodbath--new-year--same-old-rungus), the bureaucrats are busy losing files. A lost case file in a Kenyan court is as common as a matatu overlapping in traffic. It is a convenient magic trick that turns a murder charge into a decade of useless adjournments. By the time the case actually gets a hearing, the witnesses have either been intimidated into silence or have simply given up on a system that treats their pain like a bureaucratic footnote.

Then there is the blue wall of silence. It is an ironclad code where the police investigate themselves, which is basically asking a hyena to guard the meat. Ballistic reports vanish into thin air, and crime scenes are cleaned up faster than a politician-funded campaign rally. If an officer actually decides to speak up, their career is over before they can even finish their statement. This is not about law; it is about a gang that happens to have a government-issued uniform and a taxpayer-funded salary.

We were told IPOA would be the savior, the big stick that would finally make the boys in blue behave. Instead, we got an agency that is underfunded, understaffed, and treated like a nuisance by the very people it is supposed to oversee. Investigating thousands of cases is useless if the path to a conviction is blocked by a reluctant police apparatus. It is like trying to put out a forest fire with a leaking cup while the fire department is busy selling the water to the highest bidder.

Let us stop pretending that procedural rights are the real problem here. These rights are being weaponized to shield thugs who hide behind badges. Until the state decides that a poor man-s life is worth more than a cop-s convenience, we will keep running in circles. The cycle of misconduct will not end because someone wrote a nice report; it will only end when the wheels of justice stop grinding to a halt every time a senior officer is called to the dock to answer for his sins.
