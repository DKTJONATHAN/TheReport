Prof Egara Kabaji wants us to believe the world revolves around stories. That is a very academic way of saying we are all being played by people who talk for a living. In a city where the price of electricity makes you want to live in the dark, Senator Edwin Sifuna has realized that if you cannot give people bread, you should at least give them a very entertaining circus. He was at it again in Kitengela, leaning into the "Linda Mwananchi" brand as if hashtags and clever wordplay could somehow lower the cost of unga.

Sifuna is being hailed as a master of narrative because he does not have a state treasury to loot or an army to command. How convenient. It is easy to be the voice of the people when you have zero responsibility for the actual budget. While the government fumbles with its own delusional success stories, Sifuna is busy crafting a counter-narrative that smells like hope but tastes like dust. He captures the imagination because the reality of our daily lives is far too depressing to look at directly without a filter.

We are told that whoever tells the most compelling story wins the hearts of the people. But what exactly are we winning? The political elite have turned our survival into a competition of metaphors. This obsession with "narrative power" is exactly why [the great consolidation: why your vanishing middle class is a feature, not a bug](/posts/the-great-consolidation-why-your-vanishing-middle-class-is-a-feature-not-a-bug) continues to hollow out our bank accounts while we cheer for the best-delivered insult at a rally. Sifuna is not the cure; he is just the most charismatic symptom of a system that prefers talk over touchable results.

The Senator knows his audience. He knows that in Nairobi, we are suckers for a man who can speak truth to power, even if that truth is just a well-rehearsed script meant to trend on X for twenty-four hours. He does not need a development record when he has a punchline. In the public arena, he is the undisputed heavyweight champion of vibes. But let us be real for a second: you cannot eat a narrative, and you certainly cannot use a "compelling story" to pay your digital service tax or keep the bailiffs from your door.

At the end of the day, Sifuna-s significance this political season is a testament to our collective desperation. We are so starved for actual leadership that we have settled for a good narrator to describe our misery back to us. Kabaji might be right that stories rule the world, but in the trenches of Kitengela and the backstreets of the CBD, those stories are just noise. The senator will keep talking, the crowds will keep cheering, and the reality of our collapsing economy will remain unchanged by even the most poetic of speeches.
