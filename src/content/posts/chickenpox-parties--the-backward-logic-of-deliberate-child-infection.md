The sheer lunacy of "chickenpox parties" is a testament to humanity's persistent, often misguided, desire to control the uncontrollable. Before the advent of a vaccine, the prevailing wisdom was to get chickenpox "over and done with" in childhood. This was driven by the grim reality that while typically mild in youngsters, the varicella-zoster virus could wreak havoc on adolescents and adults, leading to severe complications and, as one doctor grimly recalls, even death from pneumonia.

So, instead of preventing exposure, parents actively sought it. They'd orchestrate playdates, deliberately ushering infected children into contact with their uninfected peers, a practice born not of medical counsel but of a desperate, flawed gamble. The logic was simple, albeit profoundly flawed: if a child was going to get sick anyway, better it happen when their immune system was supposedly more robust, and the consequences less dire. This widespread belief, passed around like the virus itself through schoolyards and church basements, transformed a preventable disease into a rite of passage.

The article points out that this practice, while now largely relegated to the past, was never an official medical recommendation. It was a grassroots, albeit dangerous, strategy born from a perceived inevitability. Parents would swap home remedies like oatmeal baths and calamine lotion, essentially managing the symptoms of a disease they actively courted. The idea was to have the infection occur in a "controlled environment," a euphemism for intentionally exposing vulnerable children to a contagious pathogen.

While the vast majority of children recovered within a couple of weeks, the risk of severe complications - pneumonia, serious bacterial infections, brain inflammation - was always present. Roughly three out of every 1,000 infected children faced these grim outcomes. The development of the chickenpox vaccine has, thankfully, rendered these "parties" largely obsolete, a relic of a time when ignorance and a touch of desperation trumped preventive medicine. Yet, the article hints that this backward logic, like the virus itself, hasn't vanished entirely, a chilling reminder of how easily flawed reasoning can persist.
