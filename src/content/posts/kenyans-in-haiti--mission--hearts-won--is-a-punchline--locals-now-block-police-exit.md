Haitians Block Kenyan Police Mission: "Hearts Won" Is A Joke

Your premium access has ended. The best of Nation.Africa is still within reach. Renew now to unlock exclusive stories and in-depth features.

Kenyan police officers disembark in Haiti to join an expanded multinational force with a mandate to fight gangs, at Toussaint Louverture International Airport, in Port-au-Prince, Haiti December 8, 2025.

So, the grand Kenyan police mission in Haiti, which apparently morphed into a “Gang Suppression Force” (GSF) after starting in June 2024, has delivered… something. The report claims “security gains” and “controversy.” Which is Nairobi speak for “they haven't been shot at too much yet, but the locals are starting to get suspicious.” It’s Haiti, not a holiday camp. What did anyone expect? A parade?

Now we have these scenes of Haitian civilians actually *blocking* our boys from leaving certain areas. Blocking them! This isn't exactly a ringing endorsement, is it? It screams more anxiety than admiration. They’re worried about what happens when the Kenyans pack their bags, which is probably a polite way of saying they’re worried about things getting even worse without them, or perhaps, that our boys aren't making much of a difference and are just another problem.

And let’s not forget the other shiny distractions from home. While our officers are playing soldier in the Caribbean, there’s a special rapporteur huffing and puffing about the government and Del Monte not answering her letters regarding some Mr. Kamau’s safety. Safety? In Kenya, safety is a luxury item. This whole thing feels like another grand gesture, another way to get our name in headlines while issues closer to home fester. It reminds me of the kind of political gambles we see back here, where grandstanding trumps sensible policy, like the whole [Ruto gambling with CHAN for political gains](/posts/Is-Ruto-The-Face-of-CHAN) debacle.

Meanwhile, back on the actual profitable soil of Kenya, our vital tea industry is choking under rising costs and export disruptions. So, while we’re spending good money and sending our police to play hero in Haiti, our own economy is doing a slow dive. It's the classic Nairobi playbook: fix problems far away, ignore the rot at home. And if you’ve got an ad-blocker, tough luck, you’re expected to consume the propaganda.
