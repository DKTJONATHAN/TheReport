The news that a French navy sailor managed to expose the near-real-time location of the country’s primary nuclear-powered aircraft carrier through a public Strava workout should surprise precisely no one who has paid even a modicum of attention to the perennial idiocy of modern military personnel. While global conflicts simmer and the stakes are arguably higher than ever, it appears the urge to document one's aerobic achievements on social media trumps all sensible security considerations.

A report by Le Monde details how a sailor, likely aboard the Charles de Gaulle or its escort, logged a seven-kilometer run on March 13th. The digital breadcrumbs, meticulously plotted on Strava, placed the activity in the middle of the Mediterranean Sea, northwest of Cyprus. The carrier’s general presence in the region, a move ordered to bolster allied assets amid the escalating Middle East conflict, was hardly a state secret. But the precise, near-real-time positioning? That’s the kind of detail that makes adversaries’ eyes light up.

This isn't a novel revelation. Le Monde has been diligently cataloging these "StravaLeaks" for years, demonstrating a consistent pattern of military personnel prioritizing personal digital footprints over operational security. Previous incidents have included tracking the same sailor off the coast of France and then, remarkably, in Copenhagen. The publication has identified other public Strava profiles similarly revealing ship positions and even showcasing ship decks, personnel, and equipment.

Even the security details surrounding presidents aren't immune. In 2024, French media reported on Strava activity from security teams assigned to the U.S., French, and Russian presidents, with one instance even identifying a hotel where then-President Joe Biden stayed in San Francisco in 2023. The Pentagon, in its infinite wisdom, actually banned deployed personnel from using geolocation features on apps like fitness trackers in operational areas back in 2018. Clearly, the message hasn't penetrated the collective consciousness of every uniformed individual.

The French Ministry of Armed Forces’ response, if any materializes beyond a vague statement, will likely be as predictable as it is ineffective. The French Armed Forces General Staff admitting this "does not comply with current guidelines" and promising "appropriate measures" is a hollow echo of past reassurances. "Digital hygiene for sailors" being a "prerequisite before any deployment" sounds suspiciously like a platitude designed to placate the public rather than a genuine commitment to preventing future blunders.

Strava, with its nearly 200 million users, offers privacy settings. But the onus shouldn't solely be on the platform or the individual to exercise caution. It should be on the military to instill a culture where posting one's location on a public app while operating a multi-billion dollar, nuclear-armed warship is not just a breach of guidelines, but a career-ending act of gross negligence. Until then, expect more "StravaLeaks," more exposure, and more proof that the human element remains the weakest link in any security chain, especially when vanity is involved.
