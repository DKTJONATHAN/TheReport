So, "For All Mankind" is back for its fifth season, and the sycophants are predictably gushing. Apparently, it’s "better than almost everything else" and "beautifully sets the series up for the future." Let's be real, Apple TV+ pumps out content designed to be palatable, and this alternate history space drama is no exception. It’s got that slick, big-budget feel, the kind that makes you forget for a moment it’s all just pixels and manufactured drama. The praise for its addictiveness and recommendability is just marketing noise, designed to get the uninitiated hooked.

This season, they're pushing the story further, delving deeper into Mars and, allegedly, beyond. The problem, as always when a show tries to evolve, is that some of what made it compelling in the first place has started to fray. It's still, we're told, "amazing," and the faithful will lap it up, but season five is apparently a deliberate departure, a new direction that, frustratingly, requires a significant time investment from the viewer.

We've been given a preview of eight of the ten episodes, a generous offering that still manages to avoid major spoilers, at least according to the PR team. The core plot, already telegraphed in the trailer, involves the Martian inhabitants finally making a stand for their independence against Earth. It's the 2010s in this timeline, and everyone's aged, supposedly gained wisdom, and become even more entrenched in their beliefs. Ed Baldwin, bless his aging heart, is now in his 80s, a relic of a bygone era, starting to hand over the reins to his grandson Alex and daughter Kelly. New leaders like Miles and his daughter Lily are also being groomed, indicating a clear generational handover.

The central conflict is the fight for Martian independence, playing out on multiple fronts and escalating into hostility. Billionaire CEO Dev Ayesa, played by Edi Gathegi, is a key player, while back on Earth, Margo Madison, the formerly beloved NASA genius turned double agent, is still pulling strings alongside her protégé Aleida Rosales. A new, "cold, mysterious security officer" portrayed by Mireille Enos rounds out the cast, adding yet another layer to this increasingly complex, or perhaps just convoluted, narrative.

The struggles on Mars, and their supposed "ripple effects" on Earth, are presented as "fascinating and often all too real and scary." We're talking subplots about AI taking jobs and oppressive police forces. However, the overwhelming focus on Mars makes the show feel smaller, more insular. The brilliance of "For All Mankind" was its ability to showcase the far-reaching consequences of a single deviation from history - Russia beating America to the Moon. That grand, sweeping narrative, easily grasped when the action was on Earth, is now lost in the cramped confines of Martian habitats. Life on Mars, we're told, is just "normal" life, but happening in steel corridors. The "wow" factor, the butterfly effect we used to marvel at, is now largely off camera.

This is a show in transition, and that’s the problem. The new characters, while important, are still just that: new. We haven't developed the same attachment to them as we did to the original crew. Ed and Margo are old; even Aleida and Kelly are aging. This is a story about the second and third generations, and frankly, we’re not invested in them yet. Patience is required, especially in these early stages.

Yet, despite these glaring weaknesses, the article insists that the show will eventually find its footing. Around episode four or five, the struggles on Mars are supposed to become relatable, the isolation palpable. The hope is that Mars isn't the final destination, and the show will eventually venture further into space, reigniting that familiar sense of urgency.

The final two episodes, conveniently, are predicted to be the season's strongest, picking up from a cliffhanger in episode eight. Ultimately, "For All Mankind" season five might be a bit of a dip, a necessary sacrifice to set up future storylines and characters. It's a gamble that, we are assured, will pay off. But for now, it's a slow climb, a testament to the fact that even the most celebrated shows can get bogged down in their own ambition, requiring viewers to endure the mundane before any glimmer of excitement returns.
