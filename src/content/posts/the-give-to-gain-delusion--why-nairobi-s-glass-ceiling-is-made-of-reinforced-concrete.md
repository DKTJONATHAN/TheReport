The global campaign for International Women’s Day 2026 wants us to believe in a theme called Give to Gain. It sounds like something cooked up in a high-end Nairobi boardroom by people who have never had to survive on a single mother’s budget. The idea is that if leaders just share a little power and credit, everyone wins. It is a nice, shiny sentiment for a Tuesday morning brunch, but back in the real world, the 23 percent parliamentary ceiling is not moving because of a hashtag. In Kenya, power is never given; it is hoarded by the same gatekeepers who have been recycled since the nineties.

The economic reality is even more insulting than the political one. We are told that giving is a smart way to build stronger workplaces, yet women in this country are still earning 17.7 percent less per hour than men. That is not a gap; it is a theft of school fees, rent, and retirement savings. In the same way [The Airbnb Chronicles: When ‘Clout’ Becomes the New Colonization](/posts/the-airbnb-chronicles--when--clout--becomes-the-new-colonization) explores how modern trends often mask deeper systemic exploitation, these corporate diversity themes ignore the fact that the cost of living does not care about your gender. When women in education earn 38 percent less than their male counterparts, the Give to Gain logic starts to sound like a request for women to keep working twice as hard for half the reward.

Then there is the blood and money of Kenyan politics. Running for office is not about having the best ideas for maternal health or safe transport. It is a contact sport that rewards big money and tough networks. If you are a woman, the price of entry includes smear campaigns, online harassment, and the very real threat of physical violence. Party structures are designed to sideline anyone who cannot buy their way to the top. We can talk about the two-thirds gender principle until we are blue in the face, but as long as the nominations are handled in backrooms by men who fear losing their grip, the law remains a suggestion rather than a rule.

Safety is another luxury that many Kenyan women simply cannot afford. With 34 percent of women facing physical violence and nearly a third of girls experiencing sexual violence before they turn 18, the home is often the most dangerous place to be. We are told that the police and the courts are there to protect us, yet four out of five citizens know the truth: the system is slow, biased, and often rewards the perpetrator with silence. When a survivor has to choose between a slow-moving court case and her own safety, the system has already failed.

We have some of the most beautiful laws on the continent, all written on high-quality paper and stored in air-conditioned offices. But equality will not show up because of a bold theme or a corporate pledge. It will only show up when a woman can inherit land without a fight, walk into a bank without a male co-signer, and run for office without fearing for her life. Until the rules actually bite and the gatekeepers are forced to step aside, Give to Gain is just another way of asking women to be patient while the world stays exactly the same.
