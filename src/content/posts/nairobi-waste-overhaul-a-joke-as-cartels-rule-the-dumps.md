So, City Hall is bragging they're scooping up more than 4,000 tonnes of garbage a day now. Four times what they used to manage, they claim. Sounds impressive, right? Except, the same old story unfolds: the city is still drowning in filth, and the shadowy waste cartels, who've been living large on illegal dumping and shaking down everyone for years, are still calling the shots. Nothing changes, does it?

A new report, probably written on expensive paper that will also end up in a dumpsite, talks about "expanded operations" and clearing "dozens of illegal dumpsites." Thousands of truckloads gone from estates, markets, and those informal settlements where people actually live. It's all a grand show, a bit like those [Olympic Choreographer Benoît Richaud's Viral Jacket Swaps: A Spectacle Masking Deeper Realities](/posts/olympic-choreographer-beno-t-richaud-s-viral-jacket-swaps--a-spectacle-masking-deeper-realities) - lots of movement, but does it actually solve anything?

And Dandora? That legendary dumpsite, declared "full" two decades ago, is still swallowing thousands of tonnes daily. It's poisoning communities, a constant reminder of empty promises and broken pledges. They bring in a Ghanaian company, a new "solution," no doubt another expensive experiment that will line a few pockets before it too becomes another forgotten headline.

The real issue here is the "lucrative underground economy" the original headline vaguely hinted at. These cartels are too entrenched, too well-connected. They thrive on manipulation, on keeping the system broken so they can keep profiting. Until that rotten core is dealt with, all this talk of "overhauls" and "reforms" is just noise. More garbage collected means more opportunities for them to skim off the top, or to find new ways to exploit the mess. It’s a business, after all.
