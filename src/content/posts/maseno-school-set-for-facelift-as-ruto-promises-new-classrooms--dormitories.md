Maseno School's 'Facelift': A Tale of Old Buildings and Newer Promises

The President, William Ruto, showed up at Maseno School, one of Kenya's oldest, with a grand plan to fix up the place. Apparently, the buildings are still rocking the missionary vibe from 120 years ago. So, the big man and his "friends" - who knows who they are, probably the usual suspects - pledged 40 new classrooms. That's supposed to mean 1,600 more students. And get this, 20 of those classrooms should be ready by the end of the year so they can cram in another 800 students by January 2027. "No reason why we cannot admit 5,000 students," he boomed. Right. Because that's exactly what Kenya needs - more elite schools for the chosen few, while the rest of us are scrambling for basic necessities.

It's always the same old story, isn't it? The President tours these historical institutions, lauding their illustrious past and the "leaders, scholars, clergy, scientists, public servants, and patriots" they've churned out. Jaramogi Oginga Odinga, Obama Senior - yes, all important names. But while they're busy admiring the Oseno tree and Jaramogi's old digs, the reality for most Kenyan students is a far cry from academic excellence and unwavering commitment. The education budget is climbing, sure, KSh702 billion this year, rising to KSh767 billion. But let's not forget that [Kenya turns to Mozambique for gas as Gulf turmoil deepens](/posts/kenya-turns-to-mozambique-for-gas-as-gulf-turmoil-deepens) while our own infrastructure crumbles.

So, the Ministry of Lands and Housing is going to build dormitories for 2,000 kids, and the Education Ministry will chip in with a multipurpose hall. Sounds impressive on paper, doesn't it? And the Teachers Service Commission is told to absorb 25 of the 40 teachers the school's management has been funding. The rest will be hired "in the course of the year." Vague promises, just like everything else. We've heard this song and dance before. Every few years, there's a grand announcement, a few shiny new buildings appear, and then… crickets. The core problems of underfunding, inequality, and a system that prioritizes image over substance remain.

Maseno's claim to fame is its discipline, no student strikes in its history. That's great for them. But is that the benchmark for all of Kenya's education system? Consistency and discipline are lauded, yet for many, the consistency is in the struggle. The President talks about education as a strategic investment, and the numbers do look good on the surface - 100,000 teachers hired, 23,000 classrooms, 1,600 labs. But we're still talking about an education system that, for the majority, feels like a constant uphill battle, a far cry from the lofty ideals preached at Maseno.

This "facelift" is just another PR exercise, a shiny new coat of paint on an old edifice while the foundations remain shaky. They’ll build the classrooms, the dorms, the hall, and the politicians will pat themselves on the back. Meanwhile, the majority of Kenyan students will continue to face overcrowded classrooms, inadequate resources, and a future that feels less like destiny and more like a gamble. It's the same old script: a bit of fanfare for the privileged, while the rest of us are left to pick up the pieces.
