Ruto's fertiliser plan? More like a cartel's buffet.

The President’s grand vision of boosting agriculture with subsidised fertiliser is, shocker, going down the drain. Why? Because our beloved cartels, ever so efficient, have already figured out how to siphon off the good stuff, leaving the real farmers with dry hands and empty promises. This isn't a hiccup; it's a well-oiled machine of corruption, designed to line the pockets of the connected while the rest of us stare at barren fields.

Apparently, these networks are scooping up the subsidised fertiliser in truckloads, only to repackage it and flog it back to us at twice the price. It’s the Kenyan way, isn’t it? The government tries something nice, and the sharks circle. You can bet the North Rift farmers storming those NCPB depots aren't surprised. They’re the ones who actually get their hands dirty, only to find the fruits of their labour snatched away before they even bloom.

This whole charade reeks of the same old story. Remember that senator, Hamida Kibwana, complaining about former governors treating loans like pocket money? It’s that exact same entitled attitude, just with a different flavour of public resource. We’re constantly being fed lines about progress while the same old thieves feast at the trough. And don't even get me started on how Middle East conflicts might affect our exports - as if we didn't have enough local problems to worry about.

It makes you wonder if our leaders are even trying. This feels less like governance and more like a particularly cruel game of whack-a-mole, where every time one problem is addressed, two more pop up. Perhaps the President should just adopt that one politician’s strategy: dedicate a chunk of his time to dealing with opponents. Maybe that’s the only way to cut through the noise, though I wouldn't hold my breath. For now, it seems we're still stuck in the cycle, a bit like watching Zuckerberg’s Meta buy yet another AI agent, [Slop](/posts/zuckerberg-s-meta-buys-ai-agent--slop--social-network), while our actual food security crumbles.
