Clergy Call for Unity, Warn Against Divisive Politics Amidst Election Tensions











So, the holy men and women have spoken. Easter is here, and with it comes the annual chorus of calls for peace, unity, and reflection. You know the drill: religious and political leaders, all suddenly on the same page, waxing lyrical about harmony as we hurtle towards another election in 2027. Across the country, churches are packed, the Stations of the Cross are being trotted out again, and the faithful are being encouraged to imitate Christ's suffering. It’s all very dramatic, very performative, and as usual, conveniently timed.

Father Michael Ndichu from Holy Family Basilica, bless his heart, is urging us to embrace sacrifice and service, drawing parallels between Christ's suffering and our "present challenges." Corruption, ballooning taxes - yes, Father, those are indeed "crosses pressing on the lives of Kenyans." And who’s responsible for these crosses? Not divine intervention, I assure you. It’s the same people who are now telling us to pray for one another and for our leaders to serve. The irony is thicker than a week-old ugali. And let’s not forget Archbishop Jackson Ole Sapit, advising us to anchor ourselves in faith because, apparently, God will sort out our existential national doubts. Because when has faith alone ever paid the bills or fixed a broken system? It’s like telling a drowning man to have faith in the water.

Meanwhile, in Nakuru, Eldoret, and all the other places where life is just a bit more… rural, the same script is being followed. Shops are closed, roads are quiet, and everyone is busy contemplating the divine while the real world outside the church doors continues its relentless march of problems. Bishop Dominic Kimengich in Eldoret is leading the charge, and in Trans Nzoia, Bishop Benjamin Tarus is warning against ethnic politics. It’s all very commendable, of course. But one wonders if these calls for unity are genuine or just another tactic to keep us docile and distracted while the wheels of power continue to grind us down, much like the issues discussed in [The Parliament Sticker: Your VIP Permit to Kill and Keep Partying](/posts/the-parliament-sticker--your-vip-permit-to-kill-and-keep-partying).

Even in Homa Bay, where they’re pushing the *Niko Kadi* campaign to register voters, the sentiment is the same. Bishop William Abuka talks of civic duty and peaceful elections, while Pastor Kennedy Odeny frets about low voter registration. It’s all about the ballot box, the future leadership, and making "sober choices." Archbishop Martin Kivuva in Mombasa is even more direct, warning against bribery and urging us to elect leaders we know. It’s like they’ve suddenly woken up to the fact that elections matter, as if we haven’t been telling them this for years.

And then there are the national leaders, President Ruto and Deputy President Kindiki, chiming in with messages of hope, resilience, and renewal. They link the resurrection to new beginnings, offering strength, unity, and love to our "beloved nation." It’s a nice sentiment, a comforting narrative for those who need it. But for those of us who live with the daily grind of corruption, the ever-increasing cost of living, and the sheer exhaustion of navigating a system that seems designed to keep us down, these Easter pronouncements feel less like divine inspiration and more like the same old political theatre. Another year, another set of promises, and the same old problems waiting for us after the holiday weekend.
