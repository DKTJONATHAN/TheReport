Ruto Bets on Green Economy: Kenya Hunts European Investment, Again.

Warning: Another diplomatic tour, another promise of jobs and economic growth. Don't hold your breath for the tangible results.

President Ruto's latest European jaunt, this time to Belgium and Norway, is just another chapter in Kenya's perennial quest for foreign cash. The buzzwords are all there: green investment, renewable energy, electric mobility, and the ever-elusive "clean technology." It's the same song and dance, just with a fresh coat of eco-friendly paint. They're hoping to charm their way into becoming Africa's "green investment hub," a title that sounds suspiciously like a marketing gimmick designed to attract money while the real issues, like our already drowning [Nairobi is Drowning While the 'Blessed' Crowd Scrambles for Dry Ground](/posts/nairobi-is-drowning-while-the--blessed--crowd-scrambles-for-dry-ground), remain unaddressed.

The President is busy hobnobbing with Norwegians, who apparently are the gurus of clean energy and electric cars. Meanwhile, back home, we’re supposed to believe that more geothermal and hydro power will magically translate into industrial factories and jobs for the thousands of young Kenyans flooding the market annually. It’s a nice thought, I suppose, but history shows us these grand pronouncements rarely deliver on their promises. We've heard about attracting EV assembly plants and battery tech before; let's see if this time it's more than just talk.

Then there's Belgium and the EU trade agreement. Duty-free access for our tea and coffee is old news. Now, it’s about "value addition" and "green manufacturing." They want European investors to set up shop, create jobs, and boost exports. It all sounds very optimistic, but considering the track record of government promises, it’s likely just another opportunity for politicians to collect per diems while the common mwananchi waits for crumbs.

Finland, with its talk of technology and digitization, is meant to complement this "green growth agenda." It's a familiar narrative: link every development to climate change and sustainability, hoping to tap into the current global trend of "climate-conscious financing." Kenya’s playing the stability card, hoping to be the chosen one for East African investment. The real test, however, won't be in the fancy diplomatic statements made in Helsinki or Oslo, but whether any of these "promising" investments actually materialize into jobs and a better life for us here on the ground.
