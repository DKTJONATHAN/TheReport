Men With Female Surnames: Just Another Nairobi Drama, Or What?

The story about men in Kenya, particularly Kikuyu men, taking their mother's surnames sounds like typical Nairobi theatre. It's a bit of a circus, really. Some men are doing it out of "love and respect" for their mothers, others because their fathers were nowhere to be found. It’s not really about changing attitudes, is it? It’s more about the messy reality of absent fathers and mothers having to pick up the slack. And then there are the musicians, like Peter Kĩgia wa Esther, who’s turned it into a brand. It’s all very dramatic, and frankly, a bit exhausting to keep track of.

Apparently, this whole thing has caused quite a stir. Some folks are calling it a sign of changing times, others are clutching their pearls and crying about emasculation. You've got these motivational speakers like Robert Burale yapping about masculinity, and then you have TV personalities like Fred Mũitĩrĩrĩ who apparently got depressed over it all. Honestly, it’s just another avenue for people to argue and create unnecessary drama in a country that’s got enough real problems to deal with. It’s like watching a poorly written telenovela unfold on the streets of Nairobi.

The whole "cultural anomaly" argument is just a fancy way of saying things are getting complicated. Blaming absent fathers and inheritance disputes is fine, but let's be real, it's mostly about social inconvenience and ego. The Kikuyu tracing their lineage from ten daughters is a nice story, but it doesn't pay the bills or stop the gossip. It’s all a bit of a scramble to find an identity when the traditional patriarchal structure is showing cracks, and people are scrambling to adapt. It’s not a revolution, it’s just people trying to navigate a society that’s shifting under their feet, often clumsily.

Even the academics are weighing in, talking about the "strength of women" and "modern realities." It’s all very intellectual, but at the end of the day, it’s about fathers not taking responsibility. When women have to be both mother and father, it’s not a triumph of female strength, it's a failure of male responsibility. And the backlash? It’s just men feeling threatened because their traditional roles are being challenged, and they’re lashing out. This isn’t about beating the odds; it’s about men struggling to adjust to a world where they’re not automatically the unquestioned head of the household. It makes you wonder if we'll ever escape the endless cycle of drama, much like the controversies surrounding artists like Burna Boy.
