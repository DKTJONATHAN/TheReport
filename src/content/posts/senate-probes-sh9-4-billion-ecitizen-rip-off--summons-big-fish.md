So, the Senate has decided to grace us with their presence by "probing" the eCitizen platform. Apparently, Sh9.4 billion has been spirited away, not to improve our lives, but to line the pockets of some "private firms." You know, the usual suspects. They're summoning the big names, the Principal Secretaries and the heads of entities like Equity Bank and Pesa Flow Limited. Because clearly, they're the ones who know exactly where all that money went.

The Public Accounts Committee, chaired by this Tindi Mwale fellow, wants to know how these entities got the "licensing and approvals" to collect our hard-earned cash in the first place. And, of course, how it was "irregularly diverted." Let's be real, they'll get a lot of fancy words and maybe a few apologies, but don't hold your breath for any real accountability. This is Kenya, after all. Remember how passports suddenly became a privilege for the well-connected? It’s the same old tune, just a different instrument. [Kenya’s Passport Gate: When Your Bank Account Vets You Better Than the NIS](/posts/kenya-s-passport-gate--when-your-bank-account-vets-you-better-than-the-nis).

The Auditor General's report is screaming that the government doesn't even have full control of the platform, relying on some vendor. A single point of failure, they say. As if we needed a report to tell us that. It’s like finding out the fox is guarding the henhouse, but only after half the chickens are gone.

Now, the Treasury PS, Chris Kiptoo, is out here assuring everyone that the platform is "fully owned by the government" and was handed over in January 2023. Right. And I'm sure those private firms just decided to "unconditionally hand over" all the money they collected for free. The platform has seen revenue jump from Sh60 million to Sh600 million daily. Growth, they call it. I call it a gravy train with a few extra stops.

They’re talking about draft regulations and "enhancing system integrity, accountability and transparency." It's all just noise to make us feel like something is being done. They'll probably end up blaming a junior clerk or a rogue system, and then we'll all move on until the next Sh9.4 billion goes missing.

So, buckle up, Kenyans. The investigation is on, the players are being questioned, and the excuses are already being drafted. Just another day in paradise.
