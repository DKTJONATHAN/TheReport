So, the big man himself, Murkomen, waltzes into Kiganjo and declares they’re going to be “tougher” on police recruitment. Apparently, 54 recruits were shown the door for being indisciplined and lacking integrity. Shocking, I know. It’s almost as if we’re surprised that the people meant to enforce the law sometimes have issues with, you know, the law. This whole psychometric assessment and drug testing revamp sounds like a good show, but we’ll see how long it lasts before the usual Nairobi corruption crawls back in.

The CS is promising these new recruits will help tackle gangs and political goons. Funny how that works. The same politicians wailing about "goons" yesterday are now complaining when the police actually do something about them. It’s a classic Nairobi two-step. These recruits are meant to be the cavalry against the very chaos politicians often stir up. Let's just hope they don't end up as another cog in the wheel of political intimidation.

And to top it all off, Murkomen wants a National Police Service University. A regional hub, no less. Because clearly, what Kenya needs is another institution that will probably end up needing its own cleanup down the line. We’ve got enough institutions that are more about paper-pushing and less about actual results. It's a grand vision, I suppose, but one that sounds suspiciously like a way to create more jobs for the connected.

This whole push for better discipline and professionalism feels like putting lipstick on a pig. We’ve seen these pronouncements before. The rot runs deep, and it’s not just about a few bad apples. It’s systemic. Until they address the real issues, like the economic pressures that drive some men to seek solutions from clinics offering questionable "manhood power" treatments, or the pervasive influence of those who operate with impunity, these recruitment drives are just window dressing. We've seen how the powerful operate with their "Junk Choppers and Stolen Sheets," and you can bet they’ll find a way to bypass any new regulations.

They’re talking about fighting insecurity, but the real insecurity often comes from the top, fueled by greed and a desperate need to hold onto power. Whether it's debt traps or rigged systems, the people at the bottom are always the ones paying the price. This police recruitment saga is just another chapter in the same old story. Let’s see how long it takes for the "tougher measures" to become just as flexible as the last ones.
