So, the big Africa-France Summit happened here in Nairobi, and what do you know, nearly 100 Congolese folks, mostly Banyamulenge, decided to voice their concerns. Naturally, the Kenyan police, with their hands already full managing traffic diversions that made my morning commute feel like a stroll in the park, decided these protesters were a bigger threat than a rogue matatu. Apparently, the mere presence of 31 Heads of State meant any form of demonstration, no matter how well-intentioned, was a capital offense. It's almost as if their opinions were more inconvenient than a broken-down lorry on Uhuru Highway.

Nairobi Regional Police Commander Issa Mohamud, bless his uniform, defended the arrests by saying these foreigners were just, well, being foreigners and causing a disturbance. He even mentioned a couple of South Koreans, a Brit, and a Greek were also nabbed, as if that somehow legitimizes the mass detention. But let's be real, the focus was on the Congolese. He claims they were trying to protest near hotels where these esteemed leaders were staying. Because, heaven forbid, a leader might have to witness someone expressing unhappiness while they're busy discussing "climate financing" and other such highfalutin matters. It's a classic case of prioritizing photo ops over genuine concerns, much like the convoluted excuses we hear about infrastructure projects that seem to benefit everyone but the common mwananchi.

The police boss went on to drone about unlawful assembly, obstruction, and the cardinal sin of not notifying authorities. Honestly, did they expect these individuals to fill out a permit application while dodging bullets back home? The entire operation was a masterclass in overzealousness, dressed up as fulfilling international obligations. It's the same old song and dance: when powerful people visit, the local population gets a taste of the iron fist, all for the sake of maintaining appearances. It reminds me of how sometimes, even simple technological upgrades, like [Switch 2 USB-C Docks]( /posts/switch-2-usb-c-docks--the-illusion-of-choice-for-the-gullible-gamer), are presented as groundbreaking when they're just the bare minimum.

And the security crackdown didn't stop at disgruntled foreigners. Oh no. While the big wigs were hobnobbing, the boys in blue were busy finding a pistol with ammo in Kayole and raiding suspected criminal motorcycle gangs. They even recovered a haul of stolen mobile phones. So, while the international community was busy discussing global issues, Nairobi was still dealing with its own brand of everyday chaos, with the police performing their usual balancing act between summit security and street-level crime. It’s a bit like watching [Noah Wyle's 'The Pitt'](/posts/noah-wyle-s--the-pitt---a-e-drama-exposes-healthcare-s-grim-reality-amidst-producer-s-naivete), where the drama unfolds while the real issues simmer beneath the surface.

Of course, the police are touting the summit as a success, free from diplomatic incidents. They even thanked Kenyans for their "patience and cooperation." Patience? Try resignation. Cooperation? Try coercion. The roads were a nightmare, movement was restricted, and all for what? So a bunch of leaders could have a chat and a photo opportunity. Meanwhile, back on the ground, life continues, with its own set of problems that these summits rarely address. It’s a system designed to keep the wheels of power turning, even if it means crushing a few voices along the way.

In the end, the Congolese were pardoned after embassy officials understood the "seriousness of the matter." Translation: they realized it was a PR mess waiting to happen and decided to cut their losses. So, 99 people were detained, their right to protest trampled, and for what? A smooth summit, a few handshakes, and a whole lot of traffic jams. Just another day in the life of a city trying to host the world while its own people are just trying to get by.
