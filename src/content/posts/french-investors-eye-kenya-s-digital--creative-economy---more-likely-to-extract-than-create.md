So, the French are here again, eyeing our "digital economy" and "creative industries." According to Trade and Industry Principal Secretary Regina Ombam, our youth are the golden ticket, driving "growth in digital trade and services." Naturally, the biggest trade benefit Kenya will gain is… more digital trade and services. Groundbreaking stuff. It's almost like they've discovered a new continent, except it’s just people on laptops.

Ambassador Arnaud Suquet chimes in about French startups partnering with Kenyan innovators in AI and digital innovation. Partnering. That's a nice word for it. It usually means they come with the big cheques and the even bigger ideas about how to make money off our talent. With 140 French companies now here, up from a measly 40 in 2013, it’s clear they’ve found Kenya a rather fertile ground for their ambitions.

And what are we getting out of this grand partnership? In 2024, Kenya coughed up Sh29.5 billion for French pharmaceuticals, machinery, and aircraft parts. We, on the other hand, managed to export Sh13.8 billion of flowers, fruits, and vegetables. So, while they're selling us their advanced tech, we're selling them our produce. It’s a classic exchange: we provide the raw materials and the labor, they provide the finished products and take the lion's share of the profits. It’s not unlike how AI agents are now running the back office at insurance giants, doing the work with fewer overheads.

The PS also harps on about the "biggest trade benefit." It’s always about what Kenya is *likely* to gain. These are the same empty promises we hear every time foreign money rolls into town. They talk about partnerships, innovation, and growth, but the reality is a widening trade deficit and a system that seems designed to funnel wealth upwards and outwards. We're told our youthful population is a boon, but it sounds more like a readily available, cheap workforce for someone else’s digital empire.

While we're busy celebrating "green finance booms" and "carbon markets," and the Stanbic Bank Purchasing Managers’ Index is doing its usual dive, it’s easy to get distracted. The reality is, when foreign investors "eye" our sectors, it’s not for our benefit; it’s for theirs. They see a market ripe for exploitation, a pool of eager young talent willing to work for less, and a regulatory environment that’s probably just as malleable as their business plans.
