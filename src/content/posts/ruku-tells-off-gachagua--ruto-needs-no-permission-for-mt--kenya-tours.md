So, Ruku, the Public Service and Human Capital Development CS, has decided to wade into the mud with Gachagua. Apparently, the Deputy President thinks he's the gatekeeper of Mt. Kenya, telling residents to "listen to President Ruto and let him go." Honestly, who does Gachagua think he is, the undisputed king of the mountain? Ruto is the President of *all* Kenyans, not just the ones living in Rift Valley or wherever Gachagua feels entitled to dictate terms. The man has a job to do, launching projects, inspecting development - the usual song and dance.

Ruku’s got a point, though. Gachagua trying to play kingmaker and division-monger ahead of 2027 is just sad. He keeps talking about his presidential ambitions, but Ruku’s right to bring up that impeachment talk. An impeached leader lecturing others on leadership and unity? That’s like a rat giving a sermon on sanitation. It’s all about positioning for the next election, of course. This whole political circus is starting to feel like we're just paying more coins for the boys in blue, while our actual needs get sidelined. [More Coins for the Boys in Blue: A Price Tag on Our Silence](/posts/more-coins-for-the-boys-in-blue--a-price-tag-on-our-silence).

And let’s not forget Ruku’s jabs at Gitonga Mukunji, the Manyatta MP. Apparently, Mukunji is too busy with "one-term politics" and spewing rhetoric to actually *do* anything for his constituents. Ruku's basically saying the MP is more concerned with his own gubernatorial ambitions than the people who elected him. Leadership, Ruku points out, is about delivering services, not chasing online likes or shouting empty slogans. It's a good reminder that politicians are judged by what they build, not what they boast about.

Then there's the Gachagua-Munya-Kawira triangle. Ruku's also telling off Peter Munya and Kawira Mwangaza for attacking Deputy President Kithure Kindiki. Apparently, they need to "respect him" and acknowledge his growing clout in Mt. Kenya. It’s all a scramble for influence, isn’t it? Everyone jockeying for position while the rest of us are left wondering when our lives will actually improve.

Ruku, meanwhile, is out there defending the Kenya Kwanza administration, claiming Ruto has delivered on his promises. Development projects everywhere, he says. Youth empowerment, women empowerment, better livelihoods - the usual laundry list of promises that sound good in church but often fall flat in reality. It's a convenient narrative, especially when you're trying to deflect criticism and shore up support.

Ultimately, it’s just more political noise. Gachagua wants to control the narrative, Ruku wants to counter it, and everyone else is just trying to survive the economic storm. The President can tour wherever he pleases, that's his job. The real question is, will any of this translate into tangible improvements for the average Kenyan, or is it just another round of self-serving political theater?
