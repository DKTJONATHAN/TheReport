The State House tea must be exceptionally sweet these days. President William Ruto spent his Friday playing the benevolent father figure, pledging Sh2 billion to the Kenya Methodist University (KeMU). Let us ignore the fact that the government actually owes the institution over Sh5 billion. In Nairobi, we are supposed to clap when someone pays back less than half of what they owe your budget. It is a classic move: host the church leadership, offer a fraction of the debt, and call it a "pledge" while the cameras are rolling.

This financial crumbs-tossing comes at a time when the math simply refuses to make sense. We have 270,715 students from the 2025 KCSE class looking for a future, yet the state is already choking on the tuition debt of those who came before them. The Ministry of Education is busy presenting "budgetary requirements" to committees, which is just polite government-speak for admitting the pockets are empty. We are told to celebrate [East Africa's Economic Transformation](/posts/nairobi-infrastructure) and its supposed growth surge, but that growth seems to vanish whenever it is time to pay for a student's degree.

Private universities are being told to sit down and wait for the release of billions that may never actually arrive in full. These institutions were brought in to save the state from the embarrassment of overcrowded public universities, but now they are just another line item on a list of people the government is ghosting. When the most educated minds in the country are left to rot because the state cannot honor its tuition agreements, we are not just failing a budget; we are killing the only hope this country has left.

The irony is thick enough to choke on. The President makes this assurance just one day after his officials were begging for money in Parliament. It is a cycle of empty promises and selective payments that does nothing to address the structural decay of our education system. While some might focus on global issues like [The Silicon Crisis](/posts/the-silicon-crisis--valve-s-hardware-ambitions-hit-the-wall-of-corporate-greed) and corporate greed abroad, our own brand of local corporate greed is much more efficient at destroying dreams. We are mass-producing graduates for a market that can't even keep the school gates open.

Do not hold your breath for that Sh2 billion to actually hit the bank accounts today. In this city, a promise is just a way to buy twenty-four hours of peace before the next crisis hits. The 2025 cohort is walking straight into a trap where the degrees are expensive, the debt is mounting, and the government's word is worth less than a torn ten-shilling note. KeMU got a headline, but the students got the same old story of being used as pawns in a political game of "pay-as-you-feel."
