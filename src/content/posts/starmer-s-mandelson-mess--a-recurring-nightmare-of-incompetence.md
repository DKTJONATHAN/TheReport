The persistent stench of the Peter Mandelson ambassadorship debacle is proving to be a particularly tenacious political pathogen for Sir Keir Starmer. Despite the recent spectacle of sacking Sir Olly Robbins, an act designed to appease the furious establishment and a public increasingly weary of perceived incompetence, the damage lingers, a testament to the fundamental rot at the heart of Labour's current predicament. As one insider cynically observes, Starmer’s repetitive pronouncements of anger are lost on a public who feel precisely the same way about him.

The initial decision to bestow one of the plum diplomatic posts upon Mandelson was, in retrospect, an act of breathtaking, almost cosmic, foolishness. The revelation that he failed security checks, a fact described by one government source as "absolutely mental," reveals a catastrophic lapse in judgment bordering on willful blindness. This wasn't a minor oversight; it was a deliberate act of "don't ask, don't tell" that has festered, poisoning the political atmosphere for months.

One must wade through the bureaucratic morass of Whitehall to grasp the full extent of this mess. It’s now clear that Number 10 was apprised of Mandelson's unsavory connections to the convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein as far back as September, according to the Cabinet Office's Propriety Department. Starmer's team, in a display of either remarkable naivete or wilful ignorance, saw fit to ask Mandelson a mere three follow-up questions, accepting his assurances despite now admitting he "misled them."

The subsequent "developed vetting," a supposedly forensic security check involving interviews and financial scrutiny, was conducted independently. Astonishingly, despite its exhaustive nature and the agency's recommendation *against* the appointment, no concerns were officially flagged to ministers. This is where the labyrinthine nature of process collides head on with political reality. Under the obscure Section 3 of the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act, ministers are insulated from direct influence over security vetting. The vetting agency merely offers recommendations to Foreign Office officials, much like a credit agency informs a bank’s lending decision.

The concerns, however, did reach Sir Olly Robbins, the most senior official at the Foreign Office. Yet, he apparently concluded these risks were "manageable." The notion that vetting is about "managing risk" rather than a definitive pass or fail is a convenient alibi for a catastrophic failure. The political reality is that by the time Robbins' department weighed in, Number 10 was already expecting Mandelson, a fact underscored by the Prime Minister’s personal desire for the appointment.

The decision by a supposedly seasoned official like Robbins to proceed without lodging a formal concern, especially given the paper trail, defies logic. The insistence from an ally that "He hasn't done anything wrong" rings hollow against the backdrop of the civil service code, which mandates officials to correct errors and avoid misleading ministers or Parliament. The fact that ministers, including then Foreign Secretary David Lammy, failed to probe the vetting process for such a high-profile appointment is not just astonishing; it's a blatant display of incompetence.

The crux of the matter is not merely the botched decision-making, but the protracted obfuscation. Despite repeated assurances of exhaustive reviews, the truth about Mandelson's vetting emerged not from ministerial transparency, but from persistent journalistic inquiry. The official account, that no one outside the Foreign Office was aware of the vetting issues between September and now, strains credulity to its breaking point. It simply "doesn't stack up."

For a leader like Starmer, a former chief prosecutor, to remain seemingly oblivious to the security clearance status of a controversial appointee during months of intense scrutiny is not just a personal failing; it suggests he has "never, at any point, gripped the danger." This narrative of detachment, of being a passive observer rather than an active participant in governance, further cements the perception of a leader "hopelessly out of touch with the detail, not interested in the actual processes of government and devoid of any political antenna."

The resurgence of this scandal is particularly galling given Labour’s current electoral vulnerability. The association with the dark underbelly of power, the unseemly links to Jeffrey Epstein, and the stark contrast with Starmer’s carefully cultivated "Mr Rules" persona, are deeply damaging. Allies lament that it’s "so painful to Keir," as it erodes his carefully constructed image of integrity. This is not simply an embarrassing footnote; it’s a self-inflicted wound that threatens to derail campaigns and alienate voters just weeks before crucial elections. The current bout of panic, the rapid scapegoating of Robbins, and the persistent denial of responsibility from Number 10 all point to a leadership that is reactive, incompetent, and fundamentally incapable of learning from its own catastrophic mistakes.
