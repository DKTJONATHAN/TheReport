So, the Duchess of Edinburgh, Sophie Helen Rhys-Jones, graced State House today with her presence. Another day, another royal photo-op while the rest of us are stuck in traffic or trying to figure out how to pay for sugar. President Ruto is busy singing about the 2025-30 Kenya-UK Strategic Partnership as if a fresh document can magically erase the reality of our struggling economy. They talk about "deepening collaboration," but for the average person in Nairobi, these meetings are just high-class tea parties.

Ruto is particularly excited about the Nairobi Railway City. Apparently, it is going to "transform urban mobility" and "spur commercial development." We have heard this script before. Usually, "transforming" means more fancy buildings that none of us can afford to enter, while the actual commuters are left fighting for space in dilapidated matatus. It is the classic "flagship project" trap where the elite get a new playground and the rest of us get the bill.

The trade numbers for 2025 were thrown around like confetti. Kenya exported goods worth Sh243 billion to the UK, supposedly creating a "favorable trade balance." That is wonderful for the balance sheets in some government office, but has anyone checked the price of bread lately? A trade surplus does not mean much when the profits are swallowed by the usual suspects before they ever reach the pockets of the workers.

Then there is the talk of security and "regional stability." It is hard to take these British promises seriously when their presence often leaves a trail of destruction behind. We are still waiting for full accountability even after the news that the [UK to pay KSh 500 million in compensation for devastating 2021 fire caused by British troops in Kenya](/posts/UK-to-pay-ksh-500-million-to-laikipia-county-residents), a reminder that their "partnership" often comes with a heavy price for the locals in Laikipia.

In the end, this visit is just more of the same. They talk about green growth, climate action, and sustainable development because those are the buzzwords that get donor funding moving. But for the Kenyan on the street, Sophie’s visit is just another headline in a long series of empty promises. We will keep our "strategic partnership" and they will keep their tea, while the "favorable trade balance" remains a ghost we can never quite catch.
