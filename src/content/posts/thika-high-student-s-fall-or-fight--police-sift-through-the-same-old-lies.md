THIKA HIGH STUDENT'S FALL OR FIGHT? POLICE SIFT THROUGH THE SAME OLD LIES

A 16-year-old student is dead at Thika High, and the usual song and dance has begun. Was it a fight, or a "fatal fall"? The police are investigating, the school is offering "thoughts and prayers," and the family, well, they're leaving it to God. Just another Tuesday in Kenya, where the truth is as elusive as a functioning public service.

The official line from Dr. Mugambi Nkingo of the Central Region Criminal Investigations is that it could be assault or an accident. Conveniently, a fellow student has been arrested. Because when something bad happens, especially in a school full of impressionable boys, there’s always a scapegoat ready. The mother, Ms. Noor Hassan, is surrendering to Allah’s mercies - a noble sentiment, but one that doesn’t explain how her son ended up dead. The family spokesperson, Jamal Aidid, is being "taken in circles," which is standard procedure when officialdom decides to obscure rather than clarify. They want evidence, witness statements, CCTV footage - all the things that usually go missing in these high-profile cases. It’s like Nairobi’s favorite game: [Hunt the Whistleblower While the Thief Escapes](/posts/nairobi-s-favorite-game--hunt-the-whistleblower-while-the-thief-escapes).

The school principal, Julius Muraya, is "cooperating" and assures everyone that "facts will be established." He admits there was an "altercation" and a "fatal fall," followed by a hurried trip to the hospital where the boy was pronounced dead. It’s a sad story, he says, and they are committed to justice and healing. This is the same script we hear every time. The teachers were alerted, first aid was administered, the unfortunate death was confirmed. It all sounds very orderly, very concerned. But does anyone actually believe it?

The police have opened a file, OB number 02/11/07/26, because paperwork is crucial, even if the facts are not. The preliminary report, from Thika West Police Station, mentions three witnesses who claim the deceased and the suspect "quarrelled over an issue and an altercation ensued." The crucial bit, "how the fatal fall occurred," remains unclear. This is where the investigation often grinds to a halt, buried under layers of bureaucracy and carefully worded statements.

The report recommends revisiting the scene and retrieving CCTV footage, a logical step that often yields little. The school principal, meanwhile, is grappling with the fallout, as are the parents of both students. The Teachers Service Commission is also involved, presumably to ensure that the right disciplinary measures are taken, or at least that the right people are blamed.

It's a tragic end for a 16-year-old, but in Kenya, tragic ends are often just the beginning of a long, drawn-out process of obfuscation and selective memory. The family wants justice, the police promise it, and the school expresses sorrow. But the real question is whether anyone will ever truly know what happened that night on July 10, 2026.
