One year after the "landmark" pact between Ruto and the late Raila Odinga, Kenya's so-called broad-based government is more of a joke than a revolution. Signed in March 2025 with much fanfare about calming tensions after those youth-led protests - the ones where young Kenyans risked life and limb for a better future - it’s now March 2026, and the only thing calm is the deep pockets of a few. The big question echoing through the streets of Nairobi isn't about national unity, it's about who cashed in while the rest of us are still waiting for crumbs.

This whole 10-point MoU was born out of vulnerability, a desperate scramble by Ruto's administration after the 2024 anti-tax chaos. They sold it as a "national reset," brimming with promises from the NADCO report: strengthen devolution, protect protests, create youth jobs, fight corruption, slash debt, and even compensate victims of past demonstrations. They've even floated a Ksh2 billion fund for those affected by protests since 2017. But the details? As clear as the bottom of a muddy Nairobi river. We hear talk of a court-mandated panel, but the process feels as opaque as Uncle Sam's dealings with Kenya over our medical tea. See how that works out for us? Inside ODM, it’s a circus. Oburu Oginga and Gladys Wanga want to pretend this is a never-ending love story, a partnership for stability. But Edwin Sifuna and James Orengo? They’re seeing it for what it is: an MoU that’s expired, especially with Odinga gone. They rightly point out that extending this without "Baba's" signature is a slap in the face to his legacy and a betrayal of the opposition's purpose. Godfrey Osotsi, bless his heart, is calling out the "pro-regime voices" trying to rewrite history, claiming the agreement had no timeline. Honestly, the audacity.

Despite the internal bickering, the analysts, with their fancy degrees, are saying ODM got the lion's share. Odinga's allies, like John Mbadi at Treasury and Hassan Joho in mining, are now comfortably ensconced in government. Parliament has become a lot easier to navigate for the ruling party, with less "resistance." But back in the UDA camp, there's a lot of quiet grumbling. The loyalists who expected positions after the 2022 win are now watching ODM figures get them. Ruto got his stability, yes, but at the cost of sharing the spoils with his old rival. A classic political trade-off, where the ordinary Kenyan is always the one left holding the short end of the stick.

The oversight of this whole mess has been a joke. A monitoring committee formed in August 2025 was supposed to track reforms for six months, but things fizzled out after Odinga's death in October 2025. Now, meetings are just opportunities for more arguments. UDA's Cecily Mbarire is complaining about the lack of recommendations on the gender rule, while Mathare MP Anthony Oluoch wants revisions to the committee's report. Even Ruto himself is defending the arrangement, saying commitments were made to Kenyans, not to specific factions. But when has that ever stopped politicians from looking out for themselves?

In the end, this broad-based government experiment has done what many expected: it fractured the opposition while stabilizing Ruto's administration in the short term. But it's also blurred the lines between who's supposed to be governing and who's supposed to be holding them accountable. Heading into the 2027 elections, this might just redefine what being in opposition even means. And for the rest of us? We're just waiting to see if any of those ambitious promises ever translate into actual change, or if it's just another round of political theatre.
