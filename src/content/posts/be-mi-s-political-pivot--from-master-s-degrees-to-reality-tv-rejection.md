Let’s talk about Abimbola Olamide Obafemi-Samuel, or "Be’Mi" for those of us who don’t have all day to recite a family tree. This British-Nigerian singer is the latest to try the "I’m a deep storyteller" routine to mask what looks like a standard-issue thirst for fame. She’s got an undergraduate degree in Politics and a Master’s in Global Politics and Economics. Usually, that leads to a desk at the UN or a soul-crushing job in Whitehall, but apparently, the lure of the "Queen of the Night" title was too strong. It is the classic "expensive education used as a backup plan" vibe that we see far too often in this industry.

The centerpiece of her "inspiring" story is failing to get onto Big Brother Naija. She auditioned in her fourth year of university, didn’t get picked, and then tells us it was a "full-circle moment" when her song was played in the house years later. Darling, let’s be real. Failing a reality TV audition isn't a "setback," it is a mercy for the viewers. While she’s framing this as a triumphant comeback, it feels more like the musical equivalent of [Wargames for the Elites and Empty Pockets for the Rest](/posts/wargames-for-the-elites-and-empty-pockets-for-the-rest) where the kids with the degrees still end up chasing the same 15 minutes of fame as everyone else.

Be’Mi calls herself the "Queen of the Night," claiming it reflects her energy and presence. It is a bit much for someone whose musical foundation was freestyling during lunch breaks in secondary school. The night is already crowded with every other aspiring Afro-fusion artist with a ring light and a dream. She talks about "expressing emotions" and "connecting deeply," which is industry-speak for "I hope this TikTok sound goes viral so I don't have to use that Master's degree."

Her influences are just as predictable: Rihanna, Tiwa Savage, and Nicki Minaj. It’s the standard-issue starter pack for any girl who thinks writing a poem in English class makes her the next great lyricist. She says she learned Yoruba just to incorporate it into her music, which is a nice touch of cultural branding, but we see the strategy. It’s all about the "vibe" and the "identity" until the actual music has to stand on its own without the academic CV attached.

In the end, she promises "growth." We’ve heard that one before. Usually, "growth" means a higher production budget and more expensive fashion shoots, which she admits is one of her inspirations. If she wants to reach audiences around the world, she’ll need more than a "full-circle" BBNaija anecdote to keep us interested. Sweetie, just give us a beat we can actually dance to and leave the storytelling to the people who aren't trying to sell us a "Queen" persona before they've even had a chart topper.
