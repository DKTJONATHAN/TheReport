Men with Female Surnames Standing Up to Ridicule in Kenya: More Like Crawling, Really









So, the latest hot topic in Kenya is men, particularly Kikuyu men, deciding to take their mother's name. Apparently, this is a sign of changing times, a salute to women's influence. From where I'm standing in Nairobi, it looks less like a revolution and more like another excuse for men to avoid responsibility. They're either given the name because dad vanished, or they "choose" it to "honor" their mothers. Honor? Or a convenient way to distance themselves from absent fathers, while still claiming some sort of lineage? It’s a twisted logic, even for us Kenyans.

This whole thing is supposed to be about respecting mothers. Oh, please. Peter Kĩgia, the old musician, calls it respect. Good for him, he's old enough to do what he wants. But now these young artists, plastered all over town, are using their mother's names as their stage names. "Wa Esther," "Wa Jane." It’s a catchy gimmick, sure, but are they really honoring their mothers, or just trying to sound unique in a crowded market? It’s like pretending you’re a rebel when you’re just trying to sell more tickets. And let's not forget the likes of Simon Macharia Wangũi, who deliberately chose his mother's name because his father was a ghost. So, it's about honoring Mum, or running from Dad? Make up your minds.

The backlash, of course, is louder than a matatu tout on a Friday evening. Men are worried about their masculinity. Can you believe it? Apparently, having a female surname is "a yoke around men's necks," turning them into weaklings. Fred Mũitĩrĩrĩ, the TV personality, even dropped his mother's name due to embarrassment and depression. So much for honoring Mum. It’s a mess. This whole cultural anomaly, as the experts call it, is being pushed by single-mother families becoming more common. But even then, tradition dictates an uncle should step in, unless, of course, there's property to be disputed. Suddenly, everyone's a bit too eager to avoid inheritance.

And the Kikuyu elders are weighing in, talking about lineage from Mũmbi. Yes, yes, we all know the origin story. But let's be real. While polygamy might have led to confusion with names, and women might be taking up more roles, this isn't some grand, enlightened shift. It's a symptom of breakdown. When fathers abandon their duties, and mothers are left to do everything, then sure, the child might be identified with the mother. But calling it a "strength of women" is a bit rich. It’s more like a desperate measure, and frankly, it's sad that it's being normalized. Just like promises from politicians, like Gachagua promising a single opposition candidate to 'unseat' Ruto - we'll see about that.

Some will tell you that if these men succeed with their female surnames, they’ll be seen as beating the odds. Bravo. But that just highlights the prejudice they’re supposedly overcoming. It's not about overcoming prejudice; it's about perpetuating a narrative where men are either absent or forced to adopt a name that still carries stigma. The real issue isn't the surname; it's the lack of accountability, the broken families, and the endless excuses. This whole debate is just another distraction from the real problems plaguing this country.
