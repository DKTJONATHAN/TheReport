Let’s not pretend this Brookings Institute study is some kind of benevolent beacon of hope in the AI apocalypse. It’s a calculated assessment of who gets tossed aside first and who might, *might*, land on their feet. The headline, "Workers Most at Risk of Being Hit by AI Layoffs Are Well-Positioned to Adapt," is pure corporate PR spin dressed up as academic research.

The good news, as they call it, is that out of 37.1 million workers in high AI exposure jobs, a hefty 26.5 million *supposedly* have "above-median adaptive capacity." This means they can *probably* find another job. Probably. As if the job market is a warm, welcoming place for everyone to seamlessly transition into new roles. We’ve seen the reality: Amazon, Vimeo, Pinterest, Block - thousands already on the chopping block, with CEOs like Jack Dorsey openly admitting AI is the reason for the cuts. They’re not waiting for a study to tell them who to fire; they’re acting on it now.

The study itself confirms what any cynical observer already suspects. The roles most vulnerable? Writers, customer service reps, translators. These are the jobs often filled by those with fewer safety nets, less leverage, and lower pay. And who bears the brunt of this "low adaptive capacity"? A staggering 6.1 million workers, predominantly in clerical and administrative roles, and surprise, surprise, roughly 86% of them are women. Add to this the geographical disadvantage - university towns and midsize cities in the Midwest and Mountain West - and you have a recipe for widespread economic distress for precisely those who can least afford it.

Meanwhile, the tech bros in web development, marketing, and IT, often in bustling urban centers with strong labor markets, are deemed "more likely to adapt." Of course, they are. They’re the ones building the machines that will replace everyone else.

The researchers claim they want to help policymakers focus resources. That’s a noble sentiment in a vacuum, but let’s be realistic. Policymakers are more concerned with appeasing corporate interests and managing the fallout than with genuinely supporting displaced workers. This study, while quantifying the problem, ultimately serves to underscore the inevitable restructuring of the labor market, a process driven by profit and technological advancement, with human cost as a regrettable but acceptable externality. The "well positioned" are merely those who can absorb the shock slightly better before their turn inevitably comes.
