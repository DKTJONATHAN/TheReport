So, the Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions (ODPP) is all about tackling human trafficking and the like, and they've got these new Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for crime scenes. Apparently, this is a big deal, highlighted during some "launch and Training of Trainers" event. Because what Kenya really needs is more training sessions and more paperwork.

Deputy Director Robert Oyiembo, speaking for the DPP, is gushing about technical support from CIVIPOL and their Better Migration Management Programme. He’s saying this partnership is “strengthening capacity.” We’ve heard that before. He’s also stressing that prosecutions depend on good evidence, and that police officers need to be diligent. Shocking, I know. Weak evidence leads to cases collapsing, he says. Like that’s news to anyone who’s ever seen a Kenyan court case. It’s almost as if they’re admitting the system is broken, but only *after* introducing more procedures.

These new SOPs are supposed to guide everyone on how to collect, document, preserve, and transmit evidence in trafficking cases. The ODPP reckons it will help identify evidentiary gaps early. Let’s be real, the only gaps they’re identifying are the ones that allow the actual criminals to walk free because someone forgot to dot an 'i' or cross a 't' on a form. It's a nice idea, but how many times have we seen these "guidelines" become just another hurdle to jump over, or worse, another tool for corruption? It’s like trying to fix a leaky roof with more paint.

Oyiembo claims these SOPs will create a more coordinated system between investigators and prosecutors. A more coordinated system. That’s rich. In Nairobi, coordination usually means more people shouting at each other. He also said it will enhance Kenya’s ability to respond to trafficking and other cross-border crimes, which are a threat to national security and human rights. They’re a violation of dignity, all that jazz. It’s all sounds very noble, but let’s remember the story about [The Door-to-Door Deception: Same Clowns, New Tents](/posts/the-door-to-door-deception--same-clowns--new-tents) - sometimes the biggest "initiatives" are just new packaging for the same old problems.

The ODPP is urging trainers to "fully engage" with the SOPs, "internalize the content," and "share the knowledge." Translation: more meetings, more talking, and hopefully, for their sake, less actual corruption. They’re calling it a "significant step." A significant step towards what? More job creation for trainers? More case dismissals due to procedural errors? Only time, and probably more press releases, will tell.

Ultimately, this is just another announcement from the government, another attempt to look like they're doing something about serious issues. While they’re busy training trainers on their new SOPs, the real criminals are probably already on their next flight out, or at least enjoying a nice cup of chai, thanks to the inefficiencies these new guidelines might actually create.
