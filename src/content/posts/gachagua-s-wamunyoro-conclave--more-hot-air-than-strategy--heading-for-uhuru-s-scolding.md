So, the "conclave" at Wamunyoro is happening. Forty-five days of Gachagua reportedly "strategizing" from his village. One wonders if the main strategy involves perfecting his tea-making skills or perfecting the art of complaining. They say this is where the Mt Kenya region will decide its political direction. More likely, it's where they'll decide who gets the best angle to whisper sweet nothings into Uhuru Kenyatta's ear. Because let's be real, what report are they preparing? A laundry list of grievances? A blueprint for more political drama?

The whispers are loud, of course. "Push for Gachagua to vie for the presidency," they say. Unless, of course, the courts decide to put a damper on things. It's the same old song and dance, isn't it? A bit like how Wetang'ula seems to be the "last man standing" in Kenya Kwanza's power games, for now. This alliance is apparently "grappling with mistrust, alleged 'backstabbing' and political 'set-ups'." Shocking. Who could have predicted that? It’s a predictable script, almost as predictable as George Were's widow learning about his death via social media.

They're trying to paint Gachagua as some sort of visionary, following in the footsteps of Kenyatta and Moi. Please. Kenyatta and Moi actually held power, and for a while. This sabbatical, this "retreat," smells more like a desperate attempt to stay relevant while the real power brokers in Nairobi continue their own backroom deals. The talk of "sabbatical" is just a nicer way of saying he's been sidelined and is trying to make it look like a strategic move.

The truth is, these political "conclaves" are usually just elaborate exercises in self-congratulation and blame-shifting. The "emerging developments" are likely just more promises that will be broken, more alliances that will crumble, and more citizens left wondering when politics will actually focus on their lives, instead of the ego trips of a few. We've heard the clergy call for unity, warn against divisive politics, but it seems the politicians are deaf.

Unless these folks are actually planning to solve the country's problems from a village home, this is just another sideshow. The real decisions are made in boardrooms and through hushed phone calls, not by trotting out old narratives in the name of "strategy." Let's see what report actually emerges, but my money is on it being more fiction than fact.

This whole charade about Gachagua's "conclave" is just another chapter in the never-ending Kenyan political soap opera. The only thing certain is that the audience will remain largely unimpressed, and the show will go on with or without a coherent plot.
