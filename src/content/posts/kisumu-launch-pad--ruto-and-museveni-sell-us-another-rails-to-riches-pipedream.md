Here we go again. President William Ruto is heading to Kisumu on March 20 to cut another ribbon with President Yoweri Museveni. They are calling it the official launch of the Kenya-Uganda Standard Gauge Railway link. It is the same old script: big speeches, shiny shovels, and a lot of talk about regional integration while the common Kenyan wonders how many more loans are being signed away under the cover of progress.

The promises are as sweet as a campaign trail bribe. We are told the Nairobi-Kampala trip will drop from 14 hours to a mere four. Freight costs will supposedly plunge by 35 percent. It sounds wonderful on paper, but we have heard this before. The first phase of the SGR left us with a massive bill and a railway that seemed to stop in the middle of nowhere for the longest time. Now, they want us to believe 2028 is the magic year when everything finally connects.

While our leaders were busy in Arusha patting themselves on the back, they could not help but point at Tanzania. Apparently, our neighbors are already moving 10,000 people a day between Dar es Salaam and Dodoma. It is embarrassing, really. We were the "Silicon Savannah" and the regional hub, but now we are the ones sitting through "market sounding conferences" and feasibility studies for roads like the Nairobi-Nakuru-Mau Summit highway that should have been finished years ago.

This whole infrastructure PR machine feels familiar. It is a lot like how [Tems’ Leading Vibe Initiative](/posts/tems--leading-vibe-initiative-heads-to-south-africa--empowerment-or-just-another-high-end-hennessy-ad) heads to South Africa with a lot of noise about empowerment that might just be a high-end ad. Our government loves the "leading vibe" of big projects, but the reality on the ground is usually just more taxes and more delays while the elites enjoy their expensive summits in Arusha.

They mentioned the Mombasa-Tanga-Bagamoyo road is 62 percent done. That is great for the tourists and the cargo moguls, but for the average person in Nairobi or Kisumu, these percentages do not lower the price of food. The "extraordinary summits" and "regional corridors" are just fancy words for a debt trap that our grandchildren will still be trying to escape long after these trains have started rusting.
