Darling, the streets are smelling like cheap tobacco and desperation this week. We are all supposed to be shocked that a truck full of SuperMatch cigarettes worth 55 million went missing on the Eldoret-Malaba highway, but let us be real. In a city where everyone is pretending to be a CEO on Instagram while surviving on credit and vibes, a heist this "sophisticated" just sounds like another Tuesday. The precision of the job suggests an inside connection, which is basically the Kenyan national anthem at this point. If you are not "in" on the scheme, are you even trying to afford that V8?

While the KRA is busy crying over lost excise duties and "round-tripping" schemes, the "it" crowd has moved their mess to Kilele. It is apparently an invitation-only dating app for the global elite, because Tinder is too crowded with people who do not own a yacht. Kilele promises discretion and background checks, but we all know what "confidential relationship concierge" really means in Nairobi. It is just a high-tech way for the "Select" circle to cheat on their spouses without ending up on a tea-spilling Telegram group.

It is hilarious how these people crave privacy while simultaneously dying for clout. They want "privacy-first profiles" on Kilele but then throw a fit when the public does not worship their every move. We saw this same energy recently when we discussed [The Honorable Clout-Chasers: Why Parliament’s TikTok Tantrum is a Total Flop](/posts/the-honorable-clout-chasers--why-parliament-s-tiktok-tantrum-is-a-total-flop). It is the same script every time: hide your dirty laundry in "private circles" but keep the cameras rolling whenever you are pretending to be a person of the people.

Then there is the "Gold" membership. Imagine paying extra for "verification credibility" and "media-rich communication." If you need an app to prove you are credible, you probably are not. It is for those members ready to move beyond basic interaction, which is code for "I have enough money to buy my way out of being boring." While legitimate manufacturers are losing jobs and the government is losing billions to cigarette syndicates, our socialites are busy worrying if their blurred photos on Kilele look "mysterious" enough for a match.

The irony is thicker than the smoke from those stolen SuperMatch cartons. We have a shadow economy bleeding the country dry to fund a lifestyle of "vetted communities" and "curated matches." At the end of the day, whether you are hijacking a truck in Malaba or hiding behind a "Gold" badge on a dating app, the goal is the same: looking rich without actually doing the work. Stay messy, Nairobi, because the truth always comes out when the "Select" circle starts fighting over who gets the last cigarette.
