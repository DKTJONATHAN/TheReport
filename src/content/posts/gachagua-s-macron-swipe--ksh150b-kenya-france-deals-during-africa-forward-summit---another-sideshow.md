So, Rigathi Gachagua is back, throwing shade from the sidelines while Nairobi plays host to the Africa Forward Summit. According to him, President Ruto is just parading world leaders for a photo op, a desperate attempt to get some nodding heads and a temporary break from the usual Kenyan grumbles. He’s calling it all a "sideshow," and let’s be honest, when has a big summit ever translated into more money in your pocket?

Gachagua didn't hold back, even telling the UN Secretary General that Ruto himself is the "greatest threat to Kenyan democracy." He’s painting Kenya as a "global hub for abductions and human trafficking," dragging in names like Kizza Besigye and a Tanzanian journalist. It sounds like the kind of drama you’d expect, a familiar tune in this part of the world where promises are cheap and instability is the real currency. This whole spectacle feels like the same old story, a never-ending cycle of accusations and blame, much like how AI can turn efficiency into a corporate noose, sometimes the systems designed to help end up making things worse.

Then he turns his attention to Emmanuel Macron and France, accusing them of being "accomplices" in a dodgy road contract. Apparently, some French firms got paid billions - Ksh159.27 billion to be precise - for a project that was cancelled before it even started. And surprise, surprise, the Auditor General is apparently sniffing around a Ksh7.5 billion chunk of cash that mysteriously vanished from the Fuel Levy Fund to cover this mess. Gachagua claims the project was then handed to a Chinese outfit for more money, but with less work. Classic.

Meanwhile, President Ruto and Macron are busy signing deals worth about Ksh150 billion, including fancy upgrades for the Port of Mombasa and a wind power project. Macron is all smiles, talking about investments and partnerships. It's a stark contrast to Gachagua's pronouncements, leaving the average Kenyan to wonder which story to believe and who's actually looking out for their interests amidst all these high-stakes handshakes and billion-shilling agreements.
