President William Ruto is back at it again, acting like he is the lead star in a high-stakes political thriller. He took to his social media accounts on Monday to drop some "deep" thoughts about the Middle East crisis, calling it a "perilous moment" in global history. It is giving very much "thoughts and prayers" energy, honestly. Everyone wants to be the main character of international peace until it is time to actually do the heavy lifting. It is hilarious how he is calling for multilateral institutions to step up, probably because he realized his Twitter fingers cannot actually stop a missile from flying across the desert.

Then we have the Iranian Ambassador, Dr. Ali Gholampour, sliding into the mentions like a bitter ex who refuses to let the narrative slide. He was quick to tell everyone that bilateral relations are "intact," but he did not stop there. He basically told Ruto to look at his own friends - specifically the US and Israel - and call them out for being the real aggressors. It is the kind of spicy public back-and-forth you would expect from the [No Panty Movement](/posts/is-going-commando-un-african-the-cultural-clash-over-kenyan-womens-modern-choices) drama where everyone has an opinion and nobody is actually wearing the pants in the situation.

The irony here is thicker than the makeup on a Nairobi socialite heading to a club at 3 AM. While Ruto is busy playing the role of a global peacemaker, Gholampour is out here complaining that trade between Kenya and Iran has basically tanked. Why? Because of those pesky US sanctions on the banking sector. It is a classic case of "I love you, but your best friend is a jerk and he is costing me money." The Ambassador is essentially pointing out the hypocrisy of acting like a neutral party while your economic strings are being pulled by the very people you are supposed to be "de-escalating."

If our leaders spent half as much time fixing the local cost of living as they do drafting these "defining moment" scripts for the international stage, we might actually see some progress. But no, we have to endure this high-level clapback session on social media. It is all a performance for the global audience, a "pick-me" energy on a geopolitical scale that leaves the average Kenyan wondering when the actual "multi-stakeholder engagement" will lower the price of electricity or fuel.

At the end of the day, diplomacy in the age of the internet is just high-stakes gossip with a fancy vocabulary. Ruto wants to look like a savior on the world stage, Iran wants their trade money back, and the US remains the big bully in the room that everyone is too scared to fully call out. It is a messy, complicated circus, and we are all just stuck in the front row watching the clowns juggle grenades while pretending they are doing it for "international peace and security." Stay tuned, because this script is far from over.
