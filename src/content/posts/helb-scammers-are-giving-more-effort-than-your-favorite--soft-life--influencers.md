The timeline was in absolute shambles this week, and for once, it wasn't because a socialite forgot to edit her waistline. Everyone acting like they are living the high life suddenly remembered that HELB loan they have been ghosting since 2018. A viral message claiming auctioneers were coming for your dusty subwoofers and mismatched plates sent shockwaves through WhatsApp groups faster than a leaked celebrity tape. It is truly hilarious how quickly the "manifesting wealth" crowd pivots to "how do I hide my fridge" when a scammer sends a threatening text with a few big words.

The scam was actually quite creative, unlike the repetitive dance challenges we see from local creators. These fraudsters mimicked official bureaucratic language so well that people were ready to send "processing fees" just to keep their property. It really highlights the desperation out here. Honestly, the way the internet reacted felt like [The Silver Mirage Collapses Into A Global Resource War](/posts/the-silver-mirage-collapses-into-a-global-resource-war) but with more crying emojis and less actual strategy. The audacity to target people who are already struggling to pay for their overpriced data bundles is peak villain behavior.

HELB had to do a whole press release just to tell everyone to calm down and stop being so gullible. They basically had to clarify that they aren't sending thugs to your door-at least not yet. The board pointed out that they do not charge "activation fees" to stop enforcement and that all their communication happens through verified channels. It is the classic Kenyan story: we are so used to being extorted by the system that when a random scammer tries it, we don't even blink, we just ask for the Paybill number to make the problem go away.

The irony is that while graduates were panic-tweeting, the real issue remains untouched. We have a generation of people who spent years in lecture halls only to be haunted by a debt they can't pay back because the job market is a desert. But hey, as long as you can post a photo of your brunch on Friday, who cares if the imaginary auctioneer is waiting outside? The "fake it till you make it" culture is officially being tested by people with better Photoshop skills than your favorite influencer.

So, if you received that text and started sweating, take a breath. Your second-hand sofa is safe for now. Just remember that in a world of phishing and extortion, the only thing more dangerous than a scammer is a graduate with nothing to lose and a high-speed internet connection. Stay cynical, stay broke, and for heaven's sake, stop clicking on links that promise "debt relief" from a random mobile number. The only thing they are relieving you of is the last few coins in your M-Pesa account.
