So, Farmer's Choice and the State Department for Livestock Development have thrown another "stakeholder forum" together. Apparently, the pig industry in Kenya is booming, with demand set to skyrocket by 2030. Fantastic. Except, as usual, everyone's wagging their fingers about "persistent inefficiencies" that will apparently scupper all this projected success. Sounds familiar, doesn't it? It’s like watching a rerun of Africa's economic vulnerability, where promising sectors always seem to trip over their own feet.

They're bleating about structural reforms and investment, but what’s new? We’re told pork consumption is a measly 0.4 kg per capita annually, half of what the World Health Organization (WHO) recommends, yet this sub-sector is supposedly worth a tidy Sh19.5 billion. The Principal Secretary is yapping about food security and farmer incomes, as if this is some revelation. He’s listing the usual suspects: disease, feed costs, traceability, market access. Groundbreaking stuff, truly.

Meanwhile, the actual farmers, bless their hearts, are grappling with reality. High feed costs are a constant headache, African swine fever is lurking like a bad omen, and processing capacity is a joke. Then there’s the shoddy cold-chain infrastructure and these so-called "market linkages" that are about as strong as a dry chapati. It’s a miracle anything gets done at all.

Farmer's Choice CEO is calling for "deliberate and sustained collaboration." Oh, you don't say. As if we haven't heard that song and dance before. They want to invest in farmer capacity, beef up traceability, build consumer trust, and expand market access. It all sounds lovely on paper, but let's be honest, when have these grand strategies ever translated into anything other than more meetings and a few glossy brochures?

The National Pig Value Chain Development Strategy (2025-2029) is the latest beacon of hope. It aims to foster public-private partnerships and train smallholder farmers. But given the historical track record of such initiatives, it’s more likely to be another bureaucratic exercise than a genuine turnaround. We're supposed to believe this sector is a protein deficit solver, more efficient than cows. Let's see if they can actually deliver on that promise, or if it's just more hot air.

The government is even touting an expo in 2026. I'm sure that will solve all the deep-seated problems plaguing the industry. Investors will flock, money will flow, and suddenly everything will be perfect. Or, more likely, it'll be another photo-op for the politicians, followed by a return to the status quo. We’ve seen this movie before.
