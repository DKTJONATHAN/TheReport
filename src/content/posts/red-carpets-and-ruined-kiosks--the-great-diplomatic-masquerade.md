State House was busy again this week, smelling of expensive cologne and high-end floor wax. President William Ruto stood there with a straight face, receiving credentials from the new envoys of Jordan, Mozambique, and Saudi Arabia. It is the usual choreography of "strengthening bilateral ties" and "mutual benefits" that we have heard since 1963. While the diplomats from Amman and Riyadh talk about investment and defense, the average Kenyan is still trying to figure out if these deals mean cheaper unga or just more fancy cars on the bypass.

The timing is almost poetic in its cruelty. Just a few kilometers away from the red carpet, hundreds of traders in Roysambu and Kasarani are literally counting their losses in the millions. Their stalls were flattened along the Thika Superhighway while the government was busy preparing the guest list for the new Saudi Ambassador, Saad Bin Abdullah AlNofaia. It is the classic Nairobi reality: if you are a foreign diplomat, you get "full support" and a handshake; if you are a local trader, you get a bulldozer and a cold shoulder.

We are told to be excited about Saudi Arabia’s five focus areas, including "investment cooperation." Let us be real for a second. We have seen how these international partnerships usually play out for the youth. It often feels like just another [Youth Empowerment Scam](/posts/nanyuki-s-badminton-circus--another--youth-empowerment--scam) wrapped in a diplomatic envelope, promising heaven while delivering the same old struggle. We export our labor and import "commitments" that never seem to trickle down to the person selling boiled eggs at a bus stop.

Then there is the talk of Jordan and Mozambique unlocking "full potential" in IT and agriculture. Meanwhile, the Kenya Defence Forces are being told to renovate stadiums because, apparently, the civilian government cannot be trusted with a bag of cement and a whistle. It is a strange world where the military builds sports facilities, the doctors have to beg for study leave through a "major victory" agreement, and the President is busy promising Jordan that Kenya is the land of IT and healthcare miracles.

The irony is thick enough to choke on. We are offered loans at a 4 percent interest rate with "flexible repayment," as if we are not already drowning in debt. These diplomatic missions are just another layer of the lacquer used to hide the cracks in the foundation. While the elite toast to new beginnings with Ambassador Rulan Mahmoud Samara, the rest of Nairobi is just trying to survive another day of "modernization" that seems to involve losing everything you have built.
