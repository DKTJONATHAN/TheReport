Another week, another hotel ballroom filled with the scent of expensive coffee and the sound of people pretending to "examine systemic barriers." This time, the theme was "Whose Future Is It Anyway?" as if we do not already know the answer. It is the same old story: policy analysts and IT experts gathering to discuss why the youth are broke, while the people who actually hold the money are busy planning their next luxury car upgrade. 

The forum finally admitted that the gig economy is largely a scam disguised as "freedom." Annmercy, an IT governance expert, pointed out that most startups across sub-Saharan Africa die within five years. It is hard to build a life on a "job" that vanishes every two weeks. While the "innovators" chase tech dreams that never pay out, some are finally realizing that [10 Boring Kenyan Jobs Quietly Paying Kshs 60k-120k While Everyone Chases Tech](/posts/boring-kenyan-jobs-paying-60k-plus) are the only thing standing between a young Kenyan and total financial ruin.

The talk of "Responsible Expansion" and rebuilding trust for the 2027 elections feels like a bad joke told at our expense. Youth leader Benson Gachoki tried to make us feel better by saying things are worse in countries like Uganda, but comparing our political disengagement to a neighbor's crisis does not put food on the table. Apathy is not indifference; it is a logical response to a system that treats your vote like a periodic suggestion that is immediately discarded by the ruling class.

Then there is the mental health angle. LEAP Africa’s Diana Ammabele says you cannot "optimize" leaders who are suffering from burnout and anxiety. Of course they are anxious. You cannot meditate your way out of a worsening mental health crisis when the source of that stress is an economic policy that views young people as nothing more than a cheap, disposable labor pool for short-term projects that benefit everyone but the worker.

At the end of the day, we are told these forums are about "culturally grounded" and "youth-led" solutions, but it is mostly just more buzzwords to fill a donor report. Until "Responsible Expansion" involves actually clearing the path for real employment instead of just another data-driven pilot program, the "Future" will continue to belong to the same elite few who have always owned the present. These dialogues are just a way to package our desperation into a PowerPoint presentation.
