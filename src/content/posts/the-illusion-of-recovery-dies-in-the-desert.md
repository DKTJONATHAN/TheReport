Businesses are already bracing for the impact, seeing the writing on the wall in the form of soaring freight and fertilizer costs. It is almost comical to watch the Bank of Canada suggest they can look through the oil price shock. Such rhetoric is a desperate attempt to maintain an aura of control while the fundamental inputs of modern life - fuel and food - become luxury goods for the masses. When input prices for agriculture and manufacturing spike, the "excess supply" in the economy is nothing more than a graveyard of unsold goods that people can no longer afford.

The consumer sentiment data is a death knell for the soft landing fantasy. When eighty percent of households expect economic harm, the psychological shift toward austerity becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. People are cancelling trips and delaying purchases not because they want to, but because they know the cost of living is about to devour whatever remains of their disposable income. This isn't just a Canadian trend; it is the global reality of a world where the middle class is being squeezed between rising geopolitical risks and stagnant wages.

Adding to this gloom is the quiet admission that artificial intelligence will likely serve as another boot on the neck of the working class. Even as war drives up the cost of living, workers expect AI to strip away their hours and employment prospects. We are witnessing a pincer movement where geopolitical instability destroys purchasing power while technological progress erodes the very labor value needed to survive it. The optimism of the early year has been replaced by a realization that the future is both more expensive and less certain.

Ultimately, the global realist sees this for what it is: a return to the mean. The era of stable prices and predictable growth was an anomaly, not the rule. As the conflict drags on, the disconnect between official bank indicators and the reality of an empty wallet will only widen, leaving the global population to foot the bill for a world that refuses to stop burning. Central banks can hold rates steady all they want, but they cannot print their way out of a burning oil field.
