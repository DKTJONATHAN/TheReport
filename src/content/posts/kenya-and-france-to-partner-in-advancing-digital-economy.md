Kenya and France Dancing Around the Digital Economy for "Youth"

Another summit, another round of handshakes and photo ops. The Kenya Development Corporation, bless their idealistic hearts, are pushing for laws to protect the "intellectual property" of our youth in the digital world. Apparently, we're on the cusp of a digital revolution where every young Kenyan with a laptop is a future tech mogul. This is Nairobi, not Silicon Valley. Let’s be real.

The ICT CS is chirping about solving cross-border problems with digital solutions. Meanwhile, our roads are crumbling and the water supply is a lottery. Clara Chappaz, the French Minister for AI and Digital Technologies, is talking about a "new relationship" and investment that benefits "all." That sounds suspiciously like they’ll be selling us their old tech at inflated prices while pocketing the profits. The billions in the digital economy are real, yes, but who exactly stands to benefit? The 60% youth demographic is a convenient statistic to trot out when you need to look like you're doing something for the people.

The Kenya Development Corporation’s push for IP protection is laudable, in theory. But are we creating laws for a future that might not materialize, or are we just papering over the cracks? Supporting startups is great, but how many of these digital ventures will actually survive the cutthroat reality of the market, especially when the playing field is anything but level? This whole song and dance about peer-to-peer learning feels like a sophisticated way to outsource training that should be a basic government responsibility. It’s like they expect innovation to sprout from thin air, conveniently ignoring the foundational issues, similar to how global powers only see conflict as a way to enrich themselves, leaving the rest of us in the dust. Check out [The Petro-Illusion: Why Global Conflict Only Enriches the Few while Starving the Many](/posts/the-petro-illusion--why-global-conflict-only-enriches-the-few-while-starving-the-many) for more on that.

The mention of artisanal miners adhering to safety regulations is a nice little add-on, a nod to the "real" economy, I suppose. But it feels tacked on, a distraction from the main event: the grand digital pronouncements. And let's not forget French President Macron's little outburst at the University of Nairobi about "lack of respect." If they want our youth to be respectful, maybe they should start by showing a bit more respect for the complex realities we face here on the ground, instead of just swooping in with their pronouncements and expecting applause.
