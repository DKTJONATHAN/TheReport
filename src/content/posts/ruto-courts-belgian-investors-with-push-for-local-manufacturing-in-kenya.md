Ruto Courts Belgian Investors: Another Round of Promises, Same Old Song

WARNING: President Ruto's grand pronouncements about attracting Belgian investment in Kenyan manufacturing sound suspiciously like a broken record, filled with the usual sweet talk while the underlying problems remain firmly untouched.

Brussels is buzzing, or so they say. Our President, William Ruto, has jetted off to Belgium, preaching to the choir of European investors. He’s hawking Kenya as the next big thing in manufacturing, not just a dumping ground for raw materials. He’s talking about value addition, clean energy, and resilient supply chains, all the buzzwords that sound good on paper, especially when you’re trying to charm your way to foreign cash. The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is conveniently framed as an opportunity, but let's be real, it's more about Europe protecting its own turf.

The usual suspects are trotted out: agribusiness, ports, tech, and clean energy. We grow a lot of flowers for Europe, export tea and avocados - groundbreaking stuff. Now, the President wants them to process these things *here*, not just ship them off. He’s even proposing a sister-port partnership with Antwerp-Bruges. And the Silicon Bridge idea? Taking a page out of Silicon Valley’s playbook with a Kenyan twist. It's all very ambitious, on paper. But have we forgotten about the perennial issues plaguing our own backyard, like the endless cycle of corruption that makes any real progress feel like [Eternal Delusions: A Quiet EP for a Country Loudly Drowning](/posts/eternal-delusions--a-quiet-ep-for-a-country-loudly-drowning)?

The numbers game is strong: double the companies, hit $1 billion in trade by 2030. It’s a nice, round target. He’s painting Kenya as the gateway to Africa, a market of 1.4 billion people. And let's not forget the EU-Kenya Economic Partnership Agreement, which apparently grants duty-free access. Exports to the EU are supposedly up 20%. All these figures sound impressive, but they don't magically fix the infrastructure gaps, the bureaucratic nightmares, or the fact that many Kenyans are still struggling to make ends meet.

The Belgian business community, bless their optimistic hearts, have pledged to explore opportunities. We’ve got pledges of tens of millions, and one company even sees a "golden opportunity" in tea value addition, promising 50,000 jobs. Another firm is eyeing our renewable energy potential for their carbon capture machines. It’s all very encouraging, but we’ve heard these pledges before. Will they translate into tangible progress for the average Kenyan, or will it be another case of foreigners benefiting while our own systems creak and groan under the weight of inefficiency and lack of proper oversight?
