RUTO'S NO WORRIES ABOUT SA XENOPHOBIA, JUST THE AU'S WALLET

A quick trip by President Ruto to South Africa, and suddenly the country that's a magnet for desperate migrants isn't the real problem. Oh no, it's the entire continent's governance and service delivery that's the villain. So, instead of calling out the mobs who are literally chasing people into the bushes, Ruto's busy pontificating about reforming the African Union. Because, clearly, a bigger budget and less red tape for bureaucrats is the magic bullet that will stop South Africans from hating other Africans. It's almost as if the real issue, the raw competition for scraps, is too inconvenient to mention.

While Ruto was busy absolving South Africa of any blame for the chaos that's making headlines, he also had to deal with the sideshow of setting up an Ebola isolation center for Americans in Kenya. Apparently, refusing to host it would make Kenya look "inhuman." Never mind the local resistance or the fact that we're building infrastructure for Uncle Sam while our own health system is likely still struggling with last week's cholera outbreak. It's all about looking good for the Americans, even if it means ignoring the concerns of our own people. This whole situation feels like another [Bullion Mirage](posts/the-bullion-mirage--gold-withers-under-real-world-fire), promising something shiny while the reality is a bit grimy.

Ramaphosa, bless his diplomatic heart, chimes in with the same old song: "South Africans are not xenophobic." They just want leaders to "resolve the many challenges brought about by migration." It's a neat little sidestep, isn't it? Blame the challenge, not the people perpetrating the violence. They’re even thinking of sending envoys and having "direct talks" to understand the "issue of irregular migration." Because apparently, after decades of this, they still need to *understand* why people are angry about resources. It's like waiting for a five-billion-dollar lesson to sink in.

The leaders are all about "shared prosperity" and "moving people across borders" when it benefits businesses. Lift visa requirements, and presto, more Kenyans visit South Africa. How convenient. But when the competition for those same resources boils over into violence, suddenly it's a systemic governance issue, not a human one. It’s all about making sure opportunities exist *everywhere* so people don't have to move. A nice thought, but let’s be honest, that’s a long way from happening, and in the meantime, people are fleeing for their lives.

And then there's the Ebola center saga. Ramaphosa, trying to sound pan-African, talks about strengthening African health systems while simultaneously accepting US help. Ruto defends the American taxpayer-funded facility, calling refusal "inhuman." It's a classic diplomatic dance: avoid direct confrontation, hint at differing approaches, and ultimately, stick to the script that serves the powerful. The underlying message is clear: Africa's problems are to be solved with external help and bureaucratic overhauls, not by facing uncomfortable truths about local sentiments and resource distribution.

So, while Ruto and Ramaphosa wax lyrical about continental unity and "speaking with one voice" in a shifting world order, the reality on the ground is a lot less harmonious. Xenophobia isn't just about governance; it's about fear, competition, and often, a deep-seated resentment that no amount of AU reform meetings will magically erase. It’s a performance, a well-rehearsed play where the real actors - the victims of the violence and the frustrated citizens - are kept firmly offstage.
