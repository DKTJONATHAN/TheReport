RUTO, THE 'CEO': SWEET TALK ON KENYA'S DEVELOPMENT FINANCING AGENDA

WARNING: This piece dissects President Ruto's claims of a new development financing strategy, poking holes in the promises of private capital and reduced debt. Expect more of the same old song and dance, just with a shinier, "CEO" branded wrapper.

This 'CEO' has been busy, hasn't he? Since September 2022, President William Ruto’s been on a relentless charm offensive, playing dealmaker extraordinaire. Whether it's wooing investors, begging international lenders for spare change, or patching up his own political house with old rivals, the man’s constantly juggling a circus of competing interests, all to keep his agenda rolling. And they say he’s not a traditional strongman. Please. He’s just a more sophisticated hustler.

His hands-on approach, lauded as “CEO” style, is just another way to say he’s micromanaging, probably making off-the-cuff pronouncements that leave his own people scrambling. Remember that ambitious 10-year transformation plan? Spoken about again and again, promising to boost productivity without adding to our already crushing debt or taxing us into oblivion. Sounds lovely, doesn't it? Like a politician promising you the moon while picking your pocket.

Now, the big play: the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF). They’re telling us this will unlock investments, accelerate development, and shift us from borrowing to public-private partnerships. They want us to believe this model, focusing on modernizing JKIA, expanding roads, and scaling up clean energy, will somehow bring in billions without us being further burdened. And that the "big fish" projects will be run by "experts," not politicians or connected goons. We've heard this one before. It’s the same old story, just repackaged. It reminds me of when we talked about [Exporting Souls and Empty Bonds](/posts/exporting-souls-and-empty-bonds), the lofty ideals masking the grim reality.

And the grand finale: the KPC IPO. They listed Kenya Pipeline Company on the NSE, a whole KSh112.374 billion raised, apparently. This "seed money" is meant to attract more investors, aiming for a cool KSh5 trillion over the next decade. The proceeds, unlike those from past "privatisation" schemes, will apparently flow into the NIF, not just vanish into the government’s general budget. We'll see. Meanwhile, Ruto's also jetting off to beg from China and the US, securing loans that are just loans with different names, dressed up as "debt-for-food security swaps" or "infrastructure deals." It's all smoke and mirrors, folks.

The real show is the parade of Tanzanian entrepreneurs snapping up Kenyan assets. Edha Nahdi buying Bamburi Cement, Rostam Aziz expanding his empire with Taifa Gas and now Nation Media Group, Ally Edha Awadh taking over fuel stations, and Benjamin Fernandes pushing his fintech platform. They’re investing, sure, but is this about genuine development or just another round of wealth extraction, where the "big fish" are actually foreign ones? Ruto’s shift from sovereign borrowing to "leveraging private capital" sounds great on paper, but the real beneficiaries are yet to be seen.

President William Ruto’s administration has been painting a picture of a new era in development financing, one where deal-making prowess and a hands-on "CEO" approach are set to revolutionize Kenya’s infrastructure agenda. Since taking office in September 2022, Ruto has consistently positioned himself as a relentless negotiator, skillfully balancing competing interests to push his agenda forward, whether wooing international investors, negotiating with global lenders, or stitching together local political alliances.

The narrative of a more sophisticated political operator, one who strategically incorporated elements of the opposition into his cabinet and is now reportedly in talks for further alliances, suggests a departure from the traditional strongman archetype. This "CEO" style is characterized by a personal involvement in various dockets, with reports of him personally inquiring about progress and even making policy pronouncements that can send ripples through the established order, sometimes to the discomfort of his own allies.

At the heart of this grand vision lies an ambitious 10-year national transformation program, aimed at boosting the country's productive capacity and long-term competitiveness. Ruto’s pronouncements, repeated from last year's State of the Nation Address to recent addresses to international organizations, insist on mobilizing resources without sinking the nation further into debt or burdening citizens with new taxes. This shift in strategy is to be realized through measures like the newly enacted National Infrastructure Fund (NIF), which the government claims will unlock major investments and accelerate development by leveraging public-private partnerships and blended financing.

The focus is on tangible projects like the modernization of Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA), expansion of road networks, and scaling up clean energy infrastructure. Ruto's assurance that these projects will be managed by "experts and professionals" rather than politicians or the well-connected is a familiar refrain, designed to inspire confidence that money won't be "wasted or stolen." This is a crucial element of his plan to attract billions internally, rather than solely relying on external borrowing, marking a departure from previous financing models.

A significant move in this direction was the recent privatization of Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC) Plc, which was listed on the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE). This initial public offering (IPO) successfully raised KSh112.374 billion, representing a 105.7% oversubscription. The government intends to channel these proceeds, along with those from future privatizations, into the National Infrastructure Fund to attract an additional KSh5 trillion over the next decade. This privatization is presented as a pivotal step towards a more sustainable financing model for infrastructure development, a strategy that contrasts sharply with how previous privatization proceeds were absorbed into the general budget.

Beyond domestic maneuvers, President Ruto has also been actively seeking international partnerships. He has secured loans from both China and the United States, navigating the economic and technological rivalry between these global powers. Last year, a US$1 billion debt-for-food security swap with the United States International Development Finance Corporation (DFC) was finalized, aiming to replace high-cost debt with more affordable financing. Simultaneously, agreements with China worth nearly US$1 billion focused on infrastructure and manufacturing. The President's engagement with individual investors for Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) is also a cornerstone of his administration’s shift away from sovereign borrowing towards public-private partnerships. Data from the Kenya Investment Authority highlights growth in FDI, with renewable energy, communications, manufacturing, and agriculture leading the charge. This is further exemplified by the increasing interest and investment from Tanzanian entrepreneurs in sectors such as manufacturing, oil and gas, fintech, and media, with notable acquisitions and project launches signaling a significant cross-border economic engagement.
