"For All Mankind" is back for its fifth season, and the self congratulatory buzz from critics is, predictably, deafening. Apple TV+ is once again pushing this alternate reality drama as the pinnacle of television, a claim that, while perhaps true in the vacuum of the streaming service's output, belies a show increasingly struggling under its own expanding narrative. Season five promises more of the same - deeper dives into Mars, and then, gasp, even further into space. The problem is, as the show ages and its fictional timeline marches on, some of the raw, compelling energy of its nascent days has predictably eroded, replaced by a more deliberate, and frankly, slower, pace.

We're told this season is about Mars staking its claim for independence against Earth. Years have passed, it's the 2010s, and everyone's a bit older, a bit wiser, and apparently, a lot more stubborn. Ed Baldwin, one of the last vestiges of the original cast, is now in his eighties, a fact that serves as a thinly veiled metaphor for the show itself. His role is being "gracefully" passed on to his grandson Alex and daughter Kelly, alongside new Mars leader Miles. It’s a natural progression, I suppose, but one that sacrifices the established emotional resonance for the sake of introducing a new, as yet unproven, generation.

The narrative thrust of season five centers on Mars' fight for independence, a conflict that, according to the reviews, gets "increasingly hostile." Billionaire CEO Dev Ayesa and a returning Margo Madison, the NASA genius turned traitorous spy, are apparently key players. We're assured all the old favorites are back, alongside a "cold, mysterious" security officer played by Mireille Enos, likely another cog in the machine of forced expansion.

The show's descent into subplots about AI, brutal police forces, and the general malaise of life on Mars, while perhaps intended to add realism, actually serves to shrink the spectacle. The grand, Earth-spanning implications of a Soviet moon landing that defined the show's early appeal are now confined to cramped steel corridors. The "wow" factor, once a constant companion, is now more often implied than shown, relegated to off camera events. This is the natural consequence of chasing the horizon; you lose sight of the ground beneath your feet.

This season is explicitly "transitional," a polite way of saying it's uneven. The aging of the original cast is a narrative fact, but the emotional investment in their successors simply isn't there yet. We haven't had decades to fall in love with Alex or Lily as we did with Ed or Margo. Patience is demanded, particularly at the outset, a request that smacks of the show's creators knowing they're asking the audience to endure a slow build.

Despite these admitted weaknesses, the sycophantic reviews insist that by episode four or five, the "weaknesses become strengths." We are to "relate" to Mars' struggles, feel the "pressure of being so desolate." The show's ultimate salvation, it seems, lies in its promise to eventually move *beyond* Mars, back into the vast emptiness of space. This is where the "nail biting, edge of your seat moments" are supposed to return. It's a classic bait and switch: endure the slow burn, and we might just get back to the good stuff.

The article concludes with the usual praise, suggesting that the "slight dip" in quality was a "worthy sacrifice" for future storylines. This is the perpetual hope of any long running series: that the current doldrums are merely a necessary prelude to a glorious revival. Whether "For All Mankind" can actually deliver on this promise, or if it's just setting itself up for another cycle of diminishing returns, remains to be seen. But rest assured, the show will keep pushing forward, because that's what ambitious, critically acclaimed, Apple TV+ dramas are supposed to do.
