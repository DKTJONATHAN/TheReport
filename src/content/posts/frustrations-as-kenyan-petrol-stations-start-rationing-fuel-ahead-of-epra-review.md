Fuel Station Frenzy: Kenyans Scramble as Petrol Stations Suddenly Hoard Gas Before EPRA Review!

Is it just me, or is the timing of this "fuel shortage" incredibly convenient? Kenyans are apparently waking up at the crack of dawn and fumbling for cash - because, you know, credit cards are SO last year when you're trying to fleece the public. Apparently, some petrol stations are suddenly "rationing" fuel, capping refills at a measly KSh 2,000 and only accepting grubby cash. It's almost as if they *knew* EPRA was going to adjust prices and wanted to make a tidy profit on the sly. The audacity!

Honestly, the whole situation reeks of desperation from these fuel giants. COFEK, bless their little cotton socks, are calling out Ola Energy for being "out of fuel" in Matunda, Moi's Bridge. Hoarding? Shocker. It’s the same old song and dance, isn't it? They see a global conflict, like this whole Iran kerfuffle that's sending oil prices into a tizzy, and suddenly their local operations become "affected." Please, spare me the crocodile tears. They’re just waiting for the midnight price hike like vultures circling a carcass.

And let's talk about these social media warriors. @terrikibiriti is out here spilling the tea about the KSh 2,000 cash-only limit on Ngong Road. Then you have @kenyanthoughts, who apparently "refuelled yesterday" - probably because they saw this coming a mile off. And @JoyceMw27159977, bless her dedicated heart, is out there at 6 am with a full tank. This isn't just about convenience; it's about survival for some, while others are playing the market like it's the Nairobi Securities Exchange. It reminds me of how Kenya’s 'Black Gold' is often just another black hole for our taxes, leaving the common man to fend for themselves.

Meanwhile, the world is apparently in a tizz with Brent crude hovering near $120 a barrel, thanks to the Middle East drama. Iran is even warning of $200 per barrel. While world leaders are releasing oil from emergency reserves - a move that sounds suspiciously like admitting they’ve been asleep at the wheel - here in Kenya, we're stuck with the fallout. It's a global mess, sure, but the local players are always quick to capitalize on any chaos.

So, while the big wigs are making their millions, the rest of us are left scrambling, waking up early, and digging for cash. It’s not just a fuel price review; it’s a masterclass in exploiting vulnerability. And the hypocrisy? Chef's kiss. They'll blame it on global markets, on wars, on anything but their own greedy practices.
