So, here we are again. President Ruto, during some big Madaraka Day shindig in Wajir, decided to bless the people of Northern Kenya with his presence and, naturally, a heaping dose of promises. He’s talking about ending "decades of discrimination and exclusion," promising reforms in education, ID cards, healthcare, and youth empowerment. It’s the usual song and dance, isn't it? "I feel you," he said. We’ve heard that before, haven't we? It’s like listening to a tired old record skip.

He even trotted out the story of this Bakaja Ibrahim Osman fellow, who apparently couldn't get an ID card despite being born and bred there. Oh, the humanity! And now, some young folks named Abdirahman Ali Osman and Maryam Isaak Mohamed, both born in 2007, got their IDs without "extra hurdles." Fantastic. It's almost like they're just… Kenyans. Who knew? But don't worry, he assures us national security won't be compromised. Because, you know, it's not like *anyone* in that region is ever suspected of anything, right? This feels eerily similar to when [Tanzania's Officials Get a Reality Check After Trading Truth for Tea](/posts/dashboard-drama--tanzania-s-officials-get-a-reality-check-after-trading-truth-for-tea), where promises of transparency turned out to be just more hot air.

Then there’s the education bit. More money, more teachers, more colleges. They're even thinking about bringing Madrasa and Duksi into the formal system. Which is… interesting. Maybe it will help some kids who’ve been overlooked, or maybe it’s just another way to tick a box. And healthcare? Sh8.1 billion paid in claims for three counties. Sounds like a lot, until you remember how many people are actually there and how much healthcare costs these days. Is it enough, or just enough to sound good on paper?

And youth empowerment. Business capital, training, mentorship, digital hubs. The usual buzzwords. They want the young people of Northern Kenya to believe their future isn't defined by geography, but by talent and determination. Which is a nice sentiment, until you remember how many doors are still closed, no matter how talented you are, especially if you don't have the right connections or the shiny ID card from birth.

So, is this different? Is this the real deal, or just another political performance designed to appease a region that’s been historically sidelined? My cynical Nairobi gut tells me it's the latter. They'll roll out the red carpet, listen to the speeches, maybe even get a few more ID cards, and then life will largely go on as it always has. The system is built on suspicion, and it takes more than a Madaraka Day speech to dismantle that.

The president says people like Osman and Maryam "feel seen and heard." I hope they do. But I also hope they're not just seeing and hearing another round of empty promises that will fade away once the cameras are gone and the president is back in his Nairobi office. Because feeling "seen and heard" doesn't pay the bills or guarantee opportunities that haven't been systematically denied for decades.
