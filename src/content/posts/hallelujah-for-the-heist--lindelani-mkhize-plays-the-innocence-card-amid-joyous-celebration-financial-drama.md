Oh, darling, nothing says "Christian fellowship" quite like a multi-million rand legal battle over who touched the collection plate. Dr. Lindelani Mkhize, the man who helped turn Joyous Celebration into a continental powerhouse, is currently doing damage control after the Sunday Times decided to air his dirty laundry. The allegations of financial misconduct are flying faster than a soprano hitting a high C, but Mkhize insists he is as innocent as a choir boy. He claims these nasty rumors are just "governance disputes" and that he never even had access to the company bank accounts.

Is anyone else getting a sense of déjà vu? It is the classic industry move: when the auditors start knocking, suddenly the Creative Director has no idea how a spreadsheet works. Mkhize is out here doing the rounds on shows like The Upside, trying to convince us that he was too busy with the harmonies to notice the hole in the budget. It reminds me of so many other high-profile cases where [Red Cards and Razzle Dazzle: Why Your Favorite 'Hustlers' Are Actually Just Common Thieves](/posts/red-cards-and-razzle-dazzle--why-your-favorite--hustlers--are-actually-just-common-thieves) becomes the only way to describe the behind-the-scenes chaos of a successful brand.

According to his very polished statement, the good doctor has been begging for financial clarity for ages. He has supposedly requested bank records and an independent review because apparently, being a founding member does not give you the VIP pass to the accounting office. It is a bit rich, is it not? You can run the whole stage, hire the talent, and lead the nation in praise, but you cannot see the balance sheet? The hypocrisy in the gospel industry is truly the gift that keeps on giving.

The whole mess is now headed for formal arbitration, which is just a fancy way of saying they are going to argue behind closed doors so the fans do not see the real ugliness. Mkhize says he will not "debate governance issues in the media," which is PR-speak for "please stop asking me about the money while I am trying to keep my job as Creative Director." He wants to keep the music playing while the lawyers figure out who was actually signing the checks.

Honestly, honey, we have seen this movie before. The lights go down, the choir sings about blessings, and the men in suits are fighting over the royalties in the parking lot. Whether Mkhize is a victim of bad management or just a very good actor remains to be seen. But one thing is for sure: the "Joyous" part of this celebration is looking a little thin these days. We will be watching the arbitration results like hawks, because let us face it, the truth usually comes out eventually, even if it has to be dragged out of a bank vault.
