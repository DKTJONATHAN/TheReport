So, Nanyuki is the latest hotbed of Kenyan "wisdom." Hundreds of protesters decided to throw rocks and set fires because a US quarantine centre for Ebola-exposed folks is being shoved down their throats. And the police? Oh, they just did what they do best: respond with enough tear gas and water cannons to make sure everyone remembers who's boss. Because, you know, that's how you handle public health concerns - with more chaos than the potential problem itself.

The Americans are supposedly bringing in people exposed to Ebola from Congo and Uganda, and the locals are understandably miffed. They're asking, quite reasonably, why Kenya, a country that has never even seen a sniff of Ebola, has to be the dumping ground for someone else's potential biohazard. It's like your neighbour, who keeps a pet monkey that keeps escaping, deciding to park it in your living room "just for a bit." No thanks.

But President Ruto, bless his pragmatic heart, says Kenya owes Washington for all that "support." Financial and technical, no doubt. Makes you wonder how much that $13.5 million from the US is actually worth when you're potentially dealing with a virus outbreak. Remember that Sh12bn bad fuel scandal? [Nairobi Twist](posts/sh12bn-bad-fuel-scandal-gets-another-nairobi-twist) proves that when money is involved, things have a nasty habit of going sideways, and usually, it's the mwananchi who pays the price.

Never mind that Kenya’s High Court tried to put a temporary stop to this madness. The government, along with the US, is just ploughing ahead. Because why listen to a court or the people when you've got "strategic partnerships" to maintain? It’s business as usual, where the risk is always pushed downhill to the ones who have the least say in the matter. This whole situation smells like a repeat of that [Great By-Election Charade](posts/the-great-by-election-charade--uda-wins-a-race-against-nobody), where the promises are loud, but the reality is a lot messier.
