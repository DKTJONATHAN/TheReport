If you think you have a supply cushion just because 50 million barrels of Iranian oil are floating off the coast of Malaysia, I have a bridge in Nyayo House to sell you. China has been playing a dangerous game, relying on discounted, sanctioned oil from Tehran to keep its independent refineries afloat. But as the Middle East turns into a literal furnace, that cushion is looking more like a wet rag. Analysts are already whispering about supply chains breaking, and for the teapot refineries in Shandong, the party is officially over.

These small-time refineries are the ultimate hustlers, surviving only because they can get cheap crude that the big boys are too ethical to touch. Now, with Iran facing heat from the US and Israel, that 1.3 million barrels per day is at risk. It is the same old story of relying on a shaky source while the world burns. It reminds me of the classic move of [Selling the Green Giant for Spare Change](/posts/selling-the-green-giant-for-spare-change), where short-term greed leads to a long-term headache that no amount of cheap imports can fix.

The US has already snatched the Venezuelan supply, diverting it back to American markets. So, what does China do? They go running to Russia. Moscow is currently the leading supplier for Shandong because India decided to grow a conscience - or at least find a better deal. It is not a strategy; it is a desperate shuffle. If the Strait of Hormuz actually closes, we are talking about 20 percent of the world's oil vanishing from the market. That is not a ripple; that is a tsunami that will drown any hope of cheap fuel.

If the Iranian taps run dry, these independent refiners will have to go crawling back to the global market for Brazilian crude. But here is the catch: it will cost them 15 dollars more per barrel. In a world where profit margins are thinner than a Kenyan politician's promise, that is a death sentence. We will see refinery cuts, shutdowns, and gasoline prices that will make your eyes water. The state-owned giants will be fine, of course, because they always are. It is the small guy who gets squeezed while the elites play geo-political chess.

The only silver lining, if you can call it that, is that China is building EVs so fast they might not even need the fuel in a few years. Gasoline demand is already being destroyed at a rate of 200,000 barrels a year. So while the world worries about the Strait of Hormuz, the future is leaving the oil age behind anyway. It is just another day in a world where everyone is looking for a shortcut that does not exist. Beijing can stock up all the barrels they want, but you cannot hide from the market forever.
