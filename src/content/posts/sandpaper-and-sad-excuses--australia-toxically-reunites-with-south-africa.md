Oh, look who is crawling back. Eight years after the "Sandpapergate" mess turned international cricket into a DIY hardware store disaster, Australia is heading back to South Africa this September. It has been nearly a decade since Cameron Bancroft was caught on camera trying to give a cricket ball a rough manicure with a piece of sandpaper he hid in his trousers. You would think the embarrassment would last a lifetime, but in the world of professional sports, shame has a shorter shelf life than a pint of milk in the Durban sun.

Let us talk about the audacity of Steve Smith. The former skipper was banned for a year for his part in the "let us cheat and hope nobody sees us" plot, yet here he is, still padding up and preparing to face the South African crowds. It is the ultimate "failing upwards" story. While most of us would be hiding in a hole if we got caught cheating on a global stage, these guys get a world tour and a commemorative jersey. It really makes you wonder about the moral compass of Cricket Australia, but then again, money does not have a conscience.

This return to the scene of the crime is being wrapped in a lot of PR fluff about "healing" and "sportsmanship," but let us be real. It is just business. This tour comes at a time when the entire landscape of professional competition is shifting towards whoever has the deepest pockets and the least shame. We have seen this trend before, where [THE MONOPOLY OF BLOOD: HOW TKO AND RIYADH ARE DISMANTLING THE LAST BASTION OF ATHLETE LABOR](/posts/the-monopoly-of-blood--how-tko-and-riyadh-are-dismantling-the-last-bastion-of-athlete-labor) explains how the power is being taken away from the actual spirit of the game and handed over to corporate entities. Cricket is just another piece on that board, and this South African tour is the next move.

South Africa is not coming into this as the victim anymore, though. They managed to snatch the World Test Championship at Lord’s last June, marking their first major trophy in 37 years. That has to sting for the Australians, who love to pretend they are the kings of the pitch. The matches are set to kick off in Durban on September 24, moving through Johannesburg and Potchefstroom before the actual test matches begin in October. It is a long, drawn-out schedule designed to milk every cent out of the fans.

The final showdown will take place at Newlands in Cape Town from October 27 to 31. This is the exact spot where the original scandal exploded, so expect the TV cameras to be zooming in on every pocket and trouser line like a security guard at a high-end mall. It is high-octane drama for people who think watching grass grow is a legitimate hobby. Whether they play fair or not this time, one thing is for sure: the hypocrisy of the "gentleman’s game" has never been more obvious. Stay messy, cricket.
