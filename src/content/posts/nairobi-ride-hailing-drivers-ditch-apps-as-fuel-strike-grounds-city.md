So, the transporters have finally decided to flex their muscles. Fuel prices are up, again, and surprise, surprise, the people who actually have to *buy* that fuel are not happy. Ride-hailing drivers have abandoned their apps, matatus are gathering dust, and long-distance trucks are parked. Nairobi, the so-called heart of East Africa, has ground to a halt. Thousands of us are stranded, wondering how we're supposed to get to work, pay rent, or even buy ugali. This isn't just about a few shillings per litre; it's about whether we can even afford to move in this country.

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) thinks hiking diesel prices by over KES 46 is a brilliant idea. KES 242 a litre? For diesel? And KES 214 for petrol? It's highway robbery, plain and simple. The Transport Sector Alliance is calling this one of the biggest coordinated actions in Kenya's history. Good for them. Meanwhile, we're left to navigate the mess, dodging those few drivers who've decided to go off-app, probably charging double what they used to. They're cashing in on the chaos, just like everyone else who profits from a dying global order.

This whole mess also brings up the same old questions about our fuel supply. We import almost everything, yet the government's "deal" with the UAE and Saudi Arabia was supposed to fix things. Shield us from dollar shortages, ease pressure on the shilling, stabilise prices - all that jazz. But here we are, with sporadic shortages and fears that Middle East tensions will make things even worse. It's the same old song and dance, just with more expensive fuel.

The Alliance is right, though. Why are we paying some of the highest fuel prices in the region when landlocked Ethiopia, of all places, manages to keep theirs lower? It smacks of incompetence or, more likely, corruption. No one is giving us any clear answers on how long this strike will last. The transporters aren't moving until prices are adjusted or the government intervenes. So, we wait. We always wait. And we get fleeced.

The strike has already shut down schools and sent many companies scrambling for work-from-home solutions. This is shaping up to be one of the most severe transport disruptions we've seen in years. It’s a stark reminder that when the wheels of commerce stop turning, it’s the ordinary Kenyan who suffers the most. We're the ones left stranded, the ones footing the bill, while the powers that be argue about who's to blame.

It's the same old scandals, the same old promises, and the same old pocket-change bailouts that never fix the root cause. When will we see real change instead of these recurring crises? Probably never. But hey, at least we have something new to complain about.
