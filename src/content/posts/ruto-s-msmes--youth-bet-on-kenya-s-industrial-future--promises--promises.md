President Ruto, at the KICC, declared MSMEs the 'backbone' of Kenya's industrialisation, painting a glorious picture of nurtured youth and economic transformation. It's a lovely thought, isn't it? Another grand vision unveiled, another set of promises echoing through the halls of power, while on the ground, the struggle remains as real as ever.

He spoke of 'investing in entrepreneurs,' not just businesses, and building a 'pipeline of 800 young people in every ward' through the Nyota program. Sounds impressive on paper. But forgive a Nairobi realist for asking: how many of these 'pipelines' have we seen before? How many initiatives bloom with fanfare only to wither when the camera lights fade? Are we truly building a sustainable future, or just another generation of pilot project casualties, forever waiting for that elusive 'startup capital' to materialise beyond the press release?

Then came the call to financial institutions, urging them to see the 'unbanked' as an 'untapped market,' not a risk. To embrace behavioural credit scoring and mobile technology. Bless his heart for trying. But banks operate on profit and perceived risk, not altruism. They’ve had decades to 'innovate' and reach these markets, yet high interest rates and collateral demands remain king. Will a speech truly change a deeply ingrained system, or will it be another fleeting moment of corporate nodding before they revert to business as usual, while ordinary Kenyans grapple with [Inflationary Warfare: The New Global Normal](/posts/inflationary-warfare--the-new-global-normal)?

To declare MSMEs the 'mainstream' and 'engine room' of our economy is rich, coming from a system that often treats them as an afterthought, if not an outright nuisance. One minute they're lauded as champions, the next they're battling oppressive levies, bureaucratic red tape, and shakedowns. And the Germany comparison? Oh, the famous 'hidden champions' of Europe. Lovely. But last I checked, Germany has robust infrastructure, predictable policies, and a legal framework that actually supports small businesses, not just tolerates them. Our 'industrial future' can't simply be copy-pasted from a developed nation without addressing the rot at our very foundations.

So, yes, President Ruto has 'reaffirmed the government's commitment' to strengthening entrepreneurship and creating an 'enabling environment.' We've heard it all before - the jobs, the poverty reduction, the globally competitive economy. The rhetoric is polished, the vision grand. But until we see consistent, tangible action that goes beyond photo opportunities and actually dismantles the systemic hurdles that choke our small businesses, forgive me for keeping my expectations firmly cemented in Nairobi's cynical reality.
