PENSION SAVINGS FOR "DEVELOPMENT" - JUST DON'T EXPECT TO RETIRE RICH






So, the suits in government are eyeing your pension. Apparently, they've got "infrastructure" and "housing" to build, and their own pockets are looking a bit thin. They're waving around this idea of using your retirement savings, accumulated over decades, to fund these grand projects. It sounds like a dream, right? Long-term capital, stable returns, national development. Yeah, right. This is Nairobi, not some fairy tale land where good intentions pave the way to riches for everyone.

They trot out examples from Chile, Canada, Australia - countries where somehow, magically, pension funds have funded airports and highways without disappearing. They even mention Singapore and Malaysia, which is rich, considering. But they conveniently gloss over the disasters. Argentina nationalizing pension funds in 2008 and blowing the money on government spending? Sound familiar? Or the 1MDB scandal in Malaysia, where billions vanished from a sovereign fund meant for "strategic projects"? Weak governance, political interference - these aren't minor hiccups, they're the standard operating procedure when public money is involved. It's like expecting your "dream car" to run on prayers after the last KRA tax hike; you know it's not going to end well. [The Importation Trap: Why Your 'Dream Car' Is Just a KRA Donation Drive](/posts/the-importation-trap--why-your--dream-car--is-just-a-kra-donation-drive).

Our own pension pot here in Kenya, a cool Sh2 trillion and growing, is mostly tied up in safe, boring government securities. Fine for stability, I guess, but where's the excitement? They say there's "room for diversification" into infrastructure. Translation: they want to dip their hands into it. The key is "insulating investment decisions from political interference" and having "professional institutions." Those are two words I haven't heard uttered in the same sentence as "Kenyan government" for a very long time.

The global playbook for managing pension funds is all about diversification - government bonds, equities, corporate bonds, even a bit of real estate. It’s a way to spread the risk, so if one part goes belly up, your entire retirement isn't toast. But when governments get involved, "diversification" often means concentrating everything into one big, tempting pile for them to raid. They talk about "fiduciary duties" and "rigorous due diligence," but let's be honest, the temptation to treat your pension as a personal ATM for political favors is probably overwhelming for some.

So, here's the deal: they want your pension money to build things. They promise it's for your own good, for the nation's progress. But the global record, and frankly, our own local history, screams caution. Pension funds are huge piles of cash, and in Kenya, where everything else seems to be a political drama, the risk of it being misused is sky-high. They can either be a tool for genuine development that benefits retirees, or another avenue for the usual suspects to line their pockets while your future dwindles. You tell me which one is more likely.
