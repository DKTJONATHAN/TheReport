So, AI Everything Kenya X GITEX Kenya decided to grace Nairobi with its presence. Apparently, the Sarit Expo Centre was the place to be for the "wider East African conversation" about who's going to build, fund, and regulate all this artificial intelligence jazz. Because, you know, we haven't had enough of these high-level meetings where important people talk about the "digital future" while the actual ground reality stays stubbornly the same. It’s the same old song and dance, just with more buzzwords.

They trotted out the usual suspects: C-suite delegates, chief AI officers, chief information security officers, global tech execs, investors, and a bunch of government officials. Over 400 of them, apparently, and more than 10,000 "technology executives" - whatever that really means in Nairobi terms. The ambition? To turn "fragmented innovation" into "scalable, transnational, and globally competitive AI infrastructure." Sounds impressive, doesn't it? Especially when you hear AI is supposed to magically churn out $1.5 trillion for Africa by 2030. Let’s see how much of that actually trickles down.

Kenya's Special Envoy, Philip Thigo, tried to sound urgent, saying Africa can't afford to be late to the AI party like it was with previous tech waves. He rattled off potential benefits in agriculture, health, and the creative economy. But then he admitted the "infrastructure gaps" - connectivity and energy. Shocker. He also, conveniently, praised Kenya's startup scene and regulatory framework, basically saying, "Come invest, we're open for business." Because that's what it always boils down to, doesn't it? Money. It’s a bit like when we talked about Mark Zuckerberg's Meta acquiring more AI nonsense; it's always about the next big thing, rarely about the practicalities for the average Kenyan.

Then you have Trixie LohMirmand from GITEX Global, going on about how technology is "no longer an option" and AI is "critical infrastructure." She congratulated Kenya on its digitalization, but then warned that the landscape has shifted. It’s a "global AI race," apparently led by countries, not just companies. She even mentioned sovereign AI, where countries want to *create* AI, not just use it. Because using AI makes you a market, but creating it makes you a power. Right. Meanwhile, Dr. Monica Musenero from Uganda pointed out the harsh truth: Africa is receiving AI "as a technology" and not as a new economic system. And, more importantly, she asked the crucial question: "Where is the money?" Africa, she stated, is retaining only one percent of the value in the AI economy.

The whole point, the "through line" as they call it, was ownership. Africa needs to build its own infrastructure, shape its own regulations, mobilize its own capital, and capture its own value. But let's be real, these summits are often more about the optics and the networking than actual, tangible change for the majority. They’ll talk about green minerals and data and talent, but the real challenges - the connectivity, the unreliable power, the leaky regulatory frameworks that seem to favour the connected few - those will still be here long after the delegates pack up their shiny briefcases.

So, here we are again, talking about AI's potential while the fundamental issues remain unaddressed. They’re calling it an "investment agenda," but it feels more like another opportunity for the usual players to stake their claims, leaving the rest of us to wonder if any of this AI magic will actually make our lives better, or if it’s just another way to extract value without truly empowering the continent.
