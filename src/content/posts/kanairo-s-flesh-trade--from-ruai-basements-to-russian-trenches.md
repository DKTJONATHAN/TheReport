Welcome to Nairobi, the shiny hub of East Africa and, apparently, the regional warehouse for human beings. Police just stumbled upon 70 souls in a Ruai basement - 66 Ethiopians and four Eritreans - hidden away like expired milk. We are told they are getting "assistance," which in this city usually means a cold floor and a long wait for a deportation order that never comes. It is the same old script played out in a different neighborhood.

This is the reality of the "transit route" label we wear so proudly. While the big fish run these syndicates from air-conditioned offices in Westlands, the authorities are usually busy patting themselves on the back for minor wins. It is a joke to think these networks are invisible, especially when [Boyi the Boogeyman: DCI Finally Bags a Small Fish with Big Boots](/posts/boyi-the-boogeyman--dci-finally-bags-a-small-fish-with-big-boots) is the kind of distraction we are fed while the real traffickers keep the borders open and the bribes flowing.

If you thought domestic servitude in the Middle East was the bottom of the barrel, think again. The latest intelligence figures show that we have graduated to exporting cannon fodder. There are 89 Kenyans currently dodging drones on the Russian front lines, while another 39 are rotting in foreign hospitals with injuries they did not ask for. They were promised "civilian jobs" and "military industry" roles, but the recruiters had a different job description in mind - one that involves a trench and a high probability of never coming home.

This is just another chapter in the long, dark history of [Exporting Souls and Empty Bonds](/posts/exporting-souls-and-empty-bonds) that defines our labor export policy. Families are now begging Parliament to stop the recruitment, but we all know how the wheels of justice turn in this town. When 28 people are missing in action and only one death is "formally confirmed," it is clear that the government cares more about the diplomatic paperwork than the actual human beings lost in the Ukrainian snow.

We love to talk about being a regional powerhouse, but we are really just a glorified clearing house for desperation. Whether it is a locked room in Ruai or a muddy trench in Eastern Europe, the story is the same: someone is making a killing while someone else is just dying. Investigations will "continue," files will gather dust, and the syndicates will just find a new house in a different suburb to start the whole cycle over again. Nairobi keeps moving, and the cost of doing business is always paid in blood.
