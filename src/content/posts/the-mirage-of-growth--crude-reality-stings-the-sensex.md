Global capital is not fooled by the local cheerleading. Foreign Institutional Investors have dumped nearly 7 billion dollars in Indian equities this month alone. This mass exodus is a rational response to a geopolitical landscape where Brent crude is stapled above the 100-dollar mark. For an economy like India, which remains hostage to energy imports, triple-digit oil is not just a headwind - it is a structural tax on every level of production.

The disruption in the Strait of Hormuz is the real master of the Sensex right now. While local analysts celebrate a rebound in auto and metal stocks, the reality is that the cost of shipping and the threat of an escalating Iran conflict are gutting trade balances. Even the much-vaunted services sector, which optimists claim will reach 6 trillion dollars by 2035, looks like a fever dream when the IT index has already shed nearly a quarter of its value this year.

The corporate landscape is equally fractured. Maruti Suzuki is currently locked in a multi-billion rupee tax dispute, a reminder of the regulatory minefield that awaits those who operate in the region. Meanwhile, firms like SBI Securities are launching "Women's Mode" features - a desperate pivot to capture new retail liquidity as the professional money managers exit the building.

Ultimately, these gains are a temporary distraction from the inevitable. When oil stays at 103 dollars and global supply chains are being choked by Middle Eastern conflict, a 600-point jump in Mumbai is just noise. The international community is watching a nation try to build a skyscraper on a foundation of expensive carbon and fleeing foreign capital. It is not a matter of if the correction continues, but how deep the floor really is.
