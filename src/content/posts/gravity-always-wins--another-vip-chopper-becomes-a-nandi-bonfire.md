In Kenya, if you are not dying from the latest government "innovation" or the rising cost of charcoal, you are probably falling out of the sky in a luxury metal box. Over the weekend, the North Rift reminded us that gravity does not care about your parliamentary seat or how many bodyguards follow you around. Six people, including Emurua Dikirr MP Johana Ng-eno, found out the hard way that when the fog rolls into Nandi County, even the loudest political voice gets silenced by the crunch of a failing engine.

It was meant to be a routine hop, but the weather had other plans for the legislator and his entourage. Local reports say the pilot tried a desperate emergency landing near Mosop, but visibility was basically zero. Instead of a safe touchdown, the chopper burst into flames, leaving nothing but smoke and another reason for a state-funded funeral. It is the same old story we hear every time a "mheshimiwa" decides that driving on our potholed roads is beneath them. They take to the air to escape the mess they helped create on the ground, only to meet a different kind of disaster.

President Ruto was quick with the "thoughts and prayers" on social media, as if a tweet could fix the lack of proper aviation infrastructure or the desperation of a country watching its leaders drop like flies. We are used to this script by now. It is just another episode of [Addis Appetizers and Nairobi Funerals: Ruto’s SHA Fairy Tale](/posts/addis-appetizers-and-nairobi-funerals--ruto-s-sha-fairy-tale) where the elites dine on the world stage while the reality at home remains a series of tragic accidents and broken promises. 

Along with the legislator, a pilot and four other passengers were turned into statistics. Nandi County police commander Samuel Mukusi confirmed the grim tally, though the names of the "commoners" on board were not immediately released. In this country, you are only worth a headline if you have a title before your name. To the families of the other four, this is a life-altering tragedy; to the political class, it is just a reason to hold a high-profile ceremony and give more empty speeches about "service to the nation."

Now we wait for the "investigations." We all know how those go. They will blame the weather, the fog, or perhaps a mechanical failure that should have been caught months ago if anyone actually followed the rules. But in a land where [your favorite 'hustlers' are actually just common thieves](/posts/red-cards-and-razzle-dazzle--why-your-favorite--hustlers--are-actually-just-common-thieves), expecting transparency or genuine safety standards is like expecting a matatu driver to give you back your change without a fight. Another day, another crash, and another reminder that in Kenya, the higher you fly, the harder the ground hits back.
