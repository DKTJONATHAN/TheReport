Kenya's Draft Law Still Taxes Second-Hand Clothes, And More Pain To Come
WARNING: Government unveils new Finance Bill 2026, promising more taxes on essentials like mitumba, phones, and digital services, while offering no relief to salaried workers. Expect a wider budget deficit and more headaches.
The Kenyan government is at it again, this time with the draft Finance Bill 2026, published on April 30th. They're chasing a hefty KSh 3.63 trillion in revenue for the 2026/27 fiscal year, which naturally means they need to squeeze more out of us. The budget deficit is also widening, up to 5.3 percent of GDP, so don't expect any miracles of fiscal responsibility.

This bill, set to kick in on July 1, 2026, is a masterclass in finding new ways to tax the already overburdened. Second-hand clothes, affectionately known as *mitumba*, are now on the chopping block. So are smartphones, the very tools many Kenyans use for banking, business, and just staying alive in this economy. Digital platforms are also getting hit, along with increased import duties on local products, which is just brilliant for encouraging local manufacturing, isn't it?

And for those of us with a steady job, prepare for more disappointment. The Pay As You Earn (PAYE) tax, which many were *hoping* would see some relief, has been conspicuously omitted. Faith Odhiambo, former President of the Law Society of Kenya, rightly points out this isn't a minor oversight; it's a slap in the face to every employed Kenyan. The proposed 25 percent excise duty on phones, which are not luxuries but necessities for communication and accessing services, is particularly galling.

The digital financial services sector, a lifeline for millions seeking financial inclusion, is also facing a squeeze. Existing VAT exemptions on money transfers and payment processing are being removed, making it more expensive for the very people the government claims to want to help. It seems the objective here is not a broader tax base, but a wider net for their cash grab. This whole situation reminds me of how sometimes, even with the best intentions, systems can just be a mess, much like when we discussed the complexities of [Avast Premium: The 'Smart' Cheap Antivirus is Still a Compromise](/posts/avast-premium--the--smart--cheap-antivirus-is-still-a-compromise).

Even tax filing timelines are being tightened, pushing the deadline up by two months to April 30th. And for the *mitumba* traders, a new clause deems a 5 percent profit on customs value, payable upfront, regardless of actual profit or loss. Critics are calling this inequitable, and frankly, it sounds like another way to burden small businesses that are already struggling. The increase in residential rental income tax from 7.5 to 10 percent, without a serious enforcement framework, will likely just drive more people underground.

While there are a few glimmers of what might be considered sensible measures, like VAT exemptions on electric buses and certain raw materials, and some clarity on trust taxation, these are small crumbs. The reduction in corporate tax for non-resident companies to 30 percent seems aimed at attracting foreign investment, but at what cost to the average Kenyan? It feels like the usual song and dance: we pay more, while the big players might get a sweet deal. Parliament needs to do more than just rubber-stamp this; they need to actually scrutinize it, because right now, it looks like another round of economic pain for the masses.
