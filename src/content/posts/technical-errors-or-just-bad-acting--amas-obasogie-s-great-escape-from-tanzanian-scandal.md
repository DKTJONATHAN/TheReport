Oh, the drama never stops in the world of African football, does it? One minute you are a celebrated Super Eagles bronze medalist, and the next, your club in Tanzania is treating you like a common criminal. Amas Obasogie, the man who supposedly guards the net for Singida Black Stars, has been cleared of match-fixing allegations after a three-month suspension that lasted about as long as a celebrity marriage. The club suddenly "unearthed no evidence," which is PR speak for "we realized we actually need him to win games."

Let us talk about the "technical errors" excuse. The club says the incidents that raised eyebrows were just mistakes on the pitch. Honestly, if my "technical errors" at work involved looking like I was throwing a game for cash, I would be filing for unemployment, not getting an apology tour. It is the kind of flimsy explanation that makes you wonder if [The Great Tech Divergence: Why Bureaucracy Lost the Race for the Future](/posts/the-great-tech-divergence--why-bureaucracy-lost-the-race-for-the-future) applies to football management too, because their investigative speed and logic are clearly stuck in the stone age.

Amas has reportedly apologized for the "circumstances" and promised to work on his "shortcomings." Translation: "I am sorry I got caught looking suspicious, and I will try to be more subtle next time." It is fascinating how these players always find themselves in these "misunderstandings." It is not like the fans are blind; we see the clumsy dives and the misplaced passes that conveniently lead to goals. But sure, let's call it a lack of "trust" and move on.

Obasogie is not the only Nigerian goalie with a "betting" problem in his portfolio. Remember Maduka Okoye's little Italian suspension in 2025? It seems like being a Super Eagles goalkeeper comes with a side hustle requirement these days. Whether it is Italy or Tanzania, the stench of gambling and match-fixing follows our boys like a bad perfume that just won't wash off. It is a pattern that is becoming harder to ignore, no matter how many "investigations" come back clean.

At the end of the day, the club has reversed the suspension and restored him to his duties. They want us to believe the trust is restored and everything is sunshine and rainbows. But in this industry, where money talks and ethics walk, we know better. Amas might be back in the goal, but the fans will be watching every "technical error" with a magnifying glass and a whole lot of side-eye. Welcome back, Amas, try not to trip over any more "technicalities."
