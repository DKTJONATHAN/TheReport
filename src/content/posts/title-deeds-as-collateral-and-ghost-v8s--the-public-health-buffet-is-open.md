At KNH, the management has reached a new level of audacity. They are losing Sh36 million in rental income because their occupancy is at 60 per cent, yet their brilliant solution is to raise rent by 10 per cent on the few remaining tenants. To make it even more "Nairobi," some of these houses are already marked for demolition. It is the classic Kenyan landlord move: charge more for a house that is literally falling apart while you bypass open tenders to buy cleaning materials through "restricted requests." It is not an error; it is a business model designed to benefit the few while the sick sleep on the floor.

Then we have KEMRI, the supposed center of excellence, where a Sh4 billion title deed for land in Nairobi just grew legs and walked into a private developer's hands. Apparently, this developer used public land as collateral for a private bank loan. The loan is paid, but the title deed is playing hide-and-seek between the National Bank and the Treasury. While they "look" for the paper, they are also driving 66 motor vehicles that do not exist on the asset register. In this city, if a car does not have a paper trail but still consumes fuel, someone is making a killing on "logistics."

The Pharmacy and Poisons Board is the icing on this rotten cake. While they claim they cannot stop substandard medicines because the borders are "porous," they have no trouble spending Sh5.25 million on vehicle repairs without a single inspection report. It is funny how the "gaps in surveillance" only exist when it comes to keeping us alive, but the financial engine for "maintenance" and "unrecorded land" in Machakos is running perfectly fine. If you fail to recover after taking local medicine but get well once you buy the same drug abroad, you are not just a patient; you are a victim of a system that cares more about Sh75 million land deals than your survival.

This entire circus reminds me of how the elite operate in every sector of this country. Whether it is health or education, the script is the same: collect the money, lose the assets, and then ask for a bigger budget next year. Like I said in "Degrees in Deception: How Your Tuition Funded a VC’s New V8" (/posts/degrees-in-deception--how-your-tuition-funded-a-vc-s-new-v8), the institutional rot is not a bug, it is a feature. We are just the spectators paying for a show where we always end up as the punchline.

The committee says it will issue "firm recommendations" and "possible referrals." We all know what that means in Kenyan-speak: a few headlines, a lot of shouting in Parliament, and then we wait for the 2025 reports to show us that the Sh4 billion title deed has been replaced by a Sh8 billion loss. At this point, the only thing "public" about these health institutions is the bill we pay to keep the looters in V8s. Stay skeptical, Nairobi. It is the only way to survive the "systemic weaknesses" of a government that eats its own people.
