So, Nairobi hosted another big talk shop, the International Conference on Nuclear Energy (ICoNE) 2026. We're told it’s all about “supporting Africa’s clean energy transition and long-term development goals.” Fancy words from folks like the NEA Director-General William D. Magwood, IV, who probably flew in on a private jet fueled by good intentions and maybe some offshore oil. They gathered, all 500-plus of them from 33 countries, to chat about how nuclear power can magically solve our energy security, economic transformation, and climate woes. As if we haven’t heard this song and dance before.

President Ruto himself graced the occasion, waxing lyrical about “clean, reliable electricity” and how Kenya is making “progress.” Progress. Yes, we’re progressing towards more committees, more studies, and more promises that sound suspiciously like the ones we heard when discussing, say, the CHEAP OIL MIRAGE: CHINA’S TEAPOT REFINERIES ARE ABOUT TO GET SMOKED. The real progress is probably in the catering budget for these events.

Mudavadi, the Cabinet Secretary, chimed in, telling us Africa’s energy demand is “rising rapidly” due to population growth, urbanization, and industrialization. Brilliant observation. And some countries are “exploring nuclear energy as part of a diversified energy mix.” Exploring. That’s the key word. Exploring means not doing, not building, not powering homes. It means more meetings, more consultants, and more opportunities for people to fly around the continent talking about nuclear.

The conference covered a wide range of topics, from “financing approaches” (read: how to get someone else to pay for it) to “public acceptance” (read: how to convince people to let you build a radioactive elephant in their backyard). They even discussed “youth engagement,” because apparently, the young professionals are the ones who will finally figure out how to make nuclear power cheap and safe for us all. It’s all about building capacity, strengthening co-operation, and fostering informed engagement. In other words, lots of talking, very little doing. The illusion of progress, much like THE ILLUSION OF SAFETY: ENGINEERING THE NEXT FINANCIAL TRAP, is easier to construct than the actual thing.

The whole affair wrapped up with participants sharing “experiences and lessons learned.” Lessons learned, no doubt, from countries that actually have functioning nuclear power plants, not from the endless discussions happening here. By convening a “diverse group of stakeholders,” ICoNE 2026 managed to deepen understanding, which is just a polite way of saying they all nodded at each other and agreed it’s complicated. The safe, secure, and sustainable development of the sector. We'll see about that.
