Nairobi has always been a city of tiers, and the international school circuit is the top shelf where the air is supposed to be cleaner. But as the Senate Education Committee is finally discovering, that "prestige" is often just a thin coat of paint over a crumbling foundation of illegality. We are being told that these institutions, which charge parents enough to buy a small island in Lake Victoria, are dodging NSSF, failing to remit the Housing Levy, and hiding behind "political protection." It is the classic Nairobi hustle, just wrapped in a foreign curriculum and a gated community.

The allegations are almost comical if they weren't so depressing. You have schools operating in residential houses without zoning approvals, basically turning a quiet Lavington cul-de-sac into a traffic nightmare under the guise of "education." Even worse, they are allegedly hiring foreign nationals on tourist visas to teach your kids, while qualified Kenyan teachers are left to rot on the sidelines. It is the ultimate middle finger to the local labor market. These schools sell a "global citizen" dream while acting like a backyard car wash when it comes to the law.

Senator Cherargei and his colleagues are acting shocked that these places have "diplomatic cover" to avoid scrutiny. Please. Anyone who has lived in this city for more than a week knows that if you have the right phone numbers, the law is merely a suggestion. It is the same culture of entitlement we see at [The State House Tea Party](/posts/the-state-house-tea-party--ruto-s-sh2-billion-pity-payment-for-kemu) where political favors trump public interest every single time. These schools aren't just educational centers; they are businesses that have figured out that "international" is a magic word that makes regulators look the other way.

The financial misconduct is the real kicker. Deducting statutory contributions like SHA and NSSF from staff salaries and then pocketing the money is straight-up theft. It is one thing to overcharge parents for a "holistic learning environment," but it is quite another to steal the future pensions of the people actually doing the work. The Senate says they want to safeguard students and teachers, but we have heard this script before. Usually, these probes end with a few stern words and a "donation" to the right campaign fund before everyone goes back to business as usual.

In the end, this is just another layer of the Nairobi mirage. We want to believe that by paying millions, we are buying our children a ticket out of the chaos, but the chaos is right there in the bursar's office. If these schools are falsifying assessments and expanding without approval, then they aren't institutions of learning; they are expensive daycare centers for the deluded. Don't hold your breath for the "comprehensive review." In this city, the only thing that actually gets regulated is the person too poor to pay for protection.
