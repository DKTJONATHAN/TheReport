Central banks are masters of the "look through" technique, a linguistic trick used to ignore the immediate suffering of the populace. By claiming they will look through the oil price shock, policymakers are essentially telling the public to endure the pain while they maintain the status quo. The survey data reveals that Canadian firms are already hiking their inflation forecasts for the coming year. They know what the ivory tower economists refuse to admit: when the cost of fuel, freight, and fertilizer rises, the entire supply chain becomes a conveyor belt for poverty.

The claim that businesses will "absorb" these costs is a comforting lie designed to prevent civil unrest. In reality, pricing power is a zero-sum game. If a firm cannot raise its sticker price due to a weak demand environment, it will instead engage in the slow rot of shrinkflation or service degradation. The consumer pays the price regardless, whether through a smaller box of cereal or a crumbling infrastructure that the state can no longer afford to maintain without printing more fiat currency.

On a global scale, this conflict serves to tighten the noose around trade-sensitive sectors. While the Bank of Canada notes a brief moment of optimism regarding U.S. tariffs prior to the war, that sentiment is now irrelevant. Geopolitical instability is the ultimate tariff. It increases the cost of every transaction and adds a "war premium" to every necessity. This is not a temporary glitch in the system; it is the new architecture of a fragmenting global order where stability is priced as a luxury few can afford.

The intersection of war and technology adds a final, grim layer to the outlook. As households cut back on travel and major spending, they are also staring down the barrel of artificial intelligence. The survey suggests that workers expect fewer hours and diminished prospects. We are entering an era where the cost of living is driven up by kinetic warfare, while the value of human labor is driven down by silicon. It is a pincer movement designed to crush the middle class from both ends.

Ultimately, the talk of "supply and demand" and "commodity cycles" is a smokescreen. Real inflation is the deliberate destruction of money. The war in the Middle East simply provides the smoke. While the Bank of Canada and its peers across the G7 prepare to hold rates or offer "modest" adjustments, the reality remains that the global economy is being managed for the benefit of the debtor class - the governments and mega-corporations - while the savers and workers are left to drown in the rising tide of costs. Stability is not coming back; it was never the goal.
