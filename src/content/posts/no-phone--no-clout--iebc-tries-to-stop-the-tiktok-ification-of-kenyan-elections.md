Kenya has reached a point where if you do not post a Boomerang of your marked ballot, your civic duty basically did not happen. The IEBC chairperson, Edung Ethekon, is officially over it. He is out here reminding everyone that the polling station is not a photo studio for your aesthetic "I Voted" dump. Apparently, during the November 27th by-elections, people were so desperate for engagement that they leaked their own secret ballots just to show they were on the "right" side of history.

Ethekon is citing Articles 38 and 81 of the Constitution, acting like he is the only one who still cares about the "secrecy of the ballot." He claims that sharing your vote on social media "compromises the integrity" of the process. Honey, the integrity was compromised the moment politics became a game of influencers and hashtags. But sure, let’s blame the person with an iPhone 13 for the "undue influence" and "vote-buying" that has been the backbone of Kenyan politics since the dawn of time.

It is all about the optics, is it not? Much like we saw in [Martha Njoki And The Politics Of Rescue: Mercy, Optics, And Power Outside Nairobi City Hall](/posts/martha-njoki-politics-of-rescue), everything in this city is a calculated move to look like a savior or a patriot. People are not voting because they believe in the manifesto; they are voting so they can have a story to post before they head to the nearest brunch spot in Kilimani. The IEBC trying to ban phones is like a parent trying to stop a teenager from vaping - it is cute, but ultimately useless.

The commission says they are implementing "administrative control measures" to protect us. That is just government-speak for "we are going to hover over you like annoying hawks." They are worried about vote-buying, which is hilarious because if someone is going to sell their soul for a few thousand shillings, they will find a way to do it whether there is a camera involved or not. The secrecy of the ballot is a "constitutional right," but in the age of oversharing, privacy is the one thing no one actually wants.

So, if you are planning to head to the polls this Thursday, prepare to be treated like a suspect in a high-security prison. Ethekon is determined to make sure your vote stays between you and the ballot box. But let us be honest, by Friday morning, the results will be contested, someone will be crying on Twitter, and we will all be looking for the next scandal to distract us from the fact that nothing ever really changes. Democracy is messy, but our curated feeds must remain spotless.
