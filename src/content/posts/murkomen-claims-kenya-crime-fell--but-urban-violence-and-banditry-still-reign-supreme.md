So, our esteemed Interior CS, Kipchumba Murkomen, trotted into Parliament with a report card claiming Kenya's crime rate has magically dipped by a whole 1.6% between September 2025 and February 2026. Forty-eight thousand, five hundred and forty-five cases, down from... well, slightly more cases last year. Apparently, that's something to celebrate. It’s the same old song and dance, just with a few less numbers this time. Don't start planning that street festival just yet.

Of course, the usual suspects are still topping the charts: assaults, disturbances, and the ever-reliable "other Penal Code offences." Seems Nairobians and Kenyans in general are still finding plenty of ways to annoy each other. And surprise, surprise, while the CS crows about minimal police corruption, motor vehicle theft has actually gone *up*. Perhaps the minimal corruption is just that - minimal, and the rest is business as usual.

But let's not get too comfortable with this supposed dip. Murkomen himself admits that banditry and cattle rustling are still having a field day in the North Rift and Upper Eastern, and Al-Shabaab hasn't packed their bags from Mandera and Garissa. And then there's the real gem: urban areas are seeing a rise in gang-related activity. Nairobi, Nakuru, Mombasa - they're all on the list. You'd think with all the political posturing and empty promises, like those offered to Kamket and Kassait after their son's death, something tangible would be done. But no, it's just more reports and more deployment of "security personnel" to the same old trouble spots.

The list of "high-insecurity hotspots" reads like a grim tour guide of Nairobi's less-than-glamorous corners: Kayole, Dandora, Mathare, the CBD's infamous River Road and Kirinyaga Road, Kilimani, Kawangware, and Kibra. Daylight robberies by motorbike gangs? That's the new normal. Meanwhile, in other parts of the country, it's the same old story of banditry and terrorism threats. It's almost as if the root causes of these problems are being ignored, while we're presented with statistics that conveniently ignore the daily grind of fear many Kenyans live with.

Murkomen mentioned that security personnel have been deployed. Great. How many? For how long? And are they actually tackling the problem or just being shuffled around to look busy? We've seen this movie before. The CS might be trying to paint a rosy picture, but for the average Kenyan navigating these "hotspots," the reality is far less rosy. This whole report feels like another attempt to paper over the cracks, hoping we'll just accept the numbers and move on.
