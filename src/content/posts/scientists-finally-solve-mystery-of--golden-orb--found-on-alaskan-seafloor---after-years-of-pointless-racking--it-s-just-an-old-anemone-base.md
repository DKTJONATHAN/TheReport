It appears humanity’s burning curiosity, fueled by taxpayer dollars, has once again led to an anticlimactic resolution. After nearly three years of intensive testing, genetic sequencing, and the coordinated efforts of numerous scientists, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) has finally identified the mysterious "golden orb" discovered off the Alaskan coast in 2023. Brace yourselves, for it is not an alien egg, nor a lost piece of Atlantean technology. It is, in fact, the calcified base of a giant sea anemone.

The approximately 4-inch wide object, encountered at a staggering depth of 10,827 feet, baffled researchers from the outset. Its brassy hue and peculiar mound shape, complete with a small hole suggesting attachment to a rock, prompted initial hypotheses ranging from egg casings to unknown coral formations. The object was dutifully collected and whisked away to the Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History for examination, where it continued to resist easy classification.

"We work on hundreds of different samples and I suspected that our routine processes would clarify the mystery," stated Allen Collins, zoologist and director of NOAA Fisheries’ National Systematics Laboratory, in a press release. This statement, no doubt, glosses over the considerable resources and time consumed by a "special case" that necessitated "focused efforts and expertise of several different individuals." The problem, it seems, was so "complex" it required "morphological, genetic, deep-sea and bioinformatics expertise to solve." One can only imagine the grant proposals written and the meetings held to justify such an extensive, multi-disciplinary assault on what amounts to a sea creature’s discarded foundation.

Initial laboratory analysis revealed the orb lacked typical animal anatomy, instead comprising a fibrous material interwoven with cnidocytes - the stinging cells characteristic of jellyfish, corals, and their kin. The breakthrough came when these cells showed a resemblance to those of a specimen found in 2021. Despite initial inconclusive DNA results, likely due to contamination from the ubiquitous microscopic life of the deep sea, whole-genome sequencing eventually pinpointed the orb as a remnant of the _Relicanthus daphneae_ species, a giant deep-sea anemone.

The orb, typically hidden beneath the animal when it attaches to the seafloor, is essentially a collection of dead cells. The grand mystery, the subject of years of scientific endeavor and likely significant public funding, turns out to be a glorified biological attachment point.

William Mowitt, acting director of NOAA Ocean Exploration, offered a platitude about unlocking secrets and driving economic growth, national security, and planetary sustainability. This grand vision, however, seems a bit overstated when the ultimate revelation is a glorified sea slug’s toenail. While the scientific method, in its own ponderous way, eventually arrived at an answer, the entire affair serves as a potent reminder that sometimes, the most captivating mysteries of the deep are simply… the base of an anemone. And the cost of finding that out can be astronomical.
