THE WORLD IS WRANGLING ON HIGH SEAS AMIDST CHAOS

The big boys are flexing their muscles in our waters, but don't expect it to fix anything for us. Another naval parade, another show of force.

The French, with their giant warships that could probably sink this whole city, are out here "preparing forces" for sea crimes. Piracy, drug trafficking, illegal fishing - the usual suspects that have been bleeding us dry long before the West started playing footsie with Iran. Now, with geopolitical tensions higher than a kite on the coast, it's all suddenly urgent.

Captain Jocelyn Delrieu, commander of some fancy French task force, chirps about "geopolitical uncertainties." They're patrolling the Indian and Pacific oceans, dealing with, you know, *uncertainties*. They were part of this EU Atalanta mission, supposedly tackling piracy off the Horn of Africa. But let's be real, they're doing it for their own "territories" in the Indian Ocean like Mayotte and Reunion. It's all about their interests, not ours. This whole song and dance about "freedom of navigation" is just a fancy way of saying they want to keep their trade routes clear. Just like when piracy went up, insurance premiums shot up, making our cargo more expensive. And now, with this Iran kerfuffle, shipping lines are slapping on war surcharges. It's a vicious cycle, and we're always the ones paying the price.

This whole naval presence is supposed to deter all sorts of crimes, from piracy and drug trafficking to enforcing UN arms embargoes on groups like Al-Shabaab. It's another one of those elaborate Nairobi anti-drug circuses, complete with foreign dignitaries and naval hardware. They'll patrol, they'll "repress," and then they'll sail away, leaving us to deal with the mess. It’s a cycle, and we’re stuck in it.

These nations, they talk about collective responsibility for the Indian Ocean. But when the dust settles, and the warships depart, who's left to pick up the pieces? It’s always us, the ones on the ground, dealing with the fallout of their geopolitical games. Commodity prices will continue to rise, as the UN Conference on Trade and Development so helpfully points out. It's a constant reminder that while they're busy playing their high-stakes games, our livelihoods are on the line.

This isn't some new development, though. We've seen it all before. From pirates to profits, East Africa must rule the Indian Ocean, but that's a dream for another day. For now, we just watch as the big powers parade their might, offering little more than platitudes while the real problems fester.
