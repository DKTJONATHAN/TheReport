KANO'S "SCHOLARSHIP" SCAM: EX-COMMISH'S 35 ACCOUNTS RAISE MORE THAN JUST EYEBROWS

So, it turns out Kano's foreign scholarship program for bright young minds has gone spectacularly off the rails, or perhaps, more accurately, straight into some very shady bank accounts. PLATFORM TIMES has the dirt: our beloved former Commissioner for Higher Education, Dr. Yusuf Ibrahim Kofar Mata, is apparently hoarding a cool 35 bank accounts linked to his name. Thirty-five! Does the man think he's building a personal offshore empire with all that scholarship cash?

Apparently, the state government happily tossed around N4.6 billion for 1,001 students to chase dreams of academia in places like India, Kenya, and Uganda. Sounds lovely, right? Except, surprise, surprise, only 418 lucky souls actually made it out. Where did the rest of the money go, you ask? Well, it seems a substantial chunk might have found a more permanent residence in Dr. Kofar Mata's burgeoning bank collection. It’s almost as if the whole "higher education" thing was just a fancy front for a personal ATM.

And here’s where it gets *chef's kiss* delicious. The good doctor also happened to be the sole operator of Crescent Coast Consult and Investment Ltd, a company that conveniently handled visa processing and airline tickets for these very same scholarship students. Talk about a conflict of interest so blatant it’s practically wearing a neon sign. It’s the kind of move that makes you wonder if he saw himself as a brilliant entrepreneur or just a slightly less subtle kleptomaniac. This whole charade screams "Religion: The Ultimate Get-Out-of-Jail-Free Card for Failed Politicians" waiting to happen.

Naturally, Dr. Kofar Mata is crying foul, blaming it all on "politics" and his refusal to join the APC. Oh, honey, please. Saving your own skin with a tired old political excuse? That’s about as original as a TikTok dance trend. He’s also denying his company's involvement, which, given the sheer number of accounts and the timing, makes about as much sense as believing a politician who promises to lower taxes.

The Kano State Public Complaints and Anti-Corruption Commission, bless their persistent hearts, confirmed the discovery of these suspiciously numerous accounts but are keeping the juicy details under wraps because, you know, "ongoing investigation." They've promised justice for anyone found culpable, but let's be real, in this game, the truly culpable often just end up on a different continent.

So, while the anti-graft agency does its thing, we're left with another cautionary tale about how easily public funds meant for upliftment can become personal piggy banks. Thirty-five accounts, folks. That's not just mismanagement; that's a career choice.
