Garmin, a company that seems to exist solely to churn out slightly different versions of existing tech, is apparently getting into the "quantify your suffering" business with a new wearable dubbed Cirqa. According to a trademark filing, this nonmedical, nontherapeutic gadget will measure and analyze your bio-signals and behavior, specifically focusing on recovery from stress and performance levels. In plain English, it's designed to tell you how tired and stressed you are, just like every other device currently cluttering wrists.

This isn't exactly groundbreaking. Google is already dabbling in screenless wearables that feed data into apps, presumably to keep you tethered to your phone even when you're trying to disconnect. And the Cirqa's focus on "recovery" is a direct nod to Whoop, a company that has made a name for itself by selling expensive subscriptions to tell people how badly they're burning themselves out. In fact, Whoop is already suing competitors for infringing on its designs, proving that the tech world is less about innovation and more about who can best mimic the latest cash cow before the lawyers get involved.

Garmin's previous attempt at sleep tracking, the Index Sleep Monitor, was an armband worn on the upper arm. It's a safe bet that Cirqa will just move that data collection down to the wrist, offering 24/7 insights into your increasingly monitored existence. Expect this to launch around May or June, just in time for summer when everyone pretends to be healthier. The company, predictably, declined to comment, a standard tactic when they're caught red handed trying to replicate someone else's success.

So, while the tech press will undoubtedly hail this as another step towards a healthier, happier you, let's be clear: it's just another product designed to extract more data from your life and sell it back to you in the form of anxieties and subscription fees. Embrace the analog, indeed.
