So, the latest fad has decided Nairobi's gritty streets are now a "must-do" item on the global tourist bucket list. Forget the actual city life, apparently, the real draw is watching young people perform for cameras and shake their legs in the CBD on weekends. It's not about genuine connection, it's about "creating content" - a polite way of saying turning their lives into digital fodder for foreign eyes. Suddenly, our urban sprawl is "beautiful" because a few tourists are willing to pay for the spectacle, moving beyond the usual wildlife safaris to consume our "vibrant culture."

We're told this is "meaningful creative work" and "exclusively Kenyan," according to folks like Steve Mwangi, a street photographer. Meaningful, perhaps, if you consider monetizing your daily existence for a few shillings meaningful. And "exclusively Kenyan"? As if street performances for tourists haven't been a global phenomenon forever. Dancers like Mary Wambui admit the "challenge many of them face is monetisation," with visitors often watching and leaving "without offering any form of monetary appreciation." It all sounds a bit like [Ruto's G7 Gig: Is It About Kenya's Economy or Just Another Photo Op?](/posts/ruto-s-g7-gig--is-it-about-kenya-s-economy-or-just-another-photo-op), doesn't it? Lots of grand gestures, uncertain returns.

This shift, from a personal hobby to a "reliable source of income," is painted as a success story. But what kind of reliability are we talking about when it hinges on the whims of passing tourists who "give us something small as a token of appreciation"? It's not exactly a thriving industry; it's a precarious gig economy built on informal donations, dressing up everyday life as a curated experience for those eager to "document their experiences in Kenya."

Freelance tour guide David Odhiambo claims Nairobi street photography has added a "new layer to urban tourism," suddenly making the capital a "beautiful attraction." Sure, for those who prefer their beauty packaged and performative. The demand from "hundreds of young men and women" for stakeholders to "develop a structure that will support young talent while promoting tourism" is telling. It screams of desperation, a plea for legitimate recognition and sustainable support, rather than being left to hustle for loose change on the pavements. It's always about promises of "support" when the real profits are elsewhere.
