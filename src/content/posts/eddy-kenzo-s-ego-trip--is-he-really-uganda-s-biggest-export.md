Bless his heart, Eddy Kenzo really thinks he is the blueprint for Ugandan fame. Over the weekend, the "Sitya Loss" singer decided to grace our feeds by reposting a graphic from a social media page called Africa First, claiming he is the most famous person in Uganda. It is the kind of [High-Level Hallucinations: Nairobi Prepares for Another Digital Talkfest](/posts/high-level-hallucinations--nairobi-prepares-for-another-digital-talkfest) we usually reserve for delusional influencers, but Kenzo is clearly thirsty for a crown that does not fit. This ranking, supposedly based on Google searches and social media reach, puts him in the same breath as Diamond Platnumz and Paul Kagame.

The problem with being a legend in your own head is that the internet has a very long memory and a very loud mouth. While Kenzo was busy celebrating his "global influence," the rest of the world was busy remembering names like Idi Amin and Bobi Wine. You know, people who actually make international headlines for things other than reposting dubious social media awards. It is a classic case of a celebrity trying to manufacture a legacy out of thin air while ignoring the political giants who actually occupy the global stage.

The comment section was, predictably, a bloodbath of reality checks. One user, Nantambi Namusisi, hit the nail on the head by asking if Kenzo really thinks he has more staying power than Idi Amin. Let us be real - most people outside the continent could not pick Kenzo out of a lineup, but they certainly know the names of the men who have actually shaped the country's history, for better or worse. Even his "dedicated admirers" were forced to step in and tell him to pipe down, with one fan basically saying "bino nedda" - which is polite Luganda for "stop lying to yourself."

It is funny how these "Africa First" pages always seem to find data that confirms exactly what a celebrity wants to hear. If you look at the metrics long enough, you can convince yourself you are more famous than the sun. But in the real world, fame is not just about how many bots are searching your name or how many times you repost a fake ranking. It is about actual impact. Kenzo might have the hits, but claiming he is more iconic than figures who dominate global political discourse is a stretch even for a man with his level of confidence.

At the end of the day, this is just another example of the toxic "fake it till you make it" culture that has infected the East African music scene. Instead of focusing on making music that actually breaks new ground, our stars are obsessed with digital validation and meaningless titles. Kenzo can keep reposting these graphics all he wants, but until he is actually more recognizable than the current or former residents of State House, he should probably stick to singing and leave the "most famous" talk to the history books.
