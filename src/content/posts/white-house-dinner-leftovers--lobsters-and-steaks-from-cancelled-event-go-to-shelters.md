The elaborate spread of lobster and steak, intended for the crème de la crème of Washington's political and media circles at the White House Correspondents' Dinner, has found a new, albeit less glamorous, destination: shelters for abused women and children. The annual gala, a tradition of self congratulation and carefully curated access, was unceremoniously halted Saturday night after a shooting incident that saw a Secret Service agent injured.

With the festivities abruptly terminated, a considerable quantity of food - approximately 2,600 meals - remained unserved. While the immediate aftermath saw speculation and even dumpster diving from opportunistic media outlets, the official explanation is that the Washington Hilton, the venue, donated the uneaten fare. According to Weijia Jiang, president of the journalists' association that organizes the event, the hotel "freeze dried the steak and lobster for longer shelf life" before distributing it to two shelters.

This act, while framed as charitable, neatly sidesteps the inherent wastefulness of such lavish events. The $350 per plate dinner, a symbol of the symbiotic, often self serving relationship between the press and political power, was itself a point of contention for some. Now, the uneaten remnants of this display of excess are being presented as a philanthropic victory.

The Hilton spokesperson offered a standard statement about their regular donations, adding that remaining produce was composted for agricultural use. This all sounds very responsible, of course, a convenient narrative for an organization that caters to the elite. The incident itself, the attempted assassination of President Donald Trump by Cole Allen, a Californian man who allegedly traveled to the capital with weapons, serves as a stark reminder of the volatile realities that can intrude upon these carefully constructed worlds.

Ultimately, the fate of the uneaten lobster and steak is a microcosm of the broader narrative: a grand event is disrupted, and the fallout, however opulent, is repackaged to appear benevolent. The press, as ever, is keen to report the feel good angle, conveniently overlooking the inherent absurdity of such a situation.
