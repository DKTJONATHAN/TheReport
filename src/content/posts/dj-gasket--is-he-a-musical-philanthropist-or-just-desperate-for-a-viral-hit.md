Oh look, another Lagos DJ has decided that spinning decks isn't enough to pay for the designer fits. Ikudabo Michael, better known as DJ Gasket, is the latest to transition from the Island circuit to the recording booth. He has checked all the boxes: Kogi-born, Island-bred, and currently desperate to convince us that he is more than just a man with a USB drive. He is pushing this narrative that entertainers are the new moral compass of society, but we have seen this script before. It is the classic "I am a serious artiste" re-brand.

Let us talk about these collaborations. He managed to bag Terry Apala, Magnito, and Hotkidfire for his latest release. While his PR team is screaming about "amplified profiles" and "social media conversations," those of us with ears know how the industry works. You either have the talent or you have the budget to buy a feature that makes people look your way. It is a common move to jump from the DJ booth to the mic, but usually, it is just a way to avoid being the guy standing behind the real stars while they actually perform.

The most exhausting part of Gasket's brand is the sudden "humanitarian" pivot. He claims giving has been his lifestyle since childhood. Please. Every time a B-list celebrity needs a boost in engagement, they suddenly find a camera crew to follow them to an "underserved community" for a few hours of performative kindness. In an era where visibility is a currency, it feels less like charity and more like a tax-deductible photoshoot. We have to wonder if this is genuine impact or just another casualty of the [Silicon Savannah or Digital Slaughterhouse?](/posts/silicon-savannah-or-digital-slaughterhouse) that defines our modern online existence.

His quotes on success are straight out of a mediocre self-help book. He tells us that success is about "meaningful goals" and "staying true to values" rather than streams or fame. That is easy to say when you are still chasing the streams and the fame. If the music was actually topping the charts, he would be bragging about the numbers like everyone else. Instead, he is urging other artistes to "deploy their influence for societal good," which is just industry-speak for "please like me because I am a nice guy."

At the end of the day, DJ Gasket is trying to position himself as a man of service. Whether his catalogue of music actually inspires "real change" or just fills the silence in a half-empty club remains to be seen. We will be watching to see if this "social vision" lasts longer than his next single's promotional cycle. Lagos is full of DJs who want to be saints, but we prefer our entertainment without the sanctimonious side dish. Stay in your lane or at least give us a beat that doesn't require a lecture on morality.
