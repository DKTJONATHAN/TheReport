KENYA’S NEXT PHASE OF MOBILE MONEY SUCCESS: MORE GIMMICKS THAN GOOD?

This story is about how Kenya, hailed as a digital payments pioneer, is now drowning in its own success, facing fragmentation that's a nightmare for businesses, while consumers still get their cash.

This ubiquitous mobile money ecosystem is more like a tangled web than a smooth operation for businesses.

NAIROBI - So, Kenya, the so-called global blueprint for mobile money. Everyone’s patting themselves on the back about financial inclusion, with 47 million souls hooked on their phones for cash. Ninety percent of adults, they say. And the money moving? Sixty-one percent of our entire GDP. Impressive, right? Except, while it’s a fairytale for the masses, for the businesses trying to *collect* this digital deluge, it’s turning into a chaotic mess.

This digital payment utopia has expanded beyond just sending shillings to your mama. Now it’s commerce, loans, insurance - you name it. And guess what? Our supposedly revolutionary single-rail system is buckling under the pressure. Businesses are juggling mobile money, bank transfers, cards, and whatever other fancy real-time gizmos are out there. For us consumers, it’s a breeze - fast, familiar, done. But for the poor sods running SMEs, it’s a daily battle. They’re drowning in Till and Paybill numbers, bank transfers, and probably still accepting sacks of cash under the table.

Each of these payment streams comes with its own set of rules, settlement times, and frankly, headaches. What looks like a single revenue stream on paper is actually a spaghetti junction of fragmented flows that someone, usually a perpetually stressed finance team, has to meticulously track and reconcile. Imagine trying to figure out where all your money went when it came from a dozen different leaky pipes. This isn't innovation; it's a hidden cost that’s eating into profits. We're seeing this play out everywhere, from bustling marketplaces to quiet corner shops. This whole thing is starting to feel like the same old story of promises of progress that end up making life harder for those trying to make a living. It's almost as if the government is good at introducing new ways to collect cash, but not so good at making sure businesses can actually manage it, similar to how [EXPOSED: How Gava is Using Your Taxes to Pay ‘Keyboard Warriors’ to Silence You (While the Cybercrime Bill Waits to Finish the Job)](/posts/is-the-government-using-bots).

And the competition? Fiercer than a matatu tout during rush hour. Everyone’s slashing prices and launching new features, trying to snag a piece of the pie. Consumers, bless their easily distracted hearts, are hopping between platforms depending on the day and the mood. But more options don't magically translate into simplicity, especially for businesses. Every new payment rail is another layer of paperwork, another process to manage. It’s a race to the bottom for providers, leaving businesses stuck with an increasingly complex operational nightmare. What we need is something to tie all these chaotic flows together, not just more ways to send money out.

The global trend is this so-called "embedded finance," shoving payments right into the user journey. In Kenya, we're seeing early whispers of it, with businesses finally demanding programmable payments and real-time financial clarity. It’s a shift from just enabling transactions to actually helping businesses *manage* their money. The future of payment service providers (PSPs) seems to be about integration - because we have a million payment methods but zero cohesion. Then there's visibility - tracking hundreds of billions of shillings flowing daily requires more than just blind faith. And finally, automation. We're past the point where manual reconciliation is sustainable. Players like PayKit might be the ones quietly patching up this mess, but let's be honest, it’s a mess of our own making.
