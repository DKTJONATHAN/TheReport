So, our esteemed petroleum honchos - PS Mohamed Liban, KPC MD Joe Sang, and EPRA DG Daniel Kiptoo - have decided to gracefully exit. Apparently, they were caught red-handed, allegedly "falsifying in-country fuel stock levels." Translation: they cooked the numbers to make us think we were about to run dry, all so they could pull some dodgy emergency procurement stunt. Because why solve real problems when you can manufacture fake ones for personal gain, right?

According to the Chief of Staff, Felix Koskei, this whole charade was to justify buying overpriced, substandard fuel outside the official Government-to-Government (G2G) channels. It’s the oldest trick in the book: create panic, then swoop in with a "solution" that benefits you directly. This isn't just about fuel; it's about who gets to skim off the top when the nation's resources are up for grabs. It reminds me of how some people seem to operate as if [our sovereignty became a side hustle](https://example.com/posts/passports-for-warlords--how-our-sovereignty-became-a-side-hustle).

The details are as murky as a Nairobi pothole after a downpour. We're talking about a 60,000-metric-tonne fuel consignment, meant for Angola, suddenly showing up in Mombasa. Conveniently rerouted, of course, and likely outside the established G2G framework. Saudi Aramco fuel, sold to another firm, then allegedly passed through a local importer. It's a complex web designed to obscure the trail of dirty money.

This whole mess conveniently happened when global oil prices were already doing their usual chaotic dance. They preyed on our anxieties, knowing we'd accept any "urgent" solution to avoid empty petrol stations. The investigation is ongoing, they say. We’ll see if anything actually comes of it, or if this is just another performance to appease the masses while the real players get away with it.

The arrests and resignations are just the tip of the iceberg. Deputy Director of Petroleum Joseph Wafula is also in the hot seat. While Liban Mohamed got a temporary reprieve due to "medical complications" - a classic Nairobi escape route - the others are facing the music, at least for now. Let's see how long this tune plays.

This isn't the first time we've seen this kind of dirty dealing in the energy sector. It’s a pattern, a predictable cycle of alleged corruption followed by staged accountability. We’re left with higher prices and a depleted sense of trust, while those responsible just move on to their next lucrative scheme.
