Muyoti is finally being heard, or at least he is making enough noise to ruin someone’s lunch. He has been singing this match-fixing song for five years, but in Nairobi, we only listen to the music when the speakers are already blowing up. He says the vice is eroding the game, but let us be real- the game is already a skeleton. We have coaches, players, and officials who have turned the pitch into a marketplace where the highest bidder wins before the first whistle even blows.

It is almost cute that he thinks the government or the federation will save the day. Asking the Football Kenya Federation to investigate corruption is like asking a hyena to guard the butchery. We have seen this before with Patrick Matasi, who is stuck in a bureaucratic purgatory while the DCI pretends to look for evidence. This isn't just about one goalkeeper; it is about a system that is as broken as a Nairobi pavement after the long rains.

The reality is that integrity is a luxury for people who have actually eaten. When clubs refuse to pay players on time, they aren't just being stingy- they are basically handing out the fixers' phone numbers. You cannot expect a young man with an empty stomach to care about the "sanctity of the sport" when a shady caller is offering him a year's salary to miss a tackle. It is the same desperate hustle we see in other sectors where people transition [from flexing on IG to filtering jail bars](/posts/from-flexing-on-ig-to-filtering-jail-bars--the-27--entrepreneurs--interpol-just-dragged) because the legal path was a dead end.

Muyoti joins a choir of coaches like Patrick Odhiambo and Abdallah Juma, all pointing at the fire while the house burns down. But as long as the Sports Act of 2013 remains a toothless piece of paper, nothing will change. Criminalizing match-fixing shouldn't be a debate; it should have been done yesterday. Instead, we have "investigations" that last longer than a career in the Kenyan Premier League, leaving everyone in a state of permanent "coming soon."

If something doesn't change, we won't have a league worth watching. We will just have twenty-two men running around a field for ninety minutes to satisfy a betting syndicate in a different time zone. The game is going down the drain, and the only ones with towels are the guys making a killing off our collective silence. Integrity is dead in Kenyan football, and the burial was likely paid for by a "friend" on WhatsApp.
