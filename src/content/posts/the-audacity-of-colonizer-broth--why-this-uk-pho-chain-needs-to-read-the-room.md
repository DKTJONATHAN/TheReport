Oh, look. Another day, another white-founded business trying to claim ownership over the air we breathe. This time, it is Pho Restaurant in the UK, a chain started by a London couple who "fell in love" with Vietnam and decided the best way to show their appreciation was to legally hijack the name of the country's national dish. TikTok creator iamyenlikethemoney finally decided to drag this 2007-era nonsense into the light, and the internet is serving up a piping hot bowl of cancellation that these founders clearly did not order.

The company recently dropped a statement claiming their trademark has been "misunderstood." Sweetie, there is nothing to misunderstand about a legal claim to a common noun. They are out here splitting hairs over accent marks and "brand identity" while holding a trademark on the word "pho" itself. It is the height of audacity to say the dish belongs to the people of Vietnam while your lawyers are sitting on a registration that says otherwise. It is giving "I am not a colonizer, I am a curator," and nobody is buying the rebranding.

Let us be real-this isn't their first time playing the villain. Back in 2013, they tried to bully a small, family-owned spot called Mo Pho Viet Cafe into changing its name. They only backed off because the public backlash got too loud for their marketing-heavy ears. It reminds me of [The Comedy of Errors: Why the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan) because these "authentic" origin stories are usually just calculated blueprints for corporate expansion. The Walls might have started with a trip to Vietnam, but they ended up with a private equity-backed machine that values IP over cultural respect.

To make matters even more laughable, Stephen Wall was once described as "the creator of Pho" in a 2017 interview. The level of delusion required to not correct that statement is staggering. While the chain claims they are not taking legal action "right now," the trademark does not expire until 2027. They are basically keeping the legal gun in the drawer and asking us to trust them not to use it. In a world where social media buzz can ruin a brand overnight, they better hope their "carefully constructed recipes" are enough to distract from their messy legal history.
