AFRICA'S AI AMBITIONS: MORE HOT AIR THAN ACTUAL HEAT AT SOFT POWER SUMMIT
The Africa Soft Power Summit 2026 promises grand visions of a digital Africa, but the real currency - control of infrastructure, data, and capital - remains stubbornly out of reach for most. While the continent's cultural influence grows, its digital backbone is still a flimsy affair, susceptible to the whims of foreign tech giants.

Speakers at this year's summit were all over themselves about Africa securing a "stronger stake" in AI, creator economies, and digital infrastructure. It sounds good on paper, doesn't it? But let's be real: Alex Okosi from Google is essentially saying Africa is just a consumer, a digital beggar waiting for scraps from the global AI table. Unless our languages and data are baked into these systems from the start, we're just building castles on sand, hoping for "long-term value" that will likely evaporate.

Snehar Shah from iXAfrics Data Centers dropped a bombshell: Africa, with its massive population, controls less than one percent of global digital infrastructure. Less than one percent! We're sitting on enough untapped geothermal energy in Kenya to power entire data centers, yet we're still playing catch-up. It's like having a goldmine but selling the raw ore to foreigners who then process it and sell us back the finished product at a premium. Charlette N'Guessan pointed out the glaring bias in AI systems, a direct result of our underrepresentation. We generate data, but it's not *our* data being used to build *our* future.

Standard Chartered Bank's Birju Sanghrajka chirped about African lenders exploring pension funds for AI financing. Great. More money being funneled into projects that might not even benefit the average Kenyan. He mentioned commercial use cases and the need for institutions to prepare for risks. Frankly, it sounds like more promises and paperwork, while the real power - ownership of platforms and data - stays with the big players. It's a familiar story, much like how many local businesses are still struggling to navigate the digital landscape, as seen with the challenges in onboarding processes.

Despite all the talk of innovation hubs in Kenya, Rwanda, Nigeria, and South Africa, and global giants like Google, Microsoft, and Meta expanding their reach, the fundamental issue remains: ownership. The IFC predicts a massive boost to our GDP, but if we don't own the pipes, the data, and the platforms, that money will continue to flow outwards. It's a classic case of being a participant, not a proprietor. We're good at producing cultural exports, sure, but when it comes to the infrastructure that powers the digital age, we're still relegated to the cheap seats.

The discussions are happening, investments are being made, but the core problem persists: are we truly building our own digital future, or are we just becoming more sophisticated consumers of someone else's? The summit may have ended, but the real work, the hard conversations about ownership and control, has barely begun. Until then, all this talk of "influence" and "growth" is just noise.
