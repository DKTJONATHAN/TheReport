So, Ken Oidamae Tobiko, a local investment analyst who actually bothers to look past the pretty spreadsheets, is pointing out the obvious: if the government itself is out there trying every trick in the book to raise cash, why are we all still tucking our money into the same old mattresses? This 2026/27 budget, looking all familiar with its KSh 4.8 trillion in spending and a KSh 1.1 trillion deficit, isn't just a spending plan. It’s a crystal ball showing us the market we'll be stuck in, where your diversified portfolio might just be a fancy name for being tied to the government's shaky balance sheet.

Let’s be honest, when the government is borrowing close to a trillion shillings locally, it dictates the tune for everyone. Interest rates? Liquidity? The price of everything else? It's all influenced by Uncle Sam’s thirst for cash. So, jumping from government bonds to bank stocks to other listed shares isn't exactly a daring escape. It's more like rearranging the deck chairs on the Titanic, just with different labels. Parliament’s summary confirms it: KSh 3.5 trillion for keeping the lights on and paying salaries, and a measly KSh 749 billion for anything that might actually build something for the future.

Now, don't get me wrong, the state needs to function. People need to get paid. But this relentless focus on recurrent expenditure means any real growth hinges on whether this borrowed money is being used efficiently. Can it generate enough economic bang for the buck to justify the ever-growing financing burden? This is where all those "alternative assets" stop being just buzzwords and start looking like the only sensible lifeboat. The Medium Term Debt Management Strategy is dripping with signals, listing everything from green bonds to Samurai bonds - a clear sign they're scrambling for every shilling they can find, even while the IMF is whispering about a high risk of debt distress.

The Kenyan irony is thicker than a Nairobi traffic jam. Pension funds, bless their cautious hearts, are allowed to dip their toes into private equity and venture capital, but they’re barely investing 0.1 percent. It’s not just regulations; it’s habit, fear, and the comforting familiarity of those safe, government-linked assets. But familiarity doesn't equal diversification. When the deficit is this massive and domestic borrowing is this high, clinging to the "same sovereign story" means you're not diversifying, you're just doubling down on the risk. And if investing in real infrastructure can actually boost growth and exports, as the World Bank and IMF keep telling us, then these "alternative" assets might just be our ticket to a future that isn’t solely dependent on the government’s next borrowing spree. So, instead of just asking how much they're spending, we should be asking what their desperate financing choices are revealing about the concentration risk lurking in all those supposedly "traditional" investment portfolios. Maybe it’s time to think about [How to make Kenya’s next phase of mobile money a great success], but for real productive assets.
