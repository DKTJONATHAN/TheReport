Wakenya, we are back at our favorite pastime: chasing the person who pointed at the fire instead of putting it out. In the ongoing drama between Kenya Breweries Limited and JILK Construction, the script is so predictable it’s boring. KBL claims an arbitrator was essentially on JILK’s payroll, citing "secret collaborations" at Tatu City and suspicious bank transfers of KES 150,000. But instead of asking why an arbitrator is supposedly catching "lunch" money from a party in the case, the public is shouting for the whistleblower’s head. In this city, being a snitch is a bigger sin than being a thief.

Let’s talk about those figures - KES 174,000 and KES 150,000. In the world of high-stakes corporate arbitration, that’s almost insulting. If you're going to allegedly compromise the integrity of the entire legal system, at least ask for enough to retire in Watamu. KBL says they have the receipts, the call logs, and the timelines showing the arbitrator and JILK’s counsel were as close as teeth and tongue. But here we are, treating these "SpeakUp" platforms like they actually offer protection. Honey, in Nairobi, "SpeakUp" is just a suggestion box that feeds directly into a shredder.

And then there’s the DCI. KBL has been writing letters like a jilted lover since 2022, and the response has been... crickets. If you’re waiting for a government agency to give you a "formal update" on a sensitive file involving big money, you might as well wait for the matatus on Ngong Road to start following traffic lights. KBL says the DCI actually found the evidence but then decided to keep it as a souvenir. A whole Commission on Administrative Justice had to get involved, and still, the silence is deafening. In Kenya, a "pending investigation" is just code for "we’re waiting for everyone to forget."

The real joke is the "sanctity" of arbitration. We are told it’s faster and more efficient than our clogged courts. Sure, it’s efficient - efficient at laundering dirty deals into "final" awards that you can’t challenge without climbing a mountain of technicalities. KBL is calling it a "prima facie criminal enterprise," which is just lawyer-speak for "we’ve been played." If the High Court doesn’t step in, arbitration in Kenya will officially become a private club where the person with the best "informal relationship" with the arbitrator wins the trophy.

So, while everyone is busy trying to unmask the whistleblower, the system is laughing. We love to focus on the person who broke the "omerta" because it’s easier than admitting our institutions are held together by glue and "facilitation fees." If you see something, say nothing - unless you want to be the one on trial while the people with the bank trails go back to their bespoke 3D-designed jewellery at Two Rivers Mall. Stay cynical, Nairobi. It’s the only way to survive the theater.
