Nairobi is just one big auction house, and the latest lot on the block is justice itself. Joseph Mutava, a man who already lost his job for "gross misconduct" years ago, is back in the headlines. This time, he and his three associates allegedly wanted Sh10.4 million to make a commercial dispute go away for a former Cabinet Minister. If you are actually surprised by this, you must have just landed at JKIA this morning.

The best part of this comedy is the price of freedom. For a ten-million-shilling "service fee" allegation, these guys walked out on a Sh200,000 police cash bail. In this city, that is not a punishment - it is just a small overhead cost for doing business. While you are sweating over the price of unga and fuel, the big fish are treating the Integrity Centre like a revolving door with a very reasonable cover charge.

Mutava is a seasoned veteran of the "misconduct" game. He was kicked out of the Judiciary in 2016 after a tribunal decided he was not fit for the robes. He fought it all the way to the Supreme Court and lost in 2019, but clearly, old habits die hard. It seems the elite just cannot stop trying to secure their futures at the expense of everyone else, much like how [Kenya-s elite are panicking over their legacy](/posts/shiny-rocks-and-shady-bots--why-kenya-s-elite-are-panicking-over-their-legacy) while the rest of us just try to survive the week.

The EACC is out here giving us the usual PR talk about "eroding public confidence" and "intensifying investigations." What confidence? We stopped believing the system worked the moment we realized the "rule of law" only applies if you cannot afford the "rule of the bribe." They can forward as many files to the Director of Public Prosecutions as they want, but we have seen this script play out too many times to expect a plot twist.

In the end, this is just another Monday in Kanairo. A judge who was already fired gets caught doing exactly what he was fired for, pays a tiny fee to go home, and we all wait for the next scandal to distract us from our empty pockets. The house always wins, and the house is currently located in a gated community, drinking expensive whiskey paid for by "commercial disputes" and broken promises.
