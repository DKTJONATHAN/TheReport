So, the latest buzz is that our Kenyan "creators" raked in a cool Sh296 million last year just from shilling out sponsored posts. Apparently, the whole creator economy is now worth over Sh1 billion. Good for them, I guess. While Eric Omondi is laughing all the way to the bank with Sh57 million - what’s he even doing that’s worth that much? - the rest of us are just scrolling, consuming their content, and probably still broke.

The report gloats about how entertainment, especially comedy, is what we Kenyans apparently "consume." Funny how that translates to big money for a select few. Big brands like Coca-Cola and Safaricom are throwing their cash around, probably hoping a bit of influencer dust will rub off on them. Meanwhile, companies like Fanaka Real Estate and that Block Blast game are desperate for views, showing just how much of this online fame is just smoke and mirrors.

And get this, Instagram is still the golden child for these money-makers, even though TikTok has more eyeballs. Why? Because on Instagram, they can actually turn those eyeballs into cash. On TikTok, it's mostly just noise - billions of views, but barely any of it translates into actual shillings for the creators. It’s an "attention monster" that doesn’t pay the bills, leaving many creators stuck in a discovery gap, watching others get rich while they chase viral fame. It’s like being a ghost worker in Garissa, all potential, no payoff.

The report mentions that this whole thing shifted from a hobby to a "primary source of income" post-Covid. What a revelation. People are making actual money from posting pictures and embedding links. It’s a whole new world, fueled by young people and their phones, which are apparently in 83.5 percent of hands. So, while some are busy building actual businesses or, you know, doing jobs that contribute something tangible, others are cashing in on us scrolling through their feeds.

Of course, while small businesses are doing most of the partnering, the big corporations are still calling the shots, doling out the big bucks. It’s the same old story: the rich get richer, and the platforms that make it all happen get their cut. This isn’t some new economic revolution; it’s just a new way for the same players to grab a slice of the pie, while most of us are just here, watching.

It’s just another reminder that in Kenya, it’s not always about hard work or creating something truly valuable. Sometimes, it’s just about being in the right place at the right time, with the right filters and a good enough smile, to cash in on the attention of others. And hey, if you’re one of the lucky ones, good for you. For the rest of us? Keep scrolling, I guess.
