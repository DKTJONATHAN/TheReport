POST RULING VIOLENT PROTEST POLICING MUST CHANGE - FOR WHOSE BENEFIT?











So, Kisumu High Court finally throws a few coins at the "Sufuria" protest victims. Sh 38.6 million. Sounds like a lot, right? Enough to make those beatings and bullet wounds just a bad memory? Don't make me laugh. This is just the government throwing scraps to shut people up, after all the human rights groups, the opposition jokers, and even our own media have been screaming blue murder about how the police went full rogue during those protests. They deliberately, excessively, and just plain illegally went after people. What else is new?

Imagine your neighbour's gate getting kicked in, your own door being smashed, and then being dragged out by men in uniforms who are supposed to be protecting you. Being beaten down like a dog in your own street. Live bullets flying, not at armed thugs, but at kids, workers, teenagers playing football, anyone caught in the crossfire. People you know collapsing, bleeding out, with nowhere to go. Families scrambling for missing loved ones, only to find them in overcrowded hospitals with broken bones or, worse, in the morgue. Broken jaws, collapsed lungs, intestines torn, toes gone. Just another Tuesday, apparently, if you live in the wrong neighbourhood like Nyalenda, Nyamasaria, or Kondele.

And when you try to report this circus? Police stations send you packing. IPOA claims their systems are down. Years later, the scars, both visible and invisible, remain. How did a normal day turn into a nightmare orchestrated by the very people paid to keep you safe? And more importantly, when will this madness finally stop? The court case, rallied by the Law Society of Kisumu and a whole host of NGOs who probably got a nice grant for it, managed to pin responsibility on the Inspector General, even when he conveniently forgot to produce the "operational documents" that are supposed to govern police behaviour. IPOA, of course, was supposed to step in, but that never happened, did it?

Now, Sh 38.6 million is being handed out. Families get between Sh 910,000 and Sh 1.4 million. Survivors get up to Sh 4.87 million. It's a "critical precedent" they say, for accountability and "equal protection." Equal protection for the rich folks in Milimani and Riat, while the working-class areas get the boot? The court wants regulations for public assemblies and police use of force, and they'll be checking up on it in July. This whole thing is supposed to be a "stiff deterrent" before the 2027 elections. Let's see how long that "deterrent" lasts. Remember how we saw celebrity paywalls fail the youth? This feels like another attempt to put a nice shiny cover on a rotten system.

While this judgment is supposed to be a big deal, and Justice Mabeya deserves a nod for actually finishing a case in two years, let’s not pretend this is some miraculous turning point. The compensation is still less than what you’d get in a normal civil case, and let’s hope the settlements aren't delayed. Investigations into the actual officers and commanders who did the beating and shooting? Good luck with that. This ruling is a band-aid on a gaping wound. Until the rot at the top is dealt with, and accountability actually means something more than a court order and a cheque, the streets of Nairobi will continue to be a potential warzone for the ordinary mwananchi.
