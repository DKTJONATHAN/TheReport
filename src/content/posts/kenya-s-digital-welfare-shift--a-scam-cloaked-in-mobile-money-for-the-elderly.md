Kenya's Digital Welfare Shift: Another Scam for the Elderly?

Mobile money for elder welfare sounds good, but it’s just another way for con artists and greedy relatives to fleece the old folks.

Boniface Okendo, Standard

The Kenyan government’s grand idea to pay out elder welfare through mobile money? Classic Nairobi hustle, just with a digital twist. We’re told it’s to stop caregiver fraud, which, sure, that happens. But what they’re not telling you is that they've just opened a fresh buffet for the vultures right within families, especially with our old people’s notoriously weak grip on anything that requires a PIN or a screen that isn't a basic feature phone. It's like giving a starving hyena the keys to the chicken coop.

It’s a tale as old as time, or at least as old as M-Pesa. Nasike Kisaka from Suqoon Foundation spills the beans: a grandmother asks her to send money, she pockets it, claims it never came, and deletes the message. Simple. Effective. And all thanks to digital illiteracy. These elders are stuck with phones that can barely send a text, while the government’s "Inua Jamii" programme is busy pushing them into a tech world they don’t understand. It’s not about progress; it’s about convenience for the system, and a free-for-all for the opportunists.

The government’s move from physical bank collections to M-Pesa for the Older Persons Cash Transfer (OPCT) programme was supposedly to prevent robberies. And yes, Abworo Titus from Ageing Concern Foundation has seen a caregiver short-change an elder. But let's be honest, the Sh2,000 monthly stipend isn't going to do much for anyone's financial security, let alone make them a target worth robbing at a bank. It’s barely enough to buy a packet of unga, a fact Titus himself points out. So, are we really solving a problem, or just creating new ones for the old guard?

Maurice Omollow from CPF Foundation chirps about reduced income at retirement being the "foremost challenge". Really, Maurice? It’s a challenge for anyone trying to live on a government handout that hasn't kept pace with anything. With low pension penetration, these transfers are supposed to be the safety net. But if they're easily siphoned off by family members or exploited by the digital divide, it's less a safety net and more a sieve. This whole situation feels like a rerun of the same old story, just with fancier gadgets, a bit like how the [Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan).

And the legal side? A whole lot of talk, no action. The Older Persons Bill (2024), meant to protect them from abuse, neglect, and discrimination, is gathering dust after public participation. The Social Protection Act (2025) is all about cash transfer guidelines, but for health, care, or institutional standards? Zilch. Kisaka is right; our children have an act to report abuse, but the elderly? They’re left in the dark, not knowing where to turn. It’s a system designed to fail them, and we're just watching it happen.







Kenya's grand shift to mobile money for elder welfare payments might sound like progress, but behind the digital curtain, it’s just a new battlefield for exploitation. While the government claims it's cutting down on caregiver fraud, it’s actually thrown open a brand-new front for abuse from within families, leaving our older generation even more vulnerable. It’s a classic Nairobi script: fix one problem, create three more.

The core issue? Digital illiteracy. At the 10th Monthly Discussion Forum on Ageing held at Kariokor Social Hall, civil society reps painted a grim picture. Nasike Kisaka of Suqoon Foundation practically laid out the blueprint for family theft: "My grandmother will tell me to send the money to somebody. I send it to myself. I lie the money did not come, and I delete the message." It’s that simple. Many elders are still on basic feature phones, oblivious to voice prompts, making them easy prey as banking and payment systems ditch the familiar for the confusingly new.

The government’s move of the Older Persons Cash Transfer (OPCT) programme to M-Pesa was supposedly a response to physical collection robberies. Abworo Titus from Ageing Concern Foundation shared a tale of a caregiver returning only a fraction of an elder's payout. While Titus admits the digitisation has "to a great extent" reduced financial abuse, the persistent literacy gaps are the real story. It’s not about progress; it’s about making it easier for the system to offload responsibility while leaving the most vulnerable exposed to the wolves.

But let's not forget the actual amount being transferred: a measly Sh2,000 monthly stipend for those 70 and above. Titus highlights the absurdity, noting that the cost of basic necessities like unga is the same for young and old. This "financial insecurity" isn't just a challenge; it's pushing them deeper into poverty. With low pension penetration in Kenya, these government transfers are meant to be a lifeline, not just another avenue for manipulation. This whole setup feels as predictable as the drama in [the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan).

Then there’s the stalled legal framework. The Older Persons Bill (2024), which would actually offer protections against abuse, neglect, and discrimination, is languishing after public participation. The Social Protection Act (2025) might offer cash transfer guidelines, but it’s silent on crucial aspects like health and geriatric care. We lack the structures to support geriatric care, unlike the Children’s Act, leaving older people with no clear avenue to report their issues, as Kisaka points out.

Titus echoes this, detailing horrific cases of assaulted women and evicted widows, all without a dedicated legal reporting mechanism. The Older Persons Bill is vital, Kisaka stresses, because it's not just about social assistance; it's about providing basic dignity and protection. But as it stands, Kenya's elderly are caught in a digital trap, overlooked by inadequate laws and exploited by a system that prioritizes digital convenience over genuine welfare.
