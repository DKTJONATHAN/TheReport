Nairobi is about to get more crowded and significantly less efficient. From April 27 to 30, 2026, The Edge Convention Centre will be crawling with "visionaries" for the Connected Africa Summit. They are calling it a "high-level forum," which is just government-speak for a week of expensive lunches and per diems paid for by taxpayers who are still struggling to buy a simple data bundle. After the 2025 junket in Diani, where the delegates probably spent more time on the beach than in the plenary, they are bringing their "resolutions" to the city to pretend that digital transformation is actually something that happens outside of a five-star hotel.

The theme is "Uniting Africa’s Innovation for a Single Digital Market." It sounds lovely on a PowerPoint slide, but for the average Kenyan, the only thing being united is the frustration of slow internet and high taxes. While they discuss "AI and advanced computing," most of us are just trying to figure out how to pay for electricity that disappears whenever it rains. It is the same old script: gather the ministers, regulators, and "private sector leaders" to talk about a future that seems to exist only in their air-conditioned suites. They mention the African Union’s Digital Transformation Strategy for 2020-2030, but we all know that strategy is a slow-moving beast that enjoys long naps.

We have seen this performance before. They talk about "digital inclusion" and "future skills" while the youth are forced into the gig economy with zero protections and even fewer prospects. It is hard to take their talk of "digital public infrastructure" seriously when you see the [Roadside Tyrants: Why the Kenyan Prado is a Rolling Symptom of Our National Decay](/posts/roadside-tyrants--why-the-kenyan-prado-is-a-rolling-symptom-of-our-national-decay) clearing the way for these VIP delegates. Their convoys will speed past the very people they claim to be "empowering," leaving nothing behind but exhaust fumes and another joint communiqué that no one will ever bother to read.

Eng. John Tanui and the new ICTA boss, Jessy Maruti, are already singing the praises of "Kenya’s leadership." They want us to believe this summit is about "execution" and "collective action." But let’s be real. In this town, "private sector partnership" usually means big tech firms getting massive tax breaks while the local developer in a bedsitter in Roysambu struggles to find a stable connection. They mention "cloud sovereignty" and "cybersecurity," yet our personal data is sold more often than a plate of chips mwitu on a Friday night. It is a disconnect so wide you could fit the entire Rift Valley inside it.

By the time the 1,500 delegates pack their bags and head back to their thirty different countries, the "Diani Resolutions" will be joined by the "Nairobi Declarations." It is a cycle of bureaucracy that feeds on its own buzzwords. We are promised a "Single Digital Market," but we will probably just end up with more committees, more secretariats, and more reasons for the elite to pat themselves on the back while the rest of us wait for the buffer icon to stop spinning. It is not a digital journey; it is a circular road to nowhere.
