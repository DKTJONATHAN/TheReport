Eric Omondi's Boat Rescue Plan: More Like a Puddle Jumper











So, Eric Omondi, the man who apparently peaked in comedy and decided activism was the next frontier, has decided to tackle Nairobi's floods with a boat. Yes, you read that right. A single, white, branded vessel named "Sisi Kwa Sisi" (Us for Us, how touching) is apparently the answer to a city that’s literally washing away. This comes after the Kenya Meteorological Department gave us a heads-up about the rain, as if we needed a weather report to tell us our drainage system is a joke.

The official line is that dozens are dead, over 70 vehicles have been swept away, and infrastructure is in ruins. The Nairobi River, bless its overflowing heart, decided to join the party. And Omondi’s grand solution? A boat, complete with reflector jackets so people don't get their clothes dirty while being "rescued." It’s almost as if he thinks this is a leisurely river cruise, not a desperate scramble for survival. The internet, of course, is having a field day, questioning how one boat is supposed to save everyone when the floods are practically a city-wide phenomenon.

While Omondi is busy playing captain, the city's Chief Officer for Customer Service, Geoffrey Mosiria, is busy telling people to be careful not to get trapped by flash floods. Groundbreaking advice, truly. He also reminds property developers to remove illegal structures blocking waterways, a problem that’s been festering for years. It’s like telling a patient with a gaping wound to “try not to bleed too much.” This all follows a weekend where the Nairobi and Ngong rivers decided to redecorate the city.

It's the same old story in this town: a disaster happens, officials offer platitudes, and self-appointed saviours like Omondi make a grand, ultimately pointless gesture. Remember those shiny portals that promised efficiency but turned out to be a scam? [Shiny Portals, Same Old Thieves: The eGP Scam](/posts/shiny-portals--same-old-thieves--the-egp-scam). This feels cut from the same cloth. We're bombarded with initiatives and warnings, while the actual systemic issues that cause these floods - shoddy infrastructure, rampant illegal development, and a general lack of preparedness - remain stubbornly unaddressed.

The Kenya Red Cross has been out there doing the actual work, rescuing people from marooned vehicles and flooded homes. Meanwhile, Omondi is posing for social media, a testament to the fact that in Nairobi, sometimes the loudest voice isn't the most effective. Perhaps instead of a boat, he could have focused on, I don't know, advocating for better drainage or holding the real culprits accountable. But where’s the viral video in that, eh?

At this point, the most Omondi's boat can realistically do is ferry a few confused pigeons or perhaps serve as a very expensive, very useless bath toy for the city. The real rescue plan for Nairobi isn't a comedian with a boat; it's competent governance, responsible urban planning, and a citizens' willingness to demand more than just symbolic gestures when their homes and lives are on the line.
