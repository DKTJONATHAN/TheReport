Ruto's Eid Plea: More Unity, Less Reality for Kenyans?

President Ruto's call for unity and compassion rings hollow as Kenyans grapple with the everyday hustle. While the Head of State preaches generosity and social cohesion from his presidential perch, the streets of Nairobi are a testament to a different reality, where the cost of living bites hard.

This Eid ul-Fitr, as the Muslim faithful celebrate the end of Ramadan, President Ruto has issued a statement urging Kenyans to remember the less fortunate and share kindness. He spoke of strengthening faith, fostering national harmony, and renewing optimism. It’s the usual song and dance, really. He’s reminding us of virtues like faith and gratitude, as if we’ve forgotten them while scrambling to put food on the table. The idea of "all may partake in its blessings" sounds nice, but let's be honest, some blessings are easier to partake in when your pockets aren't empty.

Ruto emphasizes unity as key to national progress, a sentiment that echoes through his calls for reflection on shared values and support for the vulnerable. He hopes the celebrations will inspire peace and strengthen community bonds. We've heard this before. The Prophet Muhammad himself established Eid as a day of thanksgiving and communal unity, but centuries later, that spirit often feels like a distant echo when you're facing rising prices and unanswered prayers for basic services. The giving of Zakat al-Fitr is meant to ensure everyone can join the festivities, a noble intention that, unfortunately, often falls short of bridging the ever-widening gap between the haves and the have-nots.

Eid ul-Fitr, the "Festival of Breaking the Fast," is indeed a symbol of unity and compassion. But for many here, the real celebration would be a break from the relentless grind, a moment where "generosity and togetherness" aren't just abstract concepts from a presidential speech, but tangible realities. After all, it’s easy to talk about national harmony when you're not the one worrying if [THE BALL STOPS, THE BILLS DON'T](/posts/the-ball-stops--the-bills-don-t).







President Ruto has sent out his Eid ul-Fitr greetings, urging Kenyans to embrace unity and compassion as the Muslim faithful mark the end of Ramadan. The Head of State, in his usual fashion, stressed the importance of generosity and social cohesion during this festive period, aiming to strengthen faith and national harmony. He reminded us that Eid is a celebration of faith, unity, and gratitude, and encouraged sharing kindness with the less fortunate.

It's all very well and good, this talk of shared values and supporting the vulnerable. President Ruto sees this occasion as a chance for Kenyans to come together, reinforcing that unity is the engine of national progress. He expressed his hopes for peace, stronger community bonds, and renewed optimism across the land. These are noble aspirations, no doubt, but one wonders how much of this resonates with the average Nairobi resident trying to make ends meet.

Eid al-Fitr, the "Festival of Breaking the Fast," is a significant occasion in Islam, marking the culmination of Ramadan's spiritual discipline. The tradition, tracing back to the 7th century, emphasizes thanksgiving and communal unity. The practice of Zakat al-Fitr, a charitable donation, is meant to ensure everyone, including the less fortunate, can partake in the joy of the festivities. It’s a beautiful concept, this idea of ensuring no one is left out.

However, in Kenya, as elsewhere, the idealism of Eid often clashes with the stark realities of daily life. While the president speaks of compassion and togetherness, the economic pressures many face can make such communal blessings feel like a distant dream.
