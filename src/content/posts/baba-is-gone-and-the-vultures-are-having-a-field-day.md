Baba is gone, and the gravitational center of Kenyan politics has collapsed, leaving us in a messy, dark room where everyone is tripping over their own ego. Raila was the only thing keeping the opposition from looking like a disorganized choir, and now that he is out of the picture, the vacuum is being filled by the loud, desperate breathing of people who have never led anything on their own. It is a spectacular disaster to watch, especially if you have given up on the idea that any of these people actually care about the country.

Take a look at ODM. It is not even a party anymore; it is a battleground for mid-tier stars like Edwin Sifuna and Gladys Wanga to see who can be the loudest voice in an empty room. While they bicker over who is the most loyal to a legacy they are currently burning down, the State House is reportedly playing them like cheap guitars. Ruto does not even need to work hard to fragment them; they are doing the heavy lifting themselves, proving that without a kingmaker, the princes are just regular guys with big titles and no plan.

The President is laughing, but he should probably check his own back for knives. His deal with ODM is just another version of [the boardroom coup](/posts/the-boardroom-coup--how-you-got-scammed-by-a-political-prenup) where the common mwananchi gets sold out for stability. But behind that fake smile of cooperation, UDA is already rotting from the inside. Succession politics has started three years too early, with everyone from Kithure Kindiki to the resurfacing Fred Matiangi networks trying to position themselves for a seat that is not even vacant yet. It is greed, plain and simple.

Then we have the regional math, the only thing these politicians actually understand. Central is recalibrating, which is a polite way of saying they are waiting to see who has the biggest bag of money. Nyanza is in an identity crisis, and the Rift Valley elites are already wondering if they can do better than the current setup. We are being told this is sophisticated political engineering, but it looks more like a bunch of scavengers fighting over a carcass in the middle of a drought. Even Okiya Omtatah joining the 2027 fray feels like another episode of a show we have already seen too many times.

Ruto’s State of the Nation address was a masterclass in fiction, highlighting achievements that do not exist in the pockets of anyone living in a bedsitter in Pipeline. He can talk about managed stability all he wants, but you cannot eat a 10-point cooperation framework. If the cost of living keeps squeezing the life out of us, no amount of masterful coalition management will save them. People are tired of personality cults, and soon they might just be tired enough to ignore the party lines and vote based on their empty stomachs. But knowing our luck, we will just get a new set of vultures with the same old appetite.
