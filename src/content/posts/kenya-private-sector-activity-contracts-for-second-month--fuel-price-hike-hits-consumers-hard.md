KENYA PRIVATE SECTOR DEALS WITH SECOND MONTH OF CONTRACTION, SURVEY CONFIRMS THE OBVIOUS

Kenya's economy is doing what we all expected: sputtering. For the second month running, businesses are seeing less activity, and while the slide might be a *little* slower this time, don't start popping champagne. It's all thanks to fuel prices, which are making life miserable for everyone, and consumers who have no money left to spend.

Stanbic Bank's survey is basically telling us what every *mwananchi* already knows. Business conditions are getting worse. Fuel costs are soaring, which means everything else is getting more expensive too. And when things get more expensive, people stop buying. It's not rocket science, but I guess some people need a fancy survey to tell them.

The government, in its infinite wisdom, decided mid-April was the perfect time to jack up fuel prices by nearly a quarter. Apparently, global crude prices and some Middle Eastern drama are to blame. This is just another layer on top of the already crushing inflation, which has crept up from 4.4% to a lovely 5.6% in April. Brilliant.

Wholesale, retail, agriculture, and services are feeling the pinch the hardest. Fancy that. When transport costs go through the roof, and you can't even get decent supplies from places like the Middle East and Asia - because, you know, conflicts - it's going to hit output and new orders. Who would have thought? It's almost as if our global interconnectedness, especially with players like those involved in the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran, has consequences, even for folks trying to run a business here. This whole mess reminds me of the whispers around [Transsion's Digital Empire: How Chinese Phones Took Over Africa's Market and Data](/posts/transsion-phones-africa-data-surveillance-threat) - where outside forces and supply chains dictate our local reality.

The statistics office is still peddling the idea that the economy will grow by 4.9% in 2026. Bless their optimistic hearts. Meanwhile, last year's growth was a measly 4.6%, barely nudging up from the year before. It’s all talk and projections while the reality on the ground is a constant struggle.







Kenya's private sector has officially hit a rough patch, contracting for the second month in a row, according to a survey that basically confirms what everyone with a pulse already feels. While the decline might be *slightly* less dramatic than last month, it's still a contraction, and that's not good news for anyone trying to make a living here. The usual suspects are to blame: fuel prices that are making everything cost more, and consumers who are tightening their belts because, frankly, they have no choice.

Stanbic Bank's Purchasing Managers’ Index showing a rise to 49.4 from 47.7 in April is the only "good" news, if you can call it that. Anything below 50 means business activity is shrinking. This is the first time since August 2025 that we've seen this sustained dip, proving that things aren't exactly booming. Stanbic itself admits that "businesses in Kenya suffered a further decline in operating conditions," thanks to rising fuel costs and a drop in customer demand. It's a vicious cycle.

The mid-April fuel price hike, a hefty 24.2% increase at the retail level, is a major kick in the teeth. Blame it on global crude prices and whatever chaos is brewing in the Middle East, disrupting petroleum supplies. This directly feeds into inflation, which has now climbed to 5.6% in April from 4.4% the month before. So, everything you buy is getting more expensive, and your salary definitely isn't keeping pace.

The sectors taking the biggest hits are wholesale and retail, agriculture, and services. It's no surprise. When transport costs skyrocket, it affects the price of goods, the cost of farming, and the ability of service providers to operate. As Christopher Legilisho from Stanbic Bank points out, concerns about rising costs, especially transport, and the difficulty in securing supplies from places like the Middle East and Asia are "weighing on output and new orders." It's a global problem with very local consequences.

Despite all this, Kenya's statistics office is still forecasting a 4.9% economic expansion for 2026. They also warn that Sub-Saharan Africa remains vulnerable to shocks from the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran. Meanwhile, last year's economic growth was a sluggish 4.6%, barely changed from 2024's 4.7%, and below the government's own targets. These growth figures feel like wishful thinking when you look at the reality of contracting private sector activity and the everyday struggle of making ends meet.
