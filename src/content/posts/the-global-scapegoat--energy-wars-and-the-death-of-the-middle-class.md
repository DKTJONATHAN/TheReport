Central banks are masterfully shifting the narrative. By focusing on "inflation expectations" driven by war, they successfully divert attention from years of reckless monetary expansion. The cynical truth is that conflict provides the perfect "act of God" to explain away the systematic debasement of currency. While households in Canada and elsewhere brace for 3.8 per cent inflation, the underlying structural rot remains unaddressed. War is the ultimate scapegoat for a cost-of-living crisis that was already in motion long before the first shot was fired in the Middle East.

Business owners are currently claiming to absorb these rising costs, but this is a temporary posture born of desperation, not altruism. With demand weakening and consumer budgets stretched to the breaking point, firms are trapped. However, the international impact of high fuel and freight costs will eventually force a capitulation. The "partial pass-through" of costs will inevitably become a full-scale assault on the consumer's remaining disposable income. This is a global domino effect: when transportation and agriculture costs spike in one corner of the world, the shockwaves are felt in every supermarket aisle from Toronto to Nairobi.

Furthermore, the integration of artificial intelligence into this bleak economic landscape adds a layer of quiet desperation for the global workforce. Even as people worry about the immediate impact of war, a secondary, more silent conflict is being waged against labor. Workers already expect to work fewer hours and see their prospects dim. The convergence of energy-driven inflation and AI-driven displacement creates a pincer movement that targets the working class from both ends. The elite maintain their margins while the average citizen is told to tighten their belt for the "greater good" of geopolitical stability.

The reality of the current global order is one of managed decline. The transition of power and the frequent eruptions of violence are not anomalies; they are the features of a system that requires constant friction to justify its failures. As seen in other regions, such as the shifts discussed in Kenya in Transition: Economic Hurdles, Political Fractures, and Global Security Shifts (/posts/kenya-in-transition--economic-hurdles--political-fractures--and-global-security-shifts), the narrative is always the same. The public is asked to pay the price for the games played by giants, while the institutions meant to protect them offer nothing but spreadsheets and hollow optimism.

Ultimately, the talk of "one-off events" and "looking through oil price shocks" is a rhetorical shield for policymakers who have run out of options. The global economy is a closed loop of escalating costs and diminishing returns. Whether it is a war in Iran or a trade dispute in the West, the result remains constant: the wealth is consolidated, the risk is socialized, and the global realist knows that the worst is likely yet to come. The only thing more certain than the next rise in inflation is the next excuse for why it is someone else's fault.
