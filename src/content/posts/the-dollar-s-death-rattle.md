The original sin of the Bretton Woods conference was the rejection of John Maynard Keynes's Bancor proposal in favor of Harry Dexter White's dollar-centric trap. By making the US dollar the world's reserve currency, the United States secured a license to print wealth out of thin air. This "exorbitant privilege" allowed Washington to finance global military expansion and endless trade deficits by essentially exporting pieces of paper. For decades, the world played along because the alternative was unthinkable, but the costs have finally come due.

The internal contradictions of this empire have birthed a political reality that is now cannibalizing the very system that made it powerful. Trump's threats to NATO and his indifference to global norms are the symptoms of a nation that can no longer afford its own hegemony. With a national debt exceeding $38 trillion and a Treasury that must move $2.5 trillion in debt every month just to keep the lights on, the guardrails of American stability have vanished. The recent failure of bond auctions suggests that the international market's patience with US profligacy is reaching its limit.

As the US retreats into isolationism and protectionism, the vacuum is being filled by a desperate search for alternatives. The revival of Keynes's "symmetrical adjustment" idea is less about economic fairness and more about survival. Nations like China and the BRICS bloc are not merely exploring new currencies; they are building a financial infrastructure designed to bypass the dollar entirely. This shift represents the most significant reordering of global power since the end of World War II.

The move toward "The New Development Bank" and algorithmic stable coins is a clear signal that the world is preparing for a post-dollar era. While some hope for a more balanced Bancor-style system, the reality will likely be far more chaotic. The transition away from a single reserve currency will strip away the liquidity that has greased the wheels of global trade for eighty years. Without a central authority to enforce the rules, we are entering a period of predatory economic regionalism.

The tragedy of the current moment is that the very tools used to build the modern world-the IMF, the World Bank, and the dollar-are now being weaponized or discarded by the nation that created them. As Trump exploits the economic anger of a hollowed-out middle class, he is simultaneously dismantling the mechanisms that gave the US its strategic edge. The world is not returning to a state of nature; it is returning to a state of fragmentation where "rules" are whatever the local powerhouse says they are.

In this new landscape, stability is a relic. The shift toward gold and alternative settlement systems is a vote of no confidence in the American project. Whether through a new digital Bancor or a fragmented series of currency blocs, the era of the exorbitant privilege is ending. The only question remaining is how much of the global economy will be incinerated in the transition.
