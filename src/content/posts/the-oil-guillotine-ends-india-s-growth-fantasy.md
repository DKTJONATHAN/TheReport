The Reserve Bank of India now finds itself in a classic central bank trap. There are no good moves left on the board. Supporting growth requires keeping rates low, which invites a currency collapse and runaway inflation. Containing inflation requires hiking rates, which will choke the very growth the government uses to justify its nationalist narrative. It is a zero-sum game where the house always loses. This is the same pattern of institutional impotence we see in other emerging markets, much like the bureaucratic theater surrounding [Kenya's SACCO Sector Overhaul](/posts/kenya-s-sacco-sector-overhaul--experts-demand-legal-and-regulatory-makeover--but-who-s-really-listening), where experts demand reform while the underlying structure rots.

Internationally, the ripple effects will be felt as India's demand for raw materials fluctuates and its role as a global back-office faces rising operational costs. The global supply chain is not a robust web but a brittle thread. One well-placed strike in the Strait of Hormuz does more to dictate Indian fiscal policy than any committee meeting in Mumbai. We are entering an era where the managed economy is revealed as a myth, and the only certainty is that the global working class will pay the price for this volatility.

The myth of the emerging market powerhouse is being dismantled by the price of Brent crude. When the cost of basic existence rises, the lofty projections of GDP growth become irrelevant to the person on the street. This is not a temporary dip in the road; it is the end of an artificial boom. The world is returning to its natural state of resource wars and economic friction, leaving central planners to realize that they never really had control in the first place.
