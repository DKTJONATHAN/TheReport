So, Yagnesh Devani, the man who once hobnobbed with Cabinet Secretaries and lived a life of fast cars and expensive suits, thought he could just shrug off the Sh7.6 billion Triton oil scandal and disappear. Sixteen years he was at large in the UK, only to be dragged back here. Now, the High Court has basically told him to take a seat and wait his turn in the criminal trial he's been trying to dodge. Justice Aburili wasn't impressed with his constitutional petition, saying his beef is with the criminal courts, not the constitutional ones.

The whole saga is a classic Nairobi drama. Back in 2008, Triton Petroleum supposedly got their hands on nearly Sh7.6 billion worth of oil from Kenya Pipeline Company, oil that was meant to be collateral for bank loans from outfits like KCB and Glencore. Of course, the banks didn't get the memo, and suddenly, millions were gone. Devani was charged in 2024, after his grand return, but then, surprise, surprise, the DPP wanted to withdraw charges in 2024, citing dead witnesses and shy ones. This whole back-and-forth is enough to make you wonder if anyone in this city actually knows what they're doing, or if it's all just a big show, a bit like watching [Trump's Golden Dome: Another Boondoggle Chasing Reagan's Ghost](/posts/trump-s-golden-dome--another-boondoggle-chasing-reagan-s-ghost).

But here we are, with the High Court saying the DPP's prosecution should continue. Justice Aburili made it clear: courts don't just step in because someone feels their rights are being stepped on. You have to show actual abuse of power or that the charges aren't about enforcing the law. Devani argued that these were just commercial disputes and that KCB had already recovered its debt. He even boasted about Triton's Sh70 billion annual turnover and 3,000 employees. Big numbers, big promises, but ultimately, it seems, big trouble.

It's the same old story, isn't it? Big players make big messes, then try to wriggle out of the consequences. The DPP even opposed Devani's petition at one point, claiming they had enough evidence. Then, for some reason, they wanted to drop everything, only for the High Court to now say, 'Nope, keep going.' It's a tangled web of alleged fraud, theft, and dodgy dealings that could make your head spin faster than a spinner at a political rally.

Devani’s lawyers filed this petition in July 2023, asking to quash four criminal cases that started way back in 2009 and 2011. He faced over 20 counts, including conspiracy to defraud and obtaining by false pretences. Triton, once a giant, collapsed in 2008 and ended up in liquidation. Now, after his 16-year "holiday" in the UK, Devani is back, and the legal wheels are starting to turn, even if they’re squeaking and grinding all the way.

The court's decision means the criminal trial will proceed, and Devani will have to face the music for the alleged irregular loss of Sh7.6 billion from KPC. It's a stark reminder that even with all the political connections and lavish lifestyles, the long arm of the law, however circuitous, can eventually catch up. We'll see if this is the beginning of the end for him, or just another chapter in this never-ending Nairobi saga.
