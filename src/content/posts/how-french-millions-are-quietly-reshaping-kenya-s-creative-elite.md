French Millions, Kenyan Elites: Who's Really Reshaping Who?

The French President’s visit to Kenya, a photo op with the usual suspects - Bien-Aimé, Ombachi, Kipchoge - it’s all the same old song and dance. Governments love these moments, a chance to bask in the reflected glory of our best and brightest. But online, the reality bites. Praise for excellence? More like a feeding frenzy, everyone with an opinion, most of them bitter.

It’s a shame our artists are still duking it out with record labels, getting their catalogues messed with, slapped with copyright strikes just for trying to make a living. While some are busy posing for French cameras, others are battling for their digital lives. And this Sh150m collected from the 'blank tape levy'? Distribution is still a ghost. Sounds about right.

Then you have Layi, educated, served in the Ministry of Justice, only to be told by his boss he doesn't have that "die for the work" grit. It’s the same old story: connections and shouting louder often win over competence. Meanwhile, a 40-year-old Nyashinski is out here trying to "show the kids" how to be legends. As if legend status is something you can just hand out.

And the government? Gazetting higher tariffs for music used by politicians. Finally, someone’s thinking about the artists’ pockets, right? Or maybe it’s just another way to squeeze more money out of the system. It all feels like a broken record, a rerun of Kenya’s identity crisis, where recognition is fleeting and the real struggles get buried under the gloss. Perhaps if our local talent spent less time chasing the French president and more time understanding the mechanics of the industry, like the issues around digital catalogues mentioned in [Tech Companies Design Robots with Cute Features to Foster Human Acceptance](/posts/tech-companies-design-robots-with-cute-features-to-foster-human-acceptance), things might actually change.

French millions are quietly reshaping Kenya’s creative elite. Or are they? It’s more like a convenient backdrop for the same old power plays. Our celebrated figures are paraded, while the real battles for artistic freedom and fair compensation rage on in the background. It’s not reshaping; it’s a performance, and we’re all just audience members watching the same predictable script.
