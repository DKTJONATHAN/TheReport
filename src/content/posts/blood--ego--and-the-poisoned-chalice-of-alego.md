Listen, if you think Kenyan politics is some holy calling or a divine right, you have probably been breathing too much teargas in the CBD. We love to talk about the grand Luo migration from Sudan like it was a majestic parade, but let us be real: it was just people looking for a place to settle where they would not be bothered. By the time we get to the 15th century in the Ramogi hills, the Joka-Kan clan was already busy preserving cow heads and building egos. This is the foundation of what we now call a political dynasty, but back then, it was just about who had the most cows and the loudest voice in the village.

The story of Justo Odima is where the fairytale starts to rot. Justo was the heir apparent, the "intellectual" who went to Maseno School but still had the impulse control of a Nairobi matatu driver on a rainy Monday. Imagine being a Native Tribunal chair and then stabbing a man in the stomach just because he taunted you about your four wives. That is not "destiny" or "the struggle" - that is just a violent ego trip that ended in a murder charge and a life sentence. It is funny how we polish these stories now, but back then, it was just another man letting his temper ruin a perfectly good chieftainship.

When Jaramogi Oginga Odinga showed up in 1943 to scout for a wife at the Migwena Sports tournament, he was not looking for a political alliance; he was looking for a middle-distance athlete named Mary Juma. He had to go all the way to the Industrial Area Prison just to ask a convicted murderer for his daughter’s hand. This is the "cycle of imprisonment" Raila talks about in his memoirs, but let us call it what it is: a family tradition of being on the wrong side of the law long before it became a badge of political honor. The Odinga family legacy is often painted as a heroic struggle, but it is actually a long-running show where the actors change but the script remains as predictable as [The Door-to-Door Deception: Same Clowns, New Tents](/posts/the-door-to-door-deception--same-clowns--new-tents) that we see every election cycle.

The ending of Justo's story is the most Kenyan thing you will ever hear. It was not the government or a rival clan that finally finished him off; it was "cleansing" and paranoia. His wife, Myriam, was so blinded by fear and the words of a medicine man that she fed her own husband a lethal potion disguised as tea. She thought she was neutralizing a curse, but she was actually doing the dirty work for his enemies. It is a classic tale of how superstition and bad advice can destroy a family faster than any court sentence ever could.

So, the next time you see the big cars and the motorcades, remember that it all started with a cow-head-preserving clan, a fatal stabbing over a polygamy joke, and a cup of poisoned tea in a prison cell. We like to wrap our leaders in flags and slogans, but their history is written in the same mess and drama that defines the rest of us. Myriam spent the rest of her life wailing in remorse, which is pretty much the unofficial national anthem for anyone who puts their faith in the "cleansing" power of Kenyan politics.
