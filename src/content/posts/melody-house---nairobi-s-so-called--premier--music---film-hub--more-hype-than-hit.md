So, the Nairobi CBD has a "quiet revolution" happening in the creative economy, apparently. Melody House, or the Music and Film Academy as its mum and dad call it, is supposed to be the "definitive music school in Nairobi." They're TVET-accredited, which sounds official, and promise "global certification" and "technical mastery." Right. Because when I think of true artistic genius, I think of certificates and mastery. It’s a jungle out there, and these aspiring producers are told this Pearson BTEC thing is their golden ticket. Let’s hope it’s not just another overpriced piece of paper.

Then there’s the sound engineering, which they claim is so deep it goes beyond basic audio capture into "high-level acoustics." For those who want to be sonic architects or get into specialised audio engineering. They boast "immediate industry placement." Funny, I’ve heard that line before. This sounds like the same old song and dance, promising the moon and stars while charging you an arm and a leg for lessons on analog signal flow. It’s a lot of jargon to justify the tuition fees, if you ask me.

And the DJs? Oh, the DJs. Apparently, they've "evolved from track-players into performance artists." Melody House is the "premier dj school in Kenya," teaching "harmonic mixing and digital turntablism." They even have "industry-standard Pioneer gear." All well and good, but are they teaching them how to actually read a crowd and not just blindly follow some pre-programmed playlist? Because that’s the real skill, not just pushing buttons on fancy equipment. It’s a lot like how some politicians perform outrage to distract from actual issues, a real performative circus.

Instrumental lessons? They're supposed to balance theory with live performance, a rare feat, apparently. Their guitar lessons are supposedly top-notch, from classical fingerstyle to jazz. Piano lessons aim for "artistic longevity." Voice lessons build "vocal resonance." Drum lessons master "rhythmic independence." It all sounds very impressive on paper. But is it actually making musicians, or just well-drilled technicians? For parents and professionals, they promise "clarity in pedagogy and transparent value." Let’s just hope that transparency extends to their pricing.

And let's not forget the visual side. NITA-certified photography courses are churning out the "next generation of visual storytellers." Then there’s videography, 4K cinematography, video editing, and film production from script to screen. They even offer graphic design courses. It’s a whole media hub, apparently. They’re not just a school; they’re also offering "elite services" - recording studios, photographers, videographers, PA systems for hire. It’s a business, plain and simple, leveraging all these "talented" students to make a quick buck. It makes you wonder if the students are getting the best education or just cheap labour for the academy's commercial ventures.

So, Melody House. A "definitive music school," a "leading hub for sonic architecture," a "premier dj school," a place for "technical precision" on guitars, and a "leading centre for photography courses." It’s a lot of superlatives for one institution tucked away on the second floor of Information House. While they might be ticking all the boxes for accreditation and offering shiny equipment, remember that behind all the glossy promises and fancy terms, it's still Nairobi. And in Nairobi, you have to keep your eyes wide open to avoid getting fleeced by the next big thing that turns out to be just a flash in the pan.
