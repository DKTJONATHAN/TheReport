So, the boys in blue in Njoro decided to play hero, swooping in to save 67 girls from a travel agency that apparently thought they could just stuff them into a dirty, insect-infested hole and call it a job fair. These poor souls were allegedly waiting for "travel documents" to whisk them off to the Gulf for "opportunities." Opportunities, in this country, usually mean someone else getting rich off your desperation.

According to the police commander, who conveniently showed up with cameras rolling, these girls were living in squalor, unable to sleep because of the filth and the bugs. One of them, bless her heart, managed to make a call. This is where you have to wonder, isn't it? How many others are still stuck in similar pits, and how many calls go unanswered? It's a classic Nairobi story, really. We've seen it before, the [Sonko's KES 609,000 Phone Purchase Exposes Kenya's Political Theater of Greed](/posts/sonko-s-kes-609-000-phone-purchase-exposes-kenya-s-political-theater-of-greed) kind of blatant display of misplaced priorities, and this feels cut from the same cloth.

Now, the operators' lawyers are hopping mad, claiming it's all a lie. They're threatening to take it to court, of course. Because when you're caught red-handed with your expired license and a facility that looks like a biohazard, the best defense is always to deny, deny, deny and drag it through the mud. It's the Kenyan way. They even trot out a "human rights lawyer" to say there were no complaints of torture, as if the overcrowding and insects themselves aren't a form of torture.

The government, as usual, is coming out with stern warnings. Apply through registered channels, they say. Check the NEA website. Don't travel on tourist visas. Don't pay with your personal M-Pesa. Yawn. We've heard this song and dance before. The Ministry of Labour even boasts that cases have "reduced significantly." Right. And my rent has reduced significantly too. These are just the few they catch, the lucky ones who get "rescued" before they disappear into the shadows.

They mention 390 cases under investigation. That's a number that makes you sick, and you know for a fact it's just the tip of the iceberg. The real tragedy is how many desperate Kenyans are still falling for these "opportunities," their dreams of a better life crushed by the greed of a few and the indifference of many. This isn't a rescue; it's a temporary pause before the cycle starts again.

So, while the police are patting themselves on the back and the lawyers are gearing up for their courtroom drama, remember this: 67 girls were saved from one particular pit. But how many other pits are still open for business, waiting for the next wave of hopefuls? Don't expect any miracles, just more of the same old grind.
