The academic publishing world, a supposedly hallowed ground for rigorous inquiry, has once again revealed its less savory underbelly. Elsevier, the behemoth of scholarly publications, saw fit to quietly retract 12 economics papers on Christmas Eve and two days later. The common thread among these now-discredited works? Professor Brian M. Lucey of Trinity College Dublin, a frequent co-author and, in some cases, editor of the journals in which these papers appeared.

The stated reason for the retractions, as articulated by Elsevier, is damningly simple: “review of this submission was overseen, and the final decision was made, by the Editor Brian Lucey, despite his role as a co-author of the manuscript. This compromised the editorial process and breached the journal’s policies.” In essence, Lucey was apparently approving his own research, a move that bypasses the very concept of peer review and smacks of a self-serving citation cartel.

This wasn't a sudden revelation. Whispers and accusations have apparently been circulating within academic circles for years, with online forums explicitly calling out Lucey's practices. His publication rate, an astonishing 56 papers in 2025 alone, or one every 6.5 days, should have been a red flag to anyone paying attention. The fact that he managed to publish 44 papers in *Finance Research Letters*, a journal he himself edited, further cements the image of an individual leveraging his position for personal gain.

These 12 retracted papers, which collectively garnered over 5,100 citations, represent a significant chunk of potentially fabricated academic output. The topics covered-ranging from corporate financial distress and climate policy uncertainty in China and the US, to the role of Bitcoin as a safe-haven asset and cryptocurrency market dynamics-were clearly attractive to researchers seeking to build upon existing literature. Lucey's apparent manipulation of the editorial process allowed him to inflate his citation counts and the perceived impact of his work, a common currency in the academic economy.

The academic publishing industry, driven by metrics and prestige, has long been susceptible to such abuses. Journals, eager to publish high-impact work and attract prolific authors, can become complicit in perpetuating flawed research. Elsevier's decision to retract these papers, while belated, is a rare admission of failure in its vetting process. However, the question remains: how many other such "citation cartels" operate unchecked within the vast ecosystem of academic publishing, and how many of their findings have already subtly shaped research and policy? The rot, as it often does, runs deeper than a single professor’s misconduct.
