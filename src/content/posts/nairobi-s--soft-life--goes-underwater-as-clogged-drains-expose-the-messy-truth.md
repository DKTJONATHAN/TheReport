Nairobi is finally getting the "pool party" nobody asked for, and honey, the aesthetic is strictly "disaster chic." While our local influencers are usually busy posting filtered shots of the skyline, the only thing trending right now is hip-high water and overturned SUVs. Eight people are dead, the military is out in the streets, and the city’s drainage system is officially a bigger flop than a mid-tier socialite’s career. It turns out that all those luxury developments don’t mean a thing when the pipes are clogged with the broken promises of city officials. 

Even the "international jet-setters" aren't safe from this aquatic nightmare. Kenya Airways had to divert flights to Mombasa, which I’m sure was a traumatic experience for anyone expecting a grand entrance at JKI Airport. Imagine being told you have to land at the coast because the capital is currently a lake. It is peak irony that the military had to be deployed to do the job the city council should have finished months ago. If only we could tax incompetence, we’d have enough money to build a second Nairobi on stilts. 

The social media streets are predictably messy, with residents like Aisha Bajaber dragging officials for their total lack of foresight. It’s the same script every rainy season: the government acts surprised that it’s raining in Kenya in April, and the citizens pay the price. This sudden reality check is a reminder that [the "soft life" just got harder](/posts/the--soft-life--just-got-harder--interpol-crashes-the-fake-billionaire-party) for anyone who thought they could ignore the crumbling infrastructure beneath their feet. You can’t filter out a flood, no matter how many "blessed" hashtags you use.

In a rare moment of "generosity," the toll road operators actually waived fees for the elevated road. How touching. It’s nice to know that if you’re rich enough to own a car and lucky enough to be near the expressway, you get a free pass while everyone else is literally swimming to work. The Kenya Red Cross is out there struggling to reach people because what used to be roads are now just rivers of regret. Meanwhile, Public Service Minister Geoffrey Ruku is "coordinating efforts," which is political speak for "we’re figuring it out as we go."

As the search and rescue operations continue and the death toll threatens to rise, let this be a lesson to the Nairobi elite. Your "grand" lifestyle is only as good as the pipes under your driveway. Stay safe, stay dry, and maybe stop voting for people who treat drainage like an optional DLC. The military is doing the heavy lifting while the people responsible for this mess are likely tucked away in dry mansions, probably drafting a tweet about "resilience" that nobody wants to read.
