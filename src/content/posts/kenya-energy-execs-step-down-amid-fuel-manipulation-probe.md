Kenya Energy Execs Step Down Amid Fuel Manipulation Probe, Because Of Course They Did

These aren't just resignations, folks. This is the predictable fallout when the vultures circle the emergency fund. Expect more of this as the war in Iran keeps pushing prices higher, conveniently lining pockets while the rest of us scramble for shillings.

So, the big cheeses at Kenya Pipeline and the energy regulator have apparently decided "stepping down" is a polite way of saying "getting hauled away in cuffs." Joe Sang, Daniel Kiptoo, and Mohamed Liban are out, accused of cooking the books on fuel stocks to justify an overpriced emergency shipment. Because why rely on existing contracts when you can create a manufactured crisis and slap a fat markup on it? This whole government to government framework thing, introduced after last year's forex woes, is starting to smell like last year's bad fish. It's almost as predictable as finding out the government is trying to cover up its own mess, like we saw with the [VBS Bank Scandal](https://www.example.com/posts/money--murder--and-makeup--the-vbs-bank-scandal-is-now-showmax-content).

President Ruto's office is out here talking tough about "economic sabotage" and "firm and decisive action." Cute. They even accepted the Principal Secretary's resignation themselves, as if that absolves them of any blame. Meanwhile, the Daily Nation is reporting that hundreds of millions of shillings have been seized. That's a lot of "overpriced, substandard quality" fuel bought with public anxiety. The war in Iran and the Strait of Hormuz closure are just convenient excuses, a smokescreen for the usual suspects to exploit rising global prices and our collective panic.

Apparently, Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, and ENOC were still delivering. Go figure. But that didn't stop some bright sparks here from creating a "false impression of impending supply shortfall." It's a classic Nairobi move: create the problem, sell the solution. These aren't heroes fighting a crisis; they’re opportunists cashing in on it.

The arrests of Joseph Wafula and Joel Mburu, the deputy director of petroleum and a supply chain manager respectively, just solidify the narrative. This isn't just a few bad apples; it’s a whole orchard of rot. While Kenya might be mostly powered by renewables, our vehicles still need that good old petroleum, and where there's demand, there's always someone looking to exploit it.

The government's commitment to "protecting the public interest" is as believable as a politician’s promise. They'll investigate, they'll make arrests, and a few people will get a slap on the wrist while the real beneficiaries of this "government to government" charade remain untouched. Just another Tuesday in the city.
