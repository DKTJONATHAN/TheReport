The Bafta Television Awards 2026 unfolded with the predictable fanfare, and the real winner, as usual, was the self perpetuating industry machine. Netflix's "Adolescence" managed to break records, snagging four awards, including best limited series and acting gongs for Stephen Graham, Owen Cooper, and Christine Tremarco. This isn't a triumph of art; it's a testament to the established formula. A "hard hitting drama" becomes a "national talking point" - code for "it generated enough clicks and think pieces."

The truly astonishing part is young Owen Cooper, at 16, becoming the youngest ever best supporting actor winner. His acceptance speech, a Beatles tribute about vision, obsession, and dreams, is precisely the kind of sentimental drivel that gets rewarded. He already boasts an Emmy, Golden Globe, National Television, Royal Television Society, and Actor Awards. This isn't about raw talent emerging; it's about a carefully cultivated career path for those who know how to play the game. Stephen Graham, finally getting his Bafta after seven nominations, spouts platitudes about "telling the human condition" and "beautiful stories." It’s a nice sentiment, but let's be honest, it's also a job.

Narges Rashidi’s win for playing Nazanin Zaghari-Ratcliffe in "Prisoner 951" offered a rare moment of genuine weight, dedicating the award to the woman herself. It’s a shame such compelling real life stories often have to be filtered through the lens of awards season to gain proper traction.

Meanwhile, "The Celebrity Traitors" scooped best reality programme. Of course it did. It was the "most watched programme of last year." Numbers don't equate to quality, they equate to marketability. Alan Carr’s win for most memorable TV moment, decided by the public, is a clear indication of what truly resonates: not groundbreaking art, but easily digestible, often superficial, entertainment.

"Last One Laughing" winning best entertainment programme further solidifies this. It’s a competition designed to be funny, not a deep dive into the human psyche. Bob Mortimer’s win for best entertainment performance is just another cog in this wheel.

Steve Coogan, winning for "How Are You? It's Alan (Partridge)," declared he’ll play the character "until I die." This is less about artistic commitment and more about clinging to a successful persona in an industry that rewards repetition.

The current affairs prize going to "Gaza: Doctors Under Attack," a documentary the BBC controversially pulled, highlights the industry's often contradictory nature. While it's good to see it recognized, the initial decision to shelve it speaks volumes about the pressures and politics that govern what we see and don't see. Ben de Pear's pointed question to the BBC about being dropped from the Bafta screening is a well deserved jab, but ultimately, it's just noise in the grand scheme of things.

Dame Mary Berry receiving a lifetime achievement award at 91 is a charming send off for a beloved figure, but it also serves to fill out the program with a sense of gravitas that the rest of the evening might lack. Martin Lewis's emotional win for a special award, dedicated to consumer journalism, is a more grounded and relatable success story.

In the end, the Bafta TV Awards are less about celebrating genuine artistic achievement and more about reinforcing the existing hierarchy, rewarding predictable narratives, and congratulating themselves on a job well done - a job that largely involves producing content that keeps people passively entertained. "Adolescence" breaking records is just another data point in the ongoing cycle of industry validation.
