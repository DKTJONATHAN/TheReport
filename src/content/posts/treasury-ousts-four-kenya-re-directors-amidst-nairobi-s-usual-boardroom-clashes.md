Treasury Kicks Out Kenya Re Directors Amidst The Usual Nairobi Drama

WARNING: Another day, another boardroom scandal. The Treasury, in its infinite wisdom, has decided to shake up the Kenya Re board, chucking out the chairman and three others. Apparently, things have been more dramatic than a Luo wedding since last year, and someone finally decided to pull the plug. Don't expect this to fix anything, though. This is Nairobi, after all.

This whole mess sounds like the usual song and dance: power plays, suspensions, court cases, and then a bit of backroom dealing. The Treasury, with its fat 60% stake, thinks it can just wave a magic wand and make all the problems disappear. They booted Chairman Erick Gumbo and a few others, but then turned around and supported Gumbo and another chap for re-election at the AGM, only to drop them a month later. Classic incompetence, or just a good old-fashioned case of "who blinked first"?

The whole thing with the CEO, Hillary Wachinga, and the HR manager, Sally Waigumo, being suspended and then reinstated before a court case even got going? Pure theatre. It's the kind of stuff that makes you wonder if these institutions are run by grown adults or a primary school playground. And now, with the minority shareholders fighting for their rightful board seats, the Treasury's "intervention" feels less like problem-solving and more like slamming the door shut. It's like trying to fix a leaky roof by throwing more mud at it.

The Treasury's proposed new board members are in, but the real drama is still unfolding. The minority shareholders are having a field day in court, arguing about share classes and board representation. The new rules, which cut board seats and try to impose some sort of suitability criteria, seem more like a way to keep certain people out than to genuinely improve governance. It’s all about who knows whom and whose pocket is deepest, just like that time we talked about how justice seems to have a ramp for some and not for others. And let's not forget the financial side of things. Kenya Re is still making money, even if profits took a dip. But the fact that they had to cancel an international event at the last minute, despite Treasury approval, just screams disarray. You can't build a business on shaky foundations, and this constant boardroom bickering is exactly that. They're trying to expand into Tanzania and India, but how can they do that effectively when they can't even sort out their own board?

So, here we are. Four directors out, a new list of names in, and the court case still looming. The Treasury might think they've "quelled tensions," but this is Nairobi. Tensions here are like cockroaches; they just find a new dark corner to breed in. We'll see who ends up chairing the board, but don't hold your breath for any real change. This is just business as usual.







The Treasury has thrown four of its representatives off the Kenya Reinsurance Corporation (Kenya Re) board, chairman Erick Gumbo included, in a desperate attempt to calm the choppy waters that have been rocking the firm since last year. A letter from the Treasury, dated June 15, confirmed the departure of Gumbo, Abdirahin Abdi, Eunice Nyala, and Zacharia Nyaaga. This move comes smack in the middle of a board spat that saw the suspension of Kenya Re CEO Hillary Wachinga and HR manager Sally Waigumo for two months in late 2025, only for them to be reinstated before a court case filed by Wachinga could even get properly underway.

The Treasury’s backing for Nyala and Nyaaga also evaporated during Kenya Re’s AGM on June 19, despite having initially supported Gumbo and Abdi for re-election. Gumbo, in fact, topped the votes for chairman. However, a month later, Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi decided to axe Gumbo and Abdi, presenting a new list of six nominees, including his own alternate. The official line is that this is an intervention to ease the fallout between management and the board, but in Nairobi, you know there’s always more to the story.

The Treasury holds a significant 60 percent stake in Kenya Re, giving them considerable clout over board appointments. Meanwhile, minority shareholders are in court, trying to force the Treasury to hand over more board seats as per the company’s revised rules, which grant them three positions. This legal fight is all about the firm's recent rule changes that have reclassified shares and reduced board membership from 11 to nine, with the government entitled to five seats and minorities to three.

“A decision had to be made,” a source familiar with the matter revealed, adding that the Treasury has made its preferred list of directors known. The appointment of a new chairman is still on hold, however, due to the ongoing court case with the minority shareholders. The Treasury has kept six directors on the board: Jackline Nyandeje, Leah Rotich, David Muthusi, Irungu Kirika, Erick Korir, and Omar Shallo. The tension has even spilled into the courts, with Wachinga suing the board over his suspension before withdrawing the case.

The fallout at Kenya Re, where Gumbo had been chairman since June last year, is seen as stemming from his alleged failure to foster harmony between the board and management. This breakdown recently led to the eleventh-hour cancellation of a Kenya Re international event meant to drum up business, despite prior Treasury approval. The Treasury’s proposed board changes are happening against a backdrop of these court wrangles, with minority shareholders feeling they were not fairly represented at the June 19 AGM.

Kenya Re is still to name a new chairman and form its various committees, including audit, HR, nominations, finance, strategy, risk, and compliance. The new Articles of Association stipulate a maximum of two three-year terms for directors and a loss of seat for missing three consecutive meetings without approval. They've also introduced stricter suitability criteria for independent directors, including a ban on affiliation with political parties for five years prior to appointment. It’s a lot of shuffling, but will it actually lead to better governance, or just a different set of faces playing the same old games?

It's worth noting that despite a 11.6 percent dip in net profit to Sh3.92 billion in the financial year ended December 2025, Kenya Re maintained an Sh839.94 million dividend. The company blamed the profit drop on underperformance in its international treaty business and its operations in Zambia and Côte d'Ivoire. This isn't just about who sits on the board; it's about whether the company can actually deliver on its strategic goals, like setting up a subsidiary in Tanzania and a representative office in India, when its internal house is clearly in disarray.
