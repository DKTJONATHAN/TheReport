This initiative is tucked under their Corporate Social Investment (CSI) umbrella, because nothing says "we are doing you a favor" like treating basic industry education as a charity project. They want to teach emerging publishers how to manage intellectual property and improve royalty streams. It is hilarious that the very organization responsible for those streams is now playing teacher. It is like a bank teaching you how to save money while they charge you a fee just for breathing their air.

Of course, the admission is "competitive and merit-based," because we all know how much these institutions love a good gatekeeping session. While they talk about "long-term commercial sustainability," the reality on the ground is much grittier. Honestly, it feels like the same old story where we are promised a bright future while the present is falling apart. We are told we are moving forward, but [30 years to the first world? we can’t even cross the road without a bribe](/posts/30-years-to-the-first-world--we-can-t-even-cross-the-road-without-a-bribe) in this climate, let alone navigate a royalty system that requires a PhD to understand.

If you are a SAMRO member looking to "build a resilient music enterprise," you better get your application in early and hope your paperwork is shinier than your talent. Just do not expect the 2026 program to fix the fact that most artists are still living hand-to-mouth while the executives collect their "administration fees." It is just another layer of bureaucracy designed to make the suits feel like they are professionalizing a sector that is fueled by raw talent and even rawer exploitation.

The program focuses on "enabling participants to better manage and exploit intellectual property," which is fancy talk for "please learn how to do our job for us so we stop getting angry emails." If they really wanted to help, they would simplify the payout process instead of making everyone jump through hoops for a chance to learn how the game is rigged. Stay cynical, kids; it is the only way to survive the music business without losing your soul.
