Oh look, Naseeb Abdul Juma Issack is back at it again, pretending he has the secret to a lasting relationship. The self-proclaimed "Lion" has dropped another track titled "Msumari," where he supposedly blends Bongo Flava with Amapiano to tell us how to secure our partners. It is honestly hilarious watching a man whose family tree is as tangled as a bag of cheap earphones try to lecture the public on "protecting their connection." If Diamond actually nailed down any of his own relationships, he wouldn't have enough baby mamas to start a football team across three different countries.

The song itself is the usual Wasafi formula, designed to dominate the airwaves through sheer repetition. It is a blatant grab for that Amapiano clout because, let us be real, Bongo Flava is struggling to keep its head above water without a South African beat to lean on lately. He calls it "versatility," but I call it a lack of originality from an artist who is terrified of becoming a legacy act. He wants lovers to "nail it down with a hammer," yet the only thing he seems to nail down is another collaboration or a fresh PR scandal to distract us from his fading "raw and soulful" vocals.

Speaking of PR stunts, we cannot ignore the constant "Inama" situation with Zuchu. The way these two dance around their "is it business or is it pleasure" routine is more exhausting than watching [the courtroom drama no one asked for: david mokaya walks while the police fumble the bag](/posts/the-courtroom-drama-no-one-asked-for--david-mokaya-walks-while-the-police-fumble-the-bag) during a slow news week. It is a tired tactic to keep the Wasafi brand in the headlines, and frankly, Zuchu deserves better than being used as a prop in Diamond's never-ending quest for relevance. Being tethered to his chaotic orbit feels less like a career boost and more like a slow descent into the gossip abyss.

At the end of the day, Diamond is a music mogul and a businessman, not a relationship therapist. He is the CEO of WCB Wasafi, which basically means he is the boss of a very expensive drama factory. Whether he is "uniting Africa" with Bruce Melodie or jumping on a RedOne track to feel "different," the goal is always the same: money and ego. "Msumari" will definitely top the charts, but not because it is a masterpiece of romantic sincerity. It will top the charts because we are all addicted to the mess he creates while he sings about a loyalty he has never actually practiced.
