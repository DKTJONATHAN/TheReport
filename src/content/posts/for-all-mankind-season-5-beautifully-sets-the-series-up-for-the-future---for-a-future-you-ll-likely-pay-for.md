"For All Mankind" is back for its fifth season, and the usual effusive praise is being trotted out, claiming it's "better than almost everything else." This alternate reality drama, lauded for its blockbuster feel and emotional depth, is predictably continuing its march into the future, this time with the narrative firmly entrenched on Mars and looking beyond. However, as the show expands and time marches on within its fictional universe, the very elements that made its earlier seasons so compelling have begun to erode, albeit subtly.

Season four concluded with Mars residents determined to remain on the Red Planet, defying Earth's directives. Season five picks up years later, in the 2010s, with life seemingly thriving on Mars. The original cast members are aging, with Ed Baldwin now in his eighties, and his grandson Alex poised to graduate from Mars High. The torch is indeed being passed, with Ed's role becoming more of a legacy figure, ceding the spotlight to Alex, his daughter Kelly, and fellow Mars leader Miles. This generational handover, while a natural progression, brings with it the inherent challenge of establishing new emotional connections with an audience already deeply invested in the original characters.

The central conflict of season five revolves around Mars' bid for independence, a struggle that escalates on multiple fronts, becoming increasingly hostile. Billionaire CEO Dev Ayesa plays a significant role in this push, while back on Earth, the ever complex Margo Madison, alongside her protégé Aleida Rosales, continues to exert influence. New faces, such as Mireille Enos's enigmatic security officer, are introduced, expanding the cast.

While the struggles on Mars and their repercussions on Earth are presented as fascinating and often disturbingly realistic, with subplots touching on AI's impact on jobs and the brutal enforcement of order, the show's canvas has shrunk. The true magic of "For All Mankind" lay in witnessing the grand, universe altering ripple effects of seemingly small historical deviations play out on a vast scale. The initial Russian moon landing was the catalyst for this expansive exploration of how a single event reshapes human history. Now, the primary "big change" is simply that humanity is on Mars. Beyond that, life on the Red Planet mirrors terrestrial existence-people work, socialize, and form relationships-albeit confined within cramped steel corridors. This shift has inevitably diluted the "wow" factor, with much of the truly awe inspiring moments now relegated to off screen.

The introduction of new characters, while crucial for the show's longevity, means audiences are still in the process of developing the same level of attachment they had for the original crew. Season five is undeniably transitional. The aging of iconic characters like Ed Baldwin and Margo Madison necessitates this shift, but it requires patience from viewers as the narrative invests in this second, and increasingly third, generation.

Despite these transitional growing pains, the review suggests that by the fourth or fifth episode, the perceived weaknesses begin to transform into strengths. The isolation and pressures faced by the Martian colonists become relatable, fostering empathy for their traumas. Crucially, Mars is not the ultimate destination. The season eventually pushes further into space, reigniting the nail biting, edge of your seat tension that "For All Mankind" is known for. In essence, while season five of "For All Mankind" may be a slight departure from its predecessors, requiring a more extended period to fully engage, it ultimately recaptures its signature brilliance. This temporary dip is framed as a necessary sacrifice, paving the way for new characters and storylines with the potential for significant future development, a prospect the reviewers sincerely hope for.

Season five of "For All Mankind" premiered on March 27th on Apple TV+, with new episodes released weekly until the finale on May 29th.
