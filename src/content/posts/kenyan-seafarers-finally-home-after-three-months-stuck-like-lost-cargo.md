So, seven of our seafarers have finally limped back to Kenya after nearly three months of being stuck in international waters. Apparently, they were in some kind of detention, facing the prospect of long jail sentences or hefty fines. It's a relief, they say, a moment of "immense relief" for Cabinet Secretary Hassan Joho, who was there to greet them with all the pomp and circumstance. Because nothing says "we care" like a photo op when the crisis is over.

Joho, bless his heart, went on to blather about how this harrowing ordeal highlighted the "vulnerabilities" of our maritime workers. You know, the ones toiling far from home, caught in the web of international laws that conveniently protect everyone but the poor sods doing the dirty work. And, surprise, surprise, the government is now "accelerating" the implementation of Seafarers' Identity Documents and pushing for "mutual recognition agreements." Sounds like a lot of fancy words for "we should have done this ages ago."

They're patting themselves on the back for this "difficult chapter" conclusion, all while promising "decisive action" so "no other crew member faces a similar situation." It's almost as if they need a reminder that our professionals are increasingly out there in the global workforce, a sector they now deem a "key pillar." Just like how football players are a "key pillar" until their careers hit a brick wall of incompetence.

This whole kerfuffle has, conveniently, drawn attention to the "growing presence of Kenyan professionals." Wonderful. They're out there, earning a living, while back home, we've got guys getting arrested for online threats about the 2027 elections, presidents launching Saccos like it's the second coming, and parties electing leaders nobody's ever heard of. And let's not forget the budget dialogues getting disrupted by goons - a real sign of our nation's progress. It’s a wonder any of our people make it back at all.
