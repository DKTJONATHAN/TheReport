The Kenya Transporters Association is dreaming again. They think that by moving traffic fines from the dusty courtrooms to M-Pesa, they will suddenly cure the cancer of corruption that keeps our roads moving. It is a sweet, naive thought. They want us to believe that a digital record will scare a policeman who has already mastered the art of eating even in the middle of a noon-day sun.

The proposal claims it will ease court congestion and restore public confidence. Please. If you have lived in Nairobi long enough, you know the system is not broken; it is working exactly as intended for those who run it. Moving the bribe to a mobile platform just saves the officer a walk to the nearest ATM. We are already dealing with a city where [Log-Drums and Lattes: Kenya’s Identity Crisis and the New Dating App for the 'Vetted' Narcissist](/posts/log-drums-and-lattes--kenya-s-identity-crisis-and-the-new-dating-app-for-the--vetted--narcissist) is the peak of our social evolution, so why not add digital extortion to the menu?

The KTA says this has been under consideration for eight years. In Kenya, eight years is exactly how long it takes for a good idea to be properly seasoned with enough loopholes for the well-connected to benefit. They talk about revenue leakage as if the state actually cares about the coins falling out of the bucket. They just want a bigger, digital bucket that is easier to monitor from the comfort of an air-conditioned office in Upper Hill.

Let us talk about behavioural correction. Do they really think a five-thousand shilling fine paid via a phone will make a matatu driver stop overlapping? The only thing it corrects is the speed at which you lose your money. Predictable and fair enforcement is a myth in a country where the law is a suggestion for the V8 drivers and a death sentence for the Probox owners.

They say we are a leader in mobile money innovation, and they are right. We have successfully innovated new ways to be broke every single day. This digital fine system is just another Paybill for the state to squeeze the last drop out of a motorist who is already dodging potholes that could swallow a small car. It is not about safety; it is about the ease of doing business for the taxman while the rest of us suffer in traffic.
