Saccos were supposed to be the "financial inclusion" savior for the common man, but they have turned into glorified pyramid schemes for the elite. Now, Nairobi and Kampala are finally scrambling to "review rules" after billions have already evaporated into thin air. It is the classic African leadership style: wait for the entire house to burn down before you start looking for a fire extinguisher or even wondering where the water is.

Look at the mess at Kuscco. They managed to "misplace" Ksh 13.3 billion while playing around with illegal deposits and housing ventures they had no business touching. Poor guys like Kelvin Kariuki have been chasing their savings for four years with nothing to show for it but frustration. If you think your money is safe just because an institution has a fancy name, you have clearly not been paying attention. It is almost as pathetic as the [Kipchoge Keino Stadium](/posts/kipchoge-keino-stadium--a-decade-of-dust-and-33-percent-progress) that has seen a decade of dust and barely any progress while officials keep making promises.

It is not just the big fish like Metropolitan and Afya Sacco making people cry in the streets. Even the "exclusive" ones like Mhasibu and Kimisitu are reporting losses linked to this so-called sector turmoil. Over in Uganda, teachers are learning the hard way that their "Liberal Teachers Union" was a bit too liberal with their cash, losing a cool million dollars. When regulators say they are "tightening oversight," what they really mean is they finally realized they were asleep at the wheel while the bus was flying off a cliff.

The new plan in Kenya is to raise the minimum membership from 10 to 100 and force smaller Saccos to merge. Because, obviously, making the group bigger makes it harder to steal, right? Wrong. It just means the next time a Sacco collapses, the "mismanagement" will be in the trillions instead of billions. Sasra is trying to look busy by taking over more Saccos, but for the millions of Kenyans who treat these institutions like their only lifeline, these reforms feel like putting a tiny Band-Aid on a massive bullet wound.

If you are still putting every cent into these institutions expecting a fat dividend and a "soft loan," you might want to check your pulse. The sector is a "crucial driver of economic growth" for everyone except the person actually doing the saving. In this Nairobi, you either eat or get eaten, and right now, the Sacco bosses are definitely the ones at the high table while you are outside writing angry emails and waiting for a miracle that is never coming.
