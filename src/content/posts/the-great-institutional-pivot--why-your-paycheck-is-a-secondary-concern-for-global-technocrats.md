The current debate over "dual mandates" - the idea that central banks should care about jobs as much as they care about the value of the currency - is being dismissed as a distinction without a difference. This is a classic gaslighting tactic of the global realist school. They claim that the "divine coincidence" ensures that fighting inflation automatically helps the worker. In reality, this is a theoretical myth used to justify keeping interest rates high enough to crush labor bargaining power while protecting the bond holdings of the wealthy.

The post-pandemic inflation surge was not just a "supply-side shock" or a "movement along a curve." It was a massive institutional failure. Now, facing deep political polarization and a shrinking fiscal space, these bankers are looking for a "deus ex machina" to save them from the consequences of their own models. Enter Artificial Intelligence. The banking elite are now pinning their hopes on AI to boost productivity and magically solve the problems of an aging, shrinking workforce and reduced immigration. It is a desperate "Hail Mary" pass from a class of people who have run out of ideas.

This institutional arrogance mirrors the failures seen in other bureaucratic behemoths. Much like we saw with the AU’s New Foreign Policy: Five Suits and a PDF Won’t Save Us, the reliance on outdated frameworks and glossy white papers is a poor substitute for the harsh reality of a world that is no longer behaving according to 1990s consensus models. The "modern consensus" they cling to is a relic. The Phillips curve is not a law of nature; it is a description of a world that no longer exists, yet it is being used to dictate the lives of millions.

The international impact of this stubbornness is grim. As central banks in the West prioritize their "credibility" over growth, the developing world faces a brutal squeeze. High interest rates in the Eurozone and the US act as a vacuum, sucking capital out of emerging markets and leaving them to deal with the wreckage of debt and stagnation. The technocrats call this "macro-prudential stability." The rest of the world calls it a slow-motion economic execution.

Ultimately, the message is clear: do not expect the people who broke the system to be the ones who fix it. They are more interested in preserving the "institutional framework" than in ensuring that the fabric of society remains intact. They will continue to hide behind the "art" of monetary policy until the reality of a fractured, angry, and impoverished global population becomes impossible to ignore. By then, no amount of AI-driven productivity will be able to bridge the gap between the boardroom and the street.
