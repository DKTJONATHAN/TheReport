Nairobi, Kenya - The Head of Public Service, bless his optimistic heart, has called another high-level shindig. This time it’s about sorting out the mess that is Kenya’s security sector. Apparently, they’ve all gathered virtually - because real meetings cost money, and who needs that? - to talk about restoring efficiency, accountability, and public trust. You know, the same things they’ve been talking about for years while the rot festers.

The guest list reads like a who's who of the very institutions implicated in the problems: Internal Security, the National Police Service (NPS), IPOA (which often feels like a paper tiger), NCIC, DCI, and a whole alphabet soup of other acronyms. The Head of Public Service even admitted, with refreshing candour, that corruption, weak investigations, and a broken criminal justice chain are the culprits. It’s almost like he’s been reading the news. He's worried about offenders walking free, which, let's be honest, is less a worry and more a daily occurrence.

The grand resolution? "Urgent reforms" to "strengthen the entire security ecosystem." This translates to more talk about discipline, zero tolerance for corruption, and better case handling. They want to close gaps between arrest and conviction, as if that's some revolutionary idea. The push for a "unified, system-wide approach" sounds good on paper, but we've seen how well "one system" works when everyone’s looking out for their own slice of the pie. Remember when we discussed how the [Clout, Caps, and Chaos: The Drill Beef Nobody Asked For](/posts/clout--caps--and-chaos--the-drill-beef-nobody-asked-for) seemed to overshadow any real sense of order? This feels like more of the same.

They're linking security to economic growth, which is all well and good, but without safety, there's no investment. That's not exactly a revelation, is it? It’s the same song and dance about how security is the "foundation" for development. The kicker is the directive for a "sector-wide reset" based on discipline and accountability. Public authority is a "trust," they say. Kenyans expect results. Well, Mr. Head of Public Service, so do we, and so far, those results are harder to find than an honest politician.

The government wants to rebuild public confidence. That’s cute. They’ll have to do a lot more than a virtual meeting and a few platitudes to earn back any trust. Every officer represents the government, he stated. Exactly. And right now, too many of those representatives are part of the problem, not the solution. This "renewed push" is just another ripple in the stagnant pond of Kenyan security.

We’ll see if any of this translates into actual change on the streets, or if it's just another elaborate performance to make it look like they're doing something. Given the history, my money's on the performance. Kenyans are tired of being promised the moon when they can't even get a decent response to a stolen phone.
