Welcome to the Nairobi where everything is for sale, including our national identity. While the rest of us sweat in queues at Nyayo House for months, it turns out that if you are the brother of a Sudanese paramilitary commander, the red carpet is rolled out with a side of total silence. The US sanctions notice listing Algoney Hamdan Dagalo Musa as a Kenyan passport holder did not just raise eyebrows; it confirmed what we already knew. Our immigration system is less of a gatekeeper and more of a concierge service for the highest bidder.

Do not expect the DRC or any other African state to keep playing nice with Ruto after this mess. This is not just a "clerical error." It is a calculated move that paints Kenya as a playground for those who tear their own countries apart. We have spent decades playing this "ambiguous" role in Sudan's conflicts, but now the mask is slipping. It turns out [the ghost in the ledger: how private equity is cannibalizing the African commons](/posts/the-ghost-in-the-ledger-how-private-equity-is-cannibalizing-the-african-commons) has a cousin in the immigration office, where national security is traded for short-term political favors.

Khartoum is already breathing down our necks, accusing President Ruto of aiding and abetting the Rapid Support Forces. It is hard to argue with them when reports suggest the RSF is preparing to launch a parallel government right here in Nairobi this Friday. We are no longer the "region's big brother." We are the landlord for a militia's home office. The silence from the State House is so loud it is actually deafening, leaving the rest of us to wonder who else is walking around with a Kenyan document in their pocket while they plan their next coup.

The integrity of our security vetting system is currently sitting in the gutter. Lawyers are shouting about the legal implications, but who is actually listening? In a city where you can buy a degree, a job, and apparently a whole new nationality, a US sanction notice is just another piece of paper to be ignored by the elites. We are watching the slow-motion car crash of our diplomatic reputation, and the people at the wheel are too busy counting the perks of their "subtle" diplomacy to hit the brakes.

If you thought Nairobi was a hub of innovation, you were right. We have innovated new ways to compromise ourselves for the sake of regional "influence" that only benefits a few at the top. By the time the government finally decides to issue a public explanation, the damage will be done and the checks will be cashed. This is the new Nairobi reality: a city that hosts the victims of war in the morning and their oppressors for dinner, all while pretending our hands are clean.
