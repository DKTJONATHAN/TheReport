The cost of this maximum-pressure campaign is being exported directly to New Delhi and Beijing. By choking the Strait of Hormuz, Washington is effectively taxing every Indian consumer and daring the Chinese leadership to blink. The upcoming summit between Trump and Xi is supposed to be a moment of detente, but you cannot cultivate peace while simultaneously setting your partner-s energy supply on fire. India-s energy shock is not a side effect; it is the collateral damage of a superpower that has forgotten how to use anything but a hammer.

Perhaps the most chilling signal is the behavior of central banks. For years, these institutions hoarded gold as the ultimate hedge against chaos. Now, they are liquidating bullion to cover immediate cash shortages. When the world-s lenders of last resort start pawning the family silver to stay liquid, the IMF-s significant downgrades to global growth look less like a warning and more like an obituary. The financial system is scrambling for dollars because the war is no longer a localized skirmish; it is a global liquidity vacuum.

In the corporate world, the vultures are circling for whatever scraps remain. ASML-s revenue beat is a reminder that the machinery of modern life-and modern warfare-requires silicon at any price. Meanwhile, Kraken-s confidential IPO filing is a classic move in the crypto playbook: find a window of market optimism to offload equity onto retail bag-holders before the next freeze sets in. Bitcoin-s recent climb is a desperate rally in a dying season, and the exchange knows it.

This cycle of full planes and empty pockets is a recurring theme in global mismanagement. Just as we have seen in the collapse of regional players, the illusion of activity often masks a fundamental rot.
