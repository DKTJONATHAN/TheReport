Well, well, well. Our favorite Leading Vibe herself, Tems, is packing her bags and taking her girl-power-for-the-gram show to South Africa on March 21, 2026. After colonizing the social media feeds of Lagos and Nairobi in 2025, the initiative is finally hitting the South. We are told this is all about "amplifying emerging women," but let us be real for a second. Is this a genuine attempt to fix a broken industry, or is it just another way for Tems to remind us she has two Grammys while the rest of the continent’s female artists struggle for a single Spotify playlist placement?

The South African edition is promising the usual suspects: masterclasses, songwriting sessions, and the ever-vague "mentorship." Applications are open until February 28, 2026, for women aged 18 to 35. It is a very specific demographic for a very specific vibe. Of course, Hennessy is back as a partner because nothing says "creative development" quite like a bottle of expensive cognac and a few Native Instruments plugins. It is giving corporate philanthropy with a side of influencer marketing, and we are all supposed to clap like it is groundbreaking.

Let’s look at the track record. Lagos was "immersive," Nairobi was "pan-African," and South Africa will likely be "vibrant." But where are the charts being topped by these mentees? We keep hearing about these programs, but the top of the charts still looks like a boys' club with Tems as the token exception. It reminds me of the [African Giant Or Corporate Puppet? The Messy War For Burna Boy's Soul](/posts/african-giant-or-corporate-puppet--the-messy-war-for-burna-boy-s-soul) where we have to wonder if these initiatives are truly about the music or just about keeping the big stars looking like saints while the labels pull the strings and collect the checks.

Tems says her goal is to help women "step into their power." It is a beautiful sentiment, truly. It sounds great in a press release next to a quote from a Hennessy executive talking about "pushing boundaries." But while the Leading Vibe brand expands globally, the actual music industry remains a shark tank. Providing a few masterclasses is like giving a glass of water to someone in a desert and calling yourself a humanitarian. It is a nice gesture, but does it actually change the weather for the thousands of women ignored by the major labels?

If you are a South African artist looking for a seat at the table, by all means, apply. Get those free tools and hope the Leading Vibe rubs off on you. Just do not be surprised if the "access" you are promised is mostly just a front-row seat to Tems' next fireside chat. At the end of the day, the industry is still more interested in the "vibe" than the actual value, and this initiative seems perfectly designed to keep that cycle spinning while everyone gets a nice tax write-off.
