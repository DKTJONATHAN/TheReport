Oh look, Spotify’s editorial team has emerged from their air-conditioned offices to tell us what we should be listening to. They’ve dropped their "100 Greatest Pop Songs of the Streaming Era" list, and while everyone is busy popping champagne because three African stars made the cut, I’m over here looking at the fine print. It’s the same old story: Western validation wrapped in a "cultural impact" ribbon. We’re supposed to be grateful that the gatekeepers finally noticed the rhythms that have been shaking our streets for decades, but let’s be real, this list is more about keeping their subscriptions up than actually honoring the craft.

Coming in at number 8 is "One Dance," the song that turned Wizkid into a global backup singer for the king of "strategic borrowing," Drake. Spotify calls Drake a "tastemaker," which is just a fancy industry term for someone who waits for a sound to get hot in the trenches before slapping his name on it. Sure, it has four billion streams and paved the way for Afrobeats in the U.S., but let’s not pretend it wasn’t a rebranding exercise for a Canadian rapper who needed a new vibe. It’s the ultimate industry irony: Wizkid’s "Ojuelegba" was the soul, but the world only cared once it was soaked in Hennessy and Drake’s monotone vocals.

Then we have Rema at number 29 with "Calm Down." Don't get me wrong, the boy has talent, but the narrative that Selena Gomez "pushed it to another level" is exhausting. It’s like the industry thinks an African song isn't "global" until a former Disney star sprinkles some easy self-assurance on it. This obsession with the "crossover" is giving me [Government Ghosting: Why the Cabinet’s Femicide Memo is Giving Major Toxic Ex Vibes](/posts/government-ghosting--why-the-cabinet-s-femicide-memo-is-giving-major-toxic-ex-vibes) because the industry keeps promising us a seat at the table while making us pay for the chair in "features" we didn't really need.

Tyla rounds out the trio at number 50 with "Water," and while I’m living for the South African representation, the obsession with the "log drum" is becoming a bit of a caricature. It took 55 years for a South African solo artist to chart on the Billboard Hot 100 after Hugh Masekela, and we’re supposed to act like the system isn't rigged? Tyla is on the "brink of superstardom" according to the suits, which usually means they’ve found a way to monetize her aesthetic until the next "exotic" sound comes along.

At the end of the day, these lists are just a way for streaming giants to pat themselves on the back for "discovering" what we’ve been dancing to since 2015. They talk about "storytelling" and "long term influence," but we know it’s about the data. If the numbers didn't scream, they wouldn't listen. It’s a cynical cycle of hype, but I suppose if it keeps the lights on for our stars, we’ll keep playing the game-even if the rules are written by people who wouldn't know a real Afrobeats party if it hit them in the face.

So, congrats to Wizkid, Rema, and Tyla for surviving the meat grinder of global pop. You’ve officially been "minted" by the gods of Spotify. Just remember that the same people who put you on a list today will replace you with an AI-generated beat tomorrow if the algorithm demands it. Stay savvy, because in the world of streaming, you’re only as great as your last "strategic" collaboration. Whether it is a playlist or a corporate memo, the truth is usually buried under layers of glossy marketing.
