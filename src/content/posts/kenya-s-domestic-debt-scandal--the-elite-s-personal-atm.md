So, the big secret everyone's whispering about is that Kenya is basically a debtor to its own elite. While we're busy blaming foreigners and international lenders, the real heist is happening on our own soil. Turns out, we owe more money to Kenyans - specifically, our banks and insurance companies - than we do to the folks outside our borders. And who owns these institutions? Surprise, surprise, it's our dear "Politically Exposed Persons," the very people supposed to be serving us.

This isn't some elaborate scheme; it's a perfectly legal transfer system. Every shilling of interest we pay on domestic debt is just money moving from your pocket, the taxpayer, straight into the coffers of banks and insurance companies controlled by the political class. They've managed to rig the game so that the government's borrowing plans are essentially their personal piggy bank. And it's only going to get worse, with plans to pump up domestic borrowing to a staggering 75% of total debt by 2028. More money for them, more problems for us.

Let's talk numbers, because that's where the real sting is. Interest payments on our domestic debt are already double what we're shelling out for foreign loans. Seventy percent of our interest bill goes to Kenyan lenders, while only thirty percent goes to the outsiders we're always complaining about. Yet, when Gen Z decided to make some noise, their anger was conveniently directed abroad, completely ignoring the local contradictions. It's that same old song and dance: blame the outsider, ignore the rot within. Just like our leaders are always napping while other nations act, we're asleep at the wheel when it comes to our own finances.

The law supposedly says domestic borrowing is for development, but we all know Treasury bonds are being used to pay for tea and biscuits, not schools or hospitals. And the kicker? While we know who's lending us money from overseas, the identities of our domestic lenders are shrouded in secrecy. This isn't right. It's like a shady deal where only one side gets to see the contract. The question of whether this domestic debt is even legal, given how it's being misused, is something that should be keeping us up at night, especially when you consider the long-term implications for future generations.

The historical roots of this mess are as old as Kenya itself. After independence, when foreign banks and insurance companies dominated, we tried to "Africanise" the sector. But instead of building new institutions, the Ndegwa Report basically gave a green light for civil servants to get into business. Fast forward to today, and the ownership of our banks and insurance companies is a tight-knit club. Scan the shareholder lists, and you'll find the names of presidential families, former Treasury bigwigs, central bank governors, top civil servants, and even police and military chiefs. It’s a self-perpetuating cycle of power and profit.

The system is designed for collusion. Those who make the policies are the same people who benefit from the credit supply. It’s a perfect storm of conflicts of interest, where "who benefits" is no longer a question, but a statement of fact. This isn't just about borrowing money; it's about the systematic siphoning of national wealth by a select few, leaving the rest of us to foot the bill and wonder why nothing ever changes.
