Police Arrest MD Sang, PS Liban and Epra Boss: Fuel Scam Uncovered

The stench of corruption in this country, it seems, is stronger than the fumes from that adulterated fuel they’re now admitting to. So, four top dogs from the Ministry of Energy, Epra, and Kenya Pipeline are suddenly finding themselves in DCI’s unwelcome embrace. Apparently, they were busy orchestrating fuel shortages and peddling dodgy diesel. Just another day at the office for Nairobi’s finest.

Energy Principal Secretary Mohammed Liban, his employee Joseph Wafula, Epra Director-General Daniel Kiptoo, and Kenya Pipeline MD Joe Sang - names that were probably whispered in awe just yesterday - are now being paraded as the masterminds behind the country’s fuel woes. Arrested from their homes, no less, with "unspecified amounts of money and documents" found. Shocking, I know. This smells like the usual Nairobi script where the small fish get caught while the big sharks swim in their ill-gotten gains. It’s almost as predictable as Runda being just a fancy name for a crime scene.

These high-flyers are facing charges under the Economic Crimes Act, for the cardinal sin of "failing to keep records, stocks and reserves of petroleum products." So, they deliberately choked the supply, triggering a shortage that they then blamed on some Middle Eastern crisis. Classic deflection. This whole G-to-G arrangement, which has been lauded as a saviour, seems to be more of a golden goose for a select few, now that we know it was possibly smuggling in fuel with enough sulphur to ruin your engine.

The whole mess was apparently flagged by a KPC quality assurance manager who had the guts to stop a bad batch. Good for them, but it’s clear they encountered some serious internal pressure to just "look the other way." This is the Nairobi way, isn't it? A little bit of inconvenience for the common man, a lot of profit for the connected. While Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi is out here ordering oil marketers to release hoarded fuel and warning about "commercially opportunistic" practices, the real culprits are being nabbed. It's all a show.

President Ruto is talking about global economic pressure and deploying Sh17 billion to "moderate pump prices." Sounds nice on paper, doesn't it? But let's be real, that money is probably just a band-aid on a gaping wound, and we all know who’s going to end up paying for it in the long run. The government insists fuel supply is stable, but the arrests tell a different story. It's a carefully orchestrated theatre, and we're all just watching the drama unfold.
