The international community should view this as a flare in the night. India is the world’s second-largest consumer of gold, and when the state tries to choke off that demand, it does not disappear. It merely migrates. The real winners here are the smugglers and the shadow financiers of the grey market. By widening the gap between domestic prices and global benchmarks, India is essentially subsidizing the very illicit networks it claims to fight. This move will distort global bullion flows and highlight the deepening cracks in the emerging market facade.

This duty hike is a direct consequence of the dollar’s crushing weight, a theme we explored in [THE DEBT TRAP’S NEW TEETH: HOW TARIFFS ARE STRANGLING THE DOLLAR’S DOMINANCE](/posts/the-debt-trap-s-new-teeth--how-tariffs-are-strangling-the-dollar-s-dominance). As the greenback remains a blunt instrument of American hegemony, nations like India are forced into increasingly draconian measures to prevent capital flight. The irony is that these tariffs often do the opposite of what is intended. They signal to the world that the domestic currency is a sinking ship, prompting those with means to find even more creative ways to park their wealth in hard assets.

Beyond the borders of the subcontinent, this shift signals a broader retreat into regionalism and economic isolation. While China tries to bypass the dollar with bond sales in Saudi Arabia and Huawei attempts to build a digital fortress with its own operating system, India is using the ancient lure of gold to tether its economy. It is a fragmented world where the so-called free market is a ghost of the past. The hike is a confession that the state has lost control over the structural appetite of its people, choosing to tax their cultural DNA rather than addressing the fiscal instability that makes gold so attractive in the first place.
