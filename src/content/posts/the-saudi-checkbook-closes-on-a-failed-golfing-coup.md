The global impact of this collapse is a stark reminder of the volatility inherent in sportswashing. For three years, the professional golf circuit served as a playground for a regime attempting to buy cultural relevance. Now that the PR utility of LIV Golf has reached its expiration date, the Saudis are discarding the league like a depreciated asset. This leaves the remaining PGA players in a position of performative moral superiority. They are not demanding penalties for the returnees out of a sense of justice, but rather to ensure that their own financial territories are protected from the men who tried to burn the house down for a paycheck.

The sight of Brooks Koepka crawling back to participate in secondary events just to regain his standing is a definitive humiliation for the breakaway movement. It demonstrates that while money can buy a player's time, it cannot create a sustainable competitive ecosystem out of thin air. The PGA Tour is not facilitating these returns out of a spirit of forgiveness. It is doing so because the global market for golf is too small to support two warring factions. The entire saga has been a masterclass in corporate cynicism, where the fans were the last priority in a battle between two equally greedy monopolies.

As the litigation ends and the legal fees are buried, the sports world is left with the wreckage of a failed coup. This was a war of attrition where the only real outcome was the erosion of the game's remaining dignity. The "bad blood" mentioned by players like Brian Harman is the inevitable result of a system where loyalty is a commodity and integrity is a negotiation tactic. The Saudi exit proves that even the most aggressive attempts at market disruption have a limit when the return on investment is nothing more than expensive resentment and a fractured audience.
