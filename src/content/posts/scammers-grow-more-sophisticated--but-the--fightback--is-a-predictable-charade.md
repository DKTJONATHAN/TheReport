The latest pronouncements on the escalating war against scams are, predictably, a masterclass in self-congratulation thinly veiled as progress. We’re told scammers are getting smarter - a revelation that would shock precisely no one. What’s truly remarkable is how readily so many fall prey to the same tired tricks, amplified by modern technology. Kirsty’s story, a textbook romance scam that bilked her of £80,000, is not an anomaly; it’s a feature of a globalized world where emotional manipulation is just another commodity. She was duped by a digital phantom, a Nigerian national posing as an English businessman, complete with a doctored online banking facade. The phone she purchased in the UK ended up in Nigeria, and the money flowed through a transnational network of beneficiaries. This is not new; it's just more efficient.

The article’s assertion that scams have surged since the COVID lockdowns is hardly groundbreaking. More people online, more opportunities for those who prey on them. Global fraud losses exceeding half a trillion dollars annually are not a data point; they are a testament to the sheer scale of human gullibility and criminal enterprise. The so-called "fightback" involves joint agreements and international cooperation, high-minded ideals that crumble against the reality of criminal operations flourishing in "almost lawless areas or in areas which are controlled by armed gangs" where governments have "very little control." Myanmar, with its scam centers born from defunct casinos, is a prime example. The chaos of civil war provides fertile ground for these operations, turning human desperation into profit.

Adding insult to injury, the article points out that many in these scam centers are themselves victims. Lured by bogus job advertisements, these individuals are trafficked and forced to perpetrate the very scams that ensnare others. The description of a vacated scam compound in Cambodia, complete with motivational slogans and records of toilet breaks, is not a picture of resistance; it’s a snapshot of organized exploitation. These are not heroic figures fighting back; they are pawns in a larger, brutal game, often facing beatings or worse for failing to meet targets.

The notion of a unified global effort is laughable. Wealthier nations, the primary victims, clamor for action, while developing nations, where these operations are rooted, are asked to do more with insufficient resources. The imbalance is stark: criminals exploit impoverished communities, and national priorities for some countries lie in more "elemental" concerns than financial crimes targeting Western bank accounts. The call for "two-way collaboration" is a polite way of saying developed nations need to stop complaining and start sharing their considerable resources and technical expertise, something they are notoriously reluctant to do when it doesn't directly benefit them.

Ultimately, the "fightback" is a predictable cycle. Scammers innovate, victims fall, and governments engage in performative summits and agreements that do little to stem the tide. The underlying human flaws-greed, loneliness, desperation-remain constant, and technology simply provides more sophisticated avenues for their exploitation. The idea that this current wave of international cooperation will fundamentally alter the landscape is wishful thinking; it’s more likely to be another round of well-intentioned gestures that ultimately change nothing for the vast majority of victims, both those who lose their money and those forced to steal it.
