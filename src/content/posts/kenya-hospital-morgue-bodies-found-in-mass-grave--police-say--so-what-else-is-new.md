So, the police, in their infinite wisdom, have "found" 33 bodies exhumed from a mass grave in Kericho. Apparently, these were bodies that "transferred" from Nyamira District Hospital to a private cemetery. Yes, transferred. Because bodies are like excess office furniture, aren't they? Just pile them up and ship them out. And the best part? They’re not even trying to hide the fact that most of them were children, including foetuses. Because, of course, who needs proper disposal when you have a gunny bag and a government vehicle?

The head of the Directorate of Criminal Investigations, Mohamed Amin, is busy telling us they need to "find out whether the bodies were disposed legally." Legally? In Kenya? Since when did that become the benchmark? The law apparently says unclaimed bodies over 14 days need a court order for disposal. But who follows rules when you can just make inconvenient truths disappear into the ground? It's not like this is the first time we've seen something like this. Remember the Kilifi forest scandal? Or the 9 bodies found in a Nairobi dumpsite this year? This is just the Kenyan way of dealing with its problems - bury them, literally.

The gravediggers, bless their souls, are the ones who alerted the police. So, the folks tasked with digging holes are now the whistleblowers. It takes a special kind of audacity to be so brazen that even the people burying your mess get suspicious. Some witnesses are even claiming a government vehicle was used to "hurriedly" bury them. Because, naturally, the most efficient way to dispose of unwanted bodies is with a government-sanctioned joyride and a quick burial. It’s almost artistic in its depravity.

And let’s not forget the context. This latest stunt happens amidst growing concerns about "alleged rights abuses," including extrajudicial killings by the police. Missing Voices is out there documenting these numbers, but who's really listening? The government is too busy orchestrating elaborate body-moving exercises, it seems. This entire situation is a perfect illustration of how things work here. We're not just talking about a simple case of neglect; this smells like something far more organised, something that makes you wonder if the rot goes deeper than just a few corrupt officials.
