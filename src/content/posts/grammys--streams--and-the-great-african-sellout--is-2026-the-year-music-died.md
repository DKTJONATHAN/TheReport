Oh look, the world finally noticed Africa has a pulse. In 2026, the industry is tripping over itself to crown Tyla the queen of everything because she won a Grammy for "Water." Let's be real: "professionalizing" the industry is just fancy talk for signing your soul over to international labels so they can dilute your sound enough for a suburban kid in Ohio to dance to it. Tyla isn't an accident; she is a carefully curated product designed to make Western execs feel cultured while they rake in the streaming revenue from Johannesburg to Paris.

Then we have Burna Boy, the self-proclaimed African Giant with a Spotify following larger than some countries' populations. He is filling the Stade de France and selling branded hoodies like he is a walking billboard. It is impressive, sure, but the ego required to sustain that level of "dominance" is reaching atmospheric levels. While he is busy being a fashion icon in Paris, the actual grit of the Port Harcourt streets that birthed his sound is being airbrushed out for the sake of a clean, marketable international aesthetic.

Rema is the poster child for this new "accessible" sound, which is basically code for "inoffensive." His remix with Selena Gomez has billions of streams, but at what cost? We are watching artists turn into [Chess Pieces and Clout Chasers: The Messy After-Party of Kenyan Politics](/posts/chess-pieces-and-clout-chasers--the-messy-after-party-of-kenyan-politics) where the goal isn't the art, but the proximity to American stardom. If you aren't collabing with a mid-tier US pop star or a Disney alum, do you even exist in the 2026 charts? It is the same old story of selling the culture to the highest bidder while pretending it is "global representation."

Davido is still hanging on, playing the role of the Afrobeats Godfather by collecting American rappers like they are limited-edition sneakers. From Chris Brown to Lil Baby, his discography is starting to look like a US immigration office filing cabinet. Meanwhile, Asake is trying to bridge the gap with Travis Scott, because apparently, you haven't "made it" until a man who mumbles over trap beats gives you a head-nod. It is a cynical cycle of seeking validation from a Western industry that only loves us as long as we are the "flavor of the month."

And don't get me started on the Amapiano "revolution." What started in the townships of Johannesburg is now being blasted in snobby Berlin clubs by people who couldn't find South Africa on a map if their lives depended on it. Uncle Waffles and Kabza De Small are pioneers, but the speed at which the West is consuming this genre feels less like appreciation and more like a smash-and-grab. Enjoy the spotlight while it lasts, because once the TikTok kids find a new beat to obsess over, the global industry will drop us faster than a leaked demo.
