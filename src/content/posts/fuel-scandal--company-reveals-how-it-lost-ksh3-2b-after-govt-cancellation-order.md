FUEL SCANDAL: COMPANY LOST 3.2 BILLION AFTER GOVT CANCELED SHIPMENT











So, Oryx Energies, a company that's apparently been around since the dinosaurs roamed Nairobi, claims they've been fleeced Ksh3.2 billion. Apparently, they were doing the government a massive favour, responding to an "urgent" request to secure fuel because of some Middle East drama. They jumped, they quoted, they got accepted for a massive shipment, all within days. Because, you know, when the government needs something, it's always a matter of extreme urgency and perfect execution, right?

The twist? The government pulls the plug on the whole deal just as the fuel ship is sailing towards Kenya. "Binding contractual arrangement"? Pfft. Apparently, that means nothing when the real strings are being pulled elsewhere. The company is crying foul, saying they've already committed resources and now they're out of pocket. It’s a classic Nairobi story: promises made, deals broken, and taxpayers left holding the bill, or in this case, a company nursing a Ksh3.2 billion wound.

Meanwhile, senators are asking all the "right" questions, like did they consult lawyers? Did they know about the government-to-government deals? And, of course, who's paying for the loss? It’s almost as if they’re surprised that a government that claims to be cracking down on cartels is somehow involved in a situation where a private firm loses a fortune. This whole saga is just another reminder that [The Great Institutional Pivot](posts/the-great-institutional-pivot--why-your-paycheck-is-a-secondary-concern-for-global-technocrats) is happening right under our noses, and our wallets are paying the price.

This Oryx debacle is just the second act in this unfolding drama. Remember One Petroleum? Their cargo was also nixed because it was deemed too expensive compared to the magical G2G deals. So, the government creates a crisis, private companies step in with their own supplies at a premium (because, surprise, the market is tight!), and then the government slaps them down, citing price control and G2G rules. It's a masterclass in how to create chaos and then blame everyone but yourselves.

And the officials? They "step down." Arrests are made. Accusations fly about manipulating stock data. It’s the usual song and dance. While they’re busy pointing fingers, Oryx is reserving its legal rights and has no active talks with the ministry. You can bet your last shilling that the "cartels" Ruto is so keen to dismantle are the ones still laughing all the way to the bank, or perhaps, to the nearest offshore account.

The Senate committee is still "inquiring," which means more theatre, more pronouncements, and probably more promises of transparency that will evaporate faster than dew on a hot Nairobi morning. As for KPC assuring us of adequate stocks? Some stations still report shortages and higher fares. So, while the big boys play their games and lose billions, we the common mwananchi are left footing the bill, both literally and figuratively.
