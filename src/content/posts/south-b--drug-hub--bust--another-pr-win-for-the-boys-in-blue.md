So, the National Police Service decided to do their jobs for a few hours in South B. They raided an apartment in Sana Sana, acting all surprised that "illegal activities" were happening in a Nairobi residential area. They found 316 rolls of weed and some sachets. In a city where you can smell the herb from the back of a moving matatu, they want us to clap for this "major" discovery like it is a groundbreaking achievement.

They arrested a "foreign national," because of course they did. It is easier to pin the label of "drug kingpin" on someone without a local ID than to look at the well-connected sharks actually running the city. They also found some "white powdery substance." We will wait for the forensic analysis, which in Nairobi time, means we might hear about it by the next election cycle if we are lucky.

This whole operation feels like a choreographed performance to justify the [Billion-Shilling Badges: The High Cost of Police 'Prosperity'](/posts/billion-shilling-badges--the-high-cost-of-police--prosperity) that keeps the upper echelons fed while the rank and file chase sachets. They recovered foreign currency too, which they claim are proceeds of crime. We all know that once those "exhibits" enter a police station, they have a funny way of shrinking or disappearing altogether before they ever see the inside of a courtroom.

Meanwhile, some poor soul in Bomet gets hit with a 50-year sentence for trafficking. Fifty years. In this country, you can steal half the national budget and get a government appointment, but carry some plants and the system suddenly remembers it has teeth. It is the same old story in the 254: the law is a spider web that catches the small flies and lets the big ones fly right through with a police escort.

The NPS says they are "intensifying crackdowns" and want us to keep sharing information. Translation: expect more roadblocks and more random apartment searches where the goal is less about "public safety" and more about meeting the month's quota of suspects. If they really wanted to stop the flow of narcotics, they would start at the ports and the high-end office blocks, not at a kitchen table in South B.
