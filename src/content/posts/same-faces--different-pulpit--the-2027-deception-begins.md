Here we go again. It is March 2026, and the air is already thick with the smell of expensive cologne and recycled promises. Rigathi Gachagua, the man who once sat at the high table of the very system he now calls a "problem," is back on the streets telling us we need to reclaim our country. Reclaim it from whom? The script is as predictable as a Nairobi traffic jam at 5:00 PM. They stand on top of their SUVs in Shamata, preaching to people whose only hope is that the next rally might drop a few hundred bob in the dust.

The entourage is a "who is who" of people who have spent decades in government offices. You have Kalonzo Musyoka, Fred Matiang-i, and JB Muturi all nodding along like they just discovered that the cost of living is high. It is a traveling circus that moves from Nyandarua to Nakuru, leaving nothing behind but dust and empty soda bottles. These [Roadside Tyrants: Why the Kenyan Prado is a Rolling Symptom of Our National Decay](/posts/roadside-tyrants--why-the-kenyan-prado-is-a-rolling-symptom-of-our-national-decay) love to talk about the "common man" while peering down from the sunroofs of cars that cost more than the entire village's annual harvest.

Gachagua is now talking about "decisive leadership" and "reviving agriculture." It is funny how these solutions only occur to our leaders when they are outside the gates of State House looking in. Between February 23 and March 1, they have covered more ground than a long-distance bus, hitting Kisii, Nyamira, and Nakuru. They call it a "quest for liberation," but for the youth in Heshima or Maai Mahiu, it is just another day of wondering where the next meal will come from while the elites map out their next five years of luxury.

The rhetoric about the "power of the ballot" is the ultimate gaslighting. They tell us the vote is our voice, but once the ink dries on our fingers, the voice usually gets muffled by the sound of sirens clearing the way for their motorcades. They promise to prioritize reforms and strengthen education, the same things they have been "prioritizing" since the 1990s. It is a loop, a glitch in the Kenyan matrix that we are forced to watch every five years without fail.

As they gear up for the 2027 General Election, the "unity" they preach is strictly for their own survival. This is not about the people; it is about who gets to hold the keys to the treasury. Gachagua says the victory is about "The People," but in this city, we know better. "The People" are just the extras in a movie where the lead actors never change, they just swap costumes and hope we have forgotten the previous season.
