Sarz has been the self-appointed gatekeeper of Afrobeats for over fifteen years, or at least that is what his PR team wants us to believe. He recently sat down with Joey Akan for the new season of Afrobeats Intelligence, looking every bit the weary veteran trying to stay relevant in a genre that moves faster than his old desktop computer. It is funny how these industry legends only start talking about "legacy" and "inspiring the future" when they realize the new kids are breathing down their necks with better plugins and significantly less ego. He claims he has worked with every star from every era, but let us be real, some of those eras should have stayed in the archives.

The real tea from the interview was Sarz admitting to a massive, ego-bruising reality check during a trip to the US. Imagine being the most "lauded" producer in Nigeria, only to have a high-level executive basically tell you your work is amateur because you do not know how to handle volume levels. He called it a "professional shock," but I call it a long-overdue wake-up call. It is the same kind of embarrassing industry realization we see when people question if [DJ Gasket: Is He A Musical Philanthropist Or Just Desperate For A Viral Hit?](/posts/dj-gasket--is-he-a-musical-philanthropist-or-just-desperate-for-a-viral-hit) is actually a talent or just another person screaming for attention. Sarz had to learn the hard way that being a local champion does not mean anything when you are trying to play on a global stage where they actually care about technical skill.

Now he is claiming he used to churn out 10 to 15 beats a day back in the day. If that were true, it explains why half the Afrobeats catalog from five years ago sounds like it was produced during a thirty-minute lunch break. Sarz is now pivoting his narrative to "inspiration" and "free creation" because, in his own words, that old hunger is gone. It is a classic move for producers who have made their money and lost their edge- they stop trying to innovate and start "curating." He is basically admitting that he is coasting on his reputation while he tries to figure out why the global hype is starting to fizzle out.

Let us talk about the "ownership" flex, which is just a fancy way of saying he is tired of making other people rich. Sarz is keeping the "good stuff" for his own projects like "Protect Sarz at all Cost." It is a smart business move, sure, but it also smells like he is bitter about sitting in the background while the artists he built are out buying private jets. He wants a catalog that keeps earning while he sleeps, which is fine, but do not wrap it in a "helping the youth" bow. We see through the PR-friendly jargon; it is about the bank account, not the culture.

Finally, the chat turned to the "decline" of black music and Nigeria’s struggling economy. It is very convenient to blame the exchange rate for why the music is not hitting the same way it used to. Sarz and Akan spent the end of the episode being "contemplative," which is just industry-speak for "we have no idea how to fix the fact that the sound is getting stale." If the music was actually as revolutionary as they claim, a macroeconomic dip would not be the primary talking point. But hey, at least Sarz finally learned how to master a beat before playing it for the big bosses. Progress is slow, I guess.
