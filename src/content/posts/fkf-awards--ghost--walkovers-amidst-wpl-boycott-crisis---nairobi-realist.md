So, the Football Kenya Federation (FKF) Leagues and Competitions Committee (LCC) has decided to play referee and goal scorer all at once. Three teams - Kenya Police Bullets, Ulinzi Starlets, and Trinity Starlets - have been gifted three points and two goals each for games that never even kicked off. Apparently, their opponents - Kisped Queens, Kayole Starlets, and Zetech Sparks - decided that boycotting a league that treats its players like charity cases was a better option than turning up to play. Typical Nairobi drama, really.

The FKF's reasoning is as twisted as a matatu driver on Mombasa Road. Standard football rules? Forget them. Apparently, you don't actually need a pitch to play on. Ulinzi Starlets rocked up to Stima Club Grounds only to find it locked, because Kayole Starlets, the home team, couldn't even be bothered to book the venue. Same story at Comboni Polytechnic Grounds, where Trinity Starlets' home turf was booked for some private shindig. But hey, the show must go on, right? Especially when it means handing out points to the "defending champions" and their buddies. It’s like saying your car won the race because the other cars were too busy dealing with potholes.

And the crackdown? Oh, it's real. Vihiga Queens, Madira Soccer Assassins, Bungoma Queens, and Kisumu All Starlets have been ordered to explain their "involvement" in this strike. They've got until midnight on Tuesday to justify why they expect players to be paid, sponsors to exist, and for that FIFA money to actually reach its intended recipients. It's a classic move: punish those who dare to demand basic respect. Reminds me of how some of these "rich suits" at places like Karen Country Club preach about "Ubuntu" while lining their own pockets, a bit like the officials here pocketing our hopes. [Rich Suits and 'Ubuntu' Boots: The High-Stakes Hypocrisy at Karen Country Club](/posts/rich-suits-and--ubuntu--boots--the-high-stakes-hypocrisy-at-karen-country-club)

This whole charade is a desperate attempt by the FKF to save face before a crucial meeting. The boycotting clubs are rightly calling it out as a distortion of the league table, a punishment for daring to ask for professional standards in a league that's become financially unsustainable. Some players are so broke, they're relying on fan donations. Meanwhile, the federation talks about "integrity." Integrity? That’s a good one.

The outcome of today's meeting will probably decide whether this season is played on the pitch or just in the sterile, air-conditioned rooms of Kandanda House, with all the excitement of watching paint dry. Either way, don't expect any miracles. This is Nairobi football, where the only predictable thing is the next scandal.

This whole mess shows that when it comes to the Women's Premier League, the FKF is more interested in maintaining the illusion of control than actually supporting the sport. They’re more concerned with boardroom battles than the actual game.
