So, Ecobank’s Jane Jelagat is here to tell us how digital banking is “reshaping” Kenya. Apparently, fathers sending money to university daughters, traders restocking in Gikomba, and commuters paying for matatus with a tap are signs of a financial revolution. It’s not just technology, she says, it’s a “fundamental change” in how we expect our money to move. Because of course, when you're trying to survive in this city, what you really need is another notification pinging on your phone.

McKinsey and the Kenya Bankers Association are quoted, saying everyone prefers self-service and digital channels. Convenience and efficiency, they rave. It’s all about comparing your bank to that slick ride-hailing app or shopping site. Because who wants to deal with actual human beings when you can just tap your way through life? This is Kenya, the “youthful and digitally savvy” population, where loyalty is “experience-driven.” Translation: if your bank doesn't give you that instant gratification, they’ll lose you faster than a rumour about free money.

Remember the good old days of filling out forms, submitting documents, and physically going to a branch? Jelagat paints this as some ancient misery, designed for neither speed nor convenience. Now, digital banking, when “properly designed,” removes the “friction.” Account opening in minutes, not days. Visibility and control anytime, anywhere. It sounds lovely, doesn't it? It almost makes you forget that "friction" was often there to stop people from being robbed blind. It makes you wonder if this is just another excuse for them to avoid the accountability we saw when those [NAKURU HOSTEL SHUTTERED, JOB SEEKERS RESCUED AMID ABUSE CLAIMS; DON'T EXPECT REAL CHANGE](/posts/nakuru-hostel-shuttered--job-seekers-rescued-amid-abuse-claims--don-t-expect-real-change) were happening.

They’re even talking about “broad economic implications” - digital savings and credit tools for resilience. And how digital channels reduce "cost-to-serve," allowing banks to design products for the “previously underserved.” This sounds like a lot of fancy words for making it easier for them to reach more pockets while cutting their own overhead. Compliance is embedded, AML screening, transaction monitoring - all good for the banks, apparently. But for the individual, it’s just more hoops to jump through, a more sophisticated way to track your every shilling.

Jelagat insists branches won't disappear entirely, just be “redefined.” Because God forbid we lose the cosy atmosphere of a bank for actual complex transactions. The real transformation, she claims, isn’t about features or downloads, but “tangible value” - time saved, access expanded, trust earned. I’m still waiting to see that tangible value. All I see are more ways for money to disappear faster, and for the banks to tell you it’s your own fault. It’s Kenya, after all. We get excited about the shiny new thing, and then we get on with the hustle.
