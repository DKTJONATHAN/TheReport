It has been four years since Savara decided he was too big for the Sauti Sol umbrella and gave us Savage Level. We were told it was a new chapter, but let us be honest, it felt more like watching a long-term couple break up and pretending the rebound is soul-searching. In Nairobi, everything is a project until the rent is due, and while Savara was busy blending Afrobeat-s and R&B, the rest of us were just trying to survive the chaos of a city that offers no safety nets.

The 14-track album was supposed to be a deep dive into his Eastlands roots, but when you are a Grammy-certified star, Eastlands is just a convenient aesthetic for your brand. He produced the whole thing himself, which is great for the ego, but sometimes you need someone in the room to tell you that 14 songs of personal narrative is a bit much for a public with a short attention span. It is hard to focus on a river with many tributaries when [Nairobi Is A Crime Scene And Nobody Is Coming To Save You](/posts/nairobi-is-a-crime-scene-and-nobody-is-coming-to-save-you) and your phone might get snatched if you get too lost in the vibes.

Savara claims that Savage Level is a mindset about discipline and growth, not aggression. That is some top-tier PR speak right there. In this town, the only savage level most people know is the daily struggle to pay bills while the government treats our taxes like a suggestion. It is easy to preach self-belief and resilience when you have a global audience and a solid support system, but for the person on the street, resilience is just another word for not having a choice.

He spent a lot of time crediting his mother and his partner for the emotional grounding of the album. That is sweet, really, but it does not change the fact that solo ventures in Kenya often end up as footnotes once the group hype dies down. Sauti Sol was a powerhouse because they were a unit. Alone, they are just talented guys competing with every other cultural entrepreneur trying to sell us a lifestyle we cannot afford to maintain.

Now he is marking an anniversary and looking at international tour dates. Good for him, I suppose. But as we look back at Savage Level, we have to wonder if it actually changed the game or if it was just another well-packaged distraction. In a city where everything is a hustle, even a solo album is just a long-form business card for the next corporate gig. Reality in Nairobi is far more savage than any album title could ever capture.
