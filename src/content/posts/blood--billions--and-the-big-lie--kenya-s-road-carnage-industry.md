Welcome to Kenya, where your death on a potholed highway is valued at five percent of the GDP before it is even a funeral. President Ruto and Chief Justice Martha Koome spent their morning at State House counting bodies and billions like they were balancing a ledger for a failing supermarket. Apparently, road accidents cost us KSh 450 billion annually. It is telling that the first thing they mention is the "economic loss" rather than the thousands of families destroyed by a system that thrives on negligence and "kitu kidogo."

Now the National Council on the Administration of Justice wants an "integrated e-transport system." In Nairobi speak, that is just a fancy name for another digital platform where your data gets lost and some well-connected tech firm "eats" the tender money. They talk about a "whole-of-government approach," which is really just a polite way of saying that everyone is responsible, so ultimately, nobody is actually accountable when a bus rolls into a valley. It is the same old script: when the bodies pile up too high, you form a committee and ask for more money.

While [Zakayo the Peacemaker: Ruto Plays Global Policeman While Local Celebs Chase Clout](/posts/zakayo-the-peacemaker--ruto-plays-global-policeman-while-local-celebs-chase-clout) focuses on fixing the world’s problems, the roads at home have become literal graveyards. We hit 5,009 deaths in a single year, an increase that they talk about like a minor fluctuation in the price of sugar. You can build all the pedestrian walkways and dual carriageways you want, but if the traffic police are still busy collecting fifty-shilling notes at every corner, those new roads will just be faster ways to reach the morgue.

Ruto is now shouting about the "National Road Safety Fund." Because of course, the solution to people dying is to create another bank account for the government to manage. They claim rising fatalities and infrastructure gaps make this "imperative," but I call it a PR stunt to distract us from the fact that the same government allows unroadworthy vehicles to fly through roadblocks. The "corruption risks" they mentioned are not risks; they are the standard operating procedure for every traffic stop from Mombasa to Malaba.

We are told not to "politicise the tragedy" by the same leaders who use these deaths to justify more taxes and new digital surveillance. The 2025 festive season saw 415 people perish, a 23 percent increase that they treat like a weather report. Until they start arresting the big fish who own the killer fleets instead of issuing "recommendations" from the comfort of State House, this "national transformation" is just another bedtime story for a country that is too tired to even scream anymore.
