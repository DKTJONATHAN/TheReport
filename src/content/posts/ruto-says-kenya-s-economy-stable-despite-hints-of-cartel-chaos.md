So, the man at the top, our President William Ruto, is out here telling us everything is sunshine and roses with the economy. Apparently, inflation has dropped to a measly 4.3 percent. Sh129 to the dollar for 18 months straight. And foreign reserves? A cool $14.6 billion. "Stable," he says. "Confidently," he adds. It’s the kind of talk that makes you wonder if he's looking at the same Kenya we are.

But hold on, because just when you’re starting to feel that warm fuzzy feeling, he whips out the "cartel" talk. This time, it's the oil sector. He’s warning them not to get greedy with this Middle East kerfuffle. He says they’ll be dealt with "firmly, decisively, and conclusively," just like those alleged cartels in the sugar, coffee, and tea sectors that, surprise surprise, have been "eliminated." One has to wonder if "eliminated" means actually gone or just reshuffled into a different briefcase.

This whole "eliminating cartels" narrative feels a bit like those stories about police crackdowns that just seem to be [dancing in circles](/posts/busia-drug-seizure-shows-police-crackdown-is-just-dancing-in-circles). He also mentioned punishing those who peddled fake fertiliser. Good, I guess. But then he pivots to unity, rejecting divisive politics and tribalism, and urging us to work for "shared prosperity." It's the usual script, isn't it? Lots of big words, not much to show for it on the ground for the common mwananchi.

Meanwhile, Mudavadi is out here suggesting electric vehicles as a solution to global oil challenges. Right. Because everyone in Nairobi can afford a Tesla, or even a decent bicycle, with current prices. And the cherry on top? Over 250 Kenyans apparently went to fight in the Russia-Ukraine war, and 47 of them have "returned home." Returned home to what, exactly? More economic stability?

One Tonkei person is quoted dismissing critics as "jealous" of the "transformation agenda." Jealous of what? The fact that fuel prices still make your eyes water? The mounting cost of basic goods? Or perhaps they're just jealous of the endless church services and pronouncements from the podium. Because while the President is busy assuring us of stability and battling imaginary cartels, the rest of us are just trying to figure out how to make ends meet. And if the global conflicts are already rewriting Kenya's economic playbook, perhaps we should be [getting ready for higher prices](/posts/global-conflicts-rewrite-kenya-s-economic-playbook---get-ready-for-higher-prices) instead of listening to fairy tales.
