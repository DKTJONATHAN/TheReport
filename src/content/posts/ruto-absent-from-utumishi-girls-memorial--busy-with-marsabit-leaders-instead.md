So, while the nation was busy shedding tears over the 16 Utumishi Girls who perished in that horrific fire, where was the big man himself, President William Ruto? Hiding at State House, of course. Apparently, listening to grassroots leaders from Marsabit is far more pressing than showing a shred of empathy for the families who lost everything. His wife, Rachel, had to step in, looking all sombre and official, while the "Head of State" was off playing host. Classic.

The scene in Gilgil Stadium must have been heartbreaking. Fifteen caskets lined up, a somber reminder of the lives cut tragically short. You’d think this would be the ONE occasion the President would put aside his schedule. But no. He’s been MIA since the fire, and this memorial was just another missed opportunity to actually *be* there for the people he supposedly leads. This whole affair has all the hallmarks of a typical Kenyan political playbook, much like [Sifuna's Teargas Circus: Just Another Day in the Kenyan Political Playbook](/posts/sifuna-s-teargas-circus--just-another-day-in-the-kenyan-political-playbook).

And let's talk about the "compensation." Sh200,000 per family. Is that supposed to be a serious offer? For the lives of 16 young girls? It’s a slap in the face, really. A token gesture to make themselves feel better, while the real pain is left to fester. It’s the same old song and dance - express condolences, throw some money around, and hope everyone forgets. But these are not just numbers, they are futures extinguished.

Meanwhile, the First Lady, the Education CS, and a whole entourage of government bigwigs were there, going through the motions. The students spoke, the Reverend preached about hope. All very touching, I’m sure, for those who weren't too busy wondering where the hell the President was. It's a shame they have to find comfort in each other while the one person who could offer a real sense of national solidarity is conspicuously absent.

It’s a shame the focus is on the ceremony itself rather than the root cause. This isn’t just about a fire; it’s about safety standards, about prioritizing our children's lives over photo ops. But don't hold your breath waiting for accountability. We’ve seen this before, and we'll likely see it again. This is Kenya, after all.
