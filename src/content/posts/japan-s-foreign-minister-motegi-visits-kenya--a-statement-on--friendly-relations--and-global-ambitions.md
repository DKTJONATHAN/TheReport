Statement by Japan’s Foreign Minister Motegi: More Hot Air Than a Nairobi Matatu in Rush Hour

This is Japan's Foreign Minister, Mr. Toshimitsu Motegi, visiting Kenya for the first time in five years. He's here to talk about "friendly relations" and "wonderful fruit" from decades of partnership. Apparently, our researchers at KEMRI are cooking up something with Nagasaki University, and JKUAT is getting a hand from some Japanese institutions for "agriculture and industry." We've also apparently sent nearly 5,000 experts here and trained over 16,000 Kenyans in Japan. Oh, and our roses are apparently a hit in Japan. How touching.

The minister is also singing praises about trade, with over 120 Japanese companies setting up shop here for the "African market." They're eyeing Mombasa as a trade hub, and Japan plans to keep "supporting the growth" with port developments. It all sounds very rosy, doesn't it? Almost as rosy as the flowers we export. It’s a classic story of developing nations providing raw materials and labor while developed nations offer "cooperation" and reap the larger rewards. This isn't exactly a new plot twist in the global economy; it’s more like a recurring character in a tired telenovela.

Then there’s the sports angle. Apparently, our athletes are great at thrilling the Japanese public, and now there are MOUs being signed for more sporting exchanges. They're hoping to expand beyond track and field to "ball sports and martial arts." This is all good and well, but let's be honest, how much of this translates into tangible benefits for the average Kenyan beyond a few photo ops and the hope that our runners keep making them look good? It’s easy to talk about vibrant exchanges when the financial stakes are clearly unequal.

The minister also mentions the Osaka-Kansai Expo and how the Kenya Pavilion was popular. He’s also aware that Japanese media is popular among our youth. It’s all about personal connections and deepening "friendly relations," right? Meanwhile, the international situation is apparently getting "increasingly severe," with all sorts of global woes from Ukraine to North Korea. Africa, too, has its share of instability. In these "pressing challenges," Japan and Africa need to "join hands" under the "Free and Open Indo-Pacific" vision. A vision that was, conveniently, first articulated here in Nairobi.

This "strategic vision" sounds a lot like a well-packaged geopolitical move. They talk about the "rule of law" and "freedom," but let's not forget the rising importance of "economic security" and the "struggle for hegemony" in new technologies. Japan wants us to become "autonomous and resilient" while they offer "Official Security Assistance." It's a familiar song and dance, with the promise of a better future that often leaves us on the sidelines, cheering for their progress while our own development is tied to their agenda. We've seen this script before, much like the ongoing saga of resources being exploited while basic needs remain unmet. Perhaps they can take a page from the stories of those struggling to find clean water, a problem that seems to conveniently disappear when the bigwigs gather.

Ultimately, Japan is coming to us, they say, for TICAD 10. They want to deepen cooperation and strengthen ties. It's a grand pronouncement, but one has to wonder who is truly benefiting in the long run. While the minister speaks of mutual prosperity, the reality on the ground often involves infrastructure projects that serve foreign interests, and aid that comes with strings attached. We'll see if this visit brings more than just polite words and more "fruit" that ripens more for them than for us.
