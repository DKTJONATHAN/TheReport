The call for a three-month moratorium and a Special Refinance Window for MSMEs is a predictable knee-jerk reaction to the crumbling illusion of global stability. This is the financial version of necromancy, where zombie enterprises are kept upright by the grace of central bank liquidity. When the CII speaks of "calibrated flexibility" in asset classification, they are asking for permission to lie about the health of their balance sheets until the smoke clears. It is a shell game where the cost of failure is deferred to a future that looks increasingly bleak, mirroring the same patterns of forced submission seen in regional sovereign sell-outs.

Equally telling is the desperate plea to exempt foreign investors from long-term capital gains tax. This is the behavior of a state terrified of "flight-to-safety" sentiment. By begging for "patient capital" through tax bribes, the CII admits that India's primary markets are not a pillar of strength, but a fragile construct dependent on the whims of global hedge funds. They are willing to sacrifice sovereign revenue just to keep the tickers green while the regional supply chains of the Middle East dissolve into ash and kinetic warfare.

The most chilling aspect of this proposal is the institutionalization of a "Shock Response Framework" that contemplates oil at $200 per barrel. This is not a contingency plan; it is an admission that the global energy market is a tinderbox and the current economic model is a paper shield. The request for forex swaps for state-owned oil firms reveals a deep-seated fear that the rupee is one supply-chain-hiccup away from a free-fall. In the cynical calculus of global trade, the industrial elite are simply trying to ensure that when the crash happens, the taxpayer provides the cushion.
