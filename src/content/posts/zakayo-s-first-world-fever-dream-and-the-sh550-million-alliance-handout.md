Our "Hustler-in-Chief" was at it again this weekend, spinning tall tales at Alliance High School about Kenya suddenly sprinting toward first-world status. It is the same old script we have heard since the sixties. Apparently, after a few chats with the leaders of Malaysia and Singapore, the President is convinced we are just one dormitory away from becoming the next South Korea. It is a nice story for the kids in blazers, but for the rest of us living in the real Nairobi, it sounds like another round of high-octane delusion served with a side of "please be patient while we tax you into poverty."

The optics were as predictable as a Nairobi traffic jam. While rural schools are literally falling apart and teachers are threatening to strike every other Tuesday, the President saw it fit to drop a cool Sh550 million on Alliance High School and another Sh295 million on Alliance Girls. It is the classic Kenyan way: if you are already at the top, the government will find more ways to polish your shoes. This blatant display of favoritism for elite institutions feels exactly like [The Parliament Sticker: Your VIP Permit to Kill and Keep Partying](/posts/the-parliament-sticker--your-vip-permit-to-kill-and-keep-partying) because it reminds us that there is one Kenya for the well-connected and another for everyone else.

Ruto claims his administration has "substantially solved" the challenges in the education sector. That is a bold statement considering the "New University Funding Model" has left thousands of students confused and stranded, and the "100,000 teachers" recruited are often struggling under precarious contracts. Calling education a "right" while making it increasingly unaffordable for the average mwananchi is the kind of gaslighting we have come to expect. You can build 23,000 classrooms, but if the curriculum is a mess and the job market is a graveyard, you are just building better-looking waiting rooms for the unemployed.

The entourage was the cherry on top of this cynical cake. Surrounded by the likes of Kipchumba Murkomen and Moses Kuria, the President spoke about "global standards" while the local standards of living continue to nose-dive. They talk about a Sh700 billion education budget like it is a gift from their personal pockets rather than our own tax money being shuffled around. We are told to "back the government’s efforts," which is usually code for "shut up and pay your taxes while we play Singapore in the leafy suburbs of Kiambu."

Let us be real: you do not reach first-world status by building fancy science complexes at schools that are already legends. You do it by fixing the rot from the bottom up. But as long as the photo ops look good and the elite schools get their millions, the administration can keep pretending we are on a highway to prosperity. To the rest of us, it just looks like the same old gravel road, full of potholes and empty promises.
