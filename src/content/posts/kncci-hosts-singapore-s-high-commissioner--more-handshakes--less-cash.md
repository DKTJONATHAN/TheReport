So, Dr. Rutto from the KNCCI decides to roll out the red carpet for Singapore’s High Commissioner, Dr. Kan. Apparently, they’ve been discussing how to deepen bilateral trade, investment, and all that jazz. ICT, logistics, ports, renewable energy, smart cities, fintech - the usual buzzwords that sound impressive in a boardroom, but what does it mean for the average Kenyan struggling to make ends meet? It’s always about the high-impact opportunities, isn't it?

Dr. Kan, who apparently presented his credentials to His Excellency William Ruto in June 2025, is singing praises about Africa’s potential. 1.44 billion people in 2026, apparently. Great numbers for PowerPoint presentations. Meanwhile, back on the ground, many of us are still wondering when any of this “potential” is going to translate into something tangible.

Then Dr. Rutto starts challenging Singapore Airlines, telling them Africa is a “serious and strategic aviation market.” Really? Because from where I’m standing, it feels more like a market that’s either too expensive or too unreliable for many, and the only thing that seems to trickle down is the frustration. This whole thing is supposed to be about adopting global best practices, inspired by Singapore's "model of excellence." Sounds nice and shiny, but remember when we heard similar lines about other countries? The result was usually just more paperwork and more photo ops.

They even ended the meeting with a fancy ceremonial plaque. A plaque. Because that’s exactly what a struggling business owner needs to pay rent. It’s always a grand gesture, a symbolic award. But the real test, as always, will be whether any of these high-level discussions actually lead to jobs, lower prices, or actual progress beyond the polished halls of diplomacy. We’ve seen enough of [Another Day, Another Billion-Shilling Heist Put on Ice]( /posts/another-day--another-billion-shilling-heist-put-on-ice) to know that talk is cheap, and plaques are even cheaper. Let's see if this time, the talk actually leads to some serious coin changing hands for the better, not just for the suits.
