So, Kenya Pipeline Company, fresh off that whole fuel scandal that saw some big wigs step down, is now playing musical chairs with its top job. Joe Sang is out, the acting MD is in, and the board, bless their Kenyan hearts, has put out an advert for a new MD. But hold up, it seems they’ve forgotten a few details, like the fact that Uganda, who coughed up a cool Sh20 billion for a piece of this pie, actually has a say in who runs the show. Talk about selective amnesia.

Apparently, the big wigs here in Nairobi revised the company’s articles of association during the IPO - you know, the one that barely scraped by. This revision gave Uganda two board seats and, crucially, the right to veto the hiring and firing of the CEO. Now, five board members are apparently squirming because they know this move is against the rules, but the chairperson, Faith Bett Boinnett, and the Treasury are playing deaf. It's a classic Nairobi move: push first, ask for forgiveness later, or maybe just deny everything.

The whole thing has Uganda absolutely fuming, and frankly, who can blame them? They expected their input, not to be treated like some distant cousin who shows up uninvited. The revised memorandum clearly states that the appointment of the MD needs the nod from both the Cabinet Secretary for the National Treasury and a director nominated by Uganda. It’s written down, in black and white, like everything else they try to brush under the carpet. It’s just another day in the wild west of Kenyan corporate governance, reminiscent of the chaos we saw when [Kenya's Fuel Market Under Strain as Tanzania Holds Its Own Against Shipping Chaos](/posts/kenya-s-fuel-market-under-strain-as-tanzania-holds-its-own-against-shipping-chaos).

Adding insult to injury, KPC stopped being just a government entity a while back and is now a public limited company. The current board, all appointed by the Kenyan government, doesn’t even represent the new shareholders properly. One insider claims their current mandate is null and void, and any decisions made since the gazette notice are illegal. And get this, the representative from the Attorney-General’s office, who would have at least tried to keep them on the straight and narrow, has been conveniently removed. So now it’s just a few independent directors calling the shots, probably for their own benefit.

Uganda’s investment wasn’t just about buying shares; it was a strategic move for regional energy cooperation. They put their money where their mouth is, and now Kenya is acting like they’re just a minor shareholder to be ignored. This is exactly the kind of shady dealings that make you wonder about the [Kenya Police Unearth Mass Grave With 33 Bodies - Surprise, Surprise.](/posts/kenya-police-unearth-mass-grave-with-33-bodies---surprise--surprise) - you think you’ve seen the worst, and then something like this pops up. Expect more backroom deals and a whole lot of denial.
