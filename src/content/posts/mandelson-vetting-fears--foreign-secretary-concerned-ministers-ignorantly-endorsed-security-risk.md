The perennial theater of British politics unfolds once more, this time with the appointment of Lord Mandelson as US ambassador serving as the latest exhibit in a long-running play of incompetence and obfuscation. David Lammy, now Deputy Prime Minister, claims Keir Starmer would have torpedoed Mandelson's nomination had he known the former minister failed security vetting. This conveniently placed assertion, delivered amidst a storm of criticism, smells less of genuine concern and more of damage control.

The core of the issue, as always, lies in the opaque machinations of power. No 10, naturally, deflects blame onto the Foreign Office, asserting that critical "red flags" in Mandelson's vetting were conveniently withheld. Liz Kendall, channeling the party line, stated Starmer was merely informed Mandelson had "developed vetting status," not that he had, in fact, failed the underlying security checks. The implication? Ignorance, not complicity. A comforting narrative for those who wish to maintain the illusion of responsible governance.

Lammy himself, as Foreign Secretary at the time, professes ignorance, claiming neither he nor his advisors were privy to the vetting process. This is hardly surprising. In the grand scheme of diplomatic postings, the meticulous details of security clearance often become casualties of the broader political agenda. The "time pressures" cited by Lammy - the need to have Mandelson in place shortly after Donald Trump's return to the White House - are the very pressures that breed such shoddy oversight. National security is clearly a secondary consideration when political expediency beckons.

The ousting of Sir Olly Robbins, the Foreign Office's senior civil servant, is presented as a decisive action, yet it reeks of a scapegoat being found. Helen MacNamara, a former senior civil servant, wisely points out that Robbins' dismissal is an attempt to find "new processes to blame, new people to blame." This is the predictable pattern: identify a problem, find someone to punish, and then move on, leaving the systemic rot untouched. The suggestion that Mandelson's risks were merely "priced in" by Foreign Office officials, who were then tasked with fulfilling the Prime Minister's desires while implementing superficial "mitigations," is a damning indictment of the system.

The opposition, predictably, is having a field day. Calls for the Prime Minister's resignation are standard fare, accusing him of misleading Parliament. Alex Burqhart, the Conservative shadow Cabinet Office minister, correctly identifies the Prime Minister as the ultimate responsible party, a sentiment echoed by Robert Jenrick and Sir Ed Davey, who question Starmer's competence. The former's assessment of Starmer as either a liar or "grossly incompetent" is a charitable dichotomy; often, the two are intertwined in the corridors of power.

The revelation that the security vetting agency advised denying Mandelson clearance, a fact not disclosed by Robbins to MPs, further complicates the narrative. Robbins' allies claim he was bound by confidentiality. This, of course, conveniently shields him from direct accountability while perpetuating the idea that such sensitive information cannot be shared, even when it pertains to a high-level diplomatic appointment.

Ultimately, this entire episode is a microcosm of how power truly operates. It is a world where optics often trump substance, where political expediency trumps rigorous process, and where accountability is a fluid concept, easily manipulated to suit the narrative. The Mandelson affair is not an anomaly; it is the norm, a stark reminder that in the grand chessboard of global politics, national security is often just another piece to be moved, sacrificed, or ignored in pursuit of a larger, more self-serving game.
