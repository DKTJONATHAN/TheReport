So, we are pretending to be shocked that our immigration system has more holes than a Githurai sweater? Please. If you have lived in Nairobi for more than a week, you know that the only thing "official" about a Kenyan passport is the gold foil on the cover. The rest is just a negotiation. While honest Kenyans are sleeping on the pavement at Nyayo House for months, international fugitives are getting their documents delivered like a Friday night pizza. It is not an administrative failure; it is a business model.

This government loves a good PR stunt to hide the rot. Whether it is the Kisumu quarry ban or the [grass and greed](/posts/grass-and-greed--the-high-cost-of-ruto-s-sporting-dreams) fueling our sporting dreams, the script is always the same: talk big, eat bigger, and leave the taxpayer to clean up the mess. Now that criminals have bought our national identity, the Kenyan passport is basically a "search me" sign for anyone trying to clear customs at Heathrow or JFK.

The diplomatic "trust" everyone is crying about was always a thin veil. Our allies in the West aren't blind. They know that if the price is right, anyone can become a Kenyan citizen by lunchtime. The result? Stricter visa rules, more "random" searches, and genuine students missing out on scholarships because their travel documents have the credibility of a conman's handshake. We are being locked out of the world because someone at a desk needed a new Subaru.

Being a regional hub for investment is a pipe dream when your identity management is this compromised. Investors don't put money in places where a "legitimate" document can be bought by a human trafficker. We are shouting about "Silicon Savannah" while our most basic security systems are being auctioned off to the highest bidder. It is embarrassing, but in this city, shame is a luxury we traded for "facilitation fees" a long time ago.

Don't expect a real cleanup. We will get a few press statements, maybe a junior clerk will be sacrificed to the gods of "accountability," and then it will be business as usual. The only difference is that next time you travel, you will be the one sweating in the interrogation room while the guy who bought his passport is already through the gates. Welcome to the realist's Kenya, where your citizenship is just another commodity on the black market.
