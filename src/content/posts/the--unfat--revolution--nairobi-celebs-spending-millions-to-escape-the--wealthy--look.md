Honey, the days of "kitambi" being a sign of a fat bank account are officially buried in the graveyard of Kenyan trends. Nowadays, if you are not "unfatting" yourself, are you even a real Nairobi celebrity? The Nairobi Bariatric Center is currently busier than a discount sale at Gikomba, with Dr. Lyudmila Shchukina raking in the coins from desperate socialites. It is funny how we went from praising "big is beautiful" to everyone suddenly having "hormonal issues" that can only be solved by an eighty-thousand-shilling Ozempic jab. Let us be real - it is not about the joints or the blood pressure; it is about the "likes" on the 'Gram.

Take Naomi Kuria, for example. The content creator decided that five months of gym sweat was just too much work and hopped on the Ozempic train before dropping seven hundred thousand shillings on something called "AirSculpt." She literally paid the price of a small car to move fat from her stomach to her legs because she felt they were too "skinny." Then she had the audacity to be "very angry" when her fans pointed out the hypocrisy. If you are going to buy a new body, Naomi, at least own the receipt without the tears. It is a shortcut, plain and simple, and calling it a "personal choice" does not hide the fact that you were throwing up your guts just to look "defined."

Ciru Muriuki is also in the mix, defending her gastric balloon and weight-loss jabs like her life depends on it. She claims it was never about looking for a "series of shortcuts," but let us look at the math. When you go from a silicone balloon to medical-grade injections, you are not exactly taking the scenic route. The "society stigma" she complains about is just the collective side-eye from people who know that most of these influencers are just terrified of being ordinary. They talk about "cyber-bullying" and being told to "unfat," but then they turn around and monetize every single step of their surgical recovery.

This entire "slimming journey" trend smells like a carefully crafted PR move. It reminds me of how everything in the 254 entertainment scene is staged for maximum engagement. Just look at [The Comedy of Errors: Why the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan) to see how branding always comes before reality. These weight-loss stories are no different; they are just another way to keep the comments section flaming while the influencers "struggle" through their seven-thousand-dollar recovery packages.

The real tragedy here is the "vanity" trap Dr. Alvin Mondoh is worried about. While the elite are spending millions to get that hourglass figure, the average Kenyan is being targeted by scammers selling fake jabs in unregulated markets. The pressure to be "tiny" is so intense that people are willing to risk their lives for a "snatched" waistline. But hey, as long as the "unfat" trend continues, the clinics will keep thriving and the influencers will keep posting their "before and after" photos, conveniently forgetting to mention the literal price of their "natural" glow-up.

So, if you are sitting at home feeling bad about your snack habits, just remember: you are not "unhealthy," you are just not spending six thousand dollars on medical intervention yet. In Nairobi, beauty is not skin deep - it is as deep as your credit limit. We are living in an era where "empowerment" means paying someone to vacuum your midsection while you cry about "stigma" on a podcast. Stay cynical, darlings, because the only thing thinner than these influencers is the truth behind their "transformations."
