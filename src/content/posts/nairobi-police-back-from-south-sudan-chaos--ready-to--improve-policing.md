So, the National Police Service is all smiles, apparently, welcoming back a crack team of eleven officers who’ve been saving South Sudan from itself for the last 18 months. They’ve apparently been busy protecting civilians, shoving aid packages around, and generally trying to keep things from exploding. All this, under the watchful eye of Silas Kiptoo, who, along with the rest of the lot, has now landed back at Jogoo House. One can only imagine the tales they have, probably more exciting than anything happening on Ngong Road.

According to Mathew Kutoh, who’s basically standing in for the big boss, these officers were paragons of virtue. Not a single disciplinary case reported. Bravo! Makes you wonder if they were actually in South Sudan or on a spa retreat. He’s even saying they were “excellent ambassadors for Kenya.” I guess when you’re dealing with a country that’s always teetering on the edge, maintaining basic discipline is considered a diplomatic triumph. It’s almost like they’re saying the rest of the force back home is a mess, isn't it?

Now, Kutoh wants them to bring their newfound “skills and experience” back home. He’s talking about turning police stations into “centres of excellence.” Right. Because what Kenya really needs is more officers who’ve seen the real-deal chaos and are now expected to magically transform our local chop-chop joints and traffic chokeholds into shining examples of law enforcement. It’s the same old song, isn’t it? Send them out, polish them up, bring them back, and then watch them get swallowed by the same old system. It reminds me of when they were selling [Wi-Fi Dreams to a Hungry Generation](/posts/selling-wi-fi-dreams-to-a-hungry-generation) - lots of hype, not much substance for the average Kenyan.

Kenya is still keen on its peacekeeping gig, apparently. Sending officers to fragile states is their thing, especially in Africa. We're a nation of peacemakers, or so they keep telling us. Just a few months ago, in February, some of these very officers, including Silas Kiptoo himself, were getting medals for their efforts. Professionalism and dedication, they said. It’s a nice pat on the back, I suppose, when you’re dodging bullets and dealing with crises. Let’s hope that medal polish lasts when they’re back dealing with Nairobi traffic jams and petty theft.

The UNMISS mission is a tough gig, involving day and night patrols to keep the peace and protect people. These eleven officers have now completed their tour and are back home. It’s the end of one deployment, but for Kenya, it’s just another day at the office, sending more officers off to wherever the UN needs a patch-up job. The question remains: will their time abroad actually translate into better policing here, or will it just be another line on their CV before they’re back to the grind, dealing with the same old Nairobi reality?
