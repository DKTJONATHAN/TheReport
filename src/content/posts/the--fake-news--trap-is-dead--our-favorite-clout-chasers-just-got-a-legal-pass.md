The streets of Nairobi are buzzing, and for once, it is not just about which socialite is faking a pregnancy for views. The Court of Appeal finally decided to do something for the culture by striking down the "false information" penalties in the Cybercrimes Act. Judges Patrick Kiage, Aggrey Muchelule, and Weldon Korir basically told the government that they cannot just lock people up for being messy on the internet. This is a huge "get-out-of-jail-free" card for every influencer whose entire career is built on filtered lies and "allegedly" captions.

Let us be honest, the government was using Sections 22 and 23 like a personal delete button for anyone who dared to expose their nonsense. We have seen this movie before when [Politicians Play Pilot's PR Team While the Internet Keeps Receipts](/posts/politicians-play-pilot-s-pr-team-while-the-internet-keeps-receipts), trying to polish their public image while the rest of us are looking at the actual data. The court finally admitted that these laws were way too vague. If "falsity" was a crime, half of the people on my timeline would be doing hard time just for posting their "blessed" lives from a rented Airbnb.

The Bloggers Association of Kenya (BAKE) and their lawyer Mercy Mutemi are popping champagne, calling this a win for truth-tellers. I call it a win for the tea-spillers who no longer have to worry about the DCI knocking on their door because they posted a rumor that turned out to be ten percent off. The court realized that if you criminalize every journalistic error or satirical tweet, you end up with a very boring-and very silent-internet. And we all know the state loves a silent public that does not ask questions about where the tax money went.

But do not get it twisted; this victory comes with some very heavy fine print. While you cannot be jailed for "fake news" anymore, the judges kept the parts of the law that let the authorities snoop through your digital life. They can still get warrants to seize your gadgets and even monitor your data in real-time. So, you might be free to post your unverified gossip, but the authorities can still watch you type it out. It is the classic Kenyan compromise: they give you your tongue back but keep their hands in your pockets.

It is hilarious to see the state try to police "truth" when they are the biggest exporters of fiction in the country. For now, the content creators and the "keyboard warriors" can sleep a little easier knowing that a typo will not lead to a prison cell. Just remember that the vipers are still watching, and while the "fake news" trap is gone, the surveillance net is still very much intact. Stay messy, but maybe keep your DMs a bit cleaner than your public feed.
