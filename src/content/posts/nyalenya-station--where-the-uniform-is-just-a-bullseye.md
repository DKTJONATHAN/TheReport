If you still believe the police uniform offers some kind of magical protection, Nyalenya Police Station has a reality check for you. In Mumias West, the protectors became the victims when a few thugs on motorbikes walked in with a lie about a stolen bike and walked out with two G3 rifles. Officer Richard Utolo, who survived a thirty-year career, was killed just two years before he could enjoy his pension. It is the Kenyan way: the system uses you until you are a headline, then leaves your family to beg for justice that will likely never come.

The reaction from the top is as predictable as a Nairobi traffic jam. Senior officers are requesting more time, which is the standard government translation for "we are clueless." While the big fish are busy enjoying [the broad-based buffet: when the vultures start biting each other](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-the-vultures-start-biting-each-other), the boots on the ground are left in remote outposts without cameras, power, or even a basic sense of situational awareness. It is hard to sell the public on security when the station itself is a soft target for anyone with a motorcycle and a bad attitude.

The locals are now shouting for Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen, hoping he can fix decades of rot with a press statement. Village elders are suggesting that officers should carry pistols because they are easier to operate. It is a tragic comedy when the public has to give tactical advice to the police. If the people supposed to guard the guns are the ones getting robbed of them, then the social contract has not just been broken-it has been shredded and sold as scrap metal.

We are told the attackers did proper research, as if it takes a genius to realize a rural police station is basically a sitting duck. Now the family of the deceased is asking the big questions: if the police are not safe, what about the rest of us? The answer is simple, but nobody wants to hear it. You are on your own. In the end, the only thing the authorities are truly hunting for are those missing rifles, because the state always cares more about its lost hardware than the man who died holding it.
