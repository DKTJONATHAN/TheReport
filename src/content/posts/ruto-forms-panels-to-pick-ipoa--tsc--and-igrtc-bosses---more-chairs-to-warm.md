So, the Big Man himself, President William Ruto, has decided it's time to shuffle the deck and "reconstitute" some of these so called "key constitutional and independent offices." Apparently, the Kenya Gazette, that dusty old book where all the important (and not so important) pronouncements end up, tells us he’s put together panels to find new faces for IPOA, IGRTC, TSC, and PSC. Just another day in Nairobi, folks.

And who are these esteemed individuals tasked with finding the "best" candidates? A mix of names you've probably never heard of, some from his own office, a couple of governors who are probably more worried about their own counties' crumbling infrastructure, and a whole lot of representatives from various "associations" and "alliances." It’s all very official, of course, with processes like advertising, shortlisting, and interviews. Just like when Mary Atieno finally got her Sh11 million Nairobi house after years of rent pains - a process, a system, but somehow the outcome always feels a bit rigged, doesn't it?

Let’s not pretend this is about noble ideals of transparency and meritocracy. These are the plum jobs, the ones that grease the wheels of power and offer ample opportunities for… influence. IPOA, meant to keep the police in check? IGRTC, meant to smooth over national and county squabbles? TSC, managing teachers? PSC, running the whole civil service machine? These are the places where the real deals are made, where accountability is a nice word to throw around before the next appointment.

The vacancies, they say, are due to "term expiries, resignations and redeployments." Code for people leaving because the heat got too much, or they got a better offer somewhere else, like that headline about corporate musical chairs. And of course, the IPOA chairperson position is vacant because the last guy, Ahmed Issack Hassan, got promoted to the Court of Appeal. Fancy that. From watching over the police to watching over the entire judiciary. It's a career path that seems to benefit the select few, while the rest of us are left wondering when our own "opportunities" will arise.

Ultimately, this is just the first act. These panels will go through the motions, churn out some names, and then the real vetting - or the political maneuvering - will begin. We’ve seen this song and dance before. Will these new appointments actually lead to better policing, smoother intergovernmental relations, or a more efficient public service? Or will they just be more warm bodies filling important chairs, waiting for their own "redeployment" or "term expiry"? My money’s on the latter.

The whole exercise is a reminder that no matter how many articles are written about constitutional principles and public confidence, the game in Nairobi often boils down to who you know and who you’re willing to please. Let’s just hope these new appointees are at least competent enough to keep the wheels from falling off completely. Though, let’s be honest, with the way things are going, even that might be too much to ask.
