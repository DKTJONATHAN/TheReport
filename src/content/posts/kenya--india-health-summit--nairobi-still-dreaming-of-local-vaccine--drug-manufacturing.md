Kenya and India are gearing up for another "high-level health investment roundtable," where Nairobi will once again declare its undying love for local vaccine and drug manufacturing. It's the usual song and dance: big names, big promises, and very little actual dirt under the fingernails. We've heard this tune before, haven't we? Every few years, there's a new grand plan to turn Kenya into a "regional hub" for medical production. Meanwhile, basic clinics still lack paracetamol.

This latest talk-shop comes as the government flails trying to roll out Taifa Care - a scheme many are still scratching their heads over - and supposedly "strengthen the country's health security." Remember when we were supposed to be ready for anything? Seems a lifetime ago, or maybe just when a court had to step in to stop a planned [US quarantine facility](/posts/ebola-outbreak--kenya-court-suspends-planned-us-quarantine-facility-in-the-country---hiiraan-com). It's always a new "strategic partnership" when the existing systems are crumbling, isn't it? CS Aden Duale and the Indian High Commissioner Adarsh Swaika met at Afya House, cooked up this plan, and now expect us to believe that "identifying investment opportunities" means anything other than identifying opportunities for well-connected individuals.

The proposed summit is set to focus on attracting "investments" in pharmaceutical manufacturing and "technology transfer." Don't hold your breath for actual factories to pop up overnight, transforming us into the "pharmacy of East Africa." More likely, it's about easier routes for Indian products to flood our market, maybe a few assembly plants that employ a handful, and a lot of talk about "reducing dependence on imported medicines." We've been "prioritizing" this since Covid-19, which only highlighted how unprepared we truly are. Lofty ambitions are cheap; actual implementation, well, that's another story entirely.

Duale, ever the optimist, is keen to "leverage India's globally recognised expertise" to achieve Universal Health Coverage through Taifa Care. He even briefed the delegation on the Social Health Insurance Fund, boasting over 31 million registrations. Numbers are easy to churn out, but whether those millions are actually *benefiting* from a working system, or just registered on a list, is a question he conveniently sidesteps. "Fast-tracking" MoUs with "clearly defined priorities" sounds like standard bureaucratic fodder designed to look good on paper, not to deliver tangible change for the mama mboga struggling to pay for medicine.
