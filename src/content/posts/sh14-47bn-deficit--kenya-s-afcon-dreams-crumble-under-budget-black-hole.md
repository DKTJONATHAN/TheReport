So, the Sports ministry has a hole in its pocket big enough to swallow a small nation's GDP - Sh14.47 billion, to be precise. And guess what's falling into that hole? Our grand plans to co-host the 2027 Africa Cup of Nations with Uganda and Tanzania. Because why would anything in this country actually *work* as planned? Elijah Mwangi, the Sports Principal Secretary, dropped this delightful bombshell in a report, presumably hoping we’d all just nod and accept that our football future is as bleak as our current fiscal situation.

Apparently, the stadium, this monument to our supposed sporting glory, will cost us a cool Sh44.7 billion by the time it’s vaguely finished. And we’re not even talking about the actual games yet. We have a measly 12 days to cough up US$30 million, which translates to Sh3.9 billion. You know, pocket change for a country that seems to specialize in accumulating debt faster than a toddler accumulates Lego bricks. This is the kind of financial wizardry that would make even the most seasoned con artist blush.

And to add insult to injury, Parliament, in its infinite wisdom, has decided to reject the Ministry of Sports’ plea to increase the budget. So, we’re supposed to host an international tournament with less money than a street vendor makes in a good day. It's a classic Nairobi two-step: promise the moon, then trip over your own feet before you even leave the ground. Reminds me of how some people chase dreams, only to end up in situations far worse than where they started, much like Sophie Turner's journey. [Sophie Turner on why being at your lowest is liberating](/posts/sophie-turner-on-why-being-at-your-lowest-is-liberating).

All signs point to us fumbling this chance spectacularly. We’re already behind, and with this kind of financial incompetence, it’s a miracle if we don’t end up hosting a local village tournament instead of Afcon. It’s the usual story: the people with the vision are either clueless or corrupt, and we, the ordinary citizens, are left holding the empty bag, watching our aspirations deflate like a cheap balloon. It’s not just sports; it’s a reflection of how things operate here.

It’s a shame, really. We have the talent, the passion, but when the money isn't flowing where it should, or when it’s being pilfered, even the most dazzling potential gets extinguished. We're left with half built stadiums and half baked plans, a testament to our enduring ability to snatch defeat from the jaws of victory.

At this rate, we’ll be lucky if we can even afford the bus fare to the stadium, let alone host the entire continent. The real winners here are the ones who’ve managed to siphon off funds, while the rest of us are left with the bitter taste of broken promises.
