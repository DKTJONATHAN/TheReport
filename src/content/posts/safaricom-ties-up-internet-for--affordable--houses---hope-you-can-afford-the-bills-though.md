The "affordable" housing dream, now with added Wi-Fi. Safaricom, that ever-present behemoth, has snagged a deal to slap fibre internet into these government-sponsored boxes, starting with the Mukuru project. Apparently, the goal is to have the infrastructure ready before anyone even steps foot inside. Because who needs basic amenities when you've got speed, right? This whole thing smells like another government-backed scheme designed to look good on paper while the reality for actual Kenyans is likely to be a lot less rosy, much like how [protests keep disrupting learning, while schools and parents pretend to prioritise student safety](/posts/protests-keep-disrupting-learning--while-schools-and-parents-pretend-to-prioritise-student-safety).

This isn't just a one-off gig; Safaricom's mum company, Vodacom, spilled the beans, and now the telco's confirming it's a nationwide rollout. They're bringing in "Wi-Fi Bamba," their budget-friendly fibre product. For a mere KSh 800 a month, you get a dazzling 15Mbps, enough to keep three devices ticking over. It's their answer to price-sensitive households, which, let's be honest, is most of Kenya. Forget the fancy KSh 2,999 packages; this is for the people who are just trying to survive.

What's truly brilliant, in a cynical sort of way, is their plan to introduce hourly, daily, and weekly payment options. Because why have a stable internet connection when you can embrace the glorious uncertainty of Kenyan living, just like you do with your mobile data? It’s all about convenience, apparently. And by embedding this into the housing projects from the get-go, Safaricom bypasses the messy business of actual competition, securing thousands of new customers before they even have a choice. Clever.

The fixed internet market in Kenya is apparently booming, with nearly 196,000 new connections in a few months. Safaricom, naturally, is king of the hill with over 35% market share. They reckon there's room for another four million connections. With the government's push for 500,000 affordable housing units by 2029, this Wi-Fi deal is just another feather in Safaricom's cap, adding to their extensive government tech work. After all, the government owns a nice chunk of Safaricom, so it’s all just a big happy family, isn't it?

Meanwhile, elsewhere, KeNHA is busy playing whack-a-mole with businesses along the highway, demolishing structures for road reserve encroachment. Seems like a more straightforward approach to "infrastructure development" than the Safaricom Wi-Fi gamble. But hey, at least the houses will have internet. Assuming anyone can afford the monthly bills after paying for the actual house.
