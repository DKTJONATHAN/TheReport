If you thought the weather in Nairobi was unpredictable, try following Gathoni Wamuchomba’s political compass. One day she is the self-appointed champion of the "oppressed" mountain, and the next, she is singing hymns about President Ruto’s supposed political grip on the region. On Sunday, February 22, 2026, she took to X to tell us that the mountain is now one big construction site. We have heard this "maendeleo" song before, usually right before the government finds a new way to pickpocket the taxpayer.

It is hilarious how a few dusty roads and a couple of bridge skeletons can suddenly wash away years of bitterness. Wamuchomba claims the mountain people love development, but in reality, they are just tired of being used as a political football. While she celebrates these construction sites, the rest of us are wondering if these projects are just another way to inflate the national debt. This kind of manufactured optimism is just another part of [The Great Decentralization: Generative AI and the Erosion of Cultural Gatekeepers](/posts/the-great-decentralization--generative-ai-and-the-erosion-of-cultural-gatekeepers) where the elite try to control the story while the ground remains hungry.

Then there is the petty swipe at the former Deputy President, Rigathi Gachagua. Calling him the one who "types and deletes" is peak high school drama, not national leadership. Wamuchomba is busy claiming Githunguri is "tick" while the man from Mathira watches his influence get dismantled by the same system he helped build. In the world of Kenyan politics, loyalty is a seasonal commodity that expires the moment a State House invite hits your inbox. It is a game of musical chairs where the music is just the sound of empty promises.

Senator Joe Nyutu is at least offering a reality check, even if he is just another player in this theatre of the absurd. Daring the President to hold an impromptu rally in Kenol or Mukuyu without the state-funded hype is the only way to see the truth. We all know how the "ground" works: the yellow buses arrive, the "volunteers" get their small allowance, and suddenly the cameras capture a "massive" turnout. If the support were genuine, Ruto would not need a choreographed church service every Sunday to prove he is still the king of the hills.

Ruto says the friendship built with the mountain has a foundation that "petty politics" cannot destroy. But foundations in this country are often built on shifting sand and broken contracts. Whether it is Gachagua’s noise or the President’s development agenda, the end result is the same for the person on the street: more taxes, more rhetoric, and a front-row seat to a circus that refuses to leave town. The mountain is not "regained"; it is just being rented for the 2027 season.
