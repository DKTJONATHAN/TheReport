So, the French are coming, again. Along with the Brits and the Americans, because apparently, Nairobi isn't already a big enough playground for foreign forces. The opposition and some noisy civil society types are wailing about "sovereignty" and "accountability," as if this is new. It’s the same old song, just with a different Western tune. They're signing defence agreements, which is a fancy word for inviting more soldiers onto our soil, complete with immunity that makes our laws look like suggestions.

Boniface Mwangi, the presidential aspirant who likes to make a scene, is clutching his pearls about French soldiers avoiding prosecution. He’s worried about citizens' safety, which is rich considering how many of us feel safe when foreign boots are stomping around. He’s right about one thing though: our own Constitution feels more like a suggestion than a law when these deals are on the table. It’s a clear sign that international friendships are far more important than ensuring justice for Kenyans, especially when the accused wear uniforms from powerful nations.

Remember Agnes Wanjiru? Killed in Nanyuki, linked to British soldiers. Years later, still no justice. This isn't a new problem; it's a recurring nightmare. These loopholes in military agreements are well-worn paths for avoiding responsibility. Now, with the French joining the party, it’s just more room for the same old song and dance. We're essentially handing over jurisdiction on our own land, a move that would make even the most naive person raise an eyebrow. It makes you wonder if we're turning into a cheap hotel for foreign armies, a thought that is as depressing as [THE COMMODIFIED CALM: UNMASKING THE LUXURY OF THE SOJOURN](/posts/the-commodified-calm--unmasking-the-luxury-of-the-sojourn).

The government, meanwhile, is busy spinning tales about enhanced security and counterterrorism. They want us to believe these partnerships are about protecting us, not about making Kenya a strategic military outpost for the West. Especially ironic when other African nations are kicking France out. We’re aligning ourselves deeper with powers that are already mired in geopolitical drama, risking being dragged into their global conflicts and economic tumbles. It’s a gamble that feels less like strategy and more like desperation.

Justin Muturi and Mwaivu Kaluka are also chiming in, calling for transparency and parliamentary oversight. Bless their hearts. They want foreign soldiers to be subject to Kenyan law. They want to ensure national sovereignty isn't weakened. It's a noble sentiment, but let's be real, these are the same arguments that have been made for years, falling on deaf ears. The deals are ratified, the soldiers arrive, and the promises of accountability fade into the usual bureaucratic fog.

So, here we are, signing away our sovereignty piece by piece, all in the name of "defence cooperation." While the government boasts about stronger security, the reality for many Kenyans is a growing sense of being a pawn in a much larger game, a game where our laws and our citizens' rights seem to be the first casualties. It’s a grim outlook, but then again, what else can you expect when you're so eager to please the powerful?
