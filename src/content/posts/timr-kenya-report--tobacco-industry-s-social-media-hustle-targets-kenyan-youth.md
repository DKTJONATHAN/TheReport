So, TIMR-Kenya has dropped another report, this one pointing out the obvious: the tobacco industry is using Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook to hook Kenya’s youth on their toxic wares. Apparently, 86 percent of these young people are glued to their phones multiple times a day, and surprise, surprise, they’re bombarded with ads for cigarettes, vapes, and those trendy nicotine pouches. It’s all about selling a "lifestyle," linking aspiration and social status to puffing away. Céline Awuor from IILA calls it "sophisticated" and "difficult to regulate." Sophisticated? More like predatory and cowardly, hiding behind shiny packaging and discounted poison.

They’re using influencers, the same ones you see parading around at parties and product launches, to showcase these products. And it’s not just about the product itself; it’s about the whole package. Flavors that scream "fun" and packaging that pops - it’s all designed to lure the vulnerable. Buy-one-get-one offers and subtle online promotions are just the cherry on top of this rot. Remember how we discussed clout-chasing and how it can blind people to reality in the Shoday's Africa Rising Moment piece? This is just another form of manufactured hype, but with deadly consequences.

Our so-called tobacco control laws, enacted ages ago in 2007 and 2014, are apparently as effective as a sieve in the digital age. They ban advertising and sponsorship, sure, but on these digital platforms, it’s a free-for-all. Age verification? A joke. Influencer marketing, ephemeral posts, cross-border content? The laws haven't even caught up to the horse-and-buggy era, let alone the speed of the internet. It's like trying to catch smoke with your bare hands.

And it gets worse. The tobacco industry isn't just passively marketing; they're actively lobbying to weaken regulations, even trying to mess with the proposed Tobacco Control Amendment Bill, 2024. They’re using litigation to delay health warnings and other sensible measures. Corporate social responsibility? A smokescreen to gain legitimacy while they push nicotine pouches and vapes as "modern," "scientific," or "less harmful." Less harmful than what, exactly? A slower death?

The report’s calling for "urgent, coordinated action." Big surprise. They want the law to explicitly cover digital marketing, influencer promotions, and sponsored content. Stronger age verification, online health warnings, regulating those sickeningly sweet flavors and packaging, and getting social media platforms to actually do something about content targeting minors. They even want digital literacy campaigns. All this after 79 percent of respondents under 35 have seen these products online. It’s a war for our youth's future, and right now, the industry is winning.

Globally, tobacco kills over 8 million people annually. Here in Kenya, it’s an estimated 12,000 deaths a year. With over 75 percent of our population under 35, this early exposure to nicotine is setting us up for a future riddled with health problems. Kenya might have a history of leading in tobacco control, but if we don't adapt and enforce these new digital battlegrounds, we’re going to be left choking on our own progress, or rather, the industry’s profits.
