The latest pronouncements from the government, attempting to distance Sir Keir Starmer from the Lord Mandelson vetting debacle, reek of self-preservation and political maneuvering. Liz Kendall’s assertion that Starmer would have blocked the appointment had he known of the security concerns rings hollow, given the apparent failure to adequately brief him. This isn’t about ignorance; it’s about the calculated management of information for political expediency.

David Lammy’s claims of being unaware of the vetting process, while serving as Foreign Secretary, are frankly astonishing, if not outright disingenuous. To suggest that a major diplomatic appointment, particularly one involving sensitive security clearances, would proceed without his direct knowledge or that of his senior advisors is to paint a picture of profound institutional incompetence or, more likely, a deliberate sidestepping of responsibility. The "time pressures" cited by Lammy regarding Donald Trump’s White House are a flimsy excuse for potentially compromising national security.

The swift ousting of Sir Olly Robbins, the Foreign Office's most senior civil servant, is a classic case of finding a convenient fall guy. Helen MacNamara’s observation that the government is scrambling for "new processes to blame, new people to blame" rings true. The suggestion that Foreign Office officials might have "priced in" the risks associated with Mandelson and prioritized "what the prime minister wanted" over security concerns is a damning indictment of the service's politicization.

The opposition parties, predictably, are baying for blood, accusing the prime minister of misleading Parliament. Alex Burqhart’s blunt assessment, placing all responsibility on the prime minister, is the most sensible take in this circus. Meanwhile, figures like Robert Jenrick and Ed Davey are engaging in predictable partisan attacks, questioning Starmer’s competence rather than dissecting the systemic failures at play.

The core of this issue lies not in Starmer's personal knowledge, but in the Foreign Office’s willingness to expedite a security clearance process, potentially overlooking or downplaying significant red flags to fulfill a political directive. The fact that the vetting process only began after Mandelson was selected, and that the initial report advised against clearance, makes the subsequent rush to judgment all the more suspect. Sir Olly Robbins' allies claiming he was bound by confidentiality is a convenient shield, but it doesn't absolve the system that allowed such a decision to be made. The entire affair is a testament to the cynical maneuvering and institutional rot that often characterize high-stakes political appointments.
