So, the President’s on a mission, eh? Telling our ambassadors to go out there and "promote Kenya's economic and strategic interests." Apparently, we're a "position of increased strength" now, with "improved economic stability" and "growing investor confidence." It’s the usual song and dance, isn't it? Every time there’s a conference or a big meeting, we get the same spiel about how Kenya is "steady" and "on the rise." Meanwhile, the rest of us are still wondering when these grand pronouncements will actually trickle down to our pockets.

He’s boasting about avoiding a "projected debt default" thanks to "deliberate policy choices" and "unsustainable subsidies" being yanked. Funny, because for many Kenyans, it feels like the only thing sustainable these days is the rising cost of pretty much everything. The GDP is supposedly around $136 billion with a 5% growth, and inflation has supposedly "declined to an average of 4.3 per cent." Sounds great on paper, but my matatu fare and the price of sukuma wiki tell a different story. And that "stabilised shilling"? Let’s just say it’s stabilised at a level that still makes importing anything a luxury.

They’re throwing around numbers like $2 billion in foreign direct investment and KSh5 trillion for infrastructure. Apparently, it's all creating jobs, like the 63,000 jobs promised from some investment conference. And let’s not forget the "300 multinational corporations" that are supposedly "backing the country's economic direction." It’s easy to sound confident when you’re looking at spreadsheets and PowerPoints, but the reality on the ground is often a lot grittier. We've heard this tune before, haven't we? It reminds me of those times when the government focuses on the flashy stuff, like that Safari Rally, while other essential areas get neglected. [Ruto Scraps Government Safari Rally Funding Post-2026 WRC, Expects Others to Foot Bill](/posts/ruto-scraps-government-safari-rally-funding-post-2026-wrc--expects-others-to-foot-bill).

And the social programmes? Thirty million under the Social Health Authority, 240,000 housing units under construction, 100,000 teachers hired. These are supposed to be the tangible outcomes, the proof that things are working. But the queues at public hospitals are still long, and the quality of those housing units is always a question mark. Even the overseas employment angle, with 540,000 Kenyans abroad sending back over $5 billion, feels like a symptom of a domestic economy that can't quite absorb its own people. It’s a reminder of the struggles many face, a far cry from the "soft life" some might dream of. [The 'Soft Life' Just Got Harder: Interpol Crashes the Fake Billionaire Party](/posts/the--soft-life--just-got-harder--interpol-crashes-the-fake-billionaire-party).

So, our diplomats are meant to be out there, "amplifying Kenya's voice" at these upcoming summits like the Africa-France Summit and the Our Ocean Conference. They'll be advocating for reforms, playing peacekeepers in Haiti and Africa. All while we’re supposed to believe that Kenya is "not only steady, but it is also on the rise." I’ll believe it when my landlord stops asking for rent in dollars and when the promises of prosperity actually show up in my bank account, not just in presidential speeches.
