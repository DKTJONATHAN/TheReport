Passaris Tells Ngunyi: Tribe Politics in Kenya? Still a Thing, And We'll Regret It.

Esther Passaris, our Nairobi Woman Rep, has finally decided to tell Mutahi Ngunyi what we all already know: Kenya’s obsession with tribe in politics is a dead end. Ngunyi, bless his analyst heart, keeps telling the Kikuyu community what to do with their votes, like they’re a flock of sheep. He’s out here predicting their doom if they don’t follow his sage advice, and frankly, it’s exhausting. Passaris wants us to believe we’re moving towards “mature national discourse.” Please.

She’s right, of course, that communities aren’t monolithic. The “mountain” isn’t a single mind. But let’s not kid ourselves, tribe still dictates a lot more than Passaris wants to admit. She talks about younger voters wanting “truth and accountability.” That’s cute. They also want jobs, roads, and food on the table. And who do they blame when that’s not happening? Their elected representatives, of course. It’s a cycle, and tribal affiliations are often the easiest way to assign blame or seek solace.

Passaris is optimistic about issue-based politics, mentioning infrastructure and opportunities. Sure, if you’re lucky enough to live in a constituency where the MP actually delivers. For the rest of us, it’s still about who’s got the biggest ethnic voting bloc. Ngunyi’s predictions of regret are almost laughable because they’re so predictable. We’ve been here before, and we’ll be here again. It’s the Kenyan political drama, season after season. Remember when Billy Mwangi was talking about state-sponsored abduction? That's the kind of real-world consequence that gets lost when we're debating tribal voting patterns.

She warns about false information, which is a nice thought. But in Nairobi, we’ve seen how easily misleading narratives can whip up a frenzy, only to leave us with the same old problems. Development projects are great, but let’s be honest, they’re often just tools to secure votes, not genuine long-term national interests. We’re still a long way from leadership being assessed on merit. Right now, it’s more about who can mobilise the most votes, and ethnicity is still the go-to mobilisation strategy for many.







Esther Passaris, our Nairobi Woman Rep, has decided it's time to tell Mutahi Ngunyi to get a clue. She’s calling for Kenya to ditch the tribal politics, a sentiment that sounds lovely on paper, but let’s be real, it’s a tough sell in this country. Ngunyi, bless his predictable analyst heart, is out there trying to dictate to the Kikuyu community how they should vote, like they’re all a single, easily manipulated unit. He’s predicting their future regrets based on past voting choices, and honestly, it’s a script we’ve seen played out countless times. Passaris wants us to believe we’re ready for some high-minded, issue-based national conversation. Good luck with that.

The reality on the ground, as Passaris acknowledges to some extent, is that communities aren't a single voice. The "mountain" isn't a monolith, and opinions are indeed diversifying. However, to suggest we're on the cusp of abandoning ethnic loyalties in favour of "truth, accountability, and long-term national interests" feels a tad optimistic, even for a politician. Younger voters might be more globally aware, but they're still living in Kenya, and the economic realities here are stark. They're looking for tangible improvements, not just abstract ideals.

Passaris highlights the importance of development - roads, markets, jobs. These are the things that actually matter to people struggling to make ends meet. But let's not pretend these projects are purely altruistic. They’re often strategic moves to shore up support, and tribal considerations still play a massive role in who gets what. Ngunyi’s constant pronouncements, while perhaps intended to provoke, only serve to reinforce the notion that ethnic voting blocs are the primary drivers of our political landscape. It's a self-fulfilling prophecy.

The warning against false information is a good one, but it’s like telling a fish not to swim. In Kenyan politics, misleading narratives are as common as matatus. They create temporary excitement, sure, but the disappointment when those hyped-up expectations evaporate is a familiar sting. While Passaris expresses optimism that Kenya is "gradually moving toward a more issue-driven political culture," the evidence is still shaky. Meritocracy is a nice dream, but for now, ethnic affiliation remains a powerful, if frustrating, currency. We’ve seen the fallout from political maneuvering before, as in the case of Billy Mwangi’s story.
