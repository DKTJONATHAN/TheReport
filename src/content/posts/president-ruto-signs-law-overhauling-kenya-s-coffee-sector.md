Ruto's Coffee Law: More Bureaucracy, Same Old Bitter Taste
President William Ruto signs reforms into law, creating new institutions and regulations for Kenya's struggling coffee sector.
The beans are on their way, but is the money?
Politics
Coffee, Kenya, Agriculture
Dirt
President William Ruto has, with much fanfare, signed a new law that's supposed to rescue Kenya's dying coffee industry. Apparently, the old system was too messy, too fragmented, and too full of people arguing over who gets what. So, they've created a brand new Coffee Board of Kenya, a shiny new regulator to tell everyone what to do, from the folks growing the beans to those selling them. It’s all about "administrative order" and a "centralized regulatory framework." Sounds familiar, doesn't it?

This grand restructuring means money from a new levy will be thrown at "sector institutions and development initiatives." Because what a broken industry needs is more committees, more paperwork, and more places to disburse funds. They’re promising to revive production and strengthen market promotion, which is the same song and dance we’ve heard for years while coffee output keeps plummeting. This sounds like a perfect recipe for another round of endless meetings, just like how our dignity seems to be perpetually on life support: [The Only Thing Dead in This Hearse is Our Dignity](/posts/the-only-thing-dead-in-this-hearse-is-our-dignity).

To add to the circus, certain financial market functions will now be handled by the Capital Markets Authority. So, even trading coffee beans is getting complicated. And of course, there's a new Coffee Research and Training Institute, because what we really need is more scientists telling farmers how to grow coffee that can magically withstand a changing climate. They'll be researching diseases and developing "climate-resilient" varieties. Let's see how that fares when the rain refuses to fall or decides to drown everything.

Everyone involved, from the farmers to the roasters, will have to register with their county governments. So, more forms, more queues, and more opportunities for local officials to figure out how to benefit from this "formal regulatory registry." While national institutions will be busy with "policy" and "market development," the counties will be collecting data and, presumably, our hard-earned cash.

Despite all these new layers of governance, the numbers don't lie. Coffee exports dipped in 2025, fewer beans going out, even if the price per kilo surprisingly went up. This new law is supposed to create a "coordinated and transparent framework." But what it looks like from here is just another elaborate scheme to shuffle responsibilities, create more jobs for people who will never see a coffee farm, and perhaps, just perhaps, siphon off a bit more money before the whole thing collapses again.
