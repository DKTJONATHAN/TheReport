So, the eggheads at the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) are telling us Kenya's economy will chug along at a "steady but subdued" pace. Four-point-six percent in 2026, then maybe a whopping 4.9 percent in 2027. Hooray for resilience in construction, services, and mining, I guess. Meanwhile, agriculture and manufacturing are apparently taking a nap, conveniently stopping us from anything resembling real progress.

And speaking of things that are not great, inflation is apparently "gradually re-emerging." Prices are nudging up, thanks to those lovely global oil prices that make everything from your matatu ride to your ugali cost more. The only silver lining they can find is that the shilling is "broadly stable," which, translated, means it's not plummeting faster than a poorly constructed building.

Let's talk about the real elephant in the room: debt. Our public debt is now hovering around 70 percent of GDP, and get this - nearly half of all government revenue is swallowed up by just paying off loans. The fiscal deficit has widened to a juicy 6.1 percent. So, while they're busy talking about "development spending," the reality is they're just juggling repayment obligations. It’s a classic Nairobi balancing act, and we’re the ones who will inevitably fall.

The EBRD even warns about political uncertainty leading up to the 2027 elections. Shocking, I know. Combine that with the never-ending energy cost hikes - a problem that seems to be a global theme, with [The Energy Noose Tightens as Global Governance Collapses](/posts/the-energy-noose-tightens-as-global-governance-collapses) - and the ongoing saga of securing a new IMF program, and you've got a recipe for a medium-term outlook that’s about as bright as a power outage.

Across the entire sub-Saharan Africa region, they're projecting a slowdown too. Higher energy costs, global trade chaos, and less investment. Some commodity-producing countries might weather the storm a bit better, but for many, including Nigeria, Senegal, Côte d’Ivoire, and Benin, the report points to fiscal pressures, inflation, and election-related spending as the usual suspects for weighing them down. Sounds familiar, doesn't it?

So, while these reports churn out numbers and fancy jargon, remember this: "steady growth" means we’re barely treading water. The real story is the relentless pressure of debt and the ever-increasing cost of just living. Don't expect miracles; expect more of the same old struggle.
