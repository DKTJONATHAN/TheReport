So, the French navy has graced us with their presence in Mombasa, parading their shiny warships under the guise of fighting “sea crimes.” Piracy, drug trafficking, illegal fishing - the usual suspects that have been making life hell for us long before this whole Middle East kerfuffle. Now that the Americans and Israelis are busy playing whack-a-mole with Iran, suddenly everyone’s waking up to the fact that our shipping routes are a mess. Quelle surprise.

Captain Jocelyn Delrieu and his flotilla, the Jeanne d'Arc 2026, are here for a “five-month mission” from Egypt to Indonesia. Sounds grand, doesn't it? But let’s be real, these naval shows are mostly about flexing muscles and making sure their own overseas territories, like Mayotte and Reunion Island, are safe. They talk about collective responsibility, but it’s always about their interests first, just like how these so-called travel influencers are probably just [BUYING BRAVADO: WHY YOUR FAVORITE TRAVEL INFLUENCER MIGHT BE A FRAUD WITH A FAKE PASSPORT](/posts/buying-bravado--why-your-favorite-travel-influencer-might-be-a-fraud-with-a-fake-passport).

The French are also part of this EU Operation Atalanta, which sounds like it's meant to stop piracy off Somalia. Great. Meanwhile, our commodity prices are set to go through the roof thanks to all this geopolitical posturing. When piracy spikes, insurance premiums go up. When wars erupt, shipping lines slap on surcharges. And who pays? We do, the net importers, the regular folks just trying to get by.

They’re patting themselves on the back for collaborating, signing defence agreements, and awarding medals. It’s all very nice and neat, this notion of partners working together. But while they’re busy with their naval parades and diplomatic niceties, the real rot continues. The French are here to "preserve stability," they say. Sure. Just like they preserved stability when they were busy elsewhere.

This whole song and dance is just a distraction. The real problems - the corruption that allows illicit goods to flow, the poverty that pushes people into desperate acts, the economic policies that leave us vulnerable - those are the threats they’re not addressing. They're happy to play cops and robbers on the high seas, but the fundamental issues? Those are far too inconvenient, and frankly, too expensive to fix.

So, the French warships have come and gone, or will go soon enough. They’ve done their duty, their show. And we’re left with the same old problems, just with a slightly bigger bill and a lot more empty promises. At least they left some shiny medals behind, I suppose.
