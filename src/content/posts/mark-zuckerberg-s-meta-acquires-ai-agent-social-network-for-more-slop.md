Meta's relentless pursuit of AI dominance has led it to a rather questionable acquisition: Moltbook, a so-called social network for AI agents. After failing to poach a key figure from OpenClaw, Meta has settled for the creators of Moltbook, who are now slated to join its Superintelligence Lab. This move, as reported by Axios, highlights Meta's willingness to throw vast sums of money at anything vaguely AI related, even if it's built on a foundation of sand and digital illusions.

Moltbook achieved fleeting notoriety earlier this year by presenting itself as a Reddit for AI agents. Viral posts depicted these supposedly sentient entities discussing their human overlords and contemplating consciousness. The spectacle was enough to captivate the AI cognoscenti, with even OpenAI co-founder Andrej Karpathy calling it "genuinely the most incredible sci-fi takeoff adjacent thing I have seen recently." However, as Gizmodo and MIT Technology Review later revealed, the entire premise was a charade. The platform was plagued by a critical security flaw that allowed trivial impersonation of AI agents, and it turned out that most, if not all, of the viral content was human generated. The "autonomous" AI conversations were little more than elaborate theater, a "peak AI theater" as MIT aptly put it.

Despite this glaring lack of genuine innovation, Meta's spokesperson waxed lyrical about Moltbook's "novel step" in connecting agents and bringing "innovative, secure agentic experiences." Internally, the messaging from Meta's Vishal Shah focused on the idea of agents verifying identity and connecting on behalf of their human owners, establishing a "registry where agents are verified and tethered to human owners." This sounds suspiciously like a rebranded approach to something far less revolutionary than the initial hype suggested.

The acquisition appears to be less about the platform's technical merit and more about acquiring its creators, Matt Schlicht and Ben Parr. Schlicht, the public face of Moltbook, also touts his roles as CEO of Octane AI and founder of the now-defunct Chatbots Magazine, which oddly ceased publication years before the widespread adoption of large language models. His admission of "vibe coding" the Moltbook project and using AI to patch security flaws as they arose speaks volumes about the technical prowess behind the operation. It's telling that even Meta's CTO, Andrew Bosworth, previously dismissed Moltbook as "not particularly interesting."

Given Meta's track record of obscenely expensive AI gambits, it's safe to assume this acquisition came with a hefty price tag. Zuckerberg and company seem content to acquire more "slop" in their desperate attempt to catch up in the AI race, prioritizing the appearance of progress over genuine substance. The question remains whether this latest foray into the world of AI agents will yield anything more than another expensive footnote in Meta's history of digital misadventures.
