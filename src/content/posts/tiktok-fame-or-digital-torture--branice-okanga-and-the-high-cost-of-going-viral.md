Welcome to the world of content creation, where the ring lights are bright but the comments are darker than a blackout in Vihiga. Branice Okanga learned the hard way that the internet is not a fan club; it is a gladiator pit. She stepped onto TikTok and Facebook looking for a career, only to be met with a barrage of height-related insults that would make even the toughest skin crawl. It is the classic influencer trap: you show up for the clout, but you stay because you are being stalked by strangers who think your physical appearance is public property.

Now, the Kenya Police Service is trying to play knight in shining armor with their new "Policare" initiative. Inspector General Douglas Kanja and his team have launched a module to tackle technology-facilitated gender-based violence. It is all very professional-sounding, with "occupational standards" and "trainer guides." But let us be real for a second. While the police are busy attending workshops, women like Branice are still being shredded in the comments section. It is a lot of bureaucratic talk for a problem that moves at the speed of a fiber optic connection.

This digital wasteland is the same place where a certain Russian national reportedly decided Kenyan women were just unconsented co-stars in his private recordings. It is a disgusting cycle of exploitation that feels uncomfortably close to the [Insecure Moms, Arrested Princes, and the Never-Ending Cycle of Tyler Perry’s Trauma Porn](/posts/insecure-moms--arrested-princes--and-the-never-ending-cycle-of-tyler-perry-s-trauma-porn) we usually see on the big screen. We are obsessed with watching people suffer for our entertainment, whether it is through scripted "trauma porn" or the real-life harassment of a girl trying to make a living on TikTok.

The government claims they are activating "cybercrime units" and working with international partners to catch this Russian suspect. They talk about "digital footprints" and "constitutional protections" like they are reading from a script. It is easy to issue warnings against sharing videos, but it is much harder to actually protect women in a space where the "block" button is the only real weapon they have. The hypocrisy is loud: we encourage everyone to "create content," but we offer almost zero protection when that content turns into a target.

Branice is telling other women not to let the bullies derail their dreams, which is a lovely sentiment for a motivational poster. But in the real world, "digital scars" are permanent. Until the police start treating a troll like a real-life criminal instead of a tech-support ticket, the glamorous life of an influencer will continue to be a nightmare disguised as a 15-second clip. Stay safe out there, because the internet is feral, and the police are still trying to figure out how to use the "delete" key.
