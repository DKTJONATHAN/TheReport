Ruto's Nairobi 'Different By December' Promise: More Roads, Lights, and Hopefully Less Rubbish?

A presidential declaration of a new Nairobi by December, complete with roads, lights, and garbage trucks, meets the usual dose of Kenyan skepticism. While infrastructure projects are touted, the reality on the ground, as always, will tell the true story.

The President's Sunday sermon in Karen was packed with grand pronouncements about transforming Nairobi. We're talking 250km of roads - some already happening, some "soon" - plus 40,000 lights to make this a proper 24-hour city. And the garbage? Oh yes, 40 new trucks are supposedly the start of a 250-truck army that will make Nairobi "garbage-free" by December. One can only hope this isn't another elaborate mirage.

On the health front, the Social Health Authority (SHA) is apparently doling out KSh13 billion for March 2026 services, a sum that dwarfs the old NHIF's six-month collection. The President claims this digitized, efficient system is a "blow to those who were used to manipulating the inefficiencies" of the past. It's a nice story, but the proof will be in how many Kenyans actually feel the difference when they need medical care, not just in government press releases. This whole SHA drama feels suspiciously like a repackaged solution to problems that have plagued us for years, reminding me of how even past "friendly fire" in politics often just means more of the same eating.

Then there's the fuel price saga. The explanation for why our petrol costs an arm and a leg? Kenya's massive road network - a staggering 20,000km, apparently bigger than all other East African countries combined, and we're building more. We're a middle-income country, you see, so we should expect to pay more, like other middle-income countries. It’s a logical leap that only makes sense when you're the one making the policy, not the one filling the tank.

And for those who dare to grumble, well, you're just "naysayers" who have lost out because of the "rot" in the old NHIF or are benefiting from inefficiencies. The President’s justification for high fuel prices, comparing them to London's exorbitant rates, feels like a classic deflection. Meanwhile, development projects in far-flung Mandera are paraded as proof that no part of the country is being left behind. We'll see if these promises translate into tangible improvements beyond the occasional presidential visit and photo op.







The President has declared that Nairobi will be a "different" city by December, a bold claim built on the promise of 250km of new and upgraded roads, the installation of 40,000 lights, and a significant boost to garbage collection with 40 new trucks already deployed, aiming for a total of 250. This ambitious infrastructure overhaul is presented as essential for a 24-hour economy, a narrative delivered from the pulpit at the African Gospel Church in Karen.

In parallel, the health sector is undergoing a supposed revolution. The Social Health Authority (SHA) is set to pay KSh13 billion for services rendered in March 2026, a figure the President highlights as vastly exceeding the collections of the defunct NHIF. He emphasizes the digitization of health processes and prompt payments, framing it as a decisive blow to those who profited from the old system's inefficiencies. This, he argues, is the tangible realization of universal healthcare, a goal long discussed but never truly achieved until now.

The explanation for Kenya's high fuel prices is predictably complex, with the President citing the nation's extensive road network, the largest in East Africa, as a primary driver. He positions Kenya as a middle-income economy aspiring to first-world status, suggesting that comparisons should be made with similar economies, not necessarily its less developed neighbors. This rationale, coupled with a reminder about the VAT reduction, aims to contextualize the rising costs within a broader economic and global framework.

The narrative of progress extends beyond the capital, with the President highlighting development in remote areas like Banisa Constituency in Mandera, where a modern market is being constructed. This is presented as evidence that no part of the country will be left behind, countering any perception of regional neglect. The government's commitment, he suggests, is to a holistic national transformation, a vision that extends beyond immediate political cycles.

However, for those accustomed to the perennial cycles of grand pronouncements and incomplete projects in Nairobi, the skepticism is palpable. While the infrastructure projects and health reforms are presented as game-changers, the true test will be in their execution and the tangible impact on the daily lives of ordinary Kenyans. The history of development promises in this city is long and often unfulfilled, making December's transformation a target many will watch with a critical eye, especially considering how past promises have often been overshadowed by deeper issues like those explored in articles concerning political maneuvering and resource allocation.

Even as allies like Senate Majority Leader Aaron Cheruiyot laud SHA's swift payments and National Assembly Majority Leader Kimani Ichung'wa defends the government's policies, the underlying sentiment is that these pronouncements often mask persistent challenges. The comparison of fuel prices to London, while an attempt to provide perspective, also highlights the steep cost of living that many Kenyans are struggling to bear. The success of these initiatives will ultimately be measured not by the speeches delivered, but by the lasting changes experienced on the ground.
