Analytical and performance metrics mentioned in standard consent forms are merely euphemisms for behavior modification and mass observation. In the context of the Kenya-Mozambique security pact, "analytical" data becomes a potential tool for state-level surveillance. The bounce rate of a webpage is a trivial concern compared to the tracking of a merchant or a citizen across newly integrated digital corridors. These agreements facilitate the flow of data just as much as the flow of goods, creating a seamless net for those who hold the keys to the servers.

Advertisement cookies are the most honest of the bunch because they represent the naked commodification of the human experience. As developing nations tighten their economic ties to join the global marketplace, they inadvertently hand over the psychological profiles of their citizens to the highest bidder. It is a trade-off that is rarely discussed in the diplomatic halls of Maputo or Nairobi. The promise of economic growth is tethered to a system that views human beings as nothing more than metrics to be harvested and sold to the global advertising machine.

Privacy is becoming a luxury for those who can afford to go offline, a choice that is increasingly impossible in a connected world. For regions trying to bootstrap their way into the global economy through bilateral agreements, the cookie is a permanent digital leash. The "consent" we are asked to provide is a ritual of submission to a system that prioritizes the efficiency of the machine over the dignity of the individual. In the end, the only thing truly "Necessary" is the continued extraction of value from every corner of the planet.
