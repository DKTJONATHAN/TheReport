It is raining in Nairobi, and surprise, surprise - the city has once again transformed into a giant, un-chlorinated swimming pool that nobody asked for. While South B residents like Roy Kabugi are basically playing "Survivor" in their own living rooms, our favorite "Main Character" influencers are safely tucked away in Kilimani, sipping lattes and pretending the world is not ending. It is the same old story: if you have the coins to hire a private handyman to clear your pipes, you are dry; if not, you are wading through a cocktail of rainwater and broken promises. The "aesthetic" rain videos on TikTok are a slap in the face to anyone currently scaling a wall in South C just to reach a boda boda.

I saw Anthony Mwangi, a mechanic, trying to go live on social media to beg for help because he has not worked in three days. Honey, the government is not watching your IG Live unless you are twerking or launching a fake foundation for "visibility." It is peak irony that we live in a digital age where everyone wants to be a "content creator," but the only content we are getting from the streets is a horror movie. While some are trying to save their welding equipment from literal explosions, others are busy polishing their [Billionaire Blues and Blind Ambition: The Ruparelia PR Machine Hits Bukedea](/posts/billionaire-blues-and-blind-ambition--the-ruparelia-pr-machine-hits-bukedea) to make sure their "brand" stays as dry as their sense of humor.

Let us talk about the "reactive" cleanup efforts that are about as effective as a celebrity apology after a cheating scandal. The County teams show up, look at the mess, take a few photos for their "work done" reports, and then leave the heaps of trash right next to the drains. It is giving "minimal effort" energy. They do the bare minimum to look busy for shows like AM Live or Fixing the Nation, but as soon as the cameras stop rolling, the debris just sits there, waiting for the next ten-minute shower to clog everything back up. It is a toxic cycle, and honestly, the residents of Mariguini are just extras in a badly produced reality show.

The divide in this city is more obvious than a bad lace front on a windy day. In South C, mothers are out here doing parkour with babies on their backs to avoid the "foul-smelling streams" caused by burst sewers. Meanwhile, the elite are probably discussing "sustainable living" on a podcast while their private drainage systems hum quietly in the background. If you are not in the "Premium Content" bracket of life, you are basically on your own. Even the Met Department’s warnings feel like a spoiler alert for a movie we have already seen ten times and hated every single time.

At the end of the day, whether you are listening to The Trend or scrolling through the latest "woe-is-me" post on Gumzo La Sato, the reality remains: Nairobi is a mess. We can talk about "thought leadership" on Business Redefined all we want, but until the drainage in South B actually works, it is all just hot air and murky water. Stay dry, if you can afford it, and if you can’t, well, I guess you better start practicing your "resilience" face for the next viral video of you losing your mattress to the Nairobi River. The hypocrisy is leaking, and it smells worse than the Jogoo Road traffic jams.
