Welcome to 2026, where the bar for entertainment in Kenya hasn't just been lowered; it's been buried in a shallow grave. Christopher Mosioma, the man we unfortunately know as Embarambamba, is back in the headlines for doing exactly what we expected: something dangerous and entirely unnecessary. If you thought the mud-rolling was peak dysfunction, the viral clip from his latest street show will remind you that there is no floor to this man's desperation.

This time, the "gospel" artist decided that the best way to spread the word was by grabbing a person dressed as an elderly woman, spinning her around like a sack of maize, and slamming her into the pavement before landing on top of her. We are told it is "art," but it looks more like a physical assault caught on a smartphone. The crowd watched, the cameras rolled, and another piece of our collective dignity evaporated into the Nairobi smog.

It is the same old script. The Kenya Film Classification Board bans his songs for indecency, the public feigns outrage on Twitter, and yet, the cycle continues. We shouldn't be surprised that our local scene is a mess when [The Global Order Is Dying and We Are Paying for the Funeral](/posts/the-global-order-is-dying-and-we-are-paying-for-the-funeral) in every other sector of life. In a world that rewards the loudest and most unhinged, a man throwing "grandmothers" onto the dirt is just another Monday morning in a country that has lost its sense of shame.

Fans and critics keep asking him to tone it down, which is like asking a bonfire to stop being hot. Embarambamba does not want to "tone it down" because that does not buy data bundles or pay the rent. He has built a career on being the human embodiment of a train wreck, and as long as we keep clicking the links and sharing the videos, he will keep jumping on moving cars and tossing people around like ragdolls.

Let's stop calling this gospel. There is nothing spiritual about potentially breaking an elderly woman's ribs for a few thousand likes. It is a performance of pure, unadulterated madness fueled by a digital economy that thrives on the grotesque. But hey, this is Kenya. We will complain today, share the memes tomorrow, and wait for the next stunt while pretending we actually care about the standards we claim to uphold.
