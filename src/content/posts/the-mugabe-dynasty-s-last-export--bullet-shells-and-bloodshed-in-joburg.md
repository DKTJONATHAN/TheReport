It seems the fruit does not just fall far from the tree; it rolls straight into a South African courtroom. Bellarmine Mugabe, the 28-year-old baby of Zimbabwe's late strongman, is currently finding out that an upmarket Johannesburg zip code and a bodyguard are not enough to hide a smoking gun. He has been slapped with attempted murder charges after a 23-year-old ended up in critical condition with a bullet in him. Searching the property during their investigation, police have found bullet cartridges but no firearm so far, leading to extra charges for defeating the ends of justice. It is the same old story of the pampered elite playing God with other people's lives while the rest of us watch from the sidelines.

While Bellarmine waits for his March 3 bail hearing, the rest of the region is stuck in the same mud. In Nairobi, KeNHA has given traders on Thika Superhighway seven days to pack their lives and leave the road reserves. They call it "safety upgrades" and "easing congestion," but we all know it is just the annual ritual of the government reminding the poor that their livelihoods are temporary. Whether it is a dictator's son in a Joburg cell or a mama mboga on the Thika Road kerb, the system only cares about you if you have the right papers or a father who ruled for 37 years.

The irony is thick enough to choke on. In Westlands, fifty makeshift houses in Kibagare slums collapsed because of heavy rain, leaving two people injured and many more homeless. Police say they have "launched investigations," which is government-speak for doing absolutely nothing while the cameras are off. It is the same brand of performative concern we see everywhere, much like the [Same Wolves, Different Sheepskin](/posts/riggy-g-s-new-gospel--same-wolves--different-sheepskin) narrative that dominates our local politics. One man's tragedy in a slum is just another Tuesday, while a rich boy's court case is a regional event.

Even the "security" situation is a joke. Three police officers were hit by an improvised explosive device in Garissa while NACADA is busy tweeting about illegal alcohol on its X account. We have real threats on the borders and real blood in the suburbs, yet the authorities are obsessed with social media optics. EACC claims to have recovered Sh281 million worth of public land in Mombasa, but for every acre they "recover," ten more are swallowed by the well-connected before the ink on the court ruling even dries. It is a cycle of greed that no amount of legal jargon can fix.

As the Kenya Meteorological Department warns us about flash floods, we see the same bridges being washed away because the protection was probably made of hope and stolen cement. According to KeNHA, the rising waters already cut off a footbridge after washing away the gabions. From Bellarmine's missing gun to the rising waters, the script never changes. If you are rich, you get a postponement; if you are poor, you get washed away. Even KUCCPS opening applications for a Diploma in Law feels like a cruel joke-why study the law when the people who run the world treat it like a suggestion?
