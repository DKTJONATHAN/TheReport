SENATE HEALTH COMMITTEE: CONGESTION AND STAFFING GAPS ARE JUST ANOTHER DAY IN THE RIFT VALLEY
WARNING: If you were hoping for some good news from our esteemed Senators' latest jaunt, keep dreaming. The Senate Health Committee has once again stumbled upon the bleeding obvious: public hospitals are overflowing, short-staffed, and generally falling apart. It's the same tired script, just a new set of counties.

Our honourable members, led by Jackson Mandago and his merry band of senators like Richard Onyonka and Samson Cherargei, have graced Nakuru, Baringo, Elgeyo-Marakwet, and Nandi with their presence. And what did they find? Shocking revelations of wards packed tighter than a matatu during rush hour, queues longer than a politician’s promise, and doctors desperately trying to triage a thousand patients with two hands. It’s not a healthcare crisis; it’s just Tuesday.

The committee’s official pronouncements, bless their X-posts, lament "severe hospital congestion due to staff shortages" and call for "urgent recruitment and improved equipment." Groundbreaking stuff. It's almost as if they forgot they had the exact same conversation after visiting Bungoma and Kakamega last year. Perhaps they should consider digitising their own committee notes; maybe then they’d remember they've seen this movie before. And let’s not even start on the "shortages of essential medicines," "sanitation gaps," and the fact that some counties apparently pull out the temporary shine just for the inspection. It’s like watching Nairobi's [Rising Towers, Human Stories](/posts/nairobi-s-rising-towers--human-stories) - all glitz on the surface, rot underneath.

Then there's the usual song and dance about the Facility Improvement Fund being underutilised and debts owed to KEMSA. Apparently, our hospitals can't even get basic supplies because someone forgot to pay the bill. And the brilliant suggestion of solar power? Wonderful. Let's solve systemic failures with a few solar panels. Meanwhile, the senators are busy compiling a report that will undoubtedly gather dust, promising "recruitment," "upgrading," and "strengthened financing." It’s the Kenyan way: identify the problem, talk about it extensively, and then… well, then we wait for the next committee to discover the same thing.

This whole charade, from Nakuru Level 5 Hospital's daily chaos to the sub-county centres struggling to stay afloat, is just another Tuesday for the taxpayer. The senators will go back to Nairobi, write their report, and in a few months, another committee will embark on another "oversight tour" to discover that nothing, absolutely nothing, has changed. They’ll find more congestion, more shortages, and more excuses. It’s a beautiful, predictable cycle of incompetence.

They even found "gaps in child immunisation programmes." Imagine that. Kids aren't getting their shots because the system is too broken, and the people tasked with fixing it are busy inspecting what they already know is broken. It’s almost enough to make you want to become a senator, just to experience the thrilling world of pointing out the obvious.
