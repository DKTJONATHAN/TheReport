HBO Max is clearly running out of original ideas if they are digging through 1980s Brazilian archives to find something to keep their subscribers from hitting that cancel button. Enter Madam Beja, the new period drama that has the internet’s "gays, girls, and theys" absolutely foaming at the mouth. It is the same old story - a woman from the 19th century "challenging social norms" while looking remarkably well-groomed for someone living in a time before indoor plumbing and decent hair spray.

The "thirst meltdown" over this show is honestly exhausting to witness. We get it, the cast is attractive and the lighting is moody, but let us not pretend this is Shakespeare. The show is essentially a high-budget soap opera dressed up in lace and historical footnotes. It is funny how a few weekly episode drops can make a predictable revenge plot seem like the second coming of prestige TV, but that is the power of a good marketing budget and a thirsty Twitter timeline.

The plot follows Ana Jacinta de Sao Jose, a historical figure whose life was apparently just one long battle against the patriarchy. While the synopsis promises a "complex character" navigating "power and revenge," it mostly feels like a checklist of every period drama cliché we have seen since Bridgerton first ruined our expectations of historical accuracy. It is all about the "independent place" she holds in society, which is just code for "she does what she wants and looks good doing it."

Watching these fictional characters fight over status and desire feels like a distraction from the real-world messes we see every day. The drama on screen is choreographed and clean, unlike [The Broad-Based Buffet](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-enemies-share-a-plate-while-you-starve) where real-life enemies share a plate while the rest of us starve for some actual substance. At least in the telenovela world, the villains have the decency to wear a sneer; in the real world, the hypocrisy is much harder to spot through the PR-polished smiles.

At the end of the day, Madam Beja is just another piece of content designed to be consumed and discarded once the next "meltdown" arrives. HBO Max knows exactly what they are doing by feeding the masses five episodes at a time. It is not about the art of the 19th-century struggle; it is about keeping you glued to the screen until the next shiny thing comes along. Enjoy the corsets while they last, because the hype will be dead by the time the finale drops.
