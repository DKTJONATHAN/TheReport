Kenya Ports Authority Explains Role in Substandard Fuel Import Scandal - Or Dodges It, More Like

Nairobi's usual soundtrack of traffic and corruption is getting louder. Now, KPA's out here "clarifying" how they handle fuel imports at Mombasa, as if the Ksh4.8 billion scandal wasn't already screaming volumes. According to their letter to the Senate, their job is done the moment a tanker docks. After that? It's "Kenya Pipeline Company's" problem. Classic deflection, isn't it? They're so clean, they wash their hands of it before the dirty work even begins.

MD William Ruto spilled some tea, or rather, some fuel details about MT Paloma. Arrived, docked, offloaded, gone. All by the book, apparently. But here's the kicker: KPA claims they have no idea what happens after berthing. "Not privy to all subsequent operations or actions," they say. So, when senators like Veronica Maina start asking about substandard fuel and why standards are being waived, KPA just shrugs. It's like complaining about a leaky roof and the builder saying, "Well, I built the walls perfectly."

Meanwhile, the likes of Senator Ledama Ole Kina are smelling a rat, or perhaps a whole fuel cartel. He's seen correspondence that screams daylight robbery, with Oryx Energy Ltd and Ministry of Energy officials apparently agreeing to import fuel at a price that makes our government's own deals look like charity. USD 253.94 per MT versus USD 84.00 per MT? Someone's making a killing, and it's definitely not the average Kenyan filling their tank. It’s a "fuel cabal," he says. And frankly, who are we to argue when the numbers are this wild? This whole situation feels like another chapter in the "Junk Choppers and Stolen Sheets: The Messy 'Influence' of East African Power Players" saga we've seen before.

The KPA, in their infinite wisdom, brags about efficiency thanks to the Kipevu Oil Terminal II. Nineteen tankers processed between March and April, bringing in PMS, AGO, and Jet A-1. They are so efficient, they can handle multiple tankers at once, cutting down on "demurrage costs." But are they efficient at ensuring the fuel isn't toxic sludge? That's a question for someone else, apparently. Their mandate, they stress, doesn't include "petroleum supply, licensing or quality assurance." Just moving the metal boxes.

So, while KPA meticulously documents berthing times and departure schedules, the real questions about who's approving what, who's getting fat off inflated prices, and why substandard fuel is even a consideration are left hanging in the salty Mombasa air. Senators are asking for clarity on future imports, and KPA General Manager Moses Taiwu reassures them that supply is "stable" and they don't have to face fuel shortages. Easy for him to say when he's not the one potentially driving a death trap with dodgy petrol.

This whole "scandal" is just another Tuesday in Nairobi. The authorities explain away their lack of oversight with bureaucratic jargon, while politicians point fingers and hint at elaborate scams. The only thing certain is that the price at the pump will keep climbing, and we'll all be left wondering where our money, and our trust, has gone.
