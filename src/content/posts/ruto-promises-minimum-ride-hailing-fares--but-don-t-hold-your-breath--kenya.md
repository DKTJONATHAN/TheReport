So, the big man himself, Ruto, has finally decided to grace us with his presence and make some noise about the ride-hailing taxi situation. Apparently, he's heard the drivers crying about fuel prices, which, let's be honest, have been a mess for ages. Now, he's pledging "urgent implementation" of minimum fare regulations. Urgent? We'll see about that. It's the same old song and dance, isn't it?

The plan is for the Ministry of Transport and NTSA to, you know, "engage" with these transport network companies and drivers. Engage. That's a fancy word for "talk a lot and achieve very little." They've been having "long-standing disputes" for years, and suddenly, after a nationwide matatu strike that saw people stranded and fares skyrocket, they're going to solve it all with a few meetings. Right.

Ruto's blaming the global fuel crisis, the Middle East conflict, the Strait of Hormuz - all the usual suspects. He even threw in a figure of Sh28.19 billion spent on "fuel stabilisation measures." That's a lot of billions, but for most of us, the pump prices are still a punch to the gut. And now he's promising an extra Sh10 off diesel. Wonderful. That'll make a huge difference when you're trying to get to work.

This whole charade is happening after those matatus decided enough was enough. They shut down the country for a couple of days, and suddenly, the government remembers transport operators exist. It's a classic case of "kick them when they're down, then offer a tiny crumb when they bite back." It makes you wonder if these promises are just to placate people until the next crisis, kind of like how some leaders offer empty gestures when controversy hits, remember that whole [Kanye West debacle](/posts/kanye-west-offers-empty-gestures-to-uk-jewish-community-amidst-wireless-controversy)?

He claims "no Kenyan should feel abandoned." That's rich. Most Kenyans feel abandoned daily, struggling to keep their heads above water while the cost of everything goes up. This minimum fare talk is supposed to "cushion transport operators," but what about the commuters? Will our fares suddenly become affordable again, or will this just be another way for the platforms to squeeze more out of us while the drivers get a slightly less pathetic cut?

Let's be clear: promises from State House, especially concerning timely action, should be taken with a very large pinch of salt. We've heard it all before. The government's approach to problems often feels like [a mess](/posts/kenya-government-s-russia-recruitment-mess--citizens-fighting-abroad--officials-dodging-blame), with officials dodging blame and citizens left to fend for themselves. We'll believe these minimum fare regulations when we see them actually implemented, and more importantly, when they actually make a difference in our pockets. Until then, it's just more hot air from Mombasa.
