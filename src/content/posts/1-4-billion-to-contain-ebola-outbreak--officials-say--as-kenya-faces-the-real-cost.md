Surprise, surprise. Another African crisis, another demand for billions. Officials from the Africa CDC are now wringing their hands, saying the Ebola outbreak needs $1.4 billion- a neat triple of earlier estimates, and *exactly* what the White House conveniently requested from Congress. Funny how these numbers always align so perfectly. Director-General Jean Kaseya declares, "If we don't have this $1.4 billion- and if we don't resolve the humanitarian issue, we will not stop this outbreak." As if the previous $518 million vanished into thin air, and a bigger check is the magic cure-all.

But while they talk billions, the real cost is paid in blood, right here in Kenya. Remember that bright idea by the Trump administration to dump their Ebola-exposed citizens in Nanyuki? A "quarantine and treatment center" for Americans, conveniently far from the US, "without the risks of a lengthy transport back to the US." Never mind that locals protested, two people died when police reportedly opened fire, and our own High Court tried to stop it. President Ruto, bless his heart, called it a "broader national preparedness system." For whom, exactly? Our own doctors union pointed out there are "no plans for Kenyans who get infected by Ebola." So much for "preparedness" when it is just offloading foreign problems onto our crowded doorstep. This is the same government that often finds itself in the thick of complex international dealings, sometimes even becoming the ultimate hideout for those causing [Passport to Chaos: Why Nairobi is the Ultimate Hideout for Problematic Paramilitary Princes](/posts/passport-to-chaos--why-nairobi-is-the-ultimate-hideout-for-problematic-paramilitary-princes) elsewhere, yet cannot protect its own citizens from foreign policy fallout right at home.

Meanwhile, in the actual epicenter, the Democratic Republic of Congo, it is business as usual: chaos. Dr. Marie-Roseline Belizaire admits "we are missing cases," because who cares about tracking when militias are attacking treatment centers, burning tents, and trying to snatch bodies? WHO Director-General Tedros Ghebreyesus, ever the optimist, talks about drawing "lessons learned" from previous outbreaks and "strengthening Africa’s capacity." Yet, he also warns the DRC is facing a "catastrophic collision of disease and conflict" and pleads for ceasefires. Sounds like the "lessons learned" have not stuck, and "capacity" means nothing when bombs are falling and trust is nonexistent.

So, while researchers are "months away" from new vaccines and Dr. Mehmet Oz promises "testing tools and mechanisms" at airports for the World Cup - a little too late, wouldn't you say? - the cycle continues. More suspected cases, more deaths, more warnings that the "epidemic is outpacing us." They will throw another $1.4 billion at it, make grand statements, and the headlines will change, but the core issues - conflict, distrust, and treating African nations as convenient dumping grounds for foreign problems - will remain. It is the same old song, just with a higher price tag this time.
