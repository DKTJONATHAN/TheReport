Zakayo is at it again, promising us heaven while we are still struggling with the basics. President Ruto wants to reopen the border with Somalia by April, acting like the last decade of blood and dust was just a minor misunderstanding. We have been here before. In May last year, they said the same thing, only to backpedal two months later when Al-Shabab reminded everyone who really controls the frontier. It is the [same script, different date: Nairobi’s annual near-miss with disaster](/posts/same-script--different-date--nairobi-s-annual-near-miss-with-disaster) where the government makes a grand announcement and we wait for the inevitable U-turn.

The 680-kilometer frontier is not just a line on a map; it is a graveyard for failed policies and a playground for insurgents. They talk about "multi-agency security teams" and doubling police deployment as if more boots on the ground magically erases the threat of IEDs. Meanwhile, the Indian Ocean is still being fought over for oil and gas that none of us will ever see the benefits of. If you think a few extra checkpoints will stop the al-Qaeda-affiliated group from crossing over, you probably still believe the price of unga is coming down next week.

Then we have the "economic analysts" and traders who are high on hope. Sure, trade is great, but how do you move goods on roads that are basically dirt tracks infested with bandits? Somalia joining the East African Community is just another way for politicians to sign papers in fancy hotels while the actual logistics remain a nightmare. Being a "gateway" sounds nice on a PowerPoint slide, but in reality, it is just opening the door to more complications we are not ready to handle.

And do not get me started on the tourists. Seeing Westerners pose in front of the Mogadishu Cathedral ruins is the kind of irony that only someone with a foreign passport can afford. They call it "adventure" and praise the "big hearts" of the locals while staying in hotels protected by private militias. It is easy to enjoy a boat ride at Liido Beach when you have an exit strategy. For the rest of us, the "Pearl of the Indian Ocean" is still a place where a truck bomb can end your afternoon coffee.

At the end of the day, this push for "connectivity" is just another distraction. Whether it is online visas or EAC passports, the reality on the ground does not change with a new travel document. We are being told that the security concern is "not that much" compared to fifteen years ago, but anyone living near Mandera or Garissa knows that is a lie told to keep the investors happy. We will open the border, something will go wrong, and then we will close it again. Rinse and repeat.
