Samsung, a company that once prided itself on setting trends, has seemingly abandoned innovation for imitation. Recent leaks suggest their upcoming "Galaxy Glasses" are a blatant rip-off of Meta's Ray-Ban AI glasses. This move, devoid of any original spark, highlights Samsung's current creative bankruptcy, especially with Apple yet to release its own smart glasses for them to clone.

The initial "Jinju" model mirrors Meta's current offering, featuring dual 12-megapixel cameras and integrated speakers and microphones, allegedly designed to interact with Google's Gemini AI. The leak, corroborated by mentions in One UI 9 source code, points to an imminent release, though functionality beyond basic photo and video capture, and AI interaction, remains conspicuously absent. Touch controls and truly revolutionary AI integrations appear to be non-existent, indicating a superficial attempt to compete.

A second iteration, codenamed "Haean" and slated for 2027, might finally incorporate a micro LED display, a feature that Meta already offers. This timeline suggests Samsung is perpetually playing catch-up in the smart glasses arena.

Samsung isn't the only one content with this derivative approach. Google is also developing its own Android-centric smart glasses, Project Aura, in collaboration with Xreal, and has other XR glasses in the pipeline. The rush to integrate Gemini AI into these devices is palpable, with Google's upcoming I/O developer conference likely to reveal more about their plans.

The strategy of partnering with established eyewear brands, seen with Samsung's ties to Gentle Monster and Google's to Warby Parker and potentially Gucci, mirrors Meta's approach. This is a clear attempt to lend credibility and fashion appeal to devices that fundamentally lack original appeal, a tactic less accessible to Chinese competitors like Huawei.

The specter of privacy concerns, a persistent issue with smart glasses, looms large. Meta has already faced significant backlash for its push towards facial recognition technology. Google Glass's original "Glasshole" notoriety may be dwarfed by future iterations, especially with the ever-expanding data collection capabilities of AI like Gemini. The company's track record suggests that user data, including texts, is fair game, making any claims of privacy improvements from Samsung or Google highly suspect. The market is saturated with copies, and the underlying ethical questions remain largely unaddressed.
