PARLIAMENT CLEARS SAFARICOM STAKE SALE WITH SIX CONDITIONS - MORE LIKE SIX HANDCUFFS

The government’s grand plan to flog off a chunk of Safaricom to Vodacom has finally gotten the nod from Parliament. Big deal, right? They're calling it the largest divestiture since independence. Translation: they’re selling off a piece of the national pie, and we’re supposed to cheer. They’ve even managed to spin a KSh 40.2 billion upfront payment, which sounds like a win, but let’s be real, it’s just a clever accounting trick to mask the fact that we’re still on the hook for KSh 55 billion. Net present value advantage? More like a net present value headache.

At least they claim they got a decent price. KSh 34.00 per share, a premium of 17 to 19% above the average. Sounds good on paper, but who are we kidding? When it comes to our precious Safaricom, which practically runs the country through M-Pesa - processing transactions four times Kenya’s GDP in 2025 - this is less about a shrewd business deal and more about appeasing the hungry masses with a few extra shillings while the real money changes hands. The "conditionality" they're so worried about is the only real power we have left.

And those six binding conditions Parliament attached? They’re not there to protect us, they’re there to make the deal look less like a fire sale. Extending business model protection to a decade and removing the redundancy time limit sounds great, but it’s just lipstick on a pig. It's a desperate attempt to show they're looking out for the little guy, when in reality, this entire transaction is about securing funds for a new National Infrastructure Fund. You know, another government pot of money that will probably disappear faster than our hopes for a better future.

The real risk, as they so delicately put it, lies in the "conditionality." But what’s the point of conditions when the core asset, M-Pesa, is essentially a national utility? This entire deal hinges on regulatory approvals from four different agencies by April 1st. If they don't get it, all this talk about premiums and net advantages is just hot air. It’s a race against the clock to secure USD 1.88 billion, and you can bet your last shilling, they’ll bend over backwards to make it happen, conditions be damned. It reminds me of how even the tech giants are pivoting their strategies, like when Alphabet started looking at long-term investments instead of just short-term gains. [Tech Titans Pivot: AI Infrastructure Overtakes Stock Buybacks in Strategic Realignment](/posts/tech-titans-pivot--ai-infrastructure-overtakes-stock-buybacks-in-strategic-realignment).

Parliament might have given their blessing, but let’s not pretend this is a victory for the common Kenyan. It’s a government divesting itself of a golden goose, supposedly for our own good, while setting up a new fund that, let’s face it, will be another playground for the politically connected. The real concern here isn't undervaluation; it's the sheer audacity of selling off a critical piece of our digital infrastructure without a second thought.

This whole KSh 40.2 billion upfront payment is just a way to make it look like they’re getting ahead, while the repayment and the subsequent management of these funds will likely be another saga of mismanagement and questionable decisions. We’re pawning off a sure thing for a promissory note and a fancy new fund. Just another day in Nairobi.
