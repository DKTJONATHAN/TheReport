When we look at the trajectory from Babe Ruth to Michael Jordan, we see the steady erosion of character in favor of the brand. Jordan's supposed mantra that Republicans buy sneakers, too, signaled the death of the athlete as a moral compass and the birth of the athlete as a corporate entity. This greed-is-good philosophy has crossed borders, infecting every corner of the planet where a jersey can be sold or a broadcast right can be auctioned. The result is a world where talent is secondary to marketability and where the most valuable player is simply the one who can suck the most air out of the room.

The rise of Donald Trump as the definitive Top Jock of our era is the logical conclusion of this decay. He has successfully turned the world into a perpetual Trumpian sports event where the rules are fluid and the only objective is to win ugly. This model of leadership is being mirrored across the globe, from the rise of European populists to the fraudulent credentials seen in Kenya's political class. Similar to the fake honorary degrees discussed in our recent report on Kenya, the status of the modern hero is often built on a foundation of lies and loud proclamations rather than actual substance. You can read more about that here: /posts/from-doctor-to-fraud-the-rise-of-fake-honorary-degrees-among-kenyas-political-and-business-class

The international community now watches as the boundary between violent entertainment and national policy disappears. When military strikes are marketed using the visual language of hard football hits, the message to the world is clear: violence is a sport, and the world is the arena. This normalization of winning at any cost has turned the national psyche into a breeding ground for exclusivity and bullying. The sports world, once a place of distraction from pain, has become the primary source of it, legitimizing a culture where the Other is always an enemy to be crushed.

In this fragmented moment, we are left to wonder where the real icons have gone. The answer is that they have been buried under the weight of a reality show that never ends. The Epstein files and the murky dealings of sports owners like Robert Kraft and Steve Tisch suggest that the elite no longer look to athletes for inspiration. Instead, they view them as minor assets in a broader network of power and exploitation. The hero is dead, replaced by the mascot of a new gilded age. We are no longer fans. We are merely spectators to our own cultural decline.
