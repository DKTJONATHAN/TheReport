So, the world's apparently gone travel-mad, which is *thrilling* if you're a legitimate travel agency. But for the rest of us, it means more opportunities for grifters to hawk their bogus "dream vacations." According to these stuffy reports, a whopping 13 million people got duped in 2025 alone, coughing up over $13 BILLION. Yeah, BILLION. You think your favorite pop star’s spending habits are bad? Try losing your life savings on a fake trip to Bali. It's almost as disappointing as discovering your favorite influencer’s "authentic" travel snaps are actually just stock photos.

These smooth operators, who clearly missed the memo on ethics and professionalism, are flooding our feeds with too-good-to-be-true deals. Think ridiculously cheap flights and holiday packages that sound like a fever dream. But surprise, surprise! Once the money’s wired, the tickets vanish, the hotels are imaginary, and poof! The "agent" is gone, probably sipping cocktails on a beach funded by your misery. And the worst part? Most victims find out just days before their supposed departure, turning what should be a joyous escape into a full-blown meltdown. Honestly, it’s almost as bad as those corporate types who talk about "synergy" while secretly planning their next expense account binge - a tale I’ve explored before in [The CEO Wish List: How to Waste Paper in 2026](/posts/the-ceo-wish-list--how-to-waste-paper-in-2026).

With Easter looming, you *know* these fraudsters are rubbing their grubby hands together, preying on our desperate need for a break. They’re banking on you being too eager to snag a last-minute bargain, too blinded by visions of sandy beaches to see the giant red flags waving in your face. The Kenya Association of Travel Agents (KATA) is out here trying to sound the alarm, begging people to use "accredited" agents. Apparently, these "certified" folks actually *do* things like issue tickets and book real hotels. Groundbreaking, I know.

KATA is even hosting this fancy shindig, KTRIPS 2026, to "address the growing challenge of fraud." Oh, bless their hearts. While they're busy having summits and championing "integrity," the industry is bleeding between $11 and $25 BILLION annually to payment fraud. That’s more money than some small countries have in their entire economy. Phishing, smishing, identity theft - it’s a whole buffet of scams out there. It makes you wonder if they're actually fighting fraud or just trying to look busy while the cash keeps flowing to the bad guys.

Look, if you're planning to jet off for Easter, do yourself a favor and use an agent who isn't trying to sell you a timeshare on the moon. These "certified" folks are supposed to have your back, offer actual advice, and generally not disappear into the digital ether with your hard-earned cash. It's not that complicated, people. Travel should be about making memories, not becoming a cautionary tale in a gossip column. Unless, of course, you *want* to be the next viral story about being tragically scammed. Your funeral.
