Nairobi has become a city of smoke and mirrors where everyone is a "CEO" or a "Global Forex Mentor" the moment they buy a second-hand BMW. Raymond Omosa, or whatever "Prince" title he gave himself, is just the latest performer in this digital circus. Smashed an iPhone 17 Pro Max? Please. We are out here grappling with the price of unga while some boy is throwing a tantrum in Dubai because he "lost" money that probably never existed outside of a demo account. It is the classic script: act rich, act reckless, and wait for the gullible to ask for "mentorship" classes at a fee.

The math does not even add up, but then again, when has logic ever stopped a clout-chaser? He claims the phone is worth a thousand dollars but spent over three hundred thousand shillings on it. That is the kind of financial illiteracy we are supposed to admire? Even the tech world is moving toward efficiency, with [Vertical AI vs ChatGPT: The Best Domain-Specific AI Tools Taking Over in 2026](/posts/vertical-ai-vs-chatgpt-domain-tools-2026) showing us that real intelligence is about precision, not performative destruction. Instead of using technology to actually build something, these kids are using it as a prop for their next viral video.

Let us talk about the "from rags to riches" story he keeps peddling. He says he begged on the streets of Nairobi. Well, newsflash: most of us are still one bad month away from the same fate, but we do not use it as a marketing gimmick. This "beggar to Lamborghini" narrative is the oldest trick in the wash-wash playbook. He wants you to believe that if you just have "the goal," you too can buy a BMW "toy" while waiting for a car that is probably stuck in a fever dream. It is a dangerous fantasy sold to desperate youth who think forex is a magic wand.

The audacity to display multiple phones for "Instagram" versus "business" is peak Nairobi comedy. It is all part of the costume. You need the flashy watch, the Dubai backdrop, and the broken screen to prove you are "too big" for small losses. But behind the smashed glass is a guy who is terrified of becoming irrelevant. In a city where your value is measured by your latest post, smashing a phone is cheaper than actually having a sustainable business. It is not resilience; it is a cry for attention from a generation that has forgotten how to be real.

At the end of the day, the only thing "Kenyan Prince" is trading is your attention. Whether the phone was real or a dummy unit from a back-alley shop in Luthuli Avenue does not matter. What matters is that we are still falling for these low-budget stunts. He says he already replaced it, which is exactly what you say when you need to maintain the illusion of infinite wealth. Do not wait for the Lamborghini; the only thing coming is another "mentorship" link in his bio and a lot of empty pockets for those who follow his lead.
