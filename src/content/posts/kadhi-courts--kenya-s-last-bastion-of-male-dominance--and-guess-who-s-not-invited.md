So, you're a woman with dreams of dispensing justice as a Kadhi in Kenya? Bless your heart. The numbers, as usual, are a cruel joke. Forty Kadhis, and not a single one of them is a woman. And the tea leaves? They're showing the same old dusty picture. Forget about any seismic shifts; this is a position as male dominated as a barbershop talk.

It’s not like there's a divine decree saying only men can wear the Kadhi robes. Even Islamic law scholars, like Fatuma Juma, pointed out in 2021 that this isn't a religious mandate. But tell that to the "decision-makers," who apparently have their ears plugged with solid gold. The Kenya Economic Survey 2026 confirms it: Kadhi courts are the ultimate boys' club. They handle all the juicy bits of personal status, marriage, divorce, and inheritance for Muslims, and apparently, women just don't have the right perspective.

This isn't just about Kadhis, though. It's a Kenyan epidemic. County assembly speakers? Two women out of 47. Clerks? A slightly better six out of 47, up from a measly three last year. County commissioners? Five women. Chiefs? A whopping 12% female representation, and assistant chiefs are a slightly more respectable 19%. It's a pattern so predictable, you could set your watch by it.

The male stranglehold extends everywhere you look: county secretaries, governors, even down to National Assembly members. The Constitution, with its lofty talk of equal treatment and not more than two-thirds of the same gender, feels more like a suggestion than a law. It’s a nice piece of paper, isn't it? Almost as inspiring as reading about how our sovereignty became a side hustle.

The judiciary, at least in the lower courts, offers a sliver of hope. Magistrates are mostly women, a healthy 58.5%. But climb up to the High Court and Court of Appeal, and suddenly it's back to the boys' league, with men making up more than half the judges. It seems the higher you go, the less room there is for women, unless you’re talking about how Chadian troops are replacing Kenyans in Haiti - now that’s a headline that gets some traction.

So, there you have it. The Kadhi courts remain Kenya’s most male-dominated space. It’s a clear testament to a system that talks a good game about equality but keeps its doors firmly shut for women in critical decision-making roles. Keep dreaming, ladies, the real world is still very much under construction.
