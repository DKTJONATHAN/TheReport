In Kisumu, the locals finally decided they’d had enough of the "investigations are ongoing" script. Five suspected robbers met their end via the people’s court, and - surprise, surprise - one was carrying a police ID. It’s funny how a badge used to command respect; now it just serves as a membership card for the most organized gang in the republic. When the public starts lynching the law, you know the social contract hasn't just been broken; it’s been shredded and used as fuel for a roadside bonfire.

Then we have the cold-blooded reality of Likoni, where "Officer" Athman decided a double-tap at close range was the best way to handle Omar. It’s the same lethal energy we saw during the June anti-Finance Bill protests. These guys aren't peacekeeping; they are hunting. Whether it’s Sh150,000 in cash or a life snuffed out in a private home, the price of "order" in this country is becoming far too expensive for the average mwananchi to pay. 

The National Police Service has the audacity to tell us that the pace of investigations depends on "lodging a formal complaint." Right. Because if there's one thing a Kenyan enjoys, it’s walking into a police station to report a policeman to his own desk-mate. It’s like asking a goat to file a formal grievance against a leopard at the leopard’s front office. Unless you have a video that goes viral on X, your "formal complaint" is just more paperwork for them to ignore while they complain about noise pollution at local clubs.

This whole circus reminds me of [THE GAVEL MEETS THE RUNGUS: MARAGA’S REALITY CHECK](/posts/the-gavel-meets-the-rungus--maraga-s-reality-check). We can talk about judicial reforms and "the rule of law" until we are blue in the face, but the reality on the ground remains the rungu and the bullet. While the elite argue about which entertainment joint is too loud, the rest of us are just trying to make sure the officer at the next roadblock isn't eyeing our smartphone as his next side-hustle.
