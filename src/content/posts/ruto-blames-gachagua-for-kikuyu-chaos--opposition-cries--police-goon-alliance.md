President Ruto has once again shown his flair for drama, calling his former deputy Rigathi Gachagua a "political conman" and blaming him for the mess in Kikuyu. Apparently, Gachagua is the mastermind behind the chaos, not the usual political machinations. Ruto, speaking from the comfort of State House, declared that no one would divide Kenyans on his watch, as if he hasn't been busy enough himself with his own brand of unity. It's always the same song and dance: leaders pointing fingers while the rest of us are left to pick up the pieces.

Meanwhile, Gachagua and his crew are crying foul, claiming the government is using "State machinery" and an "alliance" of police and goons to disrupt their rallies. They paint a picture of state-sponsored violence, accusing security forces of being an extension of the ruling party's agenda. It’s a classic case of the pot calling the kettle black, isn't it? They accuse the other side of betraying the people and dividing the country, all while they’re busy playing their own divisive games. It reminds me of [The Broad-Based Buffet: When Enemies Share a Plate While You Starve](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-enemies-share-a-plate-while-you-starve), where everyone claims to be for the people, but it's just a feast for the connected few.

The President, in his infinite wisdom, claims he wants to unite all communities, from the Coast to Western. He’s planning more tours to spread this message of unity, even heading to Kisii County, home of opposition hopeful Fred Matiang'i. It’s a bold move, or perhaps just a strategic one to try and win over more voters. He also lectured about respecting leaders and the patience required to lead, as if his own actions and rhetoric have always been a masterclass in decorum. The hypocrisy is as thick as the Nairobi smog.

Gachagua, unfazed, vows to wait for the ballot in 14 months to remove Ruto, warning against creating a situation where Kenyans remove him "outside the ballot." It's a veiled threat, a classic political maneuver to stir up sentiment and project an image of strength. Matiang'i chips in, calling the Interior Ministry politicized and a waste of resources, arguing that Rigathi Gachagua is hardly a national security threat. He’s right, of course; the real threat is the constant politicking that distracts from actual governance.

The Kajiado County police boss, Alexander Shikondi, denies all allegations, calling them lies and morally wrong. He even questions why no official complaints are filed, suggesting a convenient narrative is being spun. It’s a classic denial from the authorities, deflecting blame and questioning the credibility of the accusers. The truth, as always in this country, is probably somewhere in the murky middle, a tangled mess of political ambition, opportunism, and genuine grievances.
