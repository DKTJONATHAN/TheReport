So, the Inspector General, bless his heart, is waxing poetic about how this new "dedicated police unit" for Del Monte Kenya is "timely" and "strategic." Apparently, our police force, which struggles to keep order in Nairobi's back alleys, is now tasked with ensuring the safe passage of pineapples. Because, you know, the real threat to Kenya's economic growth is someone nicking a Cavendish banana.

"Safeguard key national assets," he says. Del Monte. A "key national asset." Beyond being a global leader in pineapple production and export. You mean the company that probably pays peanuts to its workers and then charges us through the nose for tinned fruit? Yes, that one. IG Kanja might as well be an advertisement for them, talking about "thousands of livelihoods" and "foreign exchange earnings." It’s a good thing they’re also a "major employer," otherwise, the IG might have to admit this is just more corporate welfare disguised as law enforcement.

And let's not forget the other players: G4S Managing Director Lawrence Okelo. G4S. The company that's supposed to provide security, but often feels more like a glorified security theatre. Now they’re part of this grand scheme, no doubt to beef up their own bottom line while the taxpayers foot the bill for a police unit that could probably be better deployed chasing actual criminals. It’s almost as if we’re living in a perpetual sequel to the stories we’ve seen before, like the [Roadside Tyrants: Why the Kenyan Prado is a Rolling Symptom of Our National Decay](/posts/roadside-tyrants--why-the-kenyan-prado-is-a-rolling-symptom-of-our-national-decay).

The IG goes on to say Del Monte's contribution to "food security, and industrial development cannot be overstated." I'm sure the farmers who get squeezed for every cent would agree. It's a beautiful narrative they've spun, isn't it? A powerful corporation, a struggling police force, and a whole lot of public money to make it all look good. Just another Tuesday in Nairobi.
