Forget "unexpected closeness." When Nairobi leaders cozy up to a junta, you know it's not about good governance. Colonel Michael Randrianirina, the man who grabbed power in Madagascar, was busy at the Africa-France summit, shaking hands with heads of state. Meanwhile, his country remains rightly suspended from the African Union. But don't think for a second that moral principle is stopping Kenya's President Ruto from considering a "closer relationship." It's never that simple, is it?

Kenya's government plays coy, "hesitant" to invite the Malagasy junta to official talks because of the AU's suspension. How convenient. Yet, Paris, ever the puppet master, has a "more favourable position." It tells you everything you need to know about who pulls the strings and how easily African nations can be convinced to overlook democratic principles when there's a "deal" to be made, or perhaps a bigger player whispering in their ear.

And what's Kenya's angle? It's hardly a secret. The country's own coffers aren't exactly overflowing. Kenya Airways, that perpetual money pit, is desperately "counting on the revival," in 2027, of some Ghanaian airline project to "tap into new markets." This whole venture is "uncertain due to the group's financial difficulties and its struggles to attract a buyer." Sounds like another desperate gamble, much like the wider economic woes we see, where [the debt trap’s new teeth are strangling the dollar’s dominance](/posts/the-debt-trap-s-new-teeth--how-tariffs-are-strangling-the-dollar-s-dominance). Why wouldn't they look for any and every 'opportunity,' no matter how unsavory the partner?

So, while the diplomatic speak might paint a picture of burgeoning alliances, what we're really seeing is the usual dance of self-interest. A president of "Refoundation" (read: coup leader) gets a nod of legitimacy, and Kenya's struggling government hopes to gain... something. The ordinary citizens of both nations? They'll likely see no real benefit, just more leaders making deals behind closed doors, while the real problems fester. It's the same old story, just with different faces and a new "unexpected" handshake.
