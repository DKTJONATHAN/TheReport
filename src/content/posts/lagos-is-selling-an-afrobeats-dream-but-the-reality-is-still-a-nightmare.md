Lagos is suddenly being rebranded as the "Entertainment Capital of Africa," and I can barely keep my eyes from rolling into the back of my head. The Ministry of Tourism is working overtime to convince the world that our city is a "lifestyle to be experienced," but let us be real: they are just piggybacking on the hard work of artists they probably ignored five years ago. Now that Burna Boy and Ayra Starr are selling out global arenas, the government wants to claim the "creative energy" as a state asset. It is the classic move- wait for the kids to get famous on their own, then swoop in with a "strategic alignment" to tax the fun.

The narrative of Lagos is apparently being "rewritten" by music videos, which is hilarious if you have actually spent five minutes on a Lagos road. These "cultural pilgrims" flocking to the city for the "Afrobeats experience" are in for a massive reality check when they realize the glossy, champagne-soaked aesthetic of a music video does not include the twelve-hour power cuts or the soul-crushing traffic on the Third Mainland Bridge. They want the "grit" and the "hustle" until they actually have to live it. It is all fun and games until your "experiential tourism" involves being stuck in a car for three hours just to get to a club that will charge you triple the price for a lukewarm drink.

While the hospitality sector is supposedly seeing a "revenue surge" from these curated tours, the local reality is often much bleaker. The hype machine is great for hotel occupancy rates, but it rarely trickles down to the people actually making the culture. It reminds me of how the sports world operates, like [K’Ogalo’s Broke Era? Gor Mahia Fails to Hit KSh 1M Gate Target as Fans Ghost Nyayo Stadium](/posts/k-ogalo-s-broke-era--gor-mahia-fails-to-hit-ksh-1m-gate-target-as-fans-ghost-nyayo-stadium) where the prestige of the name does not actually translate to money in the bank for the people on the ground. We are selling a high-end fantasy while the foundation is held together by sheer willpower and expensive diesel generators.

The Ministry talks about "seamless transitions" from the airport to the concert venue, which is the funniest joke I have heard all year. To make Lagos compete with global hubs, they need more than just a few "Greater Lagos Fiesta" posters; they need actual roads and a security plan that does not involve praying for the best. You cannot brand "grit" as an asset and then act surprised when international travelers find the lack of basic infrastructure a bit too "gritty" for their liking.

At the end of the day, Afrobeats is a global powerhouse because the artists escaped the limitations of the system, not because the system helped them. These fans are coming to see the "source" of the movement, but they might find that the source is a city that succeeds in spite of its leadership, not because of it. Enjoy the "vibes" and the "energy," but do not be fooled by the PR-friendly version of the story. The music is world-class, but the management is still very much in the rehearsal phase.
