In a city where most people are sweating just to find a single Ksh100 note for lunch, some genius decided that the best use for his stashes was to tear them up for a 27-minute TikTok video. I do not know what kind of dopamine hit you get from destroying legal tender, but in Nairobi, if you are not chasing a bag, you are usually chasing clout. This man thought he was the main character until the Banking Fraud Investigations Unit (BFIU) decided to give him a reality check.

The DCI confirmed the arrest on March 10, proving once again that the government has the fastest internet connection when it comes to tracking down people who hurt their feelings. While the rest of us wonder why [55 Million Up In Smoke](/posts/55-million-up-in-smoke--why-your-taxes-are-just-a-suggestion) is just another regular headline in this country, the BFIU is busy spending tax money to hunt down a guy who treated his wallet like a paper shredder. Priorities in this country are always strictly for the gallery.

Let us look at the law, which is surprisingly specific about being a nuisance with money. Section 367A of the Penal Code says you can get three months in jail or a fine of Ksh2,000 for mutilating currency. Imagine getting a criminal record over two thousand bob. It is almost funny if it was not so pathetic. You want to look like a big spender on social media, but you end up in a cold cell because you forgot that the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) does not have a sense of humor.

It is not just the people tearing notes who should be worried. The CBK is also coming for the "soft life" brigade who think cash bouquets are a personality trait. They officially warned against using banknotes for decorations, glueing them, or stapling them into flowers. Apparently, these acts render the notes unfit for circulation and mess up the ATM machines. If you cannot even gift money without breaking a law, maybe it is time we went back to gifting socks and silence.

Tampering with coins is even more dangerous, carrying a seven-year sentence for diminishing their weight. We live in a reality where the government will watch your purchasing power vanish due to inflation, but they will throw the book at you if you dare to bend a coin. It is all about protecting the "integrity of the banking sector," which is just a fancy way of saying they want their paper to stay pretty while it buys you less every single day.

At the end of the day, this man is just another symptom of a city obsessed with optics. He tore up those notes to feel important, and now he is just another file on a detective's desk. Whether it is tearing bills or [The Big Flexa's Final Bow](/posts/the-big-flexa-s-final-bow--when-platinum-plaques-aren-t-enough-to-save-you), the result is always the same. Fame in Nairobi is a fleeting high, usually followed by a very long, very sober walk to the courtroom.
