How Not to Crash the Kenyan Economy: Five Handles for Change, From a Realist

WARNING: The Kenyan political class is playing a dangerous game of musical chairs with the nation's economy, prioritizing power over people. Brace yourself for a dose of harsh reality as sales of public assets, unaccounted borrowing, and the Auditor-General's damning reports paint a grim picture. While the elite feasts on the nation's wealth, millions go hungry, and natural resources, meant to be Kenya's future, are eyed for auction. This isn't just bad governance; it's an economic suicide mission.

This is not a story of hope, but a stark examination of failure. Forget grand promises; the reality is a Treasury and Parliament colluding to dismantle the economy, a Cabinet Secretary eyeing our natural wealth like a pawn shop owner, and an opposition that’s too busy shouting to actually plot a rescue. Wanjiku, as usual, is left to pick up the pieces while the political elites squabble over the scraps.

The sheer audacity of it all is breathtaking. We have world-class skills, thanks to past reforms, but our leaders are more interested in pushing Kenyans abroad for peanuts than investing in our own backyard. The Auditor-General's reports, like the "billions for breakfasts," are just theatre; the real crime is the systematic looting and the crippling debt that will burden future generations. It’s a replay of the Moi era, but with a modern twist of corporate greed and political apathy. Just look at the Gachagua, Duale's Ksh.103B SHA mess; it's just another Nairobi scam in a long line of them.

The narrative of revenue shortfalls is a convenient excuse for more asset sales and debt. The government’s solution to a weak revenue base is to borrow more, saddling future generations with payments that consume over 80 percent of our earnings. This leaves a pittance for crucial sectors like education, health, and agriculture, the very lifelines of the majority. The banking sector, meanwhile, feasts on depositors' money, offering peanuts to customers while raking in record profits.

The proposed solutions are nothing short of a wake-up call. We need to stop the "crowding out" by the collusion of government and banks, redirecting financial support to agriculture and MSMEs, the backbone of our economy. And for goodness sake, let's stop auctioning our future. These newly discovered natural resources are not for quick cash grabs; they are the foundation for a modern, value-added economy. We have a golden window, but if we continue on this path, we'll crash and burn.













The Kenyan political class, with its eyes firmly fixed on the 2027 General Election, seems to believe that power is the only air they breathe, utterly indifferent to the oxygen needs of the people. The Treasury and Parliament, far from being stewards of the economy, are actively dismantling it through the reckless sale of public assets, rampant corruption, and the accumulation of unaccountable debt. As if that weren't enough, we have untraceable billions disappearing into the pockets of civil servants for medical expenses, according to the Auditor-General. It's a grand display of self-interest that leaves the rest of us gasping for air.

And the audacity doesn't stop there. While Wanjiku's household grapples with hunger, destroyed livestock, and flood-ravaged livelihoods, a Cabinet Secretary brazenly proposes auctioning off Kenya's unprecedented wealth in natural resources. The opposition, fueled by the righteous anger of Gen-Z, seems too busy venting to strategize. What Kenya needs isn't just anger; it's a calm, calculated plan for recovery, a macroeconomic template that the public can understand and that guides the nation's economic resuscitation.

The current government scores a flat zero on its most fundamental duty: raising investment and employment. Instead of nurturing our homegrown talent, they are exporting it, pushing Kenyans into exploitative migrant labor schemes, and watching as our educated elite, like the "Shadow Scholars" in Nairobi, work for pennies as ghostwriters for foreign universities. This is a betrayal of Mwai Kibaki's education reforms, which created a capacity that our leaders are too lazy or corrupt to utilize. The reality is stark: over 80 percent of our labor force is unemployed or stuck in the informal sector.

The fiscal inequity and inequality in taxation are a slap in the face. A tiny fraction of formal workers bears the brunt of stiff taxes, including the ludicrous housing levy, while revenue targets are consistently missed by billions. This isn't surprising when the system is perceived as corrupt, exploiting workers at every turn. Revenue leakages are rampant, and the Auditor-General and Controller of Budget have become hoarse from documenting theft and misappropriations, like the infamous "billions for breakfasts."

The government's response to these revenue weaknesses is predictable: more asset sales and more borrowing. These debts, accumulating at usurious rates, will burden future generations. Debt repayments alone consume 68-80 percent of our revenues, leaving little for essential services like education and health. We've seen this disastrous script before, and only Kibaki's reforms in 2002 pulled us back from the brink. The current challenge for Gen-Z and the United Opposition is to forge a new economic path, one that creates jobs and raises incomes, not one that bankrupts the nation through irresponsible borrowing and asset stripping. We must end the "crowding out" perpetuated by the Central Bank and Treasury, where banks profit immensely from government debt while denying credit to productive sectors.

Kenya's serendipity of natural resources - coltan, niobium, gold, manganese - offers a golden opportunity, not for auction, but for building a modern, value-added economy. Like Singapore, we can leverage these discoveries and our capacity to reach new heights. The crucial step is moving up the global manufacturing value chains, not selling off our birthright. This requires smart policies, rejecting the easy route of tax exemptions for lobby groups, and focusing support on agriculture and MSMEs, the sectors where most Kenyans actually subsist. We must recognize that a strong domestic private sector, not government forays into asset sales and borrowing, is the pipeline to future prosperity.
