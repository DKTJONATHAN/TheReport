The notion that someone has "finally" figured out how to take a decent moon photo with an iPhone is, frankly, laughable. The problem, as anyone with a modicum of photographic sense and an iPhone can attest, is not the device's inherent capability when faced with the vastness of space and a tiny, distant celestial body. The real issue is the sheer absurdity of expecting a handheld camera to capture detail from millions of miles away. The "hack" of using 4K video for granular control is a minor technical workaround for a fundamentally flawed premise. It’s like teaching someone to balance on one leg while juggling chainsaws to impress their friends at a local fair. Impressive, perhaps, but not exactly a universally applicable life skill.

But the recent spectacle involving the Artemis II astronauts takes this whole charade to its logical, cynical 

The fact that iPhones were even equipped on their suits is a bizarre flex, a symbol of how even in the vacuum of space, our obsession with consumer electronics persists. While the rest of us are fumbling with digital zoom and praying for a clear night sky, these astronauts are literally in the lunar neighborhood, bathed in its reflected light, with a clear line of sight and zero atmospheric interference. It’s akin to bragging about how you finally managed to cook a gourmet meal because your private chef did all the work.

So, to recap: the "uncopyable trick" for taking a good iPhone photo of the moon involves either a sophisticated video workaround or, more effectively, traveling to the moon. The latter, while undeniably effective, is a privilege reserved for a vanishingly small elite, funded by the collective purse of humanity. This isn't about technological innovation; it's about the profound chasm between the haves and the have-nots, a chasm so wide it now extends to our celestial photography. Good luck copying that, indeed.
