Just when you thought the government had run out of ways to spend money we do not have, the Centre for Parliamentary Studies and Training (CPST) has entered the chat. From March to May 2026, they are inviting legislative staff to Nairobi for a series of capacity-building courses. It is the same old story: Ksh 40,000 per head just to learn things that could probably be found in a YouTube tutorial or a basic PDF. But of course, a PDF does not come with a Nairobi hotel per diem or a fancy certificate to hang on a wall in a dusty county office.

The first course is Protocol, Etiquette and Events Management. Imagine that. While you are out here haggling for the price of sukuma wiki, your tax money is being used to teach PAs and protocol officers how to handle VIPs and diplomats. Because apparently, knowing which fork to use or how to bow correctly to a politician is a national priority. It is just another layer of [The 'Second-Hand' Scam: How Refurbished Electronics are Being Sold as New in Luthuli Avenue](/posts/the-second-hand-scam-how-refurbished-electronics-are-being-sold-as-new-in-luthuli-avenue) logic, where they repackage basic common sense as high-level professional training and sell it back to the taxpayer at a premium.

Then we have the Speaker-s Rules and Enforcement and Hansard Production in the Advent of AI. They want the Serjeant-at-Arms to command the stage with confidence. It sounds more like they are training for a high school play than running a serious legislative body. And as for the Hansard reporters, they are being told that AI is transforming their work. That is just code for saying your job is becoming obsolete, but here, pay 40k to learn how to slowly be replaced by a bot that does not demand a pension or a lunch break.

The list of participants is a roll call of the usual suspects. Kitui, Kwale, Bungoma, and Murang-a are already lining up. You can bet your last coin that these county assemblies will be sending half their staff to Nairobi to learn Legislative Media Relations Management in May. It is all about learning how to spin the news so that the public does not realize how little is actually being done. In this city, if you are not the one being trained, you are the one paying for the tea and mandazi at the County House Building.

At the end of the day, these courses are just another way to keep the wheels of the bureaucracy greased while the rest of us suffer. While the CPST claims these skills will enhance the legislature-s image, the image most Kenyans see is a group of people who are very well-trained at spending money that belongs to a bankrupt nation. If you want to see where your 16 percent VAT goes, look no further than the dedicated Google Forms links being circulated to every county office from Mandera to Vanga.
