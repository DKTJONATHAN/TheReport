Another day, another crisis, another fat cheque from Washington. This time, Uncle Sam wants more than $1.4 billion, or roughly Sh181.2 billion, to tackle the Ebola outbreak. But before you start thinking they've suddenly grown a heart for the poor souls in Congo, let's look at the fine print. A cool Sh103.6 billion of that is specifically for a "quarantine centre in Kenya for Americans exposed to the virus." Yes, you read that right. Our soil, their safety net.

It's a curious turn of events, isn't it? Just before this outbreak blew up, the Trump administration was busy slashing funds for foreign assistance and African public health efforts, earning them global criticism. Now, suddenly, with a virus running rampant in Congo - a severe strain infecting over 1,000 and killing 267 - they're scrambling for billions. The World Health Organization initially claimed the risk of spread was low, but now Washington is in a full-blown panic, primarily about keeping *their* citizens safe and *their* borders sealed.

The money breakdown says it all. Beyond the Kenya centre, another Sh64.8 billion is for "global health security funds" - code for preventing the virus from reaching the United States. Then there's Sh11.7 billion for "diplomatic efforts," which includes evacuating and transporting US citizens. It really makes you wonder if Africa is just a convenient staging ground, a place where they can set up shop to protect their own without the virus ever touching American shores. It smacks of the kind of transactional deal we've seen before, a sort of [Sh5.8 Billion Hustle where Africa often ends up holding the short end of the stick](/posts/the-sh5-8-billion-hustle--africa-s-global-seat-at-the-scammer-s-table).

Even Congress, where lawmakers - including some of Trump's own Republicans - are unhappy, sees the blatant hypocrisy. They're not thrilled that an administration that refused to spend allocated foreign assistance money is now demanding billions. This isn't about altruism; it's about political expediency and self-preservation, cloaked in the language of humanitarian crisis response.

So, while the headlines might shout "Ebola funds," the reality on the ground, especially here in Kenya, is that we're being positioned as a convenient outpost for American interests. They cut the aid, they ignore the warnings, and then when the chickens come home to roost, they expect us to host their escape plan. Cynical? Perhaps. But out here in Nairobi, realism is simply a survival skill.
