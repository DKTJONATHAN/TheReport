EIGHT GIRLS NABBED FOR ARSON AFTER NAIROBI SCHOOL FIRE CLAIMS 16 LIVES

Another day, another tragedy in this country. Sixteen girls gone, seventy-nine injured, and now eight more in the dock. Utumishi Girls Academy Senior School in Gilgil, west-central Kenya, decided to go up in flames in the dead of night. Police, bless their hearts, are calling it suspected arson. Preliminary investigations have "identified" eight "persons of interest." Sounds like they just rounded up a few girls and called it a day. Because, of course, it's always the students' fault, isn't it?

The Directorate of Criminal Investigation proudly announced the arrest of these eight girls. They're now in police custody, no doubt being treated to the finest of Kenyan hospitality. Parents, naturally, are fuming. They’ve been hanging around the school since dawn, desperate for answers, but apparently, information is a luxury only the authorities can afford. Some haven't even seen their children - the deceased, one presumes. Others have loved ones inside being interrogated. It’s a typical Nairobi drama, just with more smoke and tears.

Student Hilda Njeri, lucky enough to jump out a window with a badly injured leg and back, recounted the harrowing experience. No water to put out the fire, just the desperate scramble for survival. The principal, apparently a saint, ferried students to the hospital and paid the bills. Small mercies, I suppose. Meanwhile, the Education Minister, Julius Ogamba, revealed that two teachers knew about the alleged plans and did nothing. And the school? Overcrowded dorms and a locked emergency exit. Shocking, isn't it? This sounds just as chaotic as the [Kenyan School Placement Chaos 2025: The KJSEA Grade 10 Disaster, Kisumu-to-Mandera Glitch, and What Parents Must Do Now](/posts/kjsea-grade10-placement-chaos-2025).

The government has disbanded the school board, promising "appropriate legal and disciplinary action." We’ll see how that plays out. It’s not like this is a new phenomenon. Remember 2001? Kyanguli Secondary School, 67 boys killed in an arson attack. And just this year in Nyeri County, a primary school blaze claimed 21 students. This country has a knack for repeating its mistakes, especially when it comes to safety and accountability.







Eight girls are now in custody, suspected of setting fire to Utumishi Girls Academy Senior School in Gilgil, a blaze that killed 16 students and left 79 injured. The fire broke out early Thursday, and by Friday, the Directorate of Criminal Investigation had already pointed fingers, identifying eight "persons of interest" who have since been arrested. This is how it works here: tragedy strikes, and the police quickly find someone to blame, usually the youngest and most vulnerable.

Parents at the scene, understandably, were a mess. Frustration boiling over as they pleaded for information, desperately searching for their daughters. Some confirmed their worst fears, while others watched helplessly as their children were interrogated. Al Jazeera's Catherine Soi reported on the raw grief and confusion, a scene all too familiar in this city when things go horribly wrong.

One injured student, Hilda Njeri, described a desperate escape through a window, the fire raging, and the lack of any water to fight it. She was badly injured, a stark reminder of the real cost of these alleged "plans." The principal apparently stepped in to help with hospital bills, a small gesture in the face of such devastation. Meanwhile, the Education Minister dropped a bombshell: two teachers knew about the alleged plans and did nothing. And the school itself? Overcrowded dorms and a locked emergency exit. It’s a recipe for disaster, just like the [Kenyan School Placement Chaos 2025: The KJSEA Grade 10 Disaster, Kisumu-to-Mandera Glitch, and What Parents Must Do Now](/posts/kjsea-grade10-placement-chaos-2025).

The government, in a predictable move, has disbanded the school board and promised "legal and disciplinary action." We’ve heard this song and dance before. Kenya has a grim history with school fires, from the 2001 Kyanguli Secondary School arson that killed 67 boys to a recent blaze in Nyeri County that claimed 21 students. The question isn't if it will happen again, but when, and who will be held truly accountable this time.
