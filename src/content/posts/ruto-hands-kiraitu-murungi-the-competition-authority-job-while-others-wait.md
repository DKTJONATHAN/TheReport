So, Kiraitu Murungi, the former Meru governor, has landed a new gig as the Non-Executive Chairperson of the Competition Authority of Kenya. This little bit of news dropped via a gazette notice on June 26, 2026, because apparently, important decisions are best announced when no one is really paying attention. He’ll be there for three years, presumably looking out for "fair business practices" while someone else revokes the appointment of the previous chap, Charles Mahinda. Because why have one person when you can shuffle them out for another?

This isn't exactly a surprise, is it? We've seen this movie before. The "Competition Authority" is meant to keep the economy fair and protect us consumers from shady deals. But let's be honest, when positions like these get handed out, you start to wonder if the competition being safeguarded is actually the competition for who can get closest to the gravy train. It makes you think about how these institutions are run, and whether they’re truly serving the public or just a select few who know how to play the game. It reminds me of that piece about how political catchphrases are often [''Not Fit For Purpose': The Secret, Cynical History of a Deadly Political Catchphrase'](/posts/not-fit-for-purpose---the-secret--cynical-history-of-a-deadly-political-catchphrase), and these appointments sometimes feel cut from the same cloth.

And it’s not just Kiraitu. We’ve got Joseph M’eruaki M’uthari, formerly of Igembe North, back at KEPHIS for another three years. Then there’s Stanley Kiptis, the ex-Baringo governor, also getting a reappointment at the Kenya National Qualifications Authority. It’s like a revolving door, but only for certain people. They trot them out, give them a fancy title, and expect us to believe it’s all about public service.

This whole trend of recycling former politicians and public officials into these state agency roles is becoming a standard operating procedure. It’s a neat way to reward loyalty and keep allies in strategic positions. The gazette notices just rubber-stamp what we all already know: this is how things are done in Nairobi. It's not about merit; it's about who you know and what you've done for the party.

So, while Kiraitu Murungi is off to oversee market conduct, and the others are busy with plant health and qualifications, the rest of us are just left here, trying to navigate a system where connections often matter more than competence. The Competition Authority might be mandated to protect consumers, but it’s hard to shake the feeling that sometimes, the biggest competition being managed is for the best seats at the public purse.
