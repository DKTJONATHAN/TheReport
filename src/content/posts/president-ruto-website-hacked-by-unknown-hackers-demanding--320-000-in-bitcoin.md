So, the presidential website got a bit of a makeover, courtesy of some anonymous keyboard warriors. Apparently, they've managed to crash president.go.ke and are now holding it hostage for 5 bitcoins, which works out to a cool 41 million Kenyan shillings. Because, you know, official government business should always involve a high-stakes cryptocurrency transaction by Saturday evening. The hackers weren't shy, either, plastering the homepage with "offensive messages" and threats to leak "sensitive government information." Just another Tuesday in the digital age.

The Ministry of Information, Communications and The Digital Economy, bless their hearts, assures us they've activated their "cybersecurity incident response protocols." It sounds fancy, but mostly it means they're scrambling, consulting with "relevant government agencies and technical partners," and hoping their "comprehensive forensic investigation" uncovers who exactly is playing whack-a-mole with the nation's digital front door. Meanwhile, access to the website has been temporarily restricted, which in government speak probably means it's completely offline and they're trying to remember where they put the spare server.

Cabinet Secretary William Gitau, in his infinite wisdom, stated that "evidence of unauthorized access to sensitive data, data exfiltration, or loss of information no dey." He also insisted that "government systems and digital services remain secure and operational." Right. Because when your official presidential website is displaying ransom demands and insults, it's the picture of digital security. The website itself is now showing a generic "Sorry, we're performing maintenance" message, which is about as believable as a politician’s promise.

This whole kerfuffle isn't exactly unprecedented, is it? It’s barely been a few months since November 2025, when a "coordinated attack" hit several government websites. Seems like our digital defenses are about as robust as a paper umbrella in a Nairobi downpour. While the government assures us all is well, it's worth remembering that these guys are constantly looking for ways to improve [Guinea and Kenya Explore Strategic Digital Cooperation at Connected Africa Summit], and clearly, some are looking to exploit any perceived weakness. The silence from the website is deafening, and the government's reassurances feel a bit like trying to convince us the sky is green.
