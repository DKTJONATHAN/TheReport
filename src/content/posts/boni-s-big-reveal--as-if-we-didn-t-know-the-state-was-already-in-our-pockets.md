Citizen Lab in Canada - because apparently, we can’t find our own bugs - claims an Israeli tool called Cellebrite was stuffed into his hardware during that 2025 raid. It’s funny how we always have money for high-end surveillance tech but the potholes on Juja Road could swallow a probox whole. Now they have everything: his financial records, his passwords, and his inner circle’s secrets. They didn't just break into his house; they moved into his digital brain and started rearranging the furniture.

He says he’s violated and fears for his life. Join the queue, comrade. Ever since the 2024 protests turned the CBD into a teargas buffet, people have been vanishing like salary on the second day of the month. The government promises "investigations" while the surveillance apparatus gets a shiny new upgrade. If you’re going to challenge the status quo, you don't do it with a phone that’s basically a state informant. It’s the Nairobi way: they don’t need to arrest you if they can just embarrass you with your own data.

Boni is heading to court to stop them from leaking his family photos. It’s a nice sentiment, but this is a town where "files go missing" more often than reliable tap water. Once the state has your data, it’s like a secret in a hair salon - it’s going to get out eventually. He’s worried about his campaign team being watched, but let’s be real: if you’re running for the top job in this climate, you should assume even your shadow is on the government payroll.

This isn't just about one man and his political ambitions. We saw it with those filmmakers who worked on the BBC documentary. It’s a pattern of keeping the "troublemakers" on a digital leash. We spend so much time chasing after these political messiahs or, as I’ve mentioned before in /posts/the-ngara-mirage--why-nairobi-sisters-need-to-stop-chasing-mzungu-ghosts, chasing illusions of a better life while the reality is far more clinical. We are being watched, mapped, and cataloged while we argue about who’s going to save us.

2027 is still a lifetime away in Kenyan politics. Between now and then, there will be more arrests, more "leaked" documents, and more activists discovering that their smartphones are actually snitches. Boni might have the "proof" now, but in a system that feeds on surveillance, being right doesn't always mean you're safe. In this city, you either stay quiet, or you accept that your private life is just public entertainment for the people in power.
