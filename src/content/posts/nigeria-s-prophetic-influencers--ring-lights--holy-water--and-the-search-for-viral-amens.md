Who authorized this new social media circus? Nigeria has officially broken the internet again, but this time it is not a catchy Afrobeats hook or a dance challenge. It is the rise of the "Prophetic TikToker." These guys have swapped the quiet reverence of the church for the blinding glare of ring lights and the dopamine hit of a viral share. They stand on digital highways like Facebook, pointing fingers and narrating private family secrets to victims while the cameras roll. It is less about salvation and more about the engagement metrics.

The currency here is shock, and the brand is built on exposure. We have moved from genuine doctrine to a messy economy of attention where a bottle of water is used as a prop for a choreographed performance. You see them sprinkling water like it is a magic potion, waiting for the "accused" to perform their guilt for the audience. It is a desperate hustle for relevance. In fact, this spiritual scamming reminds me of the [inheritance hustle](/posts/inheritance-hustle--why-your-rich-uncle-s-will-is-probably-a-photoshopped-lie) where people use any means necessary to secure a bag, even if it means photoshopping a will or faking a revelation to look legit.

Why do we fall for it? Because we are obsessed with the unseen and addicted to the spectacle. If a prophet can guess your birthday or your village, we suddenly think they are on a direct line with the heavens. Reality check: information is cheap. Between social media footprints and a bit of psychological manipulation, anyone can look like a seer. These prophets are just showmen in cheap suits, turning the Gospel into a public interrogation for the sake of followership. They know that in seasons of economic stress, people will pay anything for a bit of certainty, even if that certainty is a scripted lie.

The real tragedy is the aftermath. Families are fracturing and reputations are being shredded in real time just so some "man of God" can hit ten thousand views. This is not evangelism; it is high-stakes bullying with a spiritual coat of paint. We have enthroned charisma over character and titles over theology for far too long. When the altar becomes a stage, the congregation becomes an audience, and the faith becomes a circus. Nigeria needs to pause and ask hard questions before hitting that like button, because right now, the only thing being resurrected is these prophets' bank accounts.
