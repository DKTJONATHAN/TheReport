So, the big wigs in our fuel sector have finally tripped over their own greed. Three senior officials, out. Several more, facing the music, as they say. Apparently, they cooked up a whole national fuel shortage out of thin air. Just conjured it up. All to exploit rising global prices and our general panic, of course. Because that's how you stabilize supply, right? By lying about it. The MV Paloma, bless its rusty hull, apparently docked with some Saudi Aramco fuel, rerouted through local importers, all part of this grand charade.

Joseph Wafula, the Deputy Director of Petroleum, and Joel Mburu, KPC's Supply and Logistics Manager, are now the poster boys for this particular brand of public service. Arrested on Thursday. It's almost poetic, isn't it? Especially when you consider the IMF and the National Treasury have been screaming for ages that this whole Government to Government (G2G) framework was already a hot mess, concentrating power and doling out favours to a select few oil companies and banks. But hey, why listen to the bean counters when you can just print your own fuel shortage?

The scheme involved manipulating data on what fuel we actually had, creating this false alarm about an impending shortfall. Then, the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum, in a move that screams "corruption," ordered an emergency fuel cargo. Not just any cargo, mind you. One procured outside the G2G rules, at a price higher than agreed, and, shocker, of substandard quality. A quality assurance manager actually had the guts to flag it - elevated sulphur levels, not up to par. This is the kind of quiet bravery that gets you into trouble in this country, leading to all this fuss and the investigators raiding homes for documents and cash. Reminds me of how even justice can be unevenly applied, like [The Temple of Justice Has No Ramp, But Runda Has a Padel Court](/posts/the-temple-of-justice-has-no-ramp--but-runda-has-a-padel-court).

The government's official line is that supply is stable, with our contracted G2G suppliers still holding up their end. We've got about two weeks of petrol and three weeks of diesel stockpiled, which sounds almost comforting until you remember how quickly things can go south. They're even telling oil marketing companies to stop hoarding fuel, as if that's the only problem. But the real kicker? Pump prices are still expected to go up from mid-April. So, you fabricate a shortage, buy overpriced, shoddy fuel, and then the public still gets hit with higher prices. Brilliant. Absolutely brilliant.

This whole mess lands right when global oil prices are doing their usual rollercoaster act thanks to the Middle East. Kenya established this G2G arrangement back in 2023 to avoid the 2022 fuel queues and forex headaches, but it seems the "solution" has become the problem. The government's even considering ditching parts of it for an open tender system. Imagine that. But for now, public confidence in the people meant to ensure we have fuel to get to work has taken a direct hit. And as we've seen with the [Imperial Fed and the $110 Barrel of Reality](/posts/the-imperial-fed-and-the--110-barrel-of-reality), these "stable" arrangements often have a shelf life.
