The Directorate of Criminal Investigations is back on X, formerly Twitter, beating its chest about catching thirteen people as if they just dismantled a global cartel. On Friday, March 6, 2026, we were treated to the usual script: a multi-agency security team consisting of the DCI, Kenya Police, NACADA, and KRA descended upon Likoni. They raided a rental house in Corner Mpya Estate and found three people "distilling" something they called SMART Vodka. In a country where [The Bitter Aftertaste of Kenya’s $3.5 Billion Tea Scam](/posts/the-bitter-aftertaste-of-kenya-s--3-5-billion-tea-scam) still lingers, it is almost poetic that the latest hustle involves fake stickers and questionable spirits.

Then the circus moved to the Kisumu-Kericho Highway. At Awasi, they flagged down a bus and found a backpack with five bales of "green plant material." We all know what that means, but the police love their adjectives. It is the same old story of the small-time mule taking the fall while the real owners of the cargo are probably sitting in a boardroom somewhere, far away from the dusty roads of Nyanza. These "intelligence-led operations" always seem to stop at the guy holding the bag, never the guy signing the checks.

Nairobi’s Mwiki area was next on the list. The ANU and TOCU teams raided another rental house and found a "creamish substance" and a collection of 43 non-serviceable mobile phones. Forty-three dead phones. It is hard to tell if this was a drug den or a graveyard for obsolete technology. They even went as far as searching a dumpsite behind the building to find a Samsung Galaxy A36. The dedication to digging through trash is impressive, but you have to wonder if that same energy is ever applied to the white-collar thieves bleeding the city dry.

The three suspects from the Mwiki raid - Josephat Mwangi Njenga, Samwel Nyota Wambui, and their associate - were escorted to Muthaiga Police Station. They will be "booked and placed in cells" while the exhibits are processed for "safe custody." It is a well-oiled machine of arrests and arraignments that keeps the headlines moving but does very little to change the reality on the ground. For every lab they shut down in Likoni, two more spring up in the shadows of the city's informal settlements.

We are expected to clap because thirteen more bodies are occupying police cells tonight. But for a cynical Nairobi resident, this is just another Friday. The "Smart Vodka" might be off the shelves for a week, but the desperation that drives people to manufacture and consume it is not going anywhere. The DCI gets its engagement on social media, the suspects get a court date, and the rest of us continue to navigate a system that is more interested in the optics of the bust than the root of the problem.
