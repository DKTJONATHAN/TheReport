Tanzania Holds the Line While Kenya's Fuel Market Sputters and Dies

The Middle East shipping chaos is exposing Kenya for the shambles it is, while our neighbors in Tanzania are somehow keeping their heads above water. Expect queues, empty tanks, and a general sense of despair here in Kenya as our government’s brilliant policy of pretending the problem doesn't exist hits home. Meanwhile, Tanzania’s authorities are patting themselves on the back, likely because they actually know how to run a country.

Kenya's so-called "independent" fuel retailers are already crying wolf about shortages, but don't be fooled. This isn't just some market fluctuation; it's the predictable fallout from Nairobi's insistence on sticking to fixed prices while global oil costs are doing the cha-cha upwards. It’s a recipe for hoarding and a bonanza for those who like to see the common mwananchi suffer. Mark my words, "total crisis" is an understatement.

Our government, through EPRA, claims "sufficient stocks." Ha! That's the same tune they sang before the South C building collapsed, proving how much we can trust their pronouncements. They're relying entirely on those government-to-government deals, which means when the Middle East sneezes, Kenya gets pneumonia. Some lawmaker is blaming "panic buying," but the real panic will be when the pumps are dry. This is classic Kenyan mismanagement, a story we've seen play out before in various forms, like with the endless mess in [Nyayo Mud and Misery](/posts/nyayo-mud-and-misery--gor-mahia-s-easy-walk-over-nairobi-s-broken-dreams).

Tanzania, on the other hand, seems to have actually planned for this. They're boasting about reserves that can last weeks, even months, and their pump prices haven't budged. Their officials are even threatening local distributors who try to pull the same stunts our Kenyan dealers are pulling. It’s almost as if they have a functioning petroleum board that doesn’t just spout empty reassurances.

The Tanzanian minister’s warning to domestic operators is a masterclass in how to handle this - no "force majeure" nonsense from their own people. They're even ordering daily inspections of private depots and real-time tracking of shipments. Meanwhile, Kenya's regulator is likely still figuring out how to turn on their computers. The risk of tankers being rerouted to more profitable markets is real, and if this drags on, we could see prices double. Get ready for more "real shock."

The irony is thick enough to cut with a panga. While Tanzania navigates this global shipping crisis with apparent competence, Kenya’s approach is a masterclass in denial and eventual, inevitable disaster. Expect the usual suspects to blame everyone but themselves, while the rest of us are left scrambling for fuel, wondering why we can't get anything right.
