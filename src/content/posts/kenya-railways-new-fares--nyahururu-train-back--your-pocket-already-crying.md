So, Kenya Railways, in their infinite wisdom, has decided to dust off the old Gilgil-Ol Kalou-Nyahururu line. Apparently, this is a "revival" and a "Hustler Express" service. Ksh 600 for economy class to Nyahururu. That's 600 bob for a supposedly "safe, comfortable and affordable" ride, which usually translates to "delayed, cramped, and overpriced" in this town. After 46 years of being a forgotten relic, it's back, and so are the excuses for why things are always so expensive.

They're calling it the "Nyahururu Safari Train." Safari. Right. Because what screams adventure more than a bumpy train ride that takes practically all day? Nairobi to Nyahururu at 10 am, arriving at 5:50 pm. That's nearly seven hours to travel a distance that a matatu would do in half the time, albeit with more questionable upholstery. And don't forget, this comes after fuel prices have been skyrocketing, making you wonder if [Kenya's Truckers Eye 14% Hike As Diesel Prices Reach New Heights](/posts/kenya-s-truckers-eye-14--hike-as-diesel-prices-reach-new-heights) were just the beginning of our financial woes.

Naturally, there are different tiers of pain. Economy is Ksh 600, first class is a whopping Ksh 1,300. For those wanting to take a shorter trip, you'll still shell out Ksh 450 to Gilgil or Ksh 550 to Ol Kalou. It's all about segmenting the suffering, isn't it? And the "convenience" of buying tickets? At Nairobi Central Station, or "official booking channels" - which we all know means navigating a website that's probably as old as the railway line itself.

They're touting this as a boost for regional economies and tourism. Let's be real, the only thing this is boosting is the Kenya Railways balance sheet. They even managed to squeeze in a mention of transporting subsidised fertilisers. Because why not throw in a little "concern for the common man" to distract from the fare hikes? This is Nairobi, after all; we expect these elaborate performances.

The whole "iconic Thomson's Falls Rail is back on track" narrative is just that - a story. A pretty one, maybe, but a story nonetheless. The reality is a resurrected piece of infrastructure that's now expected to be profitable, and guess who's footing the bill? Us. The ones who always pay, no matter how much they dress it up with flowery language about "history, adventure and convenience." Prepare your wallets, folks.
