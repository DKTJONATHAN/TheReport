So, the Nairobi Litfest, co-presented by some outfit called Book Bunk and the global Hay Festival - probably funded by people with too much time and money - is bringing in Mutoriah for a “live musical experience.” May 9th, 6 to 7 pm, at the Eastlands Library. Just one hour. Enough time to play a few hits, probably “Beta” again, collect a cheque, and disappear back into the Nairobi ether. They say it’s part of a programme blending literature and music, as if that’s some revolutionary concept. We’ve seen this movie before.

Mutoriah, they tell us, is a multi-instrumentalist. Piano, acoustic guitar, bass. Wow. He’s also worked with Sauti Sol, Wanavokali, and Bensoul. Fancy. And he was a guest producer at Coke Studio Africa in 2019. So, basically, he’s plugged into the usual Nairobi circuit. This whole "multidisciplinary artistic space" talk is just jargon for another weekend shindig where the usual suspects will turn up, pretend to be cultured, and then go complain about the matatu fares home. It’s the same old song, and honestly, it's starting to feel like a broken record, much like the endless cycle of political promises that never materialize. Just look at how quickly those “Trump rally” Bitcoin gains evaporated; that’s the kind of quick fizzle we can expect here too. [Bitcoin falls below $70,000 to wipe out ‘Trump rally’](/posts/bitcoin-falls-below-70-000-to-wipe-out-trump-rally)

The Eastlands Library is the chosen venue this year. Why? Because it’s Eastlands, I guess. Makes it sound a bit more grassroots, a bit more accessible to the people. But let’s be real, the people who will pack that library will be the same ones who can afford the entrance fees, if there are any, or those who get invited through the right channels. The rest of us will be stuck in traffic, wondering if this expensive "cultural conversation" is worth the hassle.

The festival runs from May 8th to 10th across McMillan, Kaloleni, and Eastlands Libraries. Panel discussions, workshops, readings, kids’ activities, and live performances. It’s a buffet of activities, designed to look comprehensive and inclusive. But beneath the glossy brochures and the hype, it’s just another event trying to justify its existence and the funds it’s gobbling up. Meanwhile, the real issues plaguing this city continue to fester, unaddressed, like a festering wound ignored for the sake of a pretty show.

This whole "creatives from Kenya and beyond" thing is just a way to add a touch of international flair, I suppose. Make it sound important. Make it sound like something worth writing home about. But is it? Or is it just another carefully curated event designed to keep the champagne flowing for a select few while the rest of Nairobi just tries to get by? It’s the Nairobi way, after all. All flash, no substance.

And the headline? "Mutoriah to provide live music experience at Nairobi Litfest." Riveting. It tells you nothing, and yet, it tells you everything about the predictable nature of these events. Just another name, another date, another venue. Don't expect a revelation. Expect another evening where Nairobi pats itself on the back for being "cultural."
