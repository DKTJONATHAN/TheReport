So, the heroes are back. Hugs, cheers, and all that jazz. The Kenya-led Multinational Security Support Mission in Haiti is done and dusted, handing over the reins to some Chadian folks. The families are happy, the brass is probably polishing their speeches. But let's not get too misty-eyed. These officers went to Haiti, got a decent chunk of change on top of their salaries - think $1,500 to $2,000 a month, plus hardship and risk pay. It's good money, and the insurance cover sounds nice if things go south.

Beyond the payday, we're told this was all about 'professional development'. Urban security, crisis response, multinational coordination - skills that, apparently, are as rare as an honest politician in Nairobi. They got to play with international best practices and fancy equipment, which is great, I suppose, for their CVs. Participation in a foreign mission is a golden ticket for promotions and all that jazz. So, for some, it was a stepping stone, a shiny badge of honour to hang on their wall.

The whole funding setup was a bit of a mess, wasn't it? A hybrid model, they call it. The UN gave it a nod, but Uncle Sam coughed up most of the cash for allowances, logistics, and gear. It sounds like a good deal for Kenya, keeping our own resources from being drained. But then the reality hits: securing the full funds was a struggle, and that meant our boys were out there with their hands tied, facing down heavily armed gangs with half-baked support. President Ruto himself admitted they were running at "only about 40% capacity." Brilliant.

And the equipment? Second-hand vehicles that were constantly breaking down. Imagine that. You're in a war zone, facing down some serious firepower, and your ride decides to conk out. It’s a miracle more weren't lost. We lost three officers, remember that. Police Constable Samuel Tompoi Kaetuai, Constable Benedict Kabiru, and Corporal Kennedy Mutuku Nzuve. They paid the ultimate price for a mission that, according to our own President, was severely under-resourced and under-equipped. The PS talks about honouring their memory and standing with their families, but the sting of inadequate support and unnecessary risk remains. It's a harsh reality, a far cry from the heroic narratives spun for the cameras. This whole affair feels like a PR stunt with lives on the line, much like the performative outrage we see in other global dramas.

And for those wondering about the "untold stories"? Don't hold your breath. Those are best left unsaid, tucked away with the broken-down vehicles and the unanswered questions about what exactly was achieved in that chaos. The welcome home is just a brief pause before the real grind of domestic policing, where the dangers are just as real, but the foreign deployment bonuses aren't.
