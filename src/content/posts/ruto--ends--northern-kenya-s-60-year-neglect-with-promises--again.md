So, President Ruto was in Mandera, apparently to cure the region's sixty years of being ignored. He waxed lyrical about infrastructure, electricity, irrigation, and even teachers being trained locally. It’s the same old song and dance, isn’t it? He says his administration is ditching "retrogressive politics of discrimination, neglect, hatred and tribalism." Big words. We've heard them before, haven't we? The question is, what’s actually going to stick this time, or is this just another round of feel-good rhetoric to get through until the next election cycle?

He’s talking about connecting Mandera to Ethiopia’s electricity grid, nine new generators, and a shiny new 750km road. He even pledged a stadium. Oh, and teachers colleges and 1,800 new local teachers. It all sounds very grand, very ambitious. But let's be real, when have these grand plans ever materialized without a hitch? We’ve seen grand projects promised and then forgotten, or half-finished, or plagued by corruption scandals. Remember the hype around digital safety? It’s easy to be fooled by promises of progress, but the reality on the ground is often far less glamorous.

The President also mentioned finally sorting out national IDs, making sure everyone can participate in elections. Good. That’s the bare minimum, really. And then there's the Madaraka Day bash in Wajir. More infrastructure projects, like the Banisa Water Supply Project and a market, are supposedly nearing completion. Eighty-five percent complete, ninety percent complete. These are always the buzzwords, aren't they? The percentages that sound impressive but still leave us waiting for the final delivery.

The local leaders, of course, are falling over themselves to praise the President. Governor Khalif is practically singing his praises, saying Ruto is the first to visit multiple times for "development, not politics." And Governor Abdullahi commends him for showing that "equitable development" is possible. It’s a beautiful narrative, but let’s not forget the power dynamics at play. These leaders have their own reasons for staying in the President's good graces.

Duale chimes in, saying Kenyans are seeing more development under this administration. He even frames the next election as a battle between "tribalists and those who believe that Kenya belongs to all of us." A nice, neat little box to put everyone in, isn't it? It conveniently sidesteps any real scrutiny of the actual outcomes versus the promises. It’s a familiar playbook.

So, here we are again. Promises are made, photo ops are taken, and the people of Northern Kenya are left hoping that this time, it’s different. Will the sixty years of neglect truly be a thing of the past, or will 2026 become just another year of well-intentioned pronouncements fading into the dust? Only time, and more importantly, tangible results, will tell.
