So, there's this Brookings Institute study. They've apparently "found" that most workers in jobs ripe for AI takeover aren't actually doomed. Apparently, about 26.5 million out of 37.1 million "high exposure" workers have "above median adaptive capacity." Which, in plain English, means they're probably already skilled enough or in fields where they can pivot without too much pain. Think web developers and marketing folks in big cities - the ones who are usually already ahead of the curve anyway.

This is, of course, presented as "good news." It’s the kind of good news that conveniently ignores the remaining 10.6 million workers who are apparently *not* well positioned to adapt. These are the clerical and administrative workers, predominantly women, stuck in university towns and midsize cities. Their "low adaptive capacity" is just a polite way of saying they're likely to be left scrambling, with fewer options and less leverage.

The narrative conveniently glosses over the sheer scale of the problem. Tech companies, awash in cash and driven by the eternal quest for "agile teams," are already laying off thousands. Jack Dorsey, the guru of "honesty," essentially admitted Block cut staff because AI could do their jobs. This isn't some abstract future; it's happening now. Yet, we're told AI's overall economic impact is negligible, and it won't displace jobs for a decade. These are contradictory statements designed to soothe, not inform.

The study’s hope is that policymakers will "focus their attention and resources" on the vulnerable. This is standard academic hand-wringing. The reality is that "focusing attention" rarely translates into meaningful systemic change when powerful economic forces are at play. Employers are reducing headcount because AI offers a cheaper, more efficient alternative. The workers who are "well positioned to adapt" are those who can offer something AI can't yet replicate, or who are already in high demand. The rest? They're the collateral damage in the march of technological progress.

This study is a sophisticated way of saying that the privileged tend to stay privileged, even when the ground shifts beneath them. The "well positioned" will find new roles, perhaps even better ones. The others? They're expected to adapt, to reskill, to move - all while the underlying economic structures that created their precarious position remain largely unchallenged. It's a comforting illusion of agency in a system designed for the few, not the many.
