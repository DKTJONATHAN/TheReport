GOVT FACES PRESSURE AS LOBBY GROUPS DEMAND ACCOUNTABILITY OVER FUEL SCANDAL

The government is on the spot as anti-corruption watchdogs demand answers for the importation of substandard fuel, a move they’ve dubbed ‘Profiting from Poison.’

This imported poison, worth a staggering Ksh4.8 billion, has the National Integrity Alliance (NIA) seeing red, pointing fingers at an energy sector that clearly values profit over the safety of Kenyans and their vehicles.

NIA, a heavyweight coalition including Transparency International Kenya and the Kenya Human Rights Commission, is questioning how this shoddy consignment slipped past the usual watchdogs like EPRA and KEBS. They're also raising eyebrows about whether proper procedures were followed, or if this was just another case of "we'll sort it out later" in this country. This whole mess reeks of the same old song and dance, a disturbingly familiar erosion of ethics, much like the blatant disregard for the rule of law we saw when [our passport became just a VIP pass for genocidaires](/posts/our-passport-is-just-a-vip-pass-for-genocidaires).

The coalition’s statement, dripping with righteous indignation that often amounts to little, calls for the Cabinet Secretaries for Energy and Trade to step aside. They want the Auditor General to poke around the Government-to-Government fuel framework and the EACC to do its usual song and dance of assessing corruption risks. They're demanding full transparency under Article 35, and for the DCI to hunt down every last beneficiary, especially the politically connected ones.

It’s a constitutional minefield out there, with NIA citing breaches of national values, environmental rights, consumer protection, and public service ethics. Add to this the whispers of fuel hoarding and speculative practices, and you’ve got a potent cocktail of incompetence and greed. The NIA is calling for reforms, but frankly, restoring confidence in Kenya’s petroleum supply chain feels about as likely as seeing honest politicians.

Meanwhile, the Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) is apparently expanding probes into alleged collusion networks and beneficiaries, including those with political ties. The NIA insists on reforms to restore confidence in the petroleum supply chain, a pipe dream in a country where the [Nairobi Hospital Directors remain defiant against crackdowns](/posts/nairobi-hospital-directors-defiant--ruto-s--crackdown--is-old-news).
