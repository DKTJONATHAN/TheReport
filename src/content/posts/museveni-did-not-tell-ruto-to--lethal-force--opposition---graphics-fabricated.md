Honestly, Kenyans and their thirst for drama. Now we've got these shiny graphics doing the rounds, claiming Ugandan President Yoweri Museveni told our very own William Ruto to get brutal with the opposition. "Lethal force," they say, with some fancy Kiswahili thrown in for good measure. It’s all a bit much, isn't it? Classic move to stir the pot, especially when things are already a bit spicy here with Gachagua and Matiang'i trading barbs.

The whole thing is about as believable as a politician keeping a promise. Museveni, bless his long-reigning heart, has got his own issues with "authoritarianism," as the sober folks call it. He's known for that, not for lecturing other heads of state on how to handle their internal squabbles. And the Kiswahili? Please. Unless he's been secretly binge-watching telenovelas, that "uswadi ulipewa sio ya kukata mboga" bit sounds like something a bored intern cooked up.

Plus, let's be real. Kenya and Uganda, despite all the posturing, usually keep their noses out of each other's business. If Museveni actually dropped such a bombshell, you'd have every credible news outlet from here to Kampala screaming about it. But crickets. Absolute silence. This feels like the same old song and dance, folks just wanting to see what makes us click. It’s exhausting, frankly.

It's a shame, really. You'd think with all the real issues plaguing us - the economy, the ever-present corruption that seems to follow us like a shadow - people would have better things to do than peddle these fabricated fantasies. It's like looking at the headlines about the [Liquidity Illusion](/posts/the-liquidity-illusion--why-your-digital-gold-is-choking-on-us-debt) and knowing it's all a bit of a smoke screen. This Museveni quote is just another one.

So, before you share that next viral gem that promises juicy gossip about our leaders, take a breath. Ask yourself if it makes sense. Because more often than not, especially in this age of digital noise, it's just a pretty lie designed to get you riled up. And honestly, we've got enough real problems to be riled up about without inventing new ones.
