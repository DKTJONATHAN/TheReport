Ruto's Finance Bill Sails Through Parliament, But Gen Z's Ghost Lingers

The ruling coalition, with its iron grip on parliament, has shoved another Finance Bill down our throats. Despite the opposition's pathetic whines and last-minute squeals, the Finance Bill 2026 is now just a signature away from President Ruto’s pen. This man is desperate, needing Sh100 billion to fund his 2022 promises before the elections next year. It’s a bold gamble, this tax hike, especially after the mess in 2024 that saw your average Kenyan almost storm State House. Remember that? It’s a chilling reminder of how quickly things can go south, a crude reality reminiscent of [THE CRUDE REALITY OF BITCOIN'S FRAGILE REBOUND](/posts/the-crude-reality-of-bitcoin-s-fragile-rebound).

Last time, massive tax hikes triggered the infamous Gen Z protests, a youth-led uprising that forced Ruto to backtrack, disband his cabinet, and pretend to be a man of the people. Now, he’s targeting payment transactions and mitumba imports - the very things that keep the poor from starving. But hey, this time around, he’s got parliament in his pocket, thanks to that ‘broad-based government’ deal with Raila Odinga and his ODM MPs. It’s a done deal; 122 MPs for it, a mere 40 against. Typical Nairobi politics, all about the numbers and who owes who.

While state-sponsored bots and bloggers flood social media with their nonsense, trying to paint this bill as sunshine and rainbows, the memory of 2024 isn’t fading. Families of victims and human rights groups are already planning marches for the second anniversary. They’re demanding June 25 be a public holiday to remember the young people who died, at least 60 of them according to the supposedly independent KNCHR, most from police bullets. The police are giving their blessing, as long as it’s “peaceful” and doesn’t disrupt the order they’re supposed to be maintaining.

Opposition leaders are promising the “mother of all protests” next Thursday, hoping to reignite the spirit of Gen Z. With the 2027 elections looming, these marches could easily become another flashpoint, another opportunity for things to get ugly. Ruto might have won this parliamentary battle, but the war for the hearts and minds of Kenyans, especially the youth who remember the sting of those tax hikes, is far from over. This whole saga feels like a broken record, a constant cycle of promises and pain.
