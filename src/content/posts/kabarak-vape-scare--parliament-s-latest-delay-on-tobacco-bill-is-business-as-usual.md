So, a student at some expensive school called Kabarak got caught with a vape. Big deal, right? For the do-gooders in public health, this is apparently the end of the world, proof that the tobacco industry has already got its grubby hands on our kids. They're selling us addiction in pretty colours and flavors, making it look like a trendy accessory. Because, of course, nothing says "cool" like a lung disease by age 25.

These "advocates" are begging Parliament to pass some amendment bill, the Tobacco Control (Amendment) Bill, 2024. Apparently, our laws are as outdated as a Nokia 3310. They moan about "dangerous loopholes" the industry is exploiting. Honestly, it’s the same old song and dance. These products look like pens and flash drives? Please. Parents can't tell a vape from a highlighter? That's not the industry's fault, that's just plain laziness.

The real kicker is the age they're talking about. Five. Five-year-olds being exposed to nicotine. We're talking about kids who can barely tie their shoelaces, and these companies are already lining them up for a lifetime of addiction. They say schools are the new battlegrounds. What a shocker. If it's in schools, you can bet your last shilling it's everywhere else too. It's a "child protection issue," they cry. It’s a public health issue. It’s a national development issue. Spare me.

And the health costs? Astronomical. Apparently, for every shilling the tobacco industry makes, Kenya coughs up two to three shillings in healthcare and lost productivity. Nearly half of patients with serious chronic diseases have used tobacco. It's no wonder they're pushing this bill so hard. It's not just about saving kids; it's about saving the country from going bankrupt on sick people. Maybe Parliament will finally get its act together, but don't hold your breath. We saw how long it took for [Uasin Gishu's 120,000 Residents Beg Parliament for Land Titles Amidst Decades of Delays](/posts/uasin-gishu-s-120-000-residents-beg-parliament-for-land-titles-amidst-decades-of-delays) to get anywhere.

They want to ban flavors that appeal to kids, limit nicotine, and slap massive health warnings on everything. They even want to ban disposable vapes and keep these things a good 100 meters away from schools. Internationally recognized standards, they claim. Other countries have banned them outright. But here? We're still debating while the industry keeps raking in the cash and hooking the next generation.

These so called "nicotine cessation tools" are apparently being used by university students more than adult smokers. Makes you wonder, doesn't it? If it's meant to help people quit, why is it designed to look like a toy and taste like candy? The industry's objective is crystal clear: create lifelong customers. And our parliamentarians, bless their cotton socks, are still deciding if they want to act before it's too late. Don't be surprised if they wait until our kids are coughing up their lungs before they finally sign anything.
