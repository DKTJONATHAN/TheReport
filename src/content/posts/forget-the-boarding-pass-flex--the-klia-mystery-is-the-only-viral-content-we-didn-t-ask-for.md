We all know the drill: post the passport cover, the overpriced airport latte, and a sassy caption about "destinations" to make your ex jealous. But while the rest of us were busy faking it till we make it, one Kenyan woman was living a literal psychological thriller at Kuala Lumpur International Airport. Found wandering around like she had just walked out of a "Black Mirror" episode, she could not remember her name, her family, or why she was even in Malaysia. It took social media detectives on Facebook and X to turn her trauma into a trending topic before the authorities even bothered to check their notifications.

The plot thickened faster than a soap opera script once she landed in a hospital bed. Doctors say she has "stabilized," which is medical speak for "we still do not know what happened." Apparently, she had selective amnesia that only let her remember a single phrase. But once the government stepped in, she miraculously remembered how to speak Swahili, English, and Kikuyu. It is funny how a little bit of official attention can jog the memory, or maybe she just realized that being a mystery woman in a foreign ward is not as glamorous as the "soft life" influencers promised on TikTok.

Of course, the State Department for Diaspora Affairs could not wait to jump on the PR bandwagon. Principal Secretary Roseline Njogu was quick to "confirm" the rescue, praising her team like they had just won an Oscar for Best Supporting Actor in a Crisis. It is the kind of performative efficiency we see whenever the government needs to distract us from the fact that [Kenya’s 'Black Gold' is Just Another Black Hole for Your Taxes](/posts/kenya-s--black-gold--is-just-another-black-hole-for-your-taxes). They want us to believe they have got our backs, but let us be real: you are only a priority if your plight gets enough retweets to embarrass the embassy.

This is not just an isolated case of bad luck; it is part of a growing trend of Kenyans getting sold a dream and waking up in a cage. Just this month, over 600 people were pulled out of scam compounds in Cambodia where they were working 16-hour shifts under the threat of violence. Then there were the 27 geniuses who thought they were going to Russia to paint drones, only to end up on the front lines in Ukraine. It seems the only thing being "assembled" abroad these days is a collection of horror stories that the recruitment agencies conveniently forget to mention in their shiny brochures.

Now the government is begging everyone to register on their diaspora portal. It is a classic move: "Give us your data so we can 'save' you later." They claim it is for our safety, but given their track record, it feels more like a way to keep tabs on who is actually making money elsewhere. If you are planning to fly out to chase a bag, just remember that the "rights and welfare" they talk about usually only kick in after you have become a viral sensation for all the wrong reasons. Stay safe out there, because the embassy will not see you until the internet does.
