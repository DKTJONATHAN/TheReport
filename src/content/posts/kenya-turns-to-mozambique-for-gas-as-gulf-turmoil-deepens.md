Kenya Turns to Mozambique for Gas As Gulf Turmoil Deepens - Another Day, Another Deal for Ruto











So, here we are again. President Ruto, our esteemed leader, is jetting off to secure more gas. This time, it’s Mozambique. Apparently, the usual suspects - Nigeria, the Middle East - are too risky with all this global drama. The Russia-Ukraine war, the Middle East conflict, and the lingering ghost of Covid-19 are making our energy supply look as stable as a rickety boda boda. It’s a classic case of “out of the frying pan and into the… well, let's hope it’s not another fire.”

Mozambique, bless their heart, has enough gas offshore to power Africa for decades. One hundred trillion cubic feet. That’s enough to keep the Nairobi CBD lights on and maybe even power the endless streams of meetings and forums that seem to fill our calendar. Ruto is talking about strengthening Africa's unity and regional integration. It’s a nice sentiment, I suppose, but let’s be honest, this is about securing our own backyard before it gets too cold, or too expensive.

The real driver here, as always, is us. The Kenyan household. We’re ditching charcoal and firewood faster than a politician ditches a broken promise, all for that sweet LPG cooking convenience. Epra data tells us demand is booming. So, while the presidents are busy forging alliances, the real pressure is on to keep our stoves lit. Ruto’s promising partnerships, trade fairs, and cutting down those pesky tariff barriers. Let's hope this isn't just another Addis Theatre and Runda Bubbles situation, where all the talk leads to very little action.

Meanwhile, Kenya is busy building a massive LPG facility in Mombasa. Taifa Gas is almost ready, and another one is being built with a Nigerian company. Thirty thousand tonnes of storage capacity each. That’s meant to shore up our domestic demand and make us a regional supplier. It sounds impressive, but remember, these projects have a habit of taking longer and costing more than advertised. We've seen this movie before.

President Chapo, on his visit, is touting their 20 legal instruments already in place with Kenya. Politics, diplomacy, defence, immigration, transportation - you name it, they’ve got a paper for it. He’s also singing the tune of public-private partnerships. The real question is, will this gas deal translate into tangible benefits for the average Kenyan, or will it just be another headline to boost the government’s image? Given our history, I’m not holding my breath.

This whole scramble for gas, driven by global instability, highlights a familiar pattern. We’re always looking for the next best thing to keep the lights on and the cooking gas flowing, often reliant on external sources. While strengthening regional ties is a noble goal, let's not forget the home truth: deals are only good if they actually deliver. And for now, it’s just more talk and promises, with the real impact yet to be seen.
