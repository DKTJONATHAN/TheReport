The so-called Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE), a pet project of Elon Musk that injected a cadre of inexperienced youngsters into the heart of the US government, has largely disbanded, but its legacy of disruption and questionable advancement continues. Fourteen months after its introduction, the operatives who were given keys to federal agencies with little oversight have now dispersed, many finding themselves in positions of even greater responsibility, both in public service and private ventures, often with ties back to Musk's Silicon Valley empire.

The damage wrought by DOGE is undeniable. Over 300,000 federal workers were reportedly fired. The US Agency for International Development (USAID) was systematically dismantled, a move with potentially devastating global humanitarian consequences, including the cessation of sanitation projects and the slashing of food aid for vulnerable populations, potentially leading to millions of preventable deaths. Even basic government functions, like Social Security Agency phone lines, have seen increased wait times for assistance. Yet, despite DOGE's documented chaos and failure to achieve its stated goals, its former members have been elevated, a testament to a system that rewards disruption over competence.

Edward Coristine, a 19-year-old with no prior government experience who interned at Neuralink and worked for a startup employing former blackhat hackers, is a prime example. He floated through various agencies, allegedly involved in attempts to upload sensitive data to unsecured servers at the Social Security Administration and present during forced job defense sessions at the General Services Administration. He was also part of the team that helped shutter USAID. Now, Coristine has landed as the engineering lead at the newly formed National Design Studio and continues to aid the Trump administration's efforts to target perceived "fraud," even assisting in identifying organizations for viral smear campaigns.

Gavin Kliger, another early DOGE member, moved from Databricks to advising on technology and delivery at the Office of Personnel Management. He was involved in the early disruption of agencies like USAID and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. Now, Kliger holds the title of Chief Data Officer at the Department of Defense, a position where he has been implicated in negotiations with Anthropic, a company previously banned by the administration. The Department of Defense's response to inquiries about Kliger's work remains vague, stating only that the "DOGE team continues to execute the Secretary’s priorities."

Jeremy Lewin, a former lawyer and investment banker, was instrumental in the effective shutdown of USAID. His actions led to the termination of critical treatments for diseases like tuberculosis and the reduction of humanitarian aid, with studies estimating millions of preventable deaths if these programs remain unfunded. Now serving as Undersecretary for Foreign Assistance, Humanitarian Affairs, and Religious Freedom at the State Department, Lewin approved significant funding for a controversial aid group and steered US contributions to UN programs that exclude critical regions like Yemen and Afghanistan. His stated philosophy, "The best foreign assistance is that which ends," reveals a clear agenda of dismantling rather than supporting global aid.

Nate Cavanaugh and Justin Fox, former DOGE operatives who led the takeover and subsequent dismantling of the US Institute of Peace and placed the staff of the Interagency Council on Homelessness on administrative leave, have transitioned to the private sector. They are now co-founders of an AI startup called Special, a "technology investment platform" focused on crypto, whose website features Musk and Argentine president Javier Milei. Fox even admitted to using an AI language model to scan government contracts for terms related to "diversity, equity, and inclusion," a clear indication of their continued ideological agenda.

The story of the DOGE operatives is not one of redemption or lessons learned. It is a stark illustration of how unchecked ambition, often cloaked in the guise of efficiency or anti-fraud measures, can dismantle critical institutions and elevate individuals with demonstrated records of disruption and a disregard for established processes. Their continued influence, whether in government or the private sector, suggests that the rot within the system runs deeper than a single, chaotic initiative.
