So, Covid-19 hit and suddenly everyone's scrambling, like a herd of wildebeest seeing a lion. Our "healthcare system" - if you can even call it that - cracked faster than a chapati left out in the sun. Turns out, when you’ve got more patients than doctors, things get messy. And now, Hantavirus has folks whispering about quarantines again. You know, the same quarantines that shut down everything while most Kenyans need to physically show up at a clinic just to get a headache pill. Is this system "equipped"? Please.

Now they're pushing this "telemedicine" as the saviour of the nation. Oh, how brilliant! Just whip out your smartphone, and *poof*, your health problems disappear. Apparently, it's going to overcome all the "social, economic, and geographical barriers." Right. Because a broken phone screen or a dead battery is easily fixed by a virtual doctor. And let's not forget the rising transport costs; this is just a way for the government to pretend they're helping while your pocket gets lighter. It reminds me of those stories about police SACCOs and their grand promises - a lot of talk, very little delivery. [The Blue-Uniformed Billionaires: Police SACCO’s Sh66 Billion Mirage](/posts/the-blue-uniformed-billionaires--police-sacco-sh66-billion-mirage)

They say telemedicine will boost "universal health coverage." What a joke. For most of us, "universal health coverage" means showing up at a public hospital and waiting for three days to see a doctor who's probably more tired than you are. Public hospitals are drowning in patients, and getting specialized care is like winning the lottery. Unless you're one of the connected few, you'll be stuck in the queue, just like everyone else.

And mental health? Apparently, it’s 13 percent of our problems, but primary care offers about as much help as a chocolate teapot. MindFiti is out there connecting people to therapists anonymously. That’s nice, I guess. But can an app really fix deep-seated issues when the entire system is designed to make you feel worse? It’s a band-aid on a gaping wound.

Sure, there are policies and acts trying to regulate this digital health circus, but they’re as effective as a sieve in a downpour. The Kenya Medical Practitioners and Dentists Union is trying to get a handle on it, but what happens when things go wrong? Patient safety threatened by poorly thought-out legal frameworks? Shocker.

The real challenges are the usual suspects: cost, digital literacy, and systems that can’t talk to each other. These "innovations" are expensive, and most Kenyans are still figuring out how to use their basic smartphones, let alone navigate complex health platforms. So, while they're busy talking about telemedicine's "full potential," the rest of us are still stuck in the mud, waiting for a ride that never comes.
