FINAL KENYAN COPS BACK FROM HAITI MISSION AFTER 22 MONTHS - WHAT A SHOW!

WARNING: Kenyan police are back from their expensive Haiti deployment, but don't expect miracles.

THEY'RE BACK. The last of Kenya's finest, or so we're told, have landed at JKIA after nearly two years playing cowboy in Haiti. About time, too. We've heard all the platitudes from Monica Juma about valour and laying foundations, but let's be real. This was a costly circus, and for what? Some "place of pride" among nations? We'll see how that pays the bills.

The plane landed at 1828 hours on Tuesday, a grand finale to a mission that cost us God-knows-how-much. Apparently, it was all about restoring order and curbing gang violence in Port-au-Prince. Conveniently, they managed to stabilize the airport and seaport - you know, the places you'd need to get out of quickly if things went south. And now, we hand over to some new UN-backed force. Let's hope they have better luck, or at least a better budget.

Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen and IG Douglas Kanja trotted all the way to Haiti to oversee the "final drawdown." Sounds like a taxpayer-funded holiday, if you ask me. They met with our contingent commander and Consul General. All very official, very important. Meanwhile, back home, we're still grappling with basic issues, but hey, at least we're "lit a candle of hope" somewhere else.

The mission, which kicked off in June 2024, had over 800 Kenyan officers at its peak, supported by a smattering of other nations. They claim it's stabilized key infrastructure and enabled humanitarian access. But let's not forget the sheer chaos Haiti is still in. It’s like putting a band-aid on a bullet wound. This whole operation feels like another one of those grand gestures that ends up costing a fortune with little tangible return, not unlike some of the financial markets we’ve seen [betting on a blood-soaked pause](/posts/the-two-week-delusion--global-markets-bet-on-a-blood-soaked-pause).

So, mission accomplished, they say. The officers are home, probably exhausted and ready for a break. Let's just hope the "foundation for the next phase of stabilization" they supposedly laid actually sticks this time. Because if not, this whole two-year deployment will just be another expensive footnote in our history, a costly bit of international posturing.







The last contingent of Kenyan police officers has finally touched down at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport, concluding a nearly two-year deployment in Haiti. The aircraft, operated by national carrier Kenya Airways, landed at 1828hrs on Tuesday, marking the official end of Kenya’s historic overseas security mission under the United Nations Security Council (UNSC)-mandated Multinational Security Support (MSS) Mission.

Former National Security Advisor Monica Juma confirmed the departure and arrival, praising the officers for their "valour and fidelity to their mandate." She claimed they have earned Kenya "a place of pride among the community of nations" and "lit a candle of hope" for Haiti, laying a foundation for its stabilization. Juma also acknowledged Kenya Airways' "critical logistical role" and President William Ruto's "strategic leadership."

Interior Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen and Inspector General of Police Douglas Kanja were present in Port-au-Prince on April 27 to supervise the final withdrawal and meet the remaining Kenyan contingent. They were received by MSS Contingent Commander Senior Assistant Inspector General Godfrey Otunge and Kenya’s Consul General to Haiti Noor Gabow. The Kenya-led deployment, which began in June 2024, aimed to restore order and curb gang violence in the Haitian capital.

At its peak, the multinational force included over 800 Kenyan officers, alongside personnel from various other countries. The mission is credited with stabilizing key infrastructure like the international airport, seaport, and major transport corridors, facilitating humanitarian access and restoring some public security. However, with the withdrawal complete, responsibility now shifts to a newly established 5,500-member United Nations-backed Gang Suppression Force (GSF).

The narrative of a "mission accomplished" rings a bit hollow when one considers the ongoing instability in Haiti and the substantial financial outlay this deployment likely represented. While the officers may be home, the question remains: what tangible, long-term benefits has this significant investment yielded for Kenya, beyond the pronouncements of pride and hope? It feels like another grand gesture, another headline, without a clear resolution to the underlying issues, much like the cyclical nature of global markets [betting on a blood-soaked pause](/posts/the-two-week-delusion--global-markets-bet-on-a-blood-soaked-pause).

With the forces now withdrawn, the focus shifts to whether the new Gang Suppression Force can build upon whatever foundation was laid. For now, the officers are back, and the public is left to digest the cost and the proclaimed success of a mission that spanned continents and nearly two years.
