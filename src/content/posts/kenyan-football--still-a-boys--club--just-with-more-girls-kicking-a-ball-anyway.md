So, the Harambee Starlets are off to Wafcon, first time in ten years. Big deal. And this time, it’s a woman coaching them. Revolutionary, I know. It’s meant to be proof of how far the game has come, from dusty school fields to facing down Morocco, Senegal and Algeria. But let’s be honest, Enez Mango jetting off to Romania for proper facilities and pay while the local league drowns in debt? That’s not progress, that's just showing how far behind we are. She talks about football saving her education, which is sweet, but mostly it sounds like a scramble for any opportunity that isn't tied to a stagnant local system.

And then there's the Junior Starlets making waves. Great. Another team getting a taste of the world stage while their senior counterparts are still begging for sponsorship. They talk about socio-economic empowerment and gender equality, which sounds nice on paper. But the reality is a Sh10,000 to Sh15,000 monthly stipend that barely covers a player's bus fare, let alone medical expenses. Meanwhile, their male counterparts are earning upwards of Sh50,000. It’s the same old story, just with a different jersey. This whole Wafcon appearance feels more like a desperate plea for attention than a celebration of genuine advancement. It's a good distraction, though. Much like anything else that distracts us from the real issues, [The Digital Panopticon Enters Emerging Markets], this football saga serves its purpose.

They keep mentioning the rise of new clubs and strengthening structures, but then you hear about Gaspo and Wadadia folding because there’s no money. Where’s the "strengthening" in that? The FKF has all these divisions, but for what? To have more teams struggling to keep the lights on? The mandatory female coach rule is a FIFA tick-box exercise, not a revolution. It means more women get a title, but are they getting the resources to actually make a difference, or are they just part of the façade?

Ann Aluoch, bless her heart, is still talking about potential and promising futures. Maternity policies are a "setback," medical insurance is a "major gap," and the stipends are a joke. She’s right, of course. The gap between men's and women's football is not just wide, it's a chasm. While men’s clubs get the prime stadiums, women are left with school grounds. This isn't about "growing the game"; it's about a perpetual cycle of underfunding and underappreciation, dressed up as progress.

And the government? They flag off teams with presidential handshakes and photo ops. It’s cheap publicity. While Enez is getting world-class treatment in Romania, back home, players are lucky if they get a proper physio. The idea that football is a professional career path for these women is a cruel joke when they can't even afford to get injured. The image of the country is enhanced, sure, but at what cost to the players themselves?

So yes, the Starlets are going to Wafcon. Let's watch them get hammered by Morocco, Senegal, and Algeria. It'll be a good reminder that talent isn't enough when you're fighting with one hand tied behind your back, while the other is busy begging for scraps. The real story isn't about how far the game has come, but how desperately it’s still trying to crawl out of the mud.
