The March 7 deadline is fast approaching, and anyone with a brain in Nairobi knew this "broad-based" marriage was a sham from the jump. We were told this MoU between Ruto and Raila was the magic pill for our national headaches, but it has turned out to be just another bitter dose of political theater. Now that the clock is ticking, the Orange Democratic Movement is falling apart at the seams while the ruling party sharpens its knives.

Ruto and Raila promised us a new era of cooperation, but we are used to big talk that leads to empty pockets. It is the same energy as [The AFCON 2027 Billion-Shilling Ghost: Why Ruto's Stadium Promises Might Be Kenya's Biggest Sports Scam](/posts/the-afcon-2027-billion-shilling-ghost-why-rutos-stadium-promises-might-be-kenyas-biggest-sports-scam), where the only thing actually being constructed is a new way to ghost the public. While they argue over interpretations of an agreement, the rest of us are just trying to survive the latest tax hike.

Inside ODM, the drama is peaking. They are coming for Edwin Sifuna because he refuses to read from the same dusty script as the party elites. The party is now hiding behind legal technicalities, claiming tribunals lack the jurisdiction to hear his petition. It is a classic Nairobi move: when you cannot win the argument, you try to disqualify the referee. The "Orange resolve" we used to hear about is now just a flickering candle in a very cold room.

Meanwhile, the UDA crowd is not even pretending to be partners anymore. Anthony Mwaura is already out here bragging about national backing and fielding candidates in every corner of the country. They used Raila to calm the streets, and now that the fire is out, they are ready to kick him to the curb. It was never a government of national unity; it was a temporary ceasefire in a war for resources.

By the time March 7 rolls around, the only thing that will be implemented is another round of disappointment. Whether the MoU stands or falls does not matter to the person stuck in traffic on Ngong Road. The players might change their jerseys, but the game remains rigged against the spectator. This is not politics; it is just a high-stakes hustle where the house always wins.
