Another day, another "cooperation" agreement in Nairobi that smells like a funeral for devolution. Senator Edwin Sifuna is shouting from the rooftops about this Sh80 billion deal, and for once, the man isn't just "making noise" for the cameras. It is a power grab through the back door, designed to subdue the county leadership and turn the Governor’s office into a branch office for the National Government. We are being told it's for our own good, but in this city, "help" from the state usually comes with a very heavy price tag.

Look at the math and tell me if you see a partnership. Out of the 12 members on the steering committee, a whopping two-thirds are appointees of the National Government. Sakaja isn’t a Governor anymore; according to the structure, he’s playing subservient to the Prime Cabinet Secretary. He has effectively become the new Deputy Governor of Nairobi, but without the benefit of a veto. It’s a takeover disguised as teamwork, and we are all expected to clap because someone mentioned billions of shillings.

The "public participation" bit is the biggest joke currently running in the 254. They signed the deal first and then told us they’d ask for our opinion later. That isn't engagement; it’s an insult to our intelligence. They’ve given us a measly 14 days to "amend" a document that is already set in stone. It reminds me of the tactics used in [The TikTok Begging Economy: How Kenyan Creators Extract Millions Through Emotional Appeals](/posts/tiktok-begging-syndicate-kenyan-creators-emotional-appeals-millions) - it's all about the performance and the optics while the real transaction happens where the sun doesn't shine.

Then there is the "generosity" of the Sh80 billion. President Ruto is flaunting this figure like he’s doing Nairobi a personal favor, yet the National Government owes this county over Sh100 billion in unpaid rates and bills. It is like a debtor who owes you a thousand bob giving you five hundred and expecting a "Thank You" card and a seat at your dinner table. If the state simply cleared its debts, the County Assembly could ring-fence those funds for roads, drainage, and garbage without Sakaja having to kneel at State House every Tuesday.

We have seen this movie before with the NMS experiment, and it ended in a Sh16 billion hole of pending bills and contractors who are still waiting for their paychecks. Sakaja himself once called it a misadventure, yet here he is, walking straight back into the lion’s den. Sifuna is right to be cynical; instead of creating new committees to manage Nairobi, the government should just transmit the shareable revenue by the 15th of every month as the law demands. But in Nairobi, following the law is never as trendy as signing a "historic" MOU.
