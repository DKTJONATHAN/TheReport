So, Sabastian Sawe, this chap from Barsombe village, has apparently run a marathon in under two hours. April 2026, Rotterdam, 1:59:41. Big deal. Suddenly he’s the golden boy, the first to do the impossible. Makes you wonder how many other "impossibles" are just waiting for the right sponsorship deal and a well-timed gust of wind. They’re calling it a world-record-eligible performance, but let's be honest, it’s a race for the highest bidder these days.

His story reads like a textbook "Kenyan runner rises from humble beginnings." Grew up on a farm, ran to school, parents were small-scale farmers. His mum was even a sprinter, dreams dashed by an early pregnancy. Sounds familiar, doesn't it? It’s the same old script, just with a different name this time. His grandfather, Musa Sitenei, managed to turn himself into a successful farmer. Big deal. Meanwhile, Sawe’s parents, bless their hearts, “supported his passion.” Of course they did. What else are you going to do when your kid’s got that magic in his legs that can apparently buy you a new car from the president?

The kid went to St Patrick's High School, a devoted Catholic. Mud walls, dirt floors, no electricity. All the ingredients for a gritty backstory that sells. He started as a middle-distance runner, then thanks to his uncle Abraham Chepkirwok, a former Ugandan record holder, he switched to long-distance. Apparently, he was late for a race and ended up in a 5000m. Won it. Convenient, right? Then he joined some club, trained under a coach, got injured in 2020, and missed a debut because of COVID. The usual bumps in the road for anyone trying to climb the ladder.

His “breakthrough” came in 2022 at the Seville Half Marathon. Pacemaker, then won it. Then a string of wins in Rome, Bahrain, set a Kenyan record in the one-hour run. 2023 and 2024 saw him seventh at World Cross Country, won a few more races. Then the Valencia Marathon debut in 2024, won it in 2:02:05. Followed by London and Berlin. But the real kicker? The 25 out-of-competition drug tests by the Athletics Integrity Unit. Amidst increased scrutiny of Kenyan athletes. Funny how that happens when someone starts breaking records, isn't it? It’s almost as if some of these stories about integrity have a bit more grit than the official reports, much like the drama seen in cases like [RECEIPTS OR RETRACTIONS? THE MESSY TECH DRAMA IN THE MATLALA CASE](/posts/receipts-or-retractions--the-messy-tech-drama-in-the-matlala-case).

And then April 2026, London. The sub-2-hour marathon. 1:59:30. Everyone’s hailing him, praising him. Eliud Kipchoge and Paula Radcliffe, no doubt getting their own hefty bonuses for endorsing the next big thing. Kipchoge’s Instagram post, oh, how touching. The man earned about KSh 34.5 million from that one race, not counting sponsorships from brands like Adidas. Plus, President Ruto hands him KSh 8 million and a new car with custom plates. Because that’s how you reward talent. Not with proper infrastructure or support for the grassroots, but with a car and cash for a few hours of pavement pounding.

So, here we have Sabastian Sawe, the epitome of the Kenyan success story. From mud walls to millions. He's the first official sub-2-hour marathoner. But remember, behind every fairy tale, there’s usually a shady deal, a hidden cost, or a whole lot of people who will never get a sniff of that kind of money, no matter how much they run to school or work the fields. This is just another chapter in the grand, profitable circus of elite sports.
