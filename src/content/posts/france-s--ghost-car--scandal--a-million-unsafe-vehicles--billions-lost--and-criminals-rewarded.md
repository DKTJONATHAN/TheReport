So much for efficient government. France's grand experiment in speeding up car registrations by handing over control to dealerships has achieved precisely the opposite of what it set out to do. Instead of a streamlined process, the nation is now grappling with a million "ghost cars" on its roads, a testament to the predictable consequences of misplaced trust and a rush to privatize.

The state auditor, the Cour des Comptes, has laid bare the rot: a fraudulent system that allows fake dealerships to manipulate the official vehicle licensing agency (SIV). This isn't just about lost tax revenue, which runs into hundreds of millions of euros. It’s about the fundamental erosion of public safety. Unsafe cars and lorries, with who knows what mechanical deficiencies, are now navigating French highways, a direct result of a system that was "mistakenly" built on "good faith." A rather naive assumption when dealing with human nature, wouldn't you agree?

The report explicitly states that vulnerabilities in this privatized system have opened the floodgates to "the whole gamut of criminality." From petty thieves to organized crime syndicates, all have found an easy entry point to exploit the registration system for their own illicit purposes. It’s a self-inflicted wound, born from a decision made in 2017 to outsource a core government function, supposedly to improve efficiency. The irony is as thick as the exhaust fumes from these unregistered vehicles.

The solution, it seems, was to empower car dealers with direct access to the registry, a move that bypassed the oversight of some 2,000 civil servants. This transfer of power, coupled with a naive reliance on honesty, has proven to be a catastrophic miscalculation. The result is a million unregistered vehicles, a significant financial drain on the state, and a stark reminder that when you open the door to greed, it tends to walk right in and make itself at home. France's "ghost car" scandal is not just a story about administrative blunders; it's a case study in how well-intentioned reforms can pave the road to chaos and criminality.
