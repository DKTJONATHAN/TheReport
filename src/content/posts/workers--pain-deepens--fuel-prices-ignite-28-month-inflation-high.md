So, the numbers are out. Inflation shot up again in May, hitting its highest point in over two years. Of course it did. Blame it on the Iran war, they say. As if we here in Nairobi don't have enough to worry about with our own sinking ship. Now we get to pay more for everything because of some conflict thousands of miles away. That means less money in your pocket, which, let's be honest, was already looking pretty pathetic.

The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics tells us inflation is now at 6.7 percent. Higher than last month, higher than almost any time since early last year. And what’s driving this? Transport costs up a whopping 16.5 percent. Food and drinks? Up 9.4 percent. Housing and utilities also creeping up. These aren't luxuries, people. This is the bare minimum needed to survive. Our purchasing power has been getting hammered for years, and now this. It feels like we're stuck in a perpetual downward spiral, a constant reminder of the ground reality beneath the continental dreams being sold from the top, like [The $3 Trillion Mirage: Ruto Sells Continental Dreams While Nairobi Suffers the Ground Reality](/posts/the--3-trillion-mirage--ruto-sells-continental-dreams-while-nairobi-suffers-the-ground-reality).

The Kenya Bankers Association estimates workers' purchasing power has fallen by up to 12 percent in the last five years. Twelve percent! That's not some abstract economic theory; that's your rent going up, your kids' school fees feeling heavier, and the little bit of joy you might have afforded disappearing. And it’s not just the price of things. Taxes are higher, statutory deductions like the housing levy and health insurance are draining us, and even your NSSF contributions are climbing. Your salary might look the same on paper, but what it can actually *buy* is shrinking faster than a politician's promise.

The Central Bank is probably sweating too, with this inflation spike meaning they can't just slash interest rates willy-nilly. So don't expect those loan rates to drop anytime soon. Meanwhile, the government is busy playing whack-a-mole with fuel prices. They cut VAT, they offer a Sh10 discount on diesel after protests, but the global market is still dictating prices. Petrol is now over Sh214 a litre in Nairobi. It’s a joke, a cruel, expensive joke, leaving everyone feeling the squeeze. This is just another chapter in the ongoing saga of [Police Federation Fraud Arrests Expose Another Layer of Institutional Decay](/posts/police-federation-fraud-arrests-expose-another-layer-of-institutional-decay), where the system seems designed to bleed the average person dry.
