So, our boy Paul Njoroge, a Mediamax chap, gets a beating of his life for what? Sharing a headline. Apparently, Joram Wataka, the personal assistant to some Trans Nzoia Senator Allan Chesang', didn't like that a Standard Newspaper story about his boss ended up on Njoroge's WhatsApp status. Because in this country, opinions shared on a phone are apparently cause for physical assault.

The video, courtesy of Gilbert Sitati (who, bless his heart, kept filming instead of running, unlike some of us who’d be halfway to Mombasa), shows Njoroge begging for mercy, promising to delete whatever offensive post it was. But Wataka, presumably fueled by the same sense of entitlement that makes politicians believe they're above scrutiny, went ahead and dished out the blows. It’s the classic Kenyan playbook: deny, deflect, and assault.

After the thrashing, Njoroge recounts how Wataka lured him, all about "staining Senator Chesang's image." Oh, the horror! A journalist daring to report on alleged corruption involving an ambulance tender. It’s as if these politicians think they’re living in a telenovela where their every move is sacred. This whole circus reminds me of how we’re often a nation of middlemen, scrambling for scraps while the big players play their games [A Nation of Middlemen Starving in the Cold](/posts/a-nation-of-middlemen-starving-in-the-cold).

And the icing on this pathetic cake? Both Njoroge and his attacker filed police reports. Wataka even managed to grab a P3 form, the universal get-out-of-jail-free card for the politically connected. The Police Inspector General is being called upon to act, as if he hasn't got his hands full with actual crime. But hey, at least the Kenya Union of Journalists is making noise. Let’s see if that translates to anything more than hot air.

The whole brouhaha apparently stems from a Standard exposé on an alleged Harambee House scandal involving Chesang and a dubious ambulance supply tender. The police confirmed arrests but, of course, denied any scandal link. Because that’s how it works here: deny, deny, deny, until the story fades and everyone moves on to the next manufactured drama.

This isn't just an attack on a journalist; it's an attack on the very idea that facts matter. It's a stark reminder that in this country, speaking truth to power often comes with a price tag, and sometimes that price is paid in bruises.
