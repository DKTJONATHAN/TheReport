So, the Competition Authority of Kenya (CAK) has finally decided to stop pretending everything is hunky-dory in our online marketplaces. They're "stepping up scrutiny," apparently. This comes after years of letting these online platforms run wild, fleecing Kenyans with one hand while promising convenience with the other. It's a whole $7 billion economy now, they say, and naturally, where there's quick money, there are vultures.

Government bigwigs, academics, and industry types all gathered for a chat, no doubt patting themselves on the back for this "digital revolution." Meanwhile, the rest of us are left sifting through misleading ads, hidden fees, and downright scams. They're worried about "deceptive practices" and "fraudulent online schemes" - you know, the same old song and dance that's been happening since M-Pesa first became a thing. It's almost like they think this is a new problem, not a direct consequence of letting tech giants dictate the game.

CAK Chairman Charles Mahinda is talking about evolving competition enforcement. Evolve? These platforms have been influencing what we buy, how we find it, and what we pay for it for ages. Now, they're scrambling to ensure "transparency and accountability." It's a bit like closing the stable door after the horse has not only bolted but has also been sold off for parts. They're finally noticing market concentration and unfair practices, which, shocker, disadvantage the little guys. Remember how digital drivers' pockets got squeezed after fuel hikes? This is the same story, just on a different digital stage. [Digital Drivers' Pockets Get Squeezed After Fuel Hikes](/posts/digital-drivers--pockets-get-squeezed-after-fuel-hikes)

Director-General David Kemei chirps about people understanding their rights to avoid "harmful practices." Bless his heart. As if the average Kenyan, busy trying to make ends meet, has the time or the legal know-how to decipher the labyrinthine terms and conditions of every dodgy app. They're talking about coordinating agencies, but let's be real, when has that ever translated into swift, decisive action that actually benefits the consumer?

The policymakers are supposedly balancing innovation with safeguards. They've broadened access and lowered barriers, sure, but at what cost? The "clear rules governing business conduct and consumer welfare" are still more of a wish list than a reality. More discussions are expected, naturally. Because in Kenya, we're great at discussing problems; solving them is a whole other ballgame. So, keep your eyes peeled, Kenyans. The digital economy is growing, and so is the potential for you to get fleeced.
