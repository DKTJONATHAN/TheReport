So, the yanks and their buddies decided to play tough with Iran, and guess who gets the short end of the stick? Us, the poor saps in Africa. Suddenly, jet fuel prices are going through the roof because, surprise, surprise, shipping lanes through the Strait of Hormuz are getting jammed up. It’s like these guys never learned that messing with oil flow has consequences, especially for a continent that's already practically begging for imports. Seventy percent of our fuel comes through that choked waterway, so when it gurgles, we choke.

And don’t even think about fixed prices. These charter types, the ones flying you to that emergency medical situation you probably didn't need to be in, are getting hit hard. They’re quoting prices on arrival, and by the time they land, the cost has jumped. This isn’t some abstract economic theory; it’s about people like Jannie de Klerk having to eat losses because the price of fuel decides to have a tantrum within hours. It's a recipe for disaster when you’re trying to run a business, and frankly, it’s starting to feel like Nairobi is drowning in its own chaotic mess, just like those unfortunate souls in the recent floods. [Nairobi Drowning in Its Own Mess](/posts/66-dead-in-kenya-floods--heavy-rains-continue--nairobi-drowning-in-its-own-mess).

African airlines are taking the biggest beating. While global carriers see fuel as 20-25% of their costs, for us, it’s a whopping 30-40%, and for some, like FlySafair, it’s a staggering 50-55%. They’re already talking about R35,000 extra per flight hour. And it’s not like we have much stock. Kenya’s got a couple of months, sure, but Zambia is down to ten days. Ten days! That’s not a supply, that’s a countdown to grounded planes.

The whole situation is a perfect storm of bad planning and geopolitical stupidity. Our own refineries are practically relics, and now we’re at the mercy of international conflicts we had no part in starting. Europe’s sweating because they rely on that Middle Eastern crude, but at least they’ve got bigger inventories and some countries are smart enough to hoard. We’re stuck here, watching our already expensive flights get even pricier, all thanks to a war on the other side of the planet.

Airlines are scrambling, slapping on surcharges like they’re going out of fashion, or trying to wiggle out of contracts with clauses that let them screw you over mid-journey. Airlink's not doing surcharges on existing tickets, but don’t be surprised when your next booking costs an arm and a leg. They might even start cutting flights to save a buck. It’s a race to the bottom, and the consumer is always the one who ends up at the very bottom.

So next time you book a flight, remember this: your ticket price is no longer just about getting you from A to B. It’s also about paying for a war you’re not involved in, dealing with supply chains that are about as reliable as a politician’s promise, and subsidizing the sheer incompetence of everyone involved. Enjoy the ride.
