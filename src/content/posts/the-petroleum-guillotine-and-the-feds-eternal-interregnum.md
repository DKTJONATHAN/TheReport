The Federal Reserve’s recent adjustment of long-term growth forecasts from 1.8 percent to 2.0 percent is a pathetic attempt to paint a rosy picture over a decaying canvas. While they frame this as a loosening of policy, it is actually a confession. They are acknowledging that the cost of living and the cost of capital are permanently higher. This 0.2 percent shift compounds into a future that exists only on paper, ignoring the reality of a world where the dollar’s hegemony is being systematically dismantled.

International markets are now facing the sharp edge of the petroleum guillotine. Oil at $110 a barrel is not a temporary shock; it is a structural failure. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively serving as a choke point for global energy flows, the world economy is entering a phase of demand destruction that will hollow out the middle class in the West and spark instability in the Global South. The strategic petroleum reserve is a spent force, and the suspension of shipping regulations is a desperate, short-term band-aid on a severed artery.

We are told that artificial intelligence will be our salvation, with some drawing lazy parallels to the invention of the cotton gin. This is a dark irony that ignores the human cost. The cotton gin did not liberate the worker; it entrenched a system of industrial-scale exploitation by making that labor more profitable for the owners of capital. AI will likely follow this historical blueprint. It will not reduce the need for human effort; it will simply increase the intensity and surveillance of the work required to stay relevant in a machine-dominated market.

The trajectory of the Dutch East India Company serves as a grim map for our current corporate-state entanglement. Like the VOC, today’s global giants operate with the powers of a sovereign state-waging economic war, building private infrastructures, and influencing policy-while hiding behind a facade of public trading. They are currently in the high-dividend, high-corruption phase that precedes a spectacular collapse. History shows that when the debt finally outweighs the spice, the empire is the first thing to be liquidated.
