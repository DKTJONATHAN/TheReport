So, the CCTV footage shows a middle-aged fellow, Fortune Muthina Kinyanzui, 34, being tossed into a white double-cab like a sack of potatoes. Three masked goons, the usual suspects, and poof, he’s gone. This happened on March 26, 2026, at 5:56 pm. A fortnight he’s been missing, and his family’s frantic efforts? Hitting a wall, naturally. Because in Nairobi, when you vanish, you don’t just vanish, you become a rumour.

And let's not even get started on the suffering caused by "Uhuru's movement ban." People telling the Nation about their pain. As if this country hasn't perfected the art of making life miserable for its citizens. Remember when we thought relationships were complicated? Now, courts aren't even asking if a marriage exists, just whether it’s a meaningful one. Talk about priorities.

This whole drama feels a bit like those influencer love stories, all glitter and no substance, just with higher stakes and less flattering lighting. It’s all for show, isn't it? The performative romance, the heavy cost of Kenya’s influencer industrial complex, it’s just a scaled-down version of the real-life theatrics we’re forced to endure.

Meanwhile, across the pond, a UK coroner is saying a March 2025 accident could have been avoided. Imagine that. Something actually preventable. Here, we’re just living with the avoidable, aren't we? And a 21-year-old can’t even reach her sister late at night. Because, you know, priorities. The system is more interested in whether our medical tea is fetching a good price for Uncle Sam than in basic safety.

The family’s plea is simple: "Produce him in court." As if that’s going to happen. This isn’t a courtroom drama, it’s just another Tuesday in the concrete jungle. Another name added to the ever-growing list of people who just… disappeared. And the CCTV footage? Just a fleeting glimpse of the rot.
