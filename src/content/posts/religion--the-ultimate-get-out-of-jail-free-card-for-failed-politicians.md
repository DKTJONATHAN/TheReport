It is the classic Kenyan political move: when the youth start carrying pangas because they have nothing to eat, tell them they need more "moral direction." Senator Mohammed Faki is currently singing that old song in Mombasa, suggesting that religious grounding is the magic wand that will stop the crime wave. It is the perfect distraction from the fact that the coastal economy is on life support and the only thing growing faster than the crime rate is the frustration of everyone under thirty.

He talks about "weak guidance structures" as if a few extra hours of religious instruction will magically make the "panga boys" forget they are hungry. It is much easier to fund a Qur’an recitation competition than it is to build a factory or fix the port. While the elite are busy protecting their interests, the rest are told to find solace in the divine. We have seen this brand of hypocrisy before, where the [nepo babies in handcuffs](/posts/nepo-babies-in-handcuffs-and-lax-pr-walks--the-week-the-rich-forgot-how-to-act) get a PR-managed pass while the poor kid from Kisauni is told he just needs more faith to stay on the straight and narrow.

Faki admits unemployment and narcotics are the real drivers, yet he pivots back to "ethical values" like a pro. Since when did a Bible study or a Madrasa session provide a three-course meal? It is a cynical strategy to keep the youth occupied with "constructive platforms" that do not actually move the needle on their bank balances. If faith could pay the bills, half of Nairobi would be living in Runda by now instead of dodging debt collectors.

The Senator wants both levels of government and private sponsors to pour money into these religious initiatives. It is a brilliant way to funnel cash into "mentorship" programs that are practically impossible to audit. Instead of investing in technical skills or actual job creation, we are being asked to subsidize moral policing. It is a lazy fix for a systemic collapse that no amount of prayer or organized mentorship is going to solve in a country where the cost of living is a literal crime.

In the end, it is just another performance for the gallery. The religious leaders get their funding, the politicians get their photo ops at competitions, and the youth remain exactly where they were: broke, frustrated, and one bad day away from a life of crime. It is the same old script played out in a different city, proving that in Kenya, the only thing more reliable than a politician’s empty promise is their ability to blame a lack of "values" for their own policy failures.
