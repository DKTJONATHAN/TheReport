President Ruto stood on the newly built Kangemi Overpass this week, painting a picture of a "clean and functional" Nairobi that none of us actually live in. It is the classic Nairobi script: a high-profile visit, some fresh paint on a few walls, and a series of grand promises about restoring order. While the motorcade rolls through cleared streets, the rest of us are still dodging the same potholes and breathing in the fumes of a city that feels more like a permanent construction site than a "capital of ambition."

They handed over 16 classrooms at Kangemi Primary to serve 4,200 learners. Do the math before you clap. That is a drop in the ocean for a school that is bursting at the seams. We are told 23,000 classrooms have been built nationwide, yet the reality on the ground remains one of crumbling infrastructure and overworked staff. It is the same old trick of using small wins to mask a systemic failure that leaves most children sitting on stones while the elite discuss "investment readiness" from the comfort of their SUVs.

The KSh 80 billion cooperation agreement between the National Government and City Hall sounds impressive until you remember how many billions have vanished into the Nairobi "black hole" before. We are promised fixed roads, water supply, and garbage collection, but the mountains of waste in our backyards say otherwise. This city is suffering from a massive [Log-Drums and Lattes: Kenya’s Identity Crisis and the New Dating App for the 'Vetted' Narcissist](/posts/log-drums-and-lattes--kenya-s-identity-crisis-and-the-new-dating-app-for-the--vetted--narcissist) where we pretend to be a global hub while the basic services are still a luxury for the average resident.

Ruto keeps mentioning the transition from CBC to CBET like it is a smooth gear shift in a luxury car. It is not. It is a chaotic mess for parents and teachers who are struggling to keep up with shifting goalposts and inadequate funding. Throwing KSh 700 billion at the problem might look good on a spreadsheet, but until that money stops being diverted into "consultancy fees" and "administration costs," the quality of learning will remain as stagnant as the sewage in Eastlands.

We do not need more tours or more ribbon-cutting ceremonies on overpasses that lead to nowhere. This "bold step" to fix the city feels more like a strategic photo op for a government that is obsessed with optics. Nairobi is not "investment-ready" just because a few more classrooms exist; it is a city on the edge, tired of being lied to by leaders who come to make citizens fight while pretending to be friends of the people. Reality is not found in a presidential speech; it is found in the dry taps and the blackouts they forgot to mention.
