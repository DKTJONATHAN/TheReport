The recent legal dustup between AI maker Anthropic and the Pentagon is less about a specific contract and more about a deeply ingrained pattern of government deception regarding surveillance. Anthropic's refusal to trust the Pentagon's assurances about data use isn't paranoia; it's a rational response to decades of documented overreach and semantic manipulation by intelligence agencies.

The core issue boils down to the US government's long-standing practice of redefining fundamental legal terms to expand its surveillance capabilities. This isn't a new phenomenon; it predates the current AI boom and traces back to interpretations of legislation like the Patriot Act and the secret workings of the FISA court. Executive Order 12333, ostensibly a framework for intelligence collection, has been a particularly potent tool.

What the public, armed with a standard English dictionary, understands as clear limitations on government surveillance, the National Security Agency (NSA) has consistently interpreted through its own specialized lexicon. Words like "target" have been stretched to encompass not just direct subjects of investigation but any data incidentally collected, even if it pertains to US citizens. This means that communications between two Americans, if they happen to mention a foreign national, can be swept up and retained.

This expansive interpretation is further enabled by how data is routed in the digital age. Even if communications remain within US borders, if they momentarily traverse international networks, they fall under the purview of Executive Order 12333. The NSA can legally intercept this data outside US jurisdiction and then, through so-called "backdoor searches," access information on US persons that they ostensibly shouldn't have collected in the first place. The promise of privacy for this incidentally collected data is often conditional, becoming void if a mention of a foreign entity is discovered.

The Snowden revelations were a stark, albeit delayed, confirmation of these practices. They exposed how government officials, when directly questioned about mass data collection on Americans, would either evade or outright lie, as was the case with then-Director of National Intelligence James Clapper. This history of obfuscation and reinterpretation is precisely why companies like Anthropic are wary of government assurances.

The current situation with Anthropic is a public, albeit messy, manifestation of this ongoing tension. It highlights that the expansion of the surveillance state has been a bipartisan effort over many years. Now, on the precipice of a new era of AI-driven surveillance, this historical distrust is more relevant than ever. The government's assertion that it will adhere to legal standards in its use of AI for surveillance is, given this track record, inherently suspect. The burden of proof, and the onus of trust, lies squarely on the government to demonstrate genuine transparency and adherence to the spirit, not just the manipulated letter, of the law.
