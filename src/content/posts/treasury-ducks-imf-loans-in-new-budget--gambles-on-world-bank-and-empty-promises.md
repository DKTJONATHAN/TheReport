Treasury Ducks IMF Loans in New Budget: Easier Said Than Done

Kenya's Treasury has decided to play hardball with the IMF, conveniently leaving their funding out of the upcoming budget. They're playing coy, claiming uncertainty about fresh talks and their "tough conditions." Translation: they're trying to sidestep the usual pain of higher taxes, job freezes, and budget cuts that always seem to follow the IMF's "help." After tasting the bitter pill of a terminated loan last year due to breached conditions, they're suddenly wary of those multibillion shilling loans.

It’s a classic Nairobi manoeuvre: pretend you don't need the handouts, especially when the International Monetary Fund is breathing down your neck. The World Bank, the IMF's slightly less stingy cousin, is expected to pick up the slack with its development policy operations. Apparently, these loans are "cheaper" and carry fewer conditions. We’ll see how long that lasts. Meanwhile, the IMF’s prescriptions for post-Covid recovery - more taxes, slashed deficits, and restructured state-owned enterprises - are now off the table, at least until the next crisis.

This move conveniently coincides with a budget that’s avoiding any major new taxes, a wise decision after the chaos of last year's protests. The Treasury knows a general election is looming in 2027, and nobody wants to be the one to introduce policies that make everyday Kenyans even more miserable. It’s all about optics, isn’t it? As for Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi's pronouncements that IMF resources aren't for plugging revenue shortfalls - sure, Jan. We’ve all heard that before.

The government’s messaging is a tangled mess. One minute they’re saying they want to minimize focus on the IMF, the next they’re emphasizing the importance of funded programs for cheap external financing and to offset domestic borrowing pressures. Governor Kamau Thugge even waxed lyrical about replacing expensive domestic borrowing with concessional financing from the IMF and World Bank to cut debt vulnerabilities. It’s like they can’t decide if they want to dance with the devil or not.

Let’s not forget the drama of March 2025, when the IMF program went south faster than a cheap suit in a Nairobi downpour. Kenya couldn’t meet conditions like restructuring Kenya Airways or keeping tabs on the fuel stabilization fund. Eleven out of sixteen conditions failed. It’s a shame, really, that basic financial discipline seems to be an optional extra. This whole charade plays out while the world is in a tizzy with the US-Israel war on Iran, pushing up oil prices and making international capital markets look like a casino run by toddlers.

So here we are, a country juggling its finances, trying to look like it has it all together while secretly praying for less demanding lenders. Abebe Selassie from the IMF himself noted Kenya is "switching towards market access," but admitted market access is "very volatile." It’s a tightrope walk, and if they slip, who will catch them? Probably the World Bank, with a slightly less stern lecture this time. Kenya is even formally asking the World Bank for emergency funding due to the Iran war. This whole "economic resilience" thing is starting to look like a poorly rehearsed play.
