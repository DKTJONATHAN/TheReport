Embakasi North MP James Gakuya woke up on a random Friday in 2026 and decided to tell us the sky is blue. During a radio interview, he warned us about the entrenched cartels strangling Nairobi, as if the average resident does not feel those hands around their neck every time they pay for water that never flows or space on a sidewalk that is supposedly public. It is the classic Nairobi political theater: identify a problem that you and your colleagues have ignored for years, then act like you have just stumbled upon a crime scene.

Gakuya says these networks manipulate tenders and hijack public contracts. Groundbreaking stuff, really. He claims there is a lack of political will to dismantle them, conveniently forgetting that he sits in the very building where that will is supposed to be manufactured. This is just another chapter in the long-running saga of [Tea, Tears, and the Art of the Kenyan Heist](/posts/tea--tears--and-the-art-of-the-kenyan-heist) where the script stays the same and only the actors change their costumes to suit the season.

According to our outspoken legislator, every stolen contract means a youth loses a job and every inflated price means less money for roads. It is a touching sentiment, but in this city, a multi-agency response is usually code for let us form a committee so we can eat the remaining budget while pretending to investigate who ate the first half. While he talks about the tangible effects on transport and land allocation, the rest of us are just trying to survive the daily extortion that has become the unofficial tax of the capital.

He wants to protect whistleblowers and reform procurement systems to close loopholes. We have heard this tune so many times the record is skipping. In Nairobi, a whistleblower is often just someone who has not been offered a big enough cut yet. To suggest that the city is entering a period of heightened scrutiny is a joke that is not even funny anymore. We are not entering a new era; we are just stuck in a loop where the people who benefit from the chaos are the ones promising to fix it.

At the end of the day, inequality deepens and social discontent fuels the fire, but the cartels are not some mysterious monsters under the bed. They are the people in bespoke suits sitting in high-end offices, probably nodding along to Gakuya's interview while planning their next public-private partnership. Nairobi does not need more scathing critiques from the inside; it needs a funeral for the hypocrisy that keeps these politicians talking while the city rots under the weight of their empty promises.
