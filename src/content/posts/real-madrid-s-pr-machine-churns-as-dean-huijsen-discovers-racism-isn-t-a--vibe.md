Oh, look, another "rising star" who forgot that his thumb works faster than his brain. Real Madrid defender Dean Huijsen is currently learning that the internet never forgets, even if you delete the post faster than a failing influencer hides a bad filler job. The 20-year-old managed to offend an entire continent by reposting some truly bottom-of-the-barrel racist "jokes" about Asian people. Honestly, at this point, these clubs should just take away their phones and give them those old Nokia bricks that only play Snake.

The "apology" was as predictable as a scripted reality TV fight. He went straight to Weibo because that is where the money is, darling, to tell his "Chinese friends" it was all a big accident. "Unintentional," he says. Right. Because we all accidentally click "repost" on a picture of a person with captions about dental floss and blindfolds. It is the classic PR move: keep the apology region-locked so the global brand does not take a hit while begging for forgiveness from the market that pays the bills.

Real Madrid is playing a dangerous game of PR gymnastics. They want to be the champions of justice when Vinicius Junior gets abused, and he shouldn't be, obviously, but when one of their own is the one posting the filth, suddenly it is just a "blunder." The hypocrisy is so thick you could cut it with a knife. While we see [10-Man Harambee Stars Stun Morocco in CHAN 2024 Thriller!](/posts/kenya-vs-morocco-1-0) and celebrate actual sporting achievements, the Bernabeu is busy trying to scrub the digital footprint of a kid who clearly missed the "Diversity and Inclusion" seminar.

This is not even the first time the Spanish giants have had to clean up a mess involving China. Remember that fan singing slurs at the Champions League final? The club acts like these are isolated incidents, but when you look at the pattern, it starts to look like a culture problem hidden behind a very expensive white shirt. If Florentino Perez is so worried about the club's image, maybe he should spend less time on UEFA investigations and more time teaching his players that racism is not a "social media mistake."

At the end of the day, Huijsen will probably get a slap on the wrist and be back on the pitch before the next commercial break. The fans demanding a global apology are shouting into a void because, in the world of elite football, "sorry" is just another word for "please keep buying our jerseys." It is messy, it is calculated, and it is exactly why nobody should look to these athletes as moral compasses. They are just kids with too much money and zero self-awareness.
