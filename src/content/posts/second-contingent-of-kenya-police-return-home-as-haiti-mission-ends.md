Haiti Mission Ends, Second Police Contingent Returns Home: So What Now?

The last batch of Kenyan police officers, the second contingent to be exact, is finally touching down from Haiti. After months of what the Interior PS Raymond Omollo calls "dedicated sacrifice" and "unwavering commitment," these soldiers of fortune are back. He’s gushing about them upholding "the highest standards of professionalism, discipline and courage." Funny, I thought we sent them to quell gangs, not to win beauty pageants.

Apparently, 980 of our finest were deployed to suppress gangs in Port-au-Prince. And now, with the UN waving its magic wand, the Multinational Security Support Mission (MSS) is kaput, replaced by the Gang Suppression Force (GSF). This new outfit, backed by the usual suspects like the US and Panama, will have a whopping 5,500 uniformed personnel. Because clearly, more bodies in uniform is the magic bullet for complex geopolitical messes, right? Just like how we’re told more police on the streets will solve crime in Nairobi - as if Nairobi is no sanctuary for the brave.

They even managed to honor three officers who paid the ultimate price for this venture. Omollo assures their families will be supported. Let's hope that "support" is more than just a well-placed handshake and a photo op. It’s a grim reminder that while the politicians pat themselves on the back for "flying the Kenyan flag with distinction," some families are left with a gaping hole and a pension that won't cover the rent.

So, the MSS is over, the GSF is in, and Kenya is still "committed to working alongside Haiti." What does that even mean in practice? Are we just the hired muscle for whatever international circus is in town? This constant deployment abroad feels less like nation-building and more like an expensive, high-risk gig economy for our military. We’re always ready to jump into someone else’s disaster, but when will we sort out our own backyard? Remember that passport scandal? It felt like we were all just pawns in someone's game then too.

The official line is that they’ve "sustained the gains made" and will "support long-term peace efforts." Sounds like a lot of political jargon to mask the fact that we sent our people into a war zone, lost some, and are now handing the baton to someone else, while still pledging our unwavering commitment. It’s a cycle we’ve seen before, and frankly, I'm not holding my breath for any real "long-term peace" to materialize.

At least the troops are home. Let's just hope they brought back more than just tales of bravery and a few more scars. And maybe, just maybe, the next time we’re asked to send our sons and daughters to a foreign land, we’ll have a clearer picture of what exactly we're signing up for.
