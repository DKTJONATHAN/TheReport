So, these young software whiz kids, all fired up with their "People's IEBC" platform, thought they could independently count votes in Ol Kalou before the official announcement. Using fancy AI and citizen volunteers, they managed to crunch nearly two-thirds of the results. Projected a landslide for some chap named Ngotho, while the UDA and Jubilee candidates were left licking dust. Sounds impressive, right? Almost makes you think technology can fix this mess. Almost.

But here's the punchline, folks. Turns out, when the real "officials" don't like what the little guys are doing, a few water cannons and a police cordon are all it takes to shut down your whole transparent operation. Volunteers got chased away, couldn't get their precious Forms 35A. So much for near real-time verification when the powers that be decide to play rough. The tech might work, but it's useless if you can't actually get the data. It’s like having the best recipe for ugali but no maize flour.

Then there's the "oops, we forgot" factor. Some volunteers just decided they had "other commitments" - probably a more lucrative gig, let's be honest. Left entire polling stations high and dry, with no one to upload the forms. The whole "AI verifies everything" dream hits a brick wall when you're relying on people who might bail at the last minute. And let's not forget the attempted cyberattack, trying to flood their system. Shows you how much they're willing to fight to keep things murky. It’s almost as frustrating as trying to get justice when someone hacks your phone, like that Oyugis syndicate bust.

The founder, Daniel Miringa, is all about lessons learned. Needs backup observers, needs partnerships with the old guard election watchers, needs official IEBC accreditation to even be allowed inside. It’s like these kids built a beautiful sports car, only to realize they needed a driver's license and a permission slip to get it on the road. They discovered that transparency isn’t just about fancy code; it's about navigating the same old Kenyan political minefield, complete with its roadblocks and power plays.

And the public? Well, they’re supposed to just "trust" the numbers. But Miringa wants them to download the original forms. Good luck with that, when the whole point of these elections seems to be keeping things as opaque as possible. They also learned that shouting your message on TikTok isn't enough. Need to be everywhere, all the time, if you actually want people to pay attention. It's a whole new world of election observation, and these youngsters are getting a harsh, real-world education in how this country actually works.
