Lucy Kabuu's Ex-Husband Appeals Sh70m Property Dispute Judgement, Claims Destitution

Lucy Kabuu's Sh70 million matrimonial property battle with her ex-husband, Jeremiah Maina, is far from over. Maina, an ex-police officer, is now heading to the Court of Appeal, challenging the High Court's ruling that favored Kabuu, awarding her the lion's share of their joint assets. He claims the judgement will leave him destitute, unable to even afford basic healthcare.

The High Court, on February 24, granted Kabuu an overwhelming majority of the Sh70 million worth of property, a decision Maina vehemently disputes. The court awarded Kabuu exclusive ownership of a five-floor rental flat in Umoja, properties in Bahati, Free Area, Matukanio, and two in Nyandarua, citing Kabuu's clear records of direct purchase. Even their Nyahururu matrimonial home saw Kabuu awarded an 80 percent share, leaving Maina with a measly 20 percent. The court also faulted Maina for selling at least five properties against court orders, ruling those sales would count as his share.

Maina, however, is crying foul, filing a Notice of Appeal and claiming the judgement is unfair and failed to consider his evidence. He argues that executing the orders will leave him penniless, unable to provide for his three children, who depend on rental income for school fees and upkeep. He even claims to have sacrificed his police career and significant personal wealth to manage and coach Kabuu, only to be left with nothing. He's particularly incensed that the court awarded Kabuu 100 percent of the Free Area plot, despite him footing its entire development costs.

His defence regarding the Silibwet plots, which he sold against court orders, is that the funds were reinvested into the Free Area plot. He also claims he was a man of means before the marriage, presenting land title deeds registered in his name prior to their union, suggesting Kabuu rode on his coattails. It appears this saga is far from its conclusion, a familiar tune in Nairobi where money and relationships often entangle in a messy divorce. One can only imagine the legal fees alone could rival the entire Sh70 million.

This whole affair reminds me of how quickly fortunes can shift, and how personal sacrifices often go unrewarded, much like the struggles of those trying to navigate East Africa's Economic Transformation. It’s a harsh reality check for anyone who thinks love conquers all, especially when millions are on the line.
