So, Fred Ambani, the man who used to put the ball in the net for Ingwe himself, is now singing praises for Tyson Otieno. Apparently, this "midfield magician" has gone from being a bench warmer at Kenya Police - a team that actually wins things, mind you - to some kind of telepathic genius with Ugandan striker Samuel Ssenyonjo. Ambani gushes about Otieno's "through passes" and how he makes "runs into the half spaces." Honestly, it sounds like they’re describing a software update, not a footballer.

The fact that Otieno was "unceremoniously released" from the champions should have been a red flag, not a reason for celebration. He couldn't even make it onto the pitch for them over two seasons. Now, suddenly, at Leopards, he's the cornerstone? It reeks of desperation. Some people, bless their naive hearts, already doubted his commitment, and frankly, I don't blame them. This whole "money-well-spent" narrative feels like a PR stunt to justify another questionable signing. We've seen enough of these "transformations" that fade faster than a politician's promise.

Ambani, the "gaffer," insists Otieno can "turn the game on its head." Well, he apparently did just that against Nairobi United, threading a pass for Ssenyonjo to score the winner. This one goal is supposed to erase the memory of that humiliating 4-1 thrashing by Mathare United? Please. Leopards are seven points behind Gor Mahia. That's not a gap you close with one lucky assist, no matter how much you spray it with glitter. It's a whole season of mediocrity catching up.

And let's not forget the injury to goalkeeper Humphrey Katasi. Now James Ssetuba, the man who was in goal during the "Mathare massacre," is back in the hot seat. Ambani’s excuses about Ssetuba's "shattered confidence" sound like he’s trying to protect his own skin. The man made crucial saves, apparently. Let's hope for Leopards' sake he can keep making them, because if they're relying on a midfield "magician" who was collecting dust two seasons ago, and a goalkeeper whose confidence is supposedly fragile, they’re playing with fire. It’s a classic Nairobi drama, and we’ve seen enough of these "light at the end of the tunnel" stories in Kenyan football to know how they usually end.

Ingwe's next match is against Tusker FC. Resurgent? Or just another team that’s seen Leopards’ recent form and knows it’s an easy three points? Only time will tell, but I wouldn’t hold my breath for a triumphant season. This is Nairobi football, after all. It’s more about surviving the hype than actually delivering.
