Darling, if there is one thing the East African elite love more than a tax-haven bank account, it is a well-timed charity drive. The Ruparelia Foundation has announced a three-day eye medical camp in Bukedea, set for March 2026. While the peasants are expected to line up for free cataract surgeries, the rest of us are left wondering if this is actual "compassion" or just a very expensive way to keep the family name in the headlines without paying for a full-page spread in the papers.

This whole circus is supposedly a tribute to the late Rajiv Ruparelia, who the press kit claims was a champion of "youth empowerment" and motorsport. We all know how it goes in Kampala society circles - you spend your life burning rubber and living the high life, and the moment you are gone, your family turns your hobbies into a "legacy of dedication." It is the classic billionaire playbook: turn a personal tragedy into a public relations win before the ink on the obituary even dries.

Partnering with Mulago and C-Care sounds impressive on a letterhead, but let-s be real. If our public health sector was not already a crumbling mess of empty promises, we would not need private foundations to hand out reading glasses like they are party favors. It is giving very much [The University of Grab-onomics: How Our Future Got Subdivided](/posts/the-university-of-grab-onomics--how-our-future-got-subdivided) vibes, where the structures meant to serve the public are carved up and replaced by "generous" donors who just happen to own half the city.

The outreach is targeting places like Soroti, Gulu, and Lira - regions that usually only see this much attention when there is an election or a photo-op involved. Expect the usual suspects from the "Kampala socialite" scene to descend upon Bukedea Teaching Hospital, posing for selfies with elderly patients while wearing designer sunglasses that cost more than the entire camp-s supply of antibiotic drops. The irony of wearing five-hundred-dollar shades while "curing blindness" is a look only the Ruparelias could pull off.

At the end of the day, a few hundred people will get to see better, which is great for them, but let us not pretend this is anything other than a calculated move to maintain "private-sector philanthropy" dominance. In a world where the rich get richer and the poor get a three-day window to fix their retinas, the only thing being truly "visionary" here is the Foundation-s branding department. Stay woke, honey, because these "acts of kindness" usually come with a very long receipt.
