People are acting surprised that ODM is in shambles now that the man at the top has checked out. Please. In Nairobi, we know that a political party in this country is just a personality cult with a fancy color scheme. Whether it was Jaramogi in ’94 or Raila today, the script never changes. The "Inawezekana" slogan was always a carrot on a stick, and now that the stick is broken, the vultures are fighting over the splinters while the rest of us look for fuel prices that won't make us cry.

Look at the history they keep trying to sell us as "pivotal." 1994 was supposed to be a turning point when Jaramogi died, but all it did was trigger the same old turmoil. Raila spent his life trying to fit into a system that was designed to chew him up, and he died doing exactly that - fitting like a round peg in a square hole while the square holes kept getting smaller. Now we have Oburu trying to "steady" a ship that’s already half-sunk, while Winnie plays the "restless" heir. It’s not a political succession; it’s a family inheritance dispute being played out on the national stage at our expense.

This circus brings me back to the *Faux War on Corruption*. We keep expecting these political dynasties to save us, but they are too busy saving their own bank accounts. The "struggle" isn't for the person living in a bedsitter in Roysambu or the mama mboga in Kibera; it's for the right to control the ODM machinery and the patronage that comes with it. Winnie Odinga isn't some fresh revolutionary hope; she’s just the latest version of the dynasty software, and let me tell you, the update looks just as buggy and entitled as the original.

The disarray we are seeing now isn't a bug; it's a feature of the Kenyan system. When the "founding father" leaves, the factions emerge like cockroaches when the kitchen lights go off. You have one side clinging to the nostalgia of the 90s and another trying to monetize the future. Meanwhile, the long, twisted relationship between Raila and Ruto - once a dance of "inawezekana" - has collapsed into the same predictable mess of betrayal and power-brokering that defines our so-called democracy. 

So, spare me the performative grief and the "hero" narratives. The vacuum left by Raila isn't a hole in the nation's heart; it's a vacancy in the boardroom of Kenya Inc. As the Odingas bicker over the remains of a party that lost its way a decade ago, the rest of us are still stuck in the same gridlock, paying the same bribes, and watching the same theatre. Different actors, same tired play, and the ticket prices just keep going up. Karibuni Nairobi.
