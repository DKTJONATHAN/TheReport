Ruto's Farming Fantasies: The Next Billionaires or More Empty Promises?
The President is selling a dream of farming fortunes, but a look at the ground tells a different story for a hungry continent.
Belinda Achieng.
Politics
Kenya, Agriculture, Economy
Tractors

President Ruto, bless his ambitious heart, was at it again in Diani, spouting tales of agricultural glory. He's apparently been told by some former AfDB bigwig that the next crop of billionaires will sprout from the soil. And who are we to argue with the man who's trying his hand at farming himself? Because obviously, when the unemployment queues stretch longer than a matatu ride across town, the answer is always more mud and manure.

He rattles off statistics about Africa's ballooning population, conveniently forgetting that a bigger population means more mouths to feed. And right now, feeding the Horn of Africa is a disaster zone, with millions staring hunger in the face thanks to drought, climate chaos, and the usual political instability. Apparently, we have two-thirds of the world's arable land. That's great, but what good is land if you can't afford the seeds, the water, or a government that isn't busy playing its own games, much like the intricate web of laundered money we've seen before in [The Minnesota Cartel: How Feeding Our Future Gang Laundered KSh 32.5 Billion into Kenyan Resorts and Politics](/posts/minnesota-cartel-feeding-our-future-kenya-laundering).

Let's talk about these "billionaires" Ruto keeps hinting at. While other parts of Africa boast dollar millionaires, Kenya is still trying to figure out how to mint its first one. We've got plenty of folks sitting pretty on millions of shillings, sure, but the grand vision of a farmer raking in billions while the rest of us struggle to afford ugali seems a bit far fetched. It's the same old story of [High-Flying Rhetoric and Empty Pockets: The Addis Ababa Special](/posts/high-flying-rhetoric-and-empty-pockets--the-addis-ababa-special), where promises are abundant, but tangible results are as rare as a quiet Nairobi traffic jam.

And then there’s the unemployment rate, a neat little 7.9% that conveniently hides the reality for our youth. One-third of them are either twiddling their thumbs with no job prospects, or stuck in some precarious gig that barely keeps them afloat. But sure, let’s all rush out and become farmers. Because apparently, the key to escaping poverty is to get your hands dirty while the 'thought leaders' and 'intelligence leaders' figure out how to "organise ourselves" - whatever that means.

The AfCFTA agreement is mentioned as some magic bullet, promising the world's largest single market. It sounds impressive, but the details of how this translates into actual jobs and prosperity for the average Kenyan, especially those in rural areas looking to farm their way to riches, remain as hazy as a Diani morning fog. It’s easy to dream big from a conference hall, but the reality on the ground is a lot tougher, and the path to billionaire status through farming is paved with more than just good intentions.

So, while President Ruto paints a rosy picture of agricultural abundance and billionaire farmers, let's not forget the persistent challenges of food insecurity, widespread unemployment, and a general lack of concrete plans to get us there. It's a nice idea, a catchy headline, but for many Kenyans, the dream of farming riches feels more like another [Sacco Dreams or Shylock Nightmares? The Great East African Savings Heist](/posts/sacco-dreams-or-shylock-nightmares--the-great-east-african-savings-heist) - a well-intentioned concept that often crumbles under the weight of harsh reality.
