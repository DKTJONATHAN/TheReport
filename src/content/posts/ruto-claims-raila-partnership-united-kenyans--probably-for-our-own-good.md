So, the President was out in Kwale, singing about how his "broad-based partnership" with Baba Raila Odinga was the greatest thing since sliced ugali. Apparently, this grand arrangement was all about knitting this country together, making sure everyone feels like they've got a seat at the national table, and conveniently forgetting all that tribal nonsense. He reckons it's slashed divisions and brought us all into one big, happy UDA-ODM family, working for the good of the nation. Right.

He's out there telling us not to get riled up by ethnicity or political beef, because, you know, stability and development. Apparently, healthy political competition shouldn't look like outright war or shutting out whole communities. Development projects and government services, he assures us, will be spread around like butter on a well-toasted chapati, with no favouritism. It’s all about leaders from different camps holding hands and skipping towards prosperity. Just like [Tems selling the sisterhood dream](/posts/tems-sells-the-sisterhood-dream--leading-vibe-initiative-heads-to-south-africa-for-another-round-of--empowerment), but with more suits and less singing.

The President also had a go at what he calls "outdated and retrogressive politics of marginalisation" that apparently slowed down development, especially at the Coast. He's saying that sort of rubbish has no place in today's Kenya. His administration, he claims, is diligently working to gather leaders from all political corners under this "Broad-Based Government" umbrella to boost national cohesion and make sure nobody feels left out. It's all about eradicating tribalism, intolerance, division, and discrimination, and getting us all on board for a "unified national transformation agenda."

He's warning us, loud and clear, that Kenya's progress hinges on us all pulling together, even if we disagree on who wears the loudest tie. And, of course, he assured the coastal folks they'd keep getting those shiny government projects for infrastructure, healthcare, and jobs. It's a nice story he's spinning, the kind that sounds good on paper, maybe even at a rally, but out here in Nairobi, we've heard it all before. The promise of unity and fairness is the perennial song of every politician.
