So, the National Land Commission (NLC) has finally been told to cough up Sh909 million for a piece of land they snatched for the Southern Bypass. Twelve years, can you believe it? Twelve years of this Five Star Agencies Limited fellow running around government offices, probably getting the runaround from every clerk and suit who could point a finger elsewhere. And now, a judge has had enough of their "blame game." Apparently, the NLC and KeNHA can't just hide behind each other like toddlers caught with their hands in the cookie jar.

The court's basically saying, "Stop shifting the blame, you bunch of time-wasters." They even threatened the NLC chairman and CEO with contempt. Good. Maybe that'll get their attention more than a court order. This whole saga started when they decided to "compulsorily acquire" a mere 0.4281 hectares of a prime 20-acre plot for a road meant to "ease traffic gridlock." Easy for them to say when it's not their land being taken.

The NLC, in their infinite wisdom, offered a measly Sh87 million. Then the landowner fought, got a judgment for Sh413 million back in 2014, and still couldn't get a cent. Now, thanks to interest - the government's favorite way to inflate its own debts - it's ballooned to Sh909 million. It just goes to show how much faith you can put in promises, especially when dealing with our esteemed government agencies. It reminds me of when we were talking about how [Al-Sisi Nods About Nile Water with Ruto, Because Everyone Wants Their Slice](posts/al-sisi-nods-about-nile-water-with-ruto--because-everyone-wants-their-slice), everyone's fighting for their share, and the little guy gets trampled.

The NLC, naturally, tried to pull the "it's KeNHA's fault" card, saying they just facilitate and don't hold the cash. The court, however, saw right through that flimsy excuse. These are arguments that should have been made *before* the judgment, not after. The judge basically told them to get their act together, pay up, and let the landowner finally see the "fruits of their long-awaited judgment." Ninety days, they said. Let's see if they manage to stick to that, or if another 12 years will pass before they even think about it.

This whole circus highlights the pathetic state of affairs. We have constitutional commissions, supposed to uphold justice, playing bureaucratic games while landowners are left in limbo. The CEO is the accounting officer, the judge reminded them, so their excuses about KeNHA not depositing funds don't hold water. It's a tough pill to swallow, but that's how it is. You fight, you win, and then you pray the government actually *obeys* its own courts.

So, here we are. Sh909 million. A nice sum, I'm sure, but for the landowner, it’s a victory born out of sheer stubbornness and a whole lot of wasted time. For the rest of us? Just another tale of how the system is rigged, how promises are cheap, and how sometimes, just sometimes, the courts can make them pay. But don't get too hopeful. The next person in line might not be so lucky.
