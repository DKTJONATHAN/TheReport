Let us stop romanticizing the shimmering corrugated iron roofs of Kibera. It is not a scenic backdrop for a movie; it is a furnace where dreams go to melt under the afternoon sun. Sharon Achieng is being hailed as a success story because she survived a system designed to bury her. At sixteen, when she needed a desk and a pen, her teachers gave her the cold shoulder and her neighbors gave her whispers. In this city, a pregnancy is treated like a contagious disease that disqualifies you from a future.

Lydia Anyango and her Passion to Share Foundation are doing the heavy lifting because our leaders are too busy [Buying Loyalty with Our Grandchildren's Taxes](/posts/buying-loyalty-with-our-grandchildren-s-taxes) to care about a girl in a slum. It is the classic Nairobi script: the state fails, the community suffers, and then a well-meaning NGO has to step in to provide the basic dignity that should have been a right, not a miracle. We call it "transformation" because "systemic abandonment" sounds too harsh for the morning news.

The foundation teaches fashion design and beauty therapy, which is great until you realize every second person in the estate is now a tailor or a hair stylist. Sharon is stitching church choir outfits under a single bulb, which makes for a lovely anecdote, but it highlights the desperation of our economy. We have turned survival into a craft. These girls are learning to compete for crumbs in a market that is already overflowing with people trying to sell the same ten dresses to the same broke neighbors.

The most telling part of this story is not the sewing machine or the interest-free loan. It is the fact that Sharon had to wait until she was an "accidental" mother to be seen as someone with potential. In Nairobi, you often have to hit rock bottom before anyone offers you a ladder. We celebrate Sharon because she refused to stay finished, but we should be asking why the world decided her story was over at sixteen in the first place. Resilience is just a polite word for having no other choice.
