So, here we go again. The Central Bank of Kenya, bless their hearts, are so confused about how to manage this country's money they've gone and roped in the IMF and World Bank. Apparently, they need "technical support" to figure out who gets to play with the Treasury bonds. It’s like asking your drunk uncle to mediate a fight between his kids over a toy - a recipe for more chaos, not clarity. This whole kerfuffle is about who gets to skim the cream off the top of the bond market, and naturally, the big banks want the lion's share.

The CBK's bright idea is to shift bond trading from the Nairobi Securities Exchange to platforms they control, basically handing the keys to foreign entities like Bloomberg and Refinitiv. And who do they want making all the decisions? The "top commercial banks," of course. It’s all about prioritising them as "market makers," which is just fancy talk for letting them call the shots and pocket the profits. It's a classic move, really. When has the government ever prioritised the common man or even the smaller players when there's big money involved? It reminds me of how, in Kenya, it often feels like we are just pawns in a larger game, as seen with the passport scandal.

The capital markets regulators, bless their idealistic hearts, are calling this out. They say barring non-banking institutions from this game is a sure way to create monopolies and encourage cartel-like behaviour. They want banks to be licensed by the CMA, not just handpicked. They're pointing out that banks only hold 45 percent of government securities, while everyone else - pension funds, insurance companies, you name it - holds the majority. But does the CBK listen? Probably not. They've got their foreign advisors, so why bother with local common sense?

And don't even get me started on the proposed trading platforms. The CBK wants to use platforms tied to Bloomberg and Refinitiv, completely bypassing the NSE, which already has a perfectly good system. This is just about creating new revenue streams for the foreigners and making things complicated for everyone else. The capital markets folks are already warning that this could lead to delisting securities and fragmenting the market, which is the exact opposite of what a healthy bond market needs.

The whole point of the CBK's DhowCSD was to get more retail investors involved. Now, they're talking about an overly restrictive OTC framework that will likely shut out the very people they claimed to be helping. It’s a charade. The goal is to make money, plain and simple, and these reforms are just a new way to shuffle the deck so the usual suspects get an even bigger cut. It’s a tough world out here in Nairobi, and this bond market drama just proves it.

Ultimately, the capital markets regulators are proposing an industry committee to sort this mess out. A noble thought, but let's be real, how often do these committees actually change anything when the big players have their eyes on the prize? The CBK is going to do what the CBK wants, especially with the IMF and World Bank cheering them on. Just another day in Kenya, where the system is rigged, and the promises of inclusivity are as empty as a politician's speech.
