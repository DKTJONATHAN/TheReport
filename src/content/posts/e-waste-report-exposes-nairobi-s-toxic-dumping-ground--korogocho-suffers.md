So, the geniuses at Greenpeace Africa have dropped another report. Shocking, I know. Apparently, Kenya is drowning in electronic garbage, mostly junk shipped here from wealthier nations who can't be bothered to deal with their own mess. We import about 70% of this stuff, and a good chunk of it is already on its last legs. Then it ends up as e-waste, our fastest-growing garbage problem, with a pathetic 1% actually getting recycled properly. The rest? It’s a free-for-all.

And who’s paying the real price for this “waste colonialism,” as they’re calling it? The poor souls in settlements like Korogocho. These waste pickers, often from vulnerable groups, are rummaging through our digital discards, breathing in lead, cadmium, and all sorts of carcinogenic fumes. It’s no surprise that 61% of them are reporting health problems - respiratory issues, skin infections, the works. It’s like they’re stuck in some messed up version of [Claude Code Leak Reveals Tamagotchi Pet and Always-On Agent: The Cost of Corporate Incompetence](/posts/claude-code-leak-reveals-tamagotchi-pet-and-always-on-agent--the-cost-of-corporate-incompetence), but with real-world, deadly consequences.

What’s truly sickening is that kids as young as six are involved. They’re burning electronics to get at bits of copper and silver, inhaling poisons that will haunt them for years. The report paints a grim picture: 47.2% of respondents have respiratory problems, and over a third have skin issues. These aren’t just statistics; they’re human beings, mothers, fathers, children, just trying to survive in a system that’s actively poisoning them.

Greenpeace is calling for governments to show some “political goodwill” and enforce regulations. They want Extended Producer Responsibility, policies to protect informal workers, and stricter border controls. It's a nice thought, but let's be real. We’ve got plenty of politicians more interested in lining their pockets than in protecting their own people from being treated like a global toilet. This whole mess is just another example of how easily we can be exploited, a recurring theme, wouldn’t you say?

Kenya is apparently trying to tighten import policies, which is a start, I guess. But when the problem is this massive, and the motivation for dumping is so lucrative for the senders, a few restrictions are like putting a band-aid on a gaping wound. The report was launched on the UN International Day of Zero Waste - talk about ironic. It’s all very nice and official, with policymakers and activists patting each other on the back, while out in Korogocho, people are literally dying for the scraps.
