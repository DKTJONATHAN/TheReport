So, the Americans are building another one of their little facilities in Nanyuki, this time for "Ebola preparedness." And of course, it's for their citizens coming from the DRC. Because God forbid any of them get a sniffle anywhere else, right? Never mind that Kenya has never even seen a case of this thing. But hey, who are we to question the almighty dollar and the promises of "aid"? Apparently, President Ruto thinks we owe Washington for years of support, so we'll just take their dirty virus and their fancy quarantine beds. It's a real shame about the one fellow who got shot in the head. Just another casualty in the great game of international relations.

The whole thing is a joke. They say it's for Kenyans too, but that's just talk. Washington isn't saying that. Laikipia isn't some dusty corner of the world where you can just dump your problems. People are rightly angry. They're lighting fires, throwing stones, and the police are busy playing their usual symphony of tear gas and water cannons. It's a classic Nairobi two-step: the people protest, the government cracks down, and somewhere in the middle, someone ends up dead. Just like those other two who were killed on June 1st. No one seems to know or care how exactly, but hey, it happened.

And the timing is just perfect, isn't it? Tourism, our precious lifeline, is already taking a hit. Hotels in Nairobi are seeing cancellations. Because who wants to visit a country that's suddenly a potential petri dish for a global pandemic? The government needs to get its act together and reassure people, but they're too busy counting the $13.5 million pledged by the US. It’s a familiar story, isn't it? We’ve seen similar deals before, like that controversial health data exchange for aid. It always comes down to who benefits, and it’s rarely the average Kenyan.

The World Health Organization is babbling about "local bilateral collaboration" and the importance of "community." What community? The one that's being shot at and gassed? The one that doesn't want potential Ebola carriers roaming their streets? The WHO director in the DRC is talking about dialogue, but dialogue doesn't stop bullets or reassure a panicked populace. It's all just noise. Meanwhile, the facility is nearly done, despite court orders and local politicians throwing tantrums. Because when Uncle Sam wants something, he gets it. And our leaders are happy to oblige, as long as the cheques clear. It’s not that different from when local ride-hailing drivers are forced to ditch their apps because the fuel prices are too high, [Nairobi Ride-Hailing Drivers Ditch Apps as Fuel Strike Grounds City](/posts/nairobi-ride-hailing-drivers-ditch-apps-as-fuel-strike-grounds-city). Someone always pays the price for the big decisions.
