So, Adam Scott, the supposed voice of reason in a world gone mad with serialized storytelling, is out here making promises about "Severance" Season 3. According to Deadline, he's suggesting the next installment will arrive "much sooner" than the three-year chasm between seasons one and two. This is the kind of news that passes for groundbreaking in the entertainment industry: a vague assurance that the product you've been waiting for won't take an eternity to reach you. It’s a testament to the power of a good PR spin that anyone is even paying attention to this after the last prolonged drought.

Elsewhere, the relentless march of sequels and reboots continues. Shawn Levy, a director whose career seems to be a perpetual motion machine of mid-tier sci-fi, is diving into an "emotional sci-fi" film called "Somewhere Out There" for Netflix. Apparently, it's about a grieving father talking to space. Riveting. Then there's "A Quiet Place, Part III," because clearly, the world hasn't had enough of people being aggressively silent. John Krasinski confirms filming has begun. Prepare for more whisper-quiet jump scares.

The idea of "Edge of Tomorrow 2" is still flickering, with Warner Bros. reportedly still hoping to make it happen, despite it not being in active development. This is code for "we've got nothing better, so let's revisit this one." In the realm of genuine horror, or at least what passes for it, "Lice" is slated for a 2027 release, featuring mind-controlling lice at a high school. Because nothing screams "high school experience" like parasitic brainwashing. Brad Bird’s animated noir adventure, "Ray Gunn," is also on the horizon, with concept art already circulating. Presumably, it will be as visually stunning and thematically empty as most animated features these days.

The "Masters of the Universe" franchise, perpetually trying to recapture some lost glory, is releasing a featurette asking "Who is He-Man?" This is less a question of lore and more a desperate plea for relevance. On the streaming front, Amazon is bringing the comic series "Sex Criminals" to life with Imogen Poots and John Reynolds, a story about, you guessed it, people who rob banks by stopping time with sex. Because that’s the kind of groundbreaking narrative we’ve come to expect.

Sky and AMC are delving into the untold narrative of Sherlock Holmes with "The Death of Sherlock Holmes," exploring the three years between his supposed demise and reappearance. It’s a story about filling in the blanks that perhaps Arthur Conan Doyle wisely left blank. And then there’s Marvel, or what’s left of it, with "The Punisher: One Last Kill," where Judith Light hints at a surprise villain role. Because nothing says "satisfying 

Finally, Eric Kripke, the architect of "The Boys," is talking about the upcoming spinoff "Vought Rising." He claims the audience will be asked to sympathize with Soldier Boy, but never Stormfront. Because a Nazi is a Nazi, but a problematic superhero with a daddy complex? That's fertile ground for "sympathy." It’s all about managing the audience's expectations, or rather, manipulating them. The entertainment industry, in its infinite wisdom, continues to churn out content, promising novelty while delivering the same recycled ideas with slightly different packaging. Don't get your hopes up for genuine innovation; just brace yourself for the next wave of familiar stories, served slightly faster.
