So, the news is that retired President Uhuru Kenyatta and his former nemesis, Rigathi Gachagua, decided to sit next to each other at a funeral in Kirinyaga. Apparently, this has everyone buzzing about a "united front" for 2027. It's the same old story in this country: a few smiles and handshakes at a funeral, and suddenly the political landscape is supposed to shift. Don't be fooled; this is just noise to distract you from the real issues.

The newspapers are also making a fuss about National Assembly Speaker Moses Wetang’ula potentially facing contempt proceedings for ignoring a court order. He suspended an MP, then the court said no, and Wetang’ula still found a way to keep the guy out. This is how it works here. The courts make pronouncements, and the politicians decide which ones they want to listen to. It’s a game of chess, but with the rules constantly being rewritten to suit whoever is in power. It reminds me of the endless talk about attracting investment, yet we still see reports like the one about the US government calling Kenya a "corruption-ridden environment."

Speaking of which, the US trade report is out, and it’s as bleak as you’d expect. They’re calling Kenya a place where only the well-connected get government contracts, thanks to the rampant corruption. It’s a shame, really, because the trade figures show that even with all these hurdles, business is booming. But imagine what could happen if Kenya actually cleaned up its act. Instead, we have excessive taxes, confusing regulations, and agricultural products facing ridiculous barriers. It's a broken system that benefits a few while the rest of us just get by. This isn't a far cry from the Sh44 Billion Mirage we saw reported recently; promises of wealth that never quite materialize for the common Kenyan.

And then there's the National Infrastructure Fund. President Ruto has appointed a council to steer it, aiming to shift from a debt-heavy model to one that mobilizes private capital. On paper, it sounds good. More private money for big projects, less pressure on public finances. But let’s be honest, how much of this "private capital" will actually flow without the grease of corruption? We’ve seen these grand plans before, and the reality often falls short of the hype.

The bottom line is, don't get too excited about Uhuru and Riggy G's funeral bromance. It’s politics, and in Kenya, that usually means a lot of talk and very little real change. The contempt case against the Speaker is just another example of how the system is rigged. And the US report? Well, that’s just the world telling us what we already know. We're a country with potential, but we're held back by the same old problems, year after year.
