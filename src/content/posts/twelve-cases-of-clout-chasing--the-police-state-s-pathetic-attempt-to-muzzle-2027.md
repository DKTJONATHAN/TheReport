Only 12 cases. In Nairobi, you can hear 12 counts of incitement just walking past a bus stop at rush hour. Inspector General Douglas Kanja stood before the Senate looking serious, claiming the National Police Service is monitoring the situation with professional impartiality. It is the same tired performance we get every five years. They wait until the tribal rhetoric is boiling over, then they grab a few low-level bloggers to make it look like they are actually working.

Kanja’s biggest excuse this time is that the internet is too complicated for his officers. He is crying to the Senate about foreign-hosted accounts and encrypted messages like a grandfather trying to figure out a smartphone for the first time. Apparently, if you post your hate from a VPN or a private group, the entire Kenyan security apparatus just throws its hands up in the air. It is a very convenient loophole for the people who actually fund these misinformation campaigns from their air-conditioned offices.

This is just another chapter in the government’s obsession with digital shadows. We saw this same energy during [The Honorable Clout-Chasers: Why Parliament’s TikTok Tantrum is a Total Flop](/posts/the-honorable-clout-chasers--why-parliament-s-tiktok-tantrum-is-a-total-flop) where they tried to police feelings instead of fixing the economy. Now, they want legislative reforms and international cooperation to stop a WhatsApp forward. It is not about national safety; it is about control, and they are not even particularly good at that.

The talk about the NPS Forensic Laboratory and intelligence-led operations sounds expensive and impressive on paper. In reality, it just means more taxpayers' money going into machines that will eventually gather dust while the real instigators sit in the same Senate chamber Kanja was addressing. They mention ethnic stereotyping and political incitement as if those are not the primary tools used to win any election in this city. It is a joke that nobody is laughing at.

As 2027 approaches, expect more of these vigilant warnings and toll-free reporting channels that probably lead to a disconnected line. They will talk about national unity and stability while the high-fliers continue to whip up public emotions for votes. Twelve cases is not a crackdown; it is a distraction. In a country where the big fish own the pond, the police are just busy counting the bubbles and calling it progress.
