UASIN GISHU 120,000 WAKE UP: PARLIAMENT PROMISES LAND TITLES... AGAIN.

Another day, another crowd of hopeful Kenyans packed into a schoolyard, begging politicians for something they were promised decades ago. This time it's Manzini, where over 120,000 souls are still waiting for title deeds, a wait that's seen them lose money to scammers and their dreams to bureaucratic inertia. The National Assembly's Environment committee trotted in, listened to the usual sob stories, and promised to get back to them in 90 days. Ninety days. For land they’ve been on for thirty years.

The folks at Manzini Junior Primary School sure put on a show. They’ve been singing the title deed song for three decades, with some residents personally clocking in 31 years of land ownership without the actual paperwork. They even trotted out the local church representative to make it sound more divine. Apparently, it’s all about peace and development, and these little pieces of paper are the golden ticket. You’d think after this long, they’d be singing a different tune, maybe one about building their own damn titles. It’s the same old story, just a different postcode.

The MPs, bless their cotton socks, did their best to look concerned. They’re here to "see and listen," which usually means nodding sagely and taking notes for a report that will likely gather dust. MP Joseph Emathe is promising a report to the National Assembly within 90 days, a tight deadline that’s as likely to be met as a politician keeping a campaign promise. Area MP Janet Sitienei chimed in with thanks all around, including to the President, for his "willingness" to issue titles. Willingness? Try obligation.

This whole saga reeks of the same manufactured authenticity we’re always harping on about. Everyone’s happy to be photographed, everyone’s making pronouncements, but the actual land is still in limbo. They talk about schools, hospitals, and cultural centres, all built on land that isn't officially theirs. It's a house of cards waiting for the slightest breeze.

At least MP Emathe mentioned fencing off the remaining forest area. So, while the residents wait for their "peace and development" through title deeds, the Kenya Forest Service will be busy putting up fences. Just another reminder that bureaucracy moves at its own glacial pace, and the promises made in dusty school grounds often evaporate like morning dew.







The political circus has rolled into Manzini, Uasin Gishu County, where over 120,000 people from 19 villages have been reduced to begging Parliament for something as basic as land titles. After three decades of living on land allocated by the government, they’re still exposed to land scams and economic paralysis. The National Assembly’s Departmental Committee on Environment, Forestry and Mining decided to grace them with their presence at Manzini Junior Primary School, listening to tales of woe that have been the soundtrack to these residents' lives for generations.

The church representative, bless their faithful heart, spoke of sacrificing to attend the meeting and thanking God for the day. Thirty-one years they’ve waited, and their "song" is still about title deeds. Apparently, these pieces of paper are the key to unlocking credit services and development. It’s a shame they can’t just build their own prosperity without needing a politician’s blessing in triplicate. It's the same old narrative, the same old empty promises, just a different crowd.

Legislators, led by Turkana Central MP Joseph Emathe, trotted in to "see and listen." They assured the community that the issue of de-gazettement would be decided by them, a statement that sounds good but means very little in practice. Emathe promised a report to the National Assembly within 90 days. Ninety days to resolve a problem that’s festered for thirty years. Area MP Janet Sitienei added her thanks to the committee, the National Lands Commission, the Kenya Forest Service, and the Ministry of Environment, Climate Change and Forestry, essentially thanking everyone involved in the long-delayed process.

The whole affair feels like a carefully orchestrated performance, a manufactured authenticity designed to placate a restless populace. [The PR Machine is Smoking: Why We Are Sick of Manufactured 'Authenticity'](/posts/the-pr-machine-is-smoking--why-we-are-sick-of-manufactured--authenticity). While the residents are being assured of "peace and development" through title deeds, the elders who died waiting for them are a stark reminder of the real cost of these delays. Schools and hospitals stand on land whose ownership is still a question mark.

MP Emathe also pointed out the need to fence off the remaining forest area, a practical step that, ironically, solidifies the distinction between forest land and the land these residents believe is rightfully theirs. As the committee retreats to prepare its report, the people of Manzini are left with the familiar hope that this time, maybe, just maybe, their decades-long wait will finally end. But in Kenya, hope is a cheap commodity, easily manufactured and even more easily lost.
