So, the matatus are back on the road. Fancy that. After grinding the country to a halt, these "representatives" of transport sector associations decided to "suspend the boycott" for a whole week. Apparently, the President himself, William Ruto, felt the need to descend from his ivory tower in Mombasa to have a little chat. And what's the grand solution? A promise of cheaper diesel next month and a trickle of duty-free electric vehicles. Because, you know, a few fancy cars will fix the gaping hole in our pockets caused by a war waged thousands of miles away.

The government is touting this as a win, a "reprieve" from rising social tensions. They’re throwing around subsidies and tax cuts like confetti, trying to paper over the cracks of households already struggling to keep the lights on. But let’s be real, a 24 percent jump in diesel prices isn't just about commuter fares. It’s about every single cog in this country’s economy - manufacturing, agriculture, construction - all grinding to a halt or doubling their costs. It’s a domino effect, and we’re the ones at the bottom of the pile.

And then there are the "contradicting" death tolls. Police say four, human rights groups say at least 12. This isn't a new song and dance, is it? We saw it last year during the anniversary protests, where mobs went wild, police stations were overrun, and businesses were looted. Then there were the Gen Z protests in 2024, a whole uprising against taxes that saw people overrunning parliament and threatening State House. Amnesty International says 65 died then. It’s a grim reminder of how quickly things can spiral when people have nothing left to lose.

This whole "lull before the storm" feels more like a deep breath before the plunge. June is already shaping up to be a month of reckoning. Not only is there the anniversary of the Gen Z protests looming on June 25, which opposition leaders are already rallying around for "the mother of all protests," but social media is already buzzing with anger over the new tax proposals. It's the same digital soapbox that helped fuel the 2024 chaos. The government thinks they've kicked the can down the road, but they've just rearranged the deck chairs on the Titanic. We've seen this movie before, from the empty promises of infrastructure projects in stories like [Tarmac You Can’t Eat: The KSh 100 Billion Mirage in the North](/posts/tarmac-you-can-t-eat--the-ksh-100-billion-mirage-in-the-north), to the sheer absurdity of fuel prices dictating our daily lives.

The authorities are patting themselves on the back for ending the strike, but what have they really solved? They've offered a band-aid for a gaping wound. The underlying issues - the dependency on volatile global oil markets, the ever-increasing tax burden, and the disconnect between the government and the people - remain. This isn't a victory; it's a temporary ceasefire in a war that's far from over.

And as for embracing electric vehicles? It's a nice thought, a shiny distraction. But for the average Kenyan, the dream of a duty-free electric car is about as realistic as winning the lottery. The immediate concern is making it to the end of the month, not saving the planet with a hybrid. This is just another episode in the never-ending saga of Kenyan politics, where the same problems keep resurfacing, only with a new set of promises and a fresh layer of polite lies.
