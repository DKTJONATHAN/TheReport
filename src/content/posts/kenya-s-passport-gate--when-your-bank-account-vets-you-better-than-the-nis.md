Welcome to the latest episode of "Kenya is a Playground for the Rich," where our sovereignty is apparently for sale to the highest bidder. While you are sweating in a queue at Nyayo House that stretches all the way to the archives, Zimbabwean businessman Wicknell Chivayo and a group of Sudanese nationals with ties to the RSF have reportedly been handed Kenyan passports like they were party favors. It is the ultimate slap in the face for every Kenyan who has been told for months that there are "no booklets" or that the "printer is broken." Apparently, the printer works just fine if you have the right zip code and a questionable background.

Advocate Peter Wahinya is currently the only person with the guts to ask the questions we are all thinking. He has moved to the court corridors to find out exactly how 28 individuals managed to bypass the rigorous vetting that usually takes years. We are talking about the National Intelligence Service (NIS) normally checking your history back to your kindergarten graduation, yet these guys seemingly walked in and out with a shiny new document. It is giving very much "VIP access" at a club where the bouncer is paid to look the other way while the rest of us wait behind the velvet rope.

The silence from the Ministry of Interior is louder than a failed singer’s debut album. Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen, who entered office promising more transparency than a glass house, has suddenly gone ghost. He is behaving like a celebrity caught in a messy cheating scandal, hoping that if he remains quiet long enough, we will all get distracted by the next TikTok challenge. It is almost as depressing as tracking the [Global OTC Commodity Trading Market Forecast to Reach 4.6 Billion Dollars by 2030](/posts/global-otc-commodity-trading-market-forecast-to-reach-4-6-billion-dollars-by-2030) and realizing that the only thing being traded faster than oil right now is our national security.

The hypocrisy here is truly top-tier entertainment. The government expects us to believe that the issuance of passports is a high-security process with multiple layers of oversight, yet somehow an entire squad of controversial figures managed to slip through the cracks. It suggests that the "standard vetting timelines" are only for the peasants who pay their taxes and follow the law. If you have enough clout or the right connections to the Sudanese RSF, the red tape apparently turns into a red carpet.

Wahinya has given the Director General of Immigration 21 days to produce the receipts, the application forms, and the internal memos that authorized this mess. We want to see the payment receipts and the "special clearance" documents that allowed these individuals to cut the line. In a world where ordinary Kenyans are struggling to get jobs abroad because they cannot get travel documents, seeing foreign nationals with shady histories flaunt our passport is the ultimate "eat the rich" moment. 

Don't hold your breath for a logical explanation, though. In this administration, the truth is usually buried under layers of "administrative functions" and "budgetary oversight." We will likely be told it was a technical glitch or a misunderstanding, while the individuals in question continue to use their Kenyan documents to bypass international scrutiny. Stay tuned, because this tea is boiling over, and I am here to catch every single drop of the fallout.
