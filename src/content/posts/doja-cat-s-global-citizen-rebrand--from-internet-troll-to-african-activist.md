Well, well, well, look who is suddenly the face of African "empowerment." Doja Cat, the same woman who spent years fighting her own fans in internet chat rooms and shaving her eyebrows on Instagram Live, is now the headliner for Move Afrika 2026. She is set to hit Kigali and Pretoria, promising "energy, purpose, and real change." It is a classic PR pivot. One day you are a chaotic meme-lord, and the next, you are the savior of the continent’s creative economy. We see the vision, honey, but some of us have longer memories than a TikTok algorithm.

The corporate jargon in this announcement is truly something to behold. Hugh Evans and the Global Citizen machine are back at it, using buzzwords like "pan-African music touring circuit" and "economic investment" to justify another massive spectacle. It is giving very much [THE APOLOGY PARASITE: WHY ELITE ATHLETES WEAPONIZE PERSONAL DRAMA TO STEAL THE SPOTLIGHT](/posts/the-apology-parasite--why-elite-athletes-weaponize-personal-drama-to-steal-the-spotlight) energy, except instead of athletes, it is pop stars weaponizing "activism" to distract us from their latest controversy. Doja says she is "proud to be part of something that celebrates Africa’s creativity," but let’s be honest-she is proud of that headliner check.

Let’s talk about the irony of Doja Cat being the spokesperson for "reproductive health and rights" in Africa. Since when did the girl who made a song about being a cow become the authority on primary care and health resilience? The press release claims they are "urging African nations to strengthen primary care," but they are doing it through a concert where most of the fans just want to hear "Paint The Town Red" without being lectured. It is the ultimate corporate gymnastic move: link a pop concert to a healthcare crisis and call it "progress."

They love to brag about the "90 percent local crew" and "locally sourced equipment." That is great for the guys moving the speakers, but let’s look at the top of the pyramid. If the majority of the funding and the massive headliner fee are going back to a girl from Los Angeles, are we really "redefining global touring" for the benefit of Africans? It feels like the same old story: use African soil for the aesthetic, give a few dozen local kids "paid hands-on experience," and then fly the private jets back to the Hollywood Hills once the stage is dismantled.

Even the Music Economy Development Initiative (MEDI) sounds like another way for Universal Music Group and the IFC to "simplify" how they can extract more value from local artists. They talk about "music policy reform" and "music financing," which is usually code for "how can we own the masters of the next big thing from Lagos or Kigali?" It is all wrapped in the shiny paper of "sustainable growth," but the smell of corporate greed is hard to mask.

Whether you are at the BK Arena or SunBet Arena, don’t drink the activist Kool-Aid too quickly. Move Afrika is a well-oiled machine designed to make global corporations look like they have a soul. Doja Cat is just the latest shiny object they are using to keep us from asking where the real, long-term money is actually going. Buy your tickets if you want to dance, but don't expect a pop star to fix the healthcare system. That is just bad fiction.
