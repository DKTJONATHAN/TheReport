Kenyans Recalled for Africa Cup of Nations: Another Round of False Hope









So, Kenya's volleyball coach, Geoffrey Omondi, has decided to "rebuild" the Malkia Strikers for the Africa Cup of Nations by dredging up some names we've heard before. Apparently, ten players are being "recalled," meaning they've trained with the national team, probably sweated buckets, and then got the boot. Now they're back for another shot at glory, or at least a chance to pretend they're on the path to the 2028 Olympics. It’s the usual Nairobi hustle, repackaged as a sporting endeavor.

The hopefuls include some newbies like Sharon Sandui and Mildred Cherop, alongside a familiar roster of faces who've likely seen this movie before. They’ll train in Nairobi, then jet off to Serbia for more training, all in pursuit of a championship title that's supposed to secure a ticket to the Olympics. Don’t forget, this is all happening in our own backyard, so expect the usual fanfare and misplaced optimism. It's a familiar script, much like how we see the same "safety" warnings from the CA that are really just another tax trap for the broke.

Coach Omondi prattles on about selection being based on merit and hard work, yet conveniently includes only two foreign-based players. Veronica Adhiambo in Greece and Meldina Sande in Rwanda are the exceptions, probably the ones with better agents or just luckier in their career choices. The rest are local talent, which is fine, but let’s be real, the real competition often lies beyond our borders. We're hosting, so that's supposed to give us an edge, but history shows that’s more about pressure than guaranteed success.

The ultimate goal, apparently, is to reach the final of the Africa Cup of Nations to qualify for the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics. That’s a tall order for a team that’s essentially a revolving door of players hoping for their 15 minutes of fame. While Kenya's global ranking might be decent now, don't be surprised if the reality of international competition brings us crashing back to earth. It's always the same story: lofty ambitions, a flurry of activity, and then... well, we'll see.

The coach’s statement about wanting to "improve on what we achieved at the last world championship" sounds good on paper, but let's be honest, we’re talking about a sport where the biggest drama is often off the court. With the Olympics and the World Championship on the line, the pressure is on, but whether this squad has the grit to deliver remains to be seen. It's a provisional squad of 24, which will be whittled down to 16. That’s a lot of hopefuls, and very few eventual winners.

The whole affair is a classic Nairobi spectacle: big promises, nationalistic fervor, and the faint hope that this time, it might actually be different. But until we address the systemic issues that hold our athletes back, this will just be another cycle of call-ups, training camps, and maybe, just maybe, a fleeting moment of national pride before it all fades away.
