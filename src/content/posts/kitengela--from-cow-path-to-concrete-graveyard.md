They call it a satellite town, but Kitengela is really just a sprawling concrete trap for people who cannot afford a plot in Syokimau. Back in the 80s, it was a simple trading centre where herders grabbed a soda while trekking cows to the Kenya Meat Commission in Machakos. Now, according to the 2019 census, the population has tripled. We are supposed to celebrate this "growth," but all I see is more people packed into a dust bowl, waiting for the infrastructure to catch up with the greed of developers.

The 90s saw investors rushing in like they had discovered gold in the Kajiado dirt. Fast-forward to today, and that "bustling" energy has curdled into a grim lottery of survival. Take Ayomo, for example, who went out to run errands and ended up as another statistic. In this town, you do not just "run errands"; you navigate a minefield of "goon networks" that even the politicians admit are documented in thick DCI files. It is the same old Kenyan story: the population explodes, but the safety net is made of wet tissue paper.

While we are busy being sold [Zakayo's First-World Fever Dream and the Sh550 Million Alliance Handout](/posts/zakayo-s-first-world-fever-dream-and-the-sh550-million-alliance-handout), the reality on the ground remains primitive and violent. The DCI is out here blocking buses and arresting crew members like they are in a Hollywood action flick, yet mob justice is still the preferred court system for the frustrated public. Since January last year, 266 Kenyans have been injured in mob attacks. That is what happens when people realize the police are better at paperwork than protection.

The human cost of this "boom" is what really sticks in my throat. Ask Florence Atieno, who said goodbye to her husband on a Saturday morning as she left for work. She never saw him alive again. That is the Kitengela reality that the glossy real estate brochures forget to mention. You might get a three-bedroom bungalow with a "scenic view," but you also get the crushing uncertainty of whether your family will be there when you get back from the office.

We can keep pretending that these satellite towns are the future of the Kenyan middle class, but they feel more like holding pens for the disillusioned. Between the "goon networks" and the constant threat of violence, the dream of a quiet life in Kajiado is officially dead. It is just Nairobi’s chaos with fewer streetlights, more dust, and the same old broken promises. Growth is not just about counting heads; it is about making sure those heads stay on their shoulders.
