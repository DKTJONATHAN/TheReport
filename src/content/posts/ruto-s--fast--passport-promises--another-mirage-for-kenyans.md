Ruto's "Fast" Passport Promises: Another Mirage for Kenyans?

The President claims a swift 2-3 day passport turnaround for job seekers abroad, but the reality on the ground paints a much different, more frustrating picture.

President William Ruto, speaking at the Ambassadors and High Commissioners' Conference, declared the government has streamlined passport applications to a mere two to three days, ending frustrations for Kenyans seeking jobs overseas. He boasted that this "clean channel" now ensures crucial documents are delivered within 72 hours. A bold claim, especially considering the eCitizen portal, the very gateway to this supposed efficiency, currently states 10 working days for first-time applications and five for renewals. Apparently, the digital paperwork is still faster than the physical reality of getting that stamp of approval.

While the installation of high-speed printers capable of churning out over 10,000 passports daily might sound impressive, it doesn't magically erase the systemic delays. Backlogs that once stretched for months, even up to six, are supposedly a thing of the past. However, as of March 2026, many Kenyans still report waiting 7 to 10 days, and the real bottleneck appears to be the struggle for biometric appointment slots, particularly at popular hubs like Nyayo House. It seems the promise of speed is still stuck in the queue, much like trying to get timely service anywhere else in this city.

The President also linked this supposed passport efficiency to a surge in Kenyans finding work abroad, citing over 540,000 exported workers. He even touted the Kenyan passport's improved global standing, climbing to 64th on the Henley Passport Index and solidifying its position as the strongest in East Africa. While global mobility and stronger international standing are indeed desirable, it’s hard not to feel like this is a carefully curated narrative. It’s easy to boost numbers when the bar was so abysmally low, and the actual experience for the average Kenyan is still a frustrating exercise in patience and bureaucracy, not unlike the endless wait for digital justice when dealing with data sovereignty.

This whole "fast track" passport situation feels like another classic Kenyan tale: grand pronouncements from the top, met with the grinding reality of our operational inefficiencies. We've seen this script before, whether it's about digitizing services or promising economic revival. The talk is cheap, and the paperwork, it seems, is still a marathon, not a sprint. Until Kenyans can walk into an office, get their biometrics done without a three-month wait, and actually hold their passport in hand within the promised timeframe, these pronouncements will remain just that - pronouncements.
