Samsung, a company that once prided itself on pioneering new consumer tech categories, has seemingly devolved into a digital photocopier. With Apple’s smart glasses still a theoretical threat rather than a tangible product to dissect, Samsung is now reportedly lifting designs directly from Meta’s Ray Ban AI glasses. This latest leak of alleged Samsung Galaxy Glasses paints a grim picture of a company lacking original thought, content to regurgitate what’s already on the market.

The leaked images, first surfaced by Android Headlines, reveal two purported models. The first, codenamed "Jinju," functions remarkably like Meta's current offering. It boasts twin 12 megapixel cameras positioned near the lenses for photo and video capture, alongside speakers and a microphone, presumably for interacting with AI like Google's Gemini. References in One UI 9 source code, as noted by Android Authority, lend a degree of credibility to this embarrassing revelation. What's conspicuously absent are any mentions of advanced features like touchpad controls or significant AI integrations, suggesting it’s a feature-for-feature clone.

The second model, "Haean," slated for a 2027 release, aims for a micro LED display, a feature that Meta has already implemented in its Ray Ban Display smart glasses. This timeline itself screams of a reactive strategy, waiting years to replicate technology that’s already making its debut.

Samsung isn’t the only Android player scrambling to join the smart glasses fray. Google, ever the eager participant in the surveillance industry, is collaborating with Xreal on Project Aura and has other Android XR glasses likely on the horizon, with their annual I/O developer conference offering a platform to trumpet the supposed benefits of on-the-go Gemini AI. The irony of Google’s privacy implications with Gemini is a story for another day, but it’s safe to assume data collection will be paramount.

The reliance on established eyewear brands like Gentle Monster and Warby Parker for partnerships, mirroring Meta’s strategy, further underscores Samsung’s lack of an independent vision. Google’s rumored dalliance with Gucci highlights a common tactic: leveraging brand cachet to mask a dearth of genuine innovation. Meanwhile, Chinese competitors like Huawei, with their own smart glasses, are at least attempting to carve out unique selling points, something Samsung appears incapable of at present.

The entire smart glasses market, now including Apple’s imminent foray, is a minefield of privacy concerns. Meta has already faced significant backlash for its push towards facial recognition, a feature that could easily transform these devices into ubiquitous surveillance tools. The original Google Glass earned the moniker "Glasshole" for its intrusive nature; the next generation, exemplified by these copycat devices, threatens to be even more pervasive, with the data collected likely feeding into opaque AI systems whose privacy policies are a joke. The promise of privacy in the age of connected eyewear is, unfortunately, a phantom.
