Start with the shocker: KES 20 billion. That is not small change; that is "buy-a-private-island" money. But in Kenya, we just call it Tuesday. Our premier universities are currently being stripped naked by cartels while the Ministry of Education plays blind man's buff. If you thought higher education was about research and innovation, you clearly have not been paying attention to the real estate market in Nairobi.

The Auditor General has finally realized what every mama mboga near a campus already knew: the land is vanishing. These institutions are sitting on prime acres without a single title deed to their name. It is almost funny, if it was not so pathetic. Imagine running a multi-billion-shilling institution but forgetting to secure the paperwork for the ground you stand on. That is not "administrative negligence," it is a deliberate "karibu kiti" to the land-hungry hyenas in suits.

These cartels do not work alone. You do not just wake up and subdivide university land without a "nod" from the inside. We are talking about VCs and bureaucrats who have mastered [THE NARCISSISM OF THE POST-VICTORY HIJACK](/posts/the-narcissism-of-the-post-victory-hijack), treating public assets like personal inheritance. They facilitate the theft, sign off on the fake transfers, and then have the audacity to complain about lack of funding for student hostels. It is a well-choreographed dance of greed where the students are the only ones without a partner.

The theft is so brazen it makes you want to laugh to keep from crying. We are talking about land meant for research labs and expansion being turned into commercial plazas and "luxury" apartments. While you are struggling to find a seat in a lecture hall meant for fifty people but holding two hundred, a private developer is probably picking out tiles for a penthouse on land that was supposed to be your library. This is the reality of the "booming" student population in East Africa-more students, less space, and more money in the pockets of the untouchables.

And do not get your hopes up about the EACC or "multi-agency approaches." We have heard that song before, and the remix is always the same. These cases will drag on in court for decades, hidden behind injunctions and "missing" files, until we all forget which university owned what. By the time someone is held accountable, the land will have been sold five times over to "innocent" buyers who just happened to have the right political connections.

In the meantime, the elites will keep their "bespoke" lifestyles, maybe even shopping for custom-made jewellery while the rest of us wonder why the cost of tuition keeps going up. Education is the bedrock of the nation, sure, but in Kenya, that bedrock is being excavated and sold as building ballast for the highest bidder. Welcome to the republic of grabbers, where even your dreams are being subdivided and sold per square foot.
