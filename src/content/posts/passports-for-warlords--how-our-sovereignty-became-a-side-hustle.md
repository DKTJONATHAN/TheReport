Welcome to Kenya, where the "hustler" narrative finally makes sense - it turns out the biggest hustle is selling our national identity to the highest bidder. While you are sweating in a queue at Nyayo House being told the printing machine is broken for the tenth time, Algoney Hamdan Daglo, a man the US calls a "war-financier," is reportedly walking around with a fresh Kenyan passport. It is a classic Nairobi move: keep the citizens waiting for basic rights while the elite open the back door for anyone with enough blood money to bypass the system.

The Human Rights Foundation is shouting about it, but are we even surprised? We have seen this script before where the government says one thing to the cameras and does another under the table. This is just more proof that [the DP seat is just a musical chair for the elite](/posts/the-dp-seat-is-just-a-musical-chair-for-the-elite) and our national security is just another commodity to be traded in the VIP lounges of Westlands. President Ruto wants to play the regional mediator, but it is hard to look like a peacemaker when your administration is helping the RSF evade sanctions.

According to the US Office of Foreign Assets Control, Daglo did not just have a Sudanese passport; he had a Kenyan one too. This is the same RSF involved in the siege of El-Fasher, where ethnic killings and sexual violence are the order of the day. While our leaders give flowery speeches at the UN, they are allegedly arming the very people making Sudan a graveyard. It is a level of hypocrisy that even the most hardened Nairobi cynic finds hard to swallow.

The timing is almost comedic if it was not so tragic. Kenya is currently begging to be removed from the Financial Action Task Force "grey list" by May. You do not get off a list for money laundering and terrorism financing by issuing travel documents to sanctioned warlords. The international financial watchdogs are watching, and when the sanctions eventually hit our banks, the common man will be the one paying the price while the real architects of this mess fly away on their multiple passports.

At the end of the day, our reputation as a "diplomatic hub" is just a PR stunt that has finally run out of steam. We are not mediating a conflict; we are hosting it. If you have enough cash, Kenya is not a country - it is a service provider. From El-Fasher to Nairobi, the blood trails are starting to lead straight to our doorstep, and no amount of "corrective statements" from State House will wash that stain away.
