The Strait of Hormuz has become the world's most effective choke-point, and South Korea is the first major industrial power to show signs of total asphyxiation. With 1.7 million barrels of daily crude imports at risk, the talk of "sufficient reserves" for 208 days is a fairy tale told to keep the domestic populace from rioting. In a hyper-connected global economy, a 200-day buffer is a heartbeat. Once the strategic reserves are depleted to keep the factories humming, there will be nothing left but the cold reality of a post-petroleum collapse.

The financial markets have already smelled the rot. A six per cent drop in the KOSPI and a currency plummeting toward the 1,500-won-per-dollar mark are not mere fluctuations. They are the market's way of pricing in the incompetence of a government that thinks it can spend 100 trillion won to buy its way out of a geopolitical catastrophe. Every won printed for so-called stabilization is another nail in the coffin of South Korea's credit rating, turning a localized energy shock into a systemic financial meltdown.

Looking for energy sources beyond the Middle East is a noble sentiment, but it ignores the brutal reality of global competition. Every energy-starved nation from Tokyo to Berlin is currently hunting for the same non-Middle Eastern barrels. Lee's promise to "swiftly introduce and boldly implement" these measures is the language of a command economy masquerading as a democracy. It signals to the world that South Korea has abandoned the rules of the international order because those rules no longer guarantee its survival.

The 14 per cent of natural gas sourced from the Middle East might seem small on paper, but in a world of tight margins, it is the difference between industrial output and a frozen grid. The claim that domestic supplies are unlikely to be hit because players could import from alternative sources is peak bureaucratic delusion. When the global supply chain fractures, there are no alternatives, only higher bidders. Seoul is currently bidding with money it does not have for resources that are no longer there.
