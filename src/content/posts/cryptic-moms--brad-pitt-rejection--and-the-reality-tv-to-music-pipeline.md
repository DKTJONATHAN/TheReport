Zendaya's mother, Claire Stoermer, is currently winning the award for most predictable "protective parent" on social media. By offering a cryptic response to those Tom Holland marriage rumors, she is doing exactly what every Hollywood manager dreams of: keeping the fans hungry without actually saying a single word of substance. It is the classic industry dance where the inner circle pretends to hate the spotlight while carefully adjusting the dimmer switch to keep the attention on their famous offspring. If they actually wanted privacy, they would stay off Instagram, but then how would we know they are better than us?

Speaking of being better than the rest of the world, Christina Applegate decided now was the perfect time to remind everyone that she once ditched Brad Pitt at the MTV VMAs for some random rocker. We get it, Christina; you were the "it" girl of the late eighties and even a pre-fame Brad Pitt was not enough to keep your interest for a full three-hour ceremony. It is funny how these "badass" anecdotes only seem to crawl out of the woodwork when people need a fresh round of headlines to stay relevant in the digital age.

The desperation for attention is not limited to the red carpet, though. Even survivors of near-death experiences are getting in on the "content" game. A passenger from a recent United Airlines emergency landing described the "surreal feeling" of sprinting away from a flaming plane as if they were narrating a scene from a low-budget action flick. Honestly, with the way the airline industry is going, you should probably just [Catch Flights, Not Feelings-Unless Those Feelings Are Regret Over Your Auto-Renewal](/posts/catch-flights--not-feelings-unless-those-feelings-are-regret-over-your-auto-renewal) because the risk of a mechanical failure is currently higher than the chances of getting a decent bag of pretzels in economy.

Not to be outdone in the department of shameless self-promotion, Eric Nam is releasing a new single called "How the Fire Started," and he is dragging his "The Traitors" Season 4 castmates along for the ride. Why rely on your own musical talent when you can just shove a bunch of reality TV personalities like Maura Higgins and Rob Rausch into a music video to guarantee a few extra million clicks? It is the ultimate networking move for the social media era, proving once again that in Hollywood, who you shared a scripted "reality" set with is far more important than the actual song you are trying to sell.

At the end of the day, whether it is a mother playing riddles with her daughter's engagement or a singer using reality stars to boost his streaming numbers, the underlying theme is the same: branding. Every "surreal" moment and "cryptic" post is just another brick in a very expensive wall designed to keep the public engaged while the stars laugh all the way to the bank. Stay cynical, babies, because the moment you start believing the glamor is real is the moment you have already lost the game.
