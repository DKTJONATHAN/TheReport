The entertainment industry has officially abandoned the pursuit of novelty in favor of the safe, the recycled, and the familiar. From the desperate milking of the Evil Dead brand to James Gunn’s predictable reliance on his own inner circle, the current slate of upcoming releases confirms that we are trapped in a feedback loop of creative bankruptcy. This is not art; it is a desperate attempt to maintain quarterly earnings by weaponizing nostalgia and established intellectual property.

Take the Evil Dead news as a primary example. We are now being fed two separate films in the franchise simultaneously. Francis Galluppi has his cast for an untitled project while Evil Dead Burn looms in the background. The industry is no longer content with a single sequel; it must fragment the brand into multiple streams to ensure no drop of potential profit remains unextracted. It is a cynical play for a horror audience that has been conditioned to accept quantity over substance.

James Gunn’s Man of Tomorrow further illustrates the trend of creative insulation. By tapping Sam McCurdy, the DP from Peacemaker, Gunn proves that the modern blockbuster is less about artistic vision and more about maintaining a comfortable, closed-circuit ecosystem of collaborators. This brand of professional nepotism ensures a uniform aesthetic that avoids the risk of a truly fresh perspective. Much like the repetitive cycles discussed in The Addis Theatre and Runda Bubbles: Same Script, Different Stage (/posts/the-addis-theatre-and-runda-bubbles--same-script--different-stage), Hollywood is simply building the same structures in different neighborhoods and calling it progress.

The rest of the news cycle is equally grim. Prime Video continues its march through the video game graveyard with God of War, casting Heimdall to satisfy the checklist of an adaptation that will inevitably struggle to capture the scale of its source material. Meanwhile, Star Wars: Maul-Shadow Lord proves that Disney is still incapable of letting a character stay dead if there is a cent of merchandise value left in their corpse. Even the prestige streaming options, like Peacock’s The Astrology House, are merely variations on the "rich people dying at a retreat" trope that has been beaten into the ground by every other network.

Even the rating for Ready or Not 2 feels like a marketing gimmick rather than a creative choice. An R rating for "bloody images" is the bare minimum requirement for a slasher sequel, yet it is presented as a badge of authenticity. We are being sold the illusion of edge in a world of sanded-down corporate products. If this is the Man of Tomorrow, then the future is nothing more than a high-definition replay of yesterday’s ideas.
