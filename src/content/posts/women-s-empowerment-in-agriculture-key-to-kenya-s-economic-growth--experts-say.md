Kenya Women Agriculture Economic Growth: Experts Wagging Their Tongues

A chorus of “woe is us” and “we need more” echoed through some fancy forum this week, ostensibly for International Women’s Week. Academia, research, media, and the usual suspects of civil society all gathered to “highlight” the oh-so-critical role of women in Kenya’s agricultural economy. Apparently, we’re supposed to be shocked, shocked, that women do a lot of the heavy lifting but get squat in return.

Zubeida Kananu, President of the Kenya Editors Guild, kicked things off with a sob story about her mother and her *kibanda*. Cute. She drones on about women "quietly feeding a nation" and "creating opportunity." Yes, Zubeida, we know. They always do. The real news here, if there even is any, is that these stories are "underreported." Maybe if the reporting wasn't so full of fluff and platitudes, people might actually pay attention. They’re calling for "evidence-based, gender-responsive journalism." Translation: more articles telling us how amazing women are while politely ignoring the messy details of why things are still so messed up.

Then came Professor Mary Mbithi, dropping stats that should make anyone with a functioning brain cell cringe. Women are 70-80% of the labor, but a measly 33% of formal jobs and less than 10% of credit. Yet, they work longer hours. Surprise, surprise. The "structural barriers" are land ownership, cultural norms, and access to finance. Groundbreaking. Mbithi correctly notes it’s an "economic imperative," but let’s be real, this isn't some new revelation. It's the same old song and dance. We’ve seen similar stagnation with land issues before, like with [Ruto's New Land Commission: Six Years of the Same Old Story](/posts/ruto-s-new-land-commission--six-years-of-the-same-old-story).

Dr. Lucy Wakiaga chimes in about education and policy reform. Apparently, girls aren’t going into STEM fields in agriculture, which limits their access to "higher-value segments." Meanwhile, women are still drowning in unpaid care work, meaning they can’t fully participate in economic activities. The solution? More investment in social infrastructure like childcare. And partnerships. Always partnerships. Between governments, research institutions, development partners, and the private sector. Basically, more meetings, more reports, and more promises.

The whole thing wrapped up with a call for "policy reforms," "increased investment," and "stronger data collection." They even mentioned including men and youth in these "empowerment initiatives." Because apparently, we need to remind everyone that women’s empowerment is good for the economy. Who knew? It’s all very inspiring, I’m sure, for those who can afford to attend such forums. For the women actually in the fields, it’s just another day of hard graft, hoping someone, somewhere, actually does something instead of just talking about it.
