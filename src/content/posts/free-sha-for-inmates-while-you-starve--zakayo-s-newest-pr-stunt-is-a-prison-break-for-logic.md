Leave it to this administration to find a way to make being a law-abiding citizen feel like a disadvantage. While you are out here sweating in a hospital queue, praying that the SHA system does not "glitch" before the doctor sees you, President Ruto has decided that the 60,000 people behind bars deserve a VIP pass to healthcare. During the recent graduation of over 3,800 new prison recruits in Ruiru, the man announced that inmates are being fast-tracked into the Social Health Authority. Apparently, if you want guaranteed medical cover without the headache of a failing portal, you just need to get yourself arrested.

The government is even handing out National IDs to prisoners who do not have them, just to ensure they can be registered for the Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF). It is a funny kind of efficiency. Most of us in Nairobi have been waiting months for simple government documents, but for the guys in the "Youthful Correctional Training Centres," the state is playing personal assistant. It is all part of the grand plan to hit that 30 million registration mark, even if they have to draft every person in a stripey uniform to make the numbers look good on a PowerPoint slide.

Of course, the State Department for Correctional Services will be footing the bill for these premiums. We all know what that means. Your taxes, which are already being squeezed out of every shilling you earn, are being diverted to ensure that someone who might have mugged you in the CBD gets better chronic illness cover than you do. It feels like a local chapter of [The Global Guillotine: Wealth Taxes and the Mirage of Emerging Markets](/posts/the-global-guillotine--wealth-taxes-and-the-mirage-of-emerging-markets), where the productive class is bled dry to fund "universal" dreams that only seem to benefit those who are already a burden on the state.

The rhetoric is sweet, as usual. They talk about Level 2 and Level 3 hospitals and "specialised treatment" for youthful offenders. But let us be real: half of the public hospitals in this city do not even have clean water or basic gauze. Claiming that prisoners will receive "proper health treatment just like any other Kenyan" is less of a promise and more of a threat, considering the current state of healthcare for the average "any other" Kenyan. If "proper treatment" means waiting six months for a scan, then sure, the prisoners are in for a treat.

At the end of the day, this is just more Kenya Kwanza optics. They want to call healthcare a "constitutional right" while the system remains a privilege for those with deep pockets or, apparently, a criminal record. For the rest of us living in the real Nairobi, we will just keep paying our SHIF contributions and hoping we do not get sick enough to actually need the "coverage" we are forced to buy. It is a cynical game, and as usual, the people playing by the rules are the ones losing the hardest.
