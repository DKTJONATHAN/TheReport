Ruto's Fuel Crisis "Cushioning": A Shilling Here, A Shilling There

So, the big man himself, President Ruto, is telling us the government is on our side, trying to "cushion" us from these ridiculous fuel prices. Apparently, it's all the Middle East's fault, a global oil shock that even "advanced economies" are feeling. Right. Because we all know how much Kenya's struggling masses are on par with, say, Germany when it comes to weathering financial storms. He's thrown around KSh28.19 billion in "support" and tax relief. Sounds like a lot, doesn't it? But then you see the numbers: Super Petrol would be KSh230.12 a litre without it, now it’s a mere KSh214.25. A KSh15.87 "saving" while your rent still goes up and the price of sukuma wiki has you questioning your life choices. It's a drop in the ocean, folks, a very expensive drop.

And the genius plan? Shaving off KSh10 from diesel for June-July. Ten bob! Meanwhile, they've been dipping into the Petroleum Development Fund and slas- sorry, "reduced" - VAT from 16 to 8 percent. It’s like someone's trying to patch a gaping wound with a band-aid made of used tissue. The President boasts about protecting "millions of Kenyans from even more severe economic hardship." Let's be real, for most of us, the "hardship" is already severe. This is just a slightly less severe shade of misery. It’s a classic Nairobi maneuver: make a big song and dance about a tiny concession, hoping we’re too busy trying to afford bus fare to notice the details.

Then there's the talk of scrapping taxes and levies. Ruto warns that this would mean "stalled road projects" and a collapse of public services. Oh, the horror! As if the current state of our roads and public services is a shining beacon of efficiency. It's the same old scare tactic: if you dare question the status quo, we'll all descend into chaos. It’s a bit like the arguments around ride-hailing services, where drivers are constantly squeezed while platforms rake it in, a situation that reminds me of the [Airbnb Brothel Crisis: How Nairobi Short-Term Rentals Replaced Koinange Street](/posts/airbnb-brothel-crisis-nairobi-short-term-rentals-koinange) - where convenience for some comes at a steep price for others.

But wait, there's more! We're investing in renewable energy, electric vehicles, and even developing oil in Turkana. Sounds forward-thinking, until you remember the sheer cost of these electric vehicles. Duty-free for the first 100,000? Great. How many ordinary Kenyans will actually be able to afford even a second-hand electric car when a brand new fuel-guzzler is already out of reach? It’s a plan for the elite, a PR exercise that glazes over the immediate reality for the vast majority who are just trying to get from point A to point B without selling a kidney.

And the transport sector gets its own special treatment: consultations, "temporary relief measures" for those facing financial challenges, and a review of insurance and auctioneer acts. It’s a lot of committee meetings and promises of future fairness. Meanwhile, matatu operators can keep their artwork, as long as safety standards are met. Because apparently, the biggest threat to our economy is the lack of vibrant graffiti on public transport. This is the kind of "practical intervention" that makes you wonder if they’re actually listening or just rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic.

Finally, the President calls for patience, unity, and warns against "political opportunists." Essentially, "keep quiet, don't complain, and trust us." Because collective rejection of hooliganism and violence is what we need when the real violence is the slow, steady erosion of our purchasing power. It’s a crisis, alright, and while we're busy being patient and united, the cost of living just keeps climbing, making us all just a little bit more realistic, and a lot more cynical.
