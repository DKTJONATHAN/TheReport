Ebola Outbreak Is A Major Concern For Kenya, Ruto Tells International Community, While Kenyans Die For It

WARNING: This article details government responses to a health crisis, public unrest, and potential loss of life. Reader discretion is advised.

Photo: President William Ruto speaking at the Oslo Forum 2026 High-Level Public Event on June 10, 2026.

POLITICS
Ebola, Laikipia, Protests

Facility

So, here we are. President Ruto, busy rubbing shoulders with the global elite in Finland, is still out there defending his decision to plant an Ebola containment facility smack-bang in Laikipia. Apparently, it's all for our own good, a necessary evil because Ebola is doing its scary dance in Congo and Uganda. Never mind that back home, one Kenyan has already bought the farm thanks to these "necessary" measures, with another unidentified body still cooling somewhere. Thirty-five people got arrested for daring to question this grand plan, only to be let out on a laughably small Ksh. 5,000 bail. The rest are nursing wounds and demanding justice. Justice. A foreign concept in Nairobi sometimes, isn't it?

Ruto, ever the statesman from afar, insists this is all about "proactive" preparation. He's spouting off about enhanced surveillance at border points and over 20 centres ready to spring into action. He even managed to secure a cozy arrangement with the Americans, who are apparently donating 23 isolation units. Because, of course, when things get rough, it's always the Americans who swoop in, isn't it? Perhaps they're just interested in securing their own mineral supplies, a strategy we've seen them employ before in places like [US Launches $12bn Project Vault to Secure Mineral Supplies](/posts/us-launches-12bn-project-vault-to-secure-mineral-supplies).

The justification? Thousands of Kenyans are chilling in eastern DRC, and we've got soldiers there. So, naturally, we need a fancy US-funded Ebola camp right here in Laikipia. Because that's the logical next step when your citizens are in a danger zone - bring the danger closer, but with fancy isolation units. It's a brilliant strategy, really. Who needs diplomacy when you have international aid and a convenient airbase?

Meanwhile, the protests are entering their second week. People are still out there, getting roughed up by the police, demanding to know why their lives are being put on the line. But hey, at least the President is being responsible. He's telling the "international community" that Kenya is concerned. And isn't that what matters most? That the world knows we're concerned, even if some of our own citizens are paying the ultimate price for that concern.

It's the usual song and dance, isn't it? A crisis brews somewhere else, we get involved, and suddenly our own people are the ones facing the fallout. The money flows, the politicians give speeches, and the ordinary Kenyan is left to pick up the pieces, or in this case, dodge bullets and deal with the threat of a disease we're supposedly preparing for. And we're still Ebola-free, mind you. For now.
