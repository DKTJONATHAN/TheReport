It appears the illusion of national security is as flimsy as a runner's privacy settings. Amidst a backdrop of escalating global conflict, the French military, much like its counterparts worldwide, is demonstrating a remarkable commitment to amateur espionage via consumer fitness apps. A recent exposé by Le Monde details how a French navy sailor, with what one can only assume was a profound ignorance or blatant disregard for operational security, broadcast the near-real-time location of the nation's premier nuclear-powered aircraft carrier, the Charles de Gaulle, through the popular Strava app.

The sailor's seven-kilometer run, logged on March 13th while at sea, placed the vessel squarely in the Mediterranean, northwest of Cyprus. While the carrier's deployment to the region to bolster allied assets in the Middle East was publicly announced, the precise, live location of such a critical asset is hardly the kind of detail one wants freely available on a public fitness platform. Le Monde was able to correlate the Strava data with satellite imagery taken just an hour later, confirming the carrier's proximity to the logged workout.

This is not a novel error. Le Monde has been diligently tracking these "StravaLeaks" for some time. The same sailor was previously identified in February, his movements tracked from the coast of France to Copenhagen. The outlet has identified other public Strava profiles revealing ship positions, and even sharing photos of sensitive equipment and personnel. This pattern extends to high-profile security details, with Le Monde previously identifying the hotel used by former U.S. President Joe Biden during a 2023 visit to San Francisco based on Strava activity from his security detail.

The Pentagon, back in 2018, recognized this obvious vulnerability and banned personnel from using geolocation features on devices in operational areas. France's Ministry of Armed Forces, when pressed, acknowledged that the sailor's actions "does not comply with current guidelines," and that "digital hygiene for sailors" is supposedly a prerequisite for deployment. One can only assume these guidelines are more aspirational than enforced, given the persistence of such breaches. Strava, a platform boasting nearly 200 million users, offers privacy settings, but clearly, many military personnel either don't know, don't care, or believe their actions are inconsequential. The reality is, in an era of constant surveillance and evolving geopolitical threats, a carelessly logged workout can be a significant intelligence coup for adversaries. The ease with which such sensitive information is being leaked speaks volumes about the discipline and foresight within modern militaries, or more accurately, the lack thereof.
