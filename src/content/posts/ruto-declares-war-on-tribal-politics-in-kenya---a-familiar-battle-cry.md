Another day, another presidential address, and what do we get? The same old sermon about unity and how "tribal politics" are the enemy of progress. It's almost comical how every politician, once they're in power, suddenly remembers that tribalism is bad. Funny how that sentiment often disappears when they're mobilizing their own base for votes. We're told economic progress depends on "collective effort and trust," but trust is a luxury when you're watching the cost of living skyrocket while the powerful squabble over who delivered which vote.

Speaking of squabbles, the President made sure to remind everyone that *he* personally campaigned for the Mt Kenya vote in 2022, scoffing at those who "arrogantly" claim they delivered it. It's a classic move, isn't it? Disowning your political proxies once they start getting too comfortable. As if the average Mwananchi cares about who's taking credit for votes; we're just trying to figure out if we can afford tomorrow's uji. It just goes to show that [THE DP SEAT IS JUST A MUSICAL CHAIR FOR THE ELITE](/posts/the-dp-seat-is-just-a-musical-chair-for-the-elite), and the rest of us are just spectators to their power games.

Then came the grand enumeration of achievements over the past three and a half years: 100,000 teachers hired, 23,000 classrooms built, and a record Ksh 784 billion for education. On paper, it sounds impressive. But walk into any public school in the informal settlements and tell me if those numbers translate to better learning environments or teachers who aren't overwhelmed. Similarly, the Social Health Authority has "registered" 31 million Kenyans, and Embu hospitals have supposedly received Ksh 2.5 billion. Registration doesn't guarantee access, nor does money paid to hospitals automatically mean quality care for the masses struggling to even get a diagnosis.

And let's not forget the financial wizardry. A whopping Ksh 350 billion "mobilised" for the National Infrastructure Fund from the KPC IPO and Safaricom share sales. Selling off public assets and boasting about "leveraging private capital" often just means enriching a select few while the country's future is gambled away. It makes you wonder about the real strength of our economy when such large-scale maneuvers are needed, especially with the [The Myth of the Unstoppable Rupee](/posts/the-myth-of-the-unstoppable-rupee) always lurking in the background. As for "reduced" fertilizer costs and higher coffee prices, those are the promises we hear every season, often benefiting middlemen more than the actual farmers.

So, the President says he won't allow tribal politics, calls those who divide "enemies of development," and dismisses critics as "empty vessels." Yet, the political class continues its dance of power plays and self-congratulation, while the common Kenyan is left to wonder when these grand pronouncements will actually translate into a tangible improvement in their daily struggle. It's the same old story, just with a new soundtrack.
