Welcome back to the weekly circus where everyone thinks they are a healer just because they found a MIDI pack of "rippling kora" sounds. Lubiana is out here giving us "Divine Feminine" vibes, claiming her music is medicinal and baked in since birth. Honey, it sounds like the background music for a high-end spa that overcharges for bottled water. While she is singing about "nurturing your garden," the rest of us are wondering if she realizes that spiritual depth does not actually pay the rent. It is all very "peace and love" until the streaming checks come in and the wisdom starts looking a lot like a marketing strategy.

Nasty C is also busy trying to convince us he is the "leader of the new school" now that he has escaped his Universal Music Group shackles. He is out here dropping double-entendre bars about Jhene Aiko and Gen-A that are clearly designed for TikTok scholars to dissect. It is cute that he thinks he is "one of one," but he is sweating through his designer gear just to stay relevant in a genre that moves faster than his "No Typo" flow. Let’s be real, he is fighting for airtime because [nostalgia is the only currency left for our aging music stars](/posts/nostalgia-is-the-only-currency-left-for-our-aging-music-stars) who are watching these Gen-Z kids take over with half the effort and double the aura.

Then we have Ayo Maff, who has decided he is our "spiritual mentor" and "vibe dispenser." He is layering prayers like they are choreographed blessings on "God Is Working," as if a catchy hook can settle everyone’s problems. It is the classic industry move: when you cannot innovate, just tell people "everything go settle" and hope they are too busy dancing to realize you are not actually saying anything new. It is communal hand-holding for the disillusioned, and frankly, I have had enough of artists acting like they are the solution to the world's "aching hearts."

Nonso Amadi is still playing the "smooth, gentle loverboy" character, which is getting a bit dusty at this point. His latest track "Greedy" navigates the tension between hope and disappointment, but it sounds like the same old R-and-B tropes we have been hearing since 2016. He is "Greedy" for love, or maybe he is just greedy for another radio hit. It is the same "romantic honesty" that usually masks a guy who is just bad at communicating, wrapped up in a "delicate" melody to make it palatable for the February playlist crowd.

Finally, we have the Nairobi collective We Are Nubia trying to sell us a love letter to the city with "Available." They say Nairobi "tastes so sweet," which tells me they are definitely not taking the matatu at 5:00 PM or dealing with the actual pulse of the city. It is easy to find the city's pulse "intimate" when you are filtering it through bongo flava and high-end production. Between them and the constant "Afro-tech" re-ups from people like Kilimanjaro, the industry is just one big loop of hypnotic synths designed to keep us from noticing how repetitive everything has become.
