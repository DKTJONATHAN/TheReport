NAIROBI'S 2050 MEGA-CITY DREAM: MORE PEOPLE, MORE PROBLEMS

Nairobi is apparently going to be the biggest city in Africa by 2050. According to some fancy charts from the OECD, we'll have 57.2 million people crammed in here. That's more than Cairo, more than Lagos. Sounds like a party, right? Except it means more pressure on everything - roads, houses, jobs. Basically, more of the same struggles, just on a grander, more suffocating scale.

The Kenyan government, with its "devolved system," was supposed to spread the development out. But surprise, surprise, everyone still flocks to Nairobi looking for work. School leavers, desperate for a sniff of opportunity in a city that's already choking on its own success. Businesses aren't creating jobs fast enough, so we can all look forward to more people with nothing to do and nowhere to go. It’s a recipe for more crime, a predictable outcome of our so-called progress. Remember when we talked about [Tech Giants Unite to Fight Online Scams: A Voluntary Sham?](/posts/tech-giants-unite-to-fight-online-scams--a-voluntary-sham)? This is the kind of environment that breeds those scams.

Our infrastructure is already ancient, groaning under the weight of the current population. Now imagine adding millions more. Roads will be parking lots, water taps will run dry, and the sewers will overflow. We spend peanuts on infrastructure per person, a joke compared to developed cities. They plan roads before building houses; we build houses and then complain about the lack of roads. It's backward thinking, and it costs a fortune to fix later.

And housing? Don't even get me started. Regulations and murky land ownership are strangling the supply, driving prices through the roof. People are spending half their income just to rent a tiny box. Africa needs millions of houses, and we're busy congratulating ourselves for being the biggest city, not for actually housing our people decently.

So, Nairobi becoming Africa's largest megacity by 2050 isn't a triumph; it's a warning. It's a test of whether we can manage this uncontrolled sprawl with some semblance of planning and investment. Or, more likely, it's just another chapter in the story of Africa urbanizing without the corresponding economic boost, leaving us with more people, more poverty, and more of the same old Nairobi chaos.
