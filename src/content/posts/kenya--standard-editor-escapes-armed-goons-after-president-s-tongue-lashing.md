So, President Ruto, the man who promised us "media freedom," seems to have forgotten his lines. Just days after he went on X to whine about The Standard Group’s "EXTORTIONIST propaganda HEADLINES" - a classic deflection tactic, really - one of their editors, Alex Kiprotich, nearly becomes a cautionary tale. Three heavily armed jokers in a Probox, a car that screams "state-sanctioned thuggery" in Kenya, tried to snatch him right out of his car. Apparently, just doing your job, pointing out the government's fumbles, is now a high-risk sport.

Kiprotich, bless his bold heart, knew he was a target for calling out the administration. He locked his doors and sped off. Smart move. Because in this country, when the president rails against a media house, and then men with AK-47s show up in a car linked to the police's own intelligence unit, it’s not a coincidence. It's a message. And the message is clear: keep your mouth shut, or risk becoming another "enforced disappearance," a trend that's become depressingly common. It reminds you of the countless activists who vanish, their stories buried before they can even be told.

The Committee to Protect Journalists is predictably up in arms, calling for an investigation and justice. Bless their hearts, they still believe in the system. But we Nairobi realists know the drill. This "investigation" will likely go nowhere, and the "those behind the incident" will remain conveniently unidentified, probably sipping tea with someone important. Remember that Pakistani journalist, Arshad Sharif? Shot dead, a court said police were unlawfully involved, and yet, here we are, same old story.

It’s funny, isn’t it? Ruto, who claimed he wanted journalists to "criticize us when we go wrong," is now presiding over an environment where journalists are being threatened for doing just that. Especially with the 2027 elections looming. The Standard Group, with their critical reporting, like that KTN News series "The Gallery of Ruto’s Lies," is a thorn in his side. And when the stakes are high, like in the power games we've seen play out, [Wetang'ula: The Last Man Standing in Kenya Kwanza's Power Games, for Now](/posts/wetang-ula--the-last-man-standing-in-kenya-kwanza-s-power-games--for-now), any dissenting voice becomes a target.

The police spokesperson, Muchiri Nyaga, dribbling around the question with a dismissive "Police and abduction in the same line? You need to get your facts right." That’s a masterclass in deflection, folks. The facts are exactly what the CPJ is shouting about: men with guns, targeting a journalist after a presidential tirade, in a car linked to police. But sure, "get your facts right." Maybe he meant get *new* facts, the kind that don't implicate the boys in blue.

So, Alex Kiprotich escaped. For now. But this isn't just about one journalist. It’s about the erosion of any semblance of democratic space. It’s about the chilling effect this has on anyone who dares to hold power accountable. And it’s about the tired, predictable script we’re forced to watch play out, over and over again.
