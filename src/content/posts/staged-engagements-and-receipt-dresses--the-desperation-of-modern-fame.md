The music industry has officially reached a new low of thirstiness, and Harper Grace is leading the pack. The 24-year-old country singer spent over a month faking an engagement just to promote a single titled "if daddy says no." She even went as far as staging engagement photos. This brand of "marketing" is becoming increasingly predatory, treating the audience like lab rats in a social media experiment. It is a cynical tactic that reminds me of how [Western pharmaceuticals in Kenya](/posts/Western-pharmaceuticals-in-kenya) have historically viewed people as mere data points for their own gain. She claims it helped her work through a "real situation," but let us be honest: it was all for the algorithm.

While Harper Grace is busy faking a life, the Beckhams are busy faking a happy family. Victoria and David have been very public with their "love" for Brooklyn on social media lately, clearly trying to bury the rumors of a deep-seated feud with the Peltz family. It is high-octane damage control. If the family was actually on speaking terms, they would be having Sunday roast together, not tagging each other in filtered photos to satisfy the British tabloids. The irony of these celebrities using "love" as a PR shield is never lost on me.

Then we have Jessie Buckley, who has gone viral for giving her husband a "me or the cats" ultimatum. Imagine the level of narcissism required to tell a partner to rehome their pets just to secure your presence in the house. It is cold, it is calculated, and it is a perfect example of the "main character syndrome" that plagues Hollywood. It seems some stars do not just want to own the screen; they want to own every living thing in their vicinity.

But if we are talking about being "extra," Lily Allen takes the crown this week. She is currently touring a breakup album about her marriage to David Harbour, and her latest costume is a dress literally made of receipts for things he allegedly bought for other women. It is a level of petty that would be iconic if it did not feel so desperate for a headline. It is one thing to process your trauma through art, but it is another to turn your bank statements into a tour outfit while your marriage is supposedly on the rocks.

Finally, we have the sobering story of a son from a family that "dropped off the radar" 50 years ago finally being identified. It is a haunting contrast to the world of influencers and pop stars. While these celebrities are faking engagements and wearing their drama as fashion statements, some people actually manage to disappear from the face of the earth. Most of these stars could not handle five minutes of actual anonymity, let alone five decades of being truly lost.
