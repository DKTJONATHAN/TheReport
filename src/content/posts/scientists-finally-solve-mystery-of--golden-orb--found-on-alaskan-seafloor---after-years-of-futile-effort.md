The grand pronouncement has arrived: the baffling "golden orb" found lurking on the Alaskan seafloor is, in fact, just the dried-up foot of a giant sea anemone. After years of rigorous testing, genetic sequencing, and the collective brainpower of numerous so-called experts, NOAA and the Smithsonian have declared victory. One can only imagine the urgency and national security implications they’ll try to spin this into.

Discovered in August 2023 at a depth that clearly discourages casual observation, this 4-inch brass colored anomaly stumped researchers. They initially pegged it as everything from an egg casing to a peculiar coral, a testament to their broad but ultimately shallow understanding of the deep sea. Naturally, it was collected and whisked away to a prestigious lab for further, presumably costly, examination.

Even in controlled conditions, the orb proved stubbornly uncooperative. Zoologist Allen Collins, director of NOAA Fisheries’ National Systematics Laboratory, waxed poetic about the "complex mystery" requiring "focused efforts and expertise." This translates to significant funding allocated to what ultimately amounts to identifying a fancy bit of biological scaffolding.

The orb's fibrous composition and the presence of cnidocytes, those delightful stinging cells, pointed towards its cnidarian origins. While preliminary DNA tests were, predictably, messy - a common occurrence when you’re sifting through the microbial soup of the ocean floor - whole genome sequencing eventually linked it to a species named *Relicanthus daphneae*.

So, after 2.5 years of dedicated scientific endeavor, we have our answer. It's not aliens, it's not a lost treasure, it's just… a dead anemone part. William Mowitt, acting director of NOAA Ocean Exploration, shamelessly claims this solves "captivating mysteries" and justifies continued exploration for "economic growth" and "national security." One has to admire the sheer audacity of justifying vast expenditures on identifying sea slug leftovers by invoking economic prosperity and defense. The deep sea, it seems, is less a frontier of groundbreaking discovery and more an expensive dumping ground for scientific curiosity.
