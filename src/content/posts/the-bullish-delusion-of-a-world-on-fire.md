At the heart of this rally is the "TACO" trade, a cynical bet that the American presidency is governed by a lack of conviction. The belief that Trump will always chicken out when the economic pain becomes too visible turns global diplomacy into a high-stakes game of chicken where the passengers are the ones betting on the driver to swerve. It is a fragile foundation for a global economy, suggesting that stability now depends entirely on the perceived weakness of its most powerful actors.

This market resilience is further propped up by the hollow promise of artificial intelligence. While the Middle East burns and 20 percent of the world's oil supply is held at gunpoint, tech stocks continue to soar on the back of algorithms and automation dreams. This decoupling shows a dangerous indifference to the physical world. A "tech boom" cannot manufacture the energy required to run the servers if the global supply chain for oil and natural gas is permanently severed by a protracted conflict.

The international impact of this delusion is profound. Emerging markets and energy-dependent nations are forced to watch as Wall Street celebrates while their own economies face the blunt force of supply shocks. This mirrors the desperation seen in other parts of the world, such as Seoul's price cap gambit, where nations are forced into increasingly frantic maneuvers to survive a global collapse that the stock market refuses to acknowledge.

If the script fails-if the ceasefire collapses or the "chicken out" pivot never happens-the resulting correction will be more than just a 10 percent dip. It will be a violent reconciliation with a reality that has been ignored for too long. The market's current euphoria is not a sign of resilience, but a collective shrug at the prospect of a world where the cost of doing business is measured in more than just dollars.

For the average observer, the advice to ignore the noise and stay the course sounds increasingly like a command to remain seated while the Titanic takes on water. The "long-term perspective" is a luxury that only those insulated from the immediate fallout of war and inflation can afford. For everyone else, the record highs are just a brightly colored curtain hiding a stage that is rapidly falling apart.
