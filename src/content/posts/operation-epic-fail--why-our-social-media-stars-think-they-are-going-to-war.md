The National Police Service finally had to step in and play parent because our local keyboard warriors and thirsty influencers couldn't stop sharing fake news. Apparently, the rumor mill was working overtime claiming that Kenyan officers were packing their bags for the Middle East following the joint US-Israeli strikes. Honey, please. The NPS has unequivocally debunked these claims, reminding everyone that while the world is shaking over Operation Epic Fury and the alleged death of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, our officers are staying right here. It is honestly embarrassing how quickly people will hit the share button on WhatsApp just to feel like they are part of a global crisis.

It is the height of hypocrisy to see these social media personalities acting like military analysts when they can barely manage their own lives. They are busy posting "Pray for Tehran" while ignoring the fact that their own "strategic partnerships" are as fake as their followers. While they play-act at being informed, the reality of the situation is that [Manners for the Elite, Bills for the Rest of Us](/posts/manners-for-the-elite--bills-for-the-rest-of-us) remains the true Kenyan anthem. They want to talk about international war zones but won't mention how they are going to afford the fuel for their German cars once the Strait of Hormuz gets blocked and prices skyrocket.

Speaking of things they can't afford, have you noticed how many of these "A-listers" are still rocking shattered iPhone screens? They want to flex about the new MacBook Pro with the M4 chip and its 24-hour battery life, but they are too cheap to get a simple screen replacement. It is classic Nairobi: wanting the Liquid Retina XDR display experience while living on a cracked budget. If you are going to pretend to be a high-flying tech enthusiast, at least fix your current device before you start manifesting an M4 that you definitely cannot buy without a trade-in deal.

The Inspector General, Douglas Kanja, had to waste perfectly good government time to tell the public that we are not sending anyone to combat zones in the Middle East. Our security apparatus is already busy enough with regional stabilization and commitments in Haiti. But of course, the drama-hungry crowd ignores the boring truth for a spicy lie. They would rather talk about deserts in the Middle East than the very real economic battle happening at the local fuel pump. It is much easier to post a fake dispatch than it is to face the inflationary pressure that is about to hit every single one of us.

At the end of the day, Kenya's battle is economic, not military, no matter how much the gossip circles want to spice it up. While our influencers are busy monitoring global crude prices just so they can complain about the cost of living, the rest of us are watching them struggle to maintain their glamorous facades. The government might be monitoring fuel subsidies, but I am monitoring the sheer desperation of people trying to stay relevant in a digital ecosystem that is increasingly vulnerable to propaganda. Stay in your lane, fix your screens, and leave the geopolitics to the professionals.
