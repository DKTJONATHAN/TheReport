So, William Ruto has decided to ditch the "cool, collected leader" act and dive headfirst into the gutter, lobbing insults like a disgruntled market vendor. Apparently, the President of Kenya thinks mocking his rivals' weight, eating habits, and "sexual life" is the way to campaign for 2027. Let's be clear, this isn't about policy; this is about cheap shots and desperation. He’s worried, plain and simple.

The former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua and ex-ministers Matiang’i and Wamalwa are rightfully calling him out. They’re saying he’s demeaning the presidency. But what do they expect? This is Kenya. Our leaders are masters of theatrics, and Ruto’s little outburst, triggered by Gachagua daring to question his health, proves he's just as human - and thin-skinned - as the rest of them. His "personal discipline" excuse for weight loss? Please.

The scary part isn't just the lack of decorum; it's the precedent. We've seen this movie before, haven't we? The Waki Commission report chillingly detailed how hate speech, spread through SMS and radio, ignited the 2007-2008 post-election violence. Now, with 16 months to go before another election, and the "United Alternative Government" already strategizing against Ruto, these bare-knuckle tactics are a flashing red warning sign. It feels like we're already on the path to another messy showdown, and the opposition's own accusations of corruption and state-sponsored killings don't exactly paint a picture of national unity.

It’s a shame, really. Instead of focusing on the economy, on healthcare, on jobs, our leaders are reduced to playground bullies. It makes you wonder if any of them genuinely care about this country, or if it’s all just a grand, cynical game of power. Remember when things got ugly and the ODM party was tearing itself apart? [The Orange Divorce: Inside the Civil War Threatening to Sink ODM](/posts/the-orange-divorce--inside-the-civil-war-threatening-to-sink-odm). That’s the kind of internal chaos and political maneuvering that distracts from real issues, and Ruto’s current performance is just another brand of that same old drama.

Ruto’s little temper tantrum, fueled by his own insecurities about his health, is a stark reminder that the political landscape in Kenya is as unpredictable as the weather. He’s trying to project strength by tearing others down, but it just exposes his own fragility. We're in for a long, ugly 16 months, and if this is how it’s starting, I shudder to think how it will end.
