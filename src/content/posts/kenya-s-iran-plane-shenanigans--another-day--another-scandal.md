So, January 2025. A Madagascan outfit, probably run by someone with more ambition than sense, wants to ferry some Boeing 777s from China to Kenya for a quick sprucing up. Sounds straightforward, right? A bit of paperwork, a bit of plane-jiggling. Except, of course, it wasn't. Turns out, these fancy planes, meant for a polish in Nairobi, ended up in the hangar of a sanctioned Iranian airline. Colour me surprised.

Naturally, Kenya’s spooks started sniffing around. When you're already convinced Iran is cozying up to Somalia, and then these phantom planes pop up, suspicion turns into a full blown conspiracy theory. It's like that moment you realise your friend who promised to help you move has mysteriously acquired a new couch from somewhere you definitely didn't see them buy it.

And this isn't exactly new territory for Kenya. Remember the good old Cold War days? When Nairobi was a player, apparently wrangling F5 jets out of Washington like it was going out of fashion. Classified documents, no less, whispering tales of aerial acrobatics and political favors. It seems our leaders have always had a knack for getting involved in international drama, sometimes as the star, sometimes as the bewildered extra.

Then there’s the 1976 incident. Israeli jets breezing through Kenyan airspace, refueling at JKIA on their merry way to Entebbe. All very hush hush, of course, until the world found out. It's a pattern, isn't it? Kenya, or rather, certain people within Kenya, seem to have a history of facilitating… well, let’s just call them "transits." Makes you wonder about the true cost of such "friendships," especially when you start looking at the bigger picture, similar to how one might scrutinise the safety of money market funds in Kenya.

This latest plane charade is just another reminder that in this part of the world, nothing is ever as simple as it seems. A Madagascan aviation company, five Boeings, a detour to Iran, and Kenya caught in the middle. It’s a plot thicker than a poorly managed financial trap. One can only assume the maintenance fees for these particular aircraft were *very* attractive.

So, while we fret about the price of ugali and the latest Nairobi matatu trends, the big players are busy orchestrating these elaborate schemes. Kenya gets caught in the crossfire, looking less like a strategic partner and more like a pawn that occasionally gets a shiny new, albeit slightly dodgy, toy. Another scandal, another round of finger pointing, and life in Nairobi goes on, albeit with a bit more cynicism.
