This scenario provides a grim blueprint for international athletic bodies. From FIFA to the International Olympic Committee, the message is clear: if you can afford a legal mercenary, the governing body becomes a mere footnote. Texas Tech’s decision to cut ties with Sorsby despite his courtroom victory shows that institutions no longer protect their own. They are terrified of the collateral damage caused by the Big 12 or the CFP making up rules on the fly. It is a total breakdown of the social contract between the athlete and the school, replacing it with a cold, risk-averse calculation.

Across the globe, observers see a system where a quarterback is forced into a supplemental draft not because he failed on the field, but because the legal world melted down. This is the same brand of administrative rot seen in international vanity projects like /posts/talanta-s--final-phase----another-shiny-project--same-old-promises where the optics of progress are prioritized over the reality of the structure. In the American context, the structure is now just a series of lawsuits waiting to happen, rendering the concept of student-athlete eligibility entirely obsolete.

The move to the NFL is presented by Sorsby's camp as a prudent decision, but in reality, it is a forced extraction. Sorsby had to leave to avoid being a man without a league, a victim of judicial mayhem that the university was too weak to navigate. This is the new global reality: the individual is always expendable when the bureaucratic entities start suing each other. The supplemental draft is not an opportunity here; it is an escape pod from a burning building that the NCAA and the Big 12 helped set on fire.

Ultimately, this creates a vacuum where loyalty is a liability. If a school like Texas Tech will push out its winning quarterback to avoid a headache with the conference office, why should any athlete, or any fan, invest in the institution? The judicial mayhem cited as the reason for his departure is now the primary product of college sports. The actual game is just the background noise for the settlement negotiations, proving once and for all that in the modern era, the courtroom is the only stadium that matters.
