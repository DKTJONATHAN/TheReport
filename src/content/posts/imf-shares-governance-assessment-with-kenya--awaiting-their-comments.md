IMF Shares Governance Assessment With Kenya: Expect More Shambles

This is just another round of the same old song and dance. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) claims it's handed over a draft of its governance assessment to the Kenyan government. They're waiting for comments, they say. This is supposed to be about flagging governance weaknesses and corruption. You know, the usual song and dance that happens before they dole out more money, which, let's be honest, will probably disappear into the usual black holes.

The publication of this assessment? Oh, that’s up to the Kenyan government's consent. So, don't hold your breath waiting for the unvarnished truth. They'll likely cherry-pick what they want the public to see, just enough to keep the taps flowing. This whole "feedback" process sounds more like a negotiation to make sure the report doesn't paint too ugly a picture, because frankly, the picture is already quite grim.

And then there's this "Article IV consultation" - their fancy way of checking the economy's pulse. They're discussing when the next annual visit will happen. Remember the last program? It was abandoned in early 2025. Abandoned. Just like that. Probably after they'd squeezed what they could and the promises went south, which, let's face it, is practically a national sport here.

CNBC Africa is still trying to flog their newsletters, promising insights from "experts across the continent." Experts. Right. More like glorified spin doctors who'll tell you what you want to hear, or what their advertisers pay them to hear. They want you to sign up for their "breaking business news" and "updates." What you'll get is a filtered, sugar-coated version of reality, if you get anything useful at all. This whole circus reminds me of the endless dramas we've seen, like the way our roads turn into a bloodbath for profit in [Kenya’s Road Carnage Industry](/posts/blood--billions--and-the-big-lie--kenya-s-road-carnage-industry).

So, the IMF has "shared" something, and Kenya will "comment." The economy is being "consulted." It's all very official, very bureaucratic. But for the average Kenyan trying to make ends meet, it just means more reports, more discussions, and probably the same old problems festering. Don't expect any miracles. Just expect more jargon and more waiting.







The International Monetary Fund (IMF) announced it has sent a draft of its governance diagnostic assessment to the Kenyan government and is now waiting for their input. This assessment is designed to highlight weaknesses in governance and identify vulnerabilities to corruption, crucial elements that could influence ongoing negotiations for a new IMF-backed program for Kenya. However, the publication of the assessment hinges entirely on the Kenyan government’s approval, meaning we might not see the full, unvarnished truth.

Adding to the usual international financial scrutiny, the IMF also indicated that discussions are underway regarding the timing of the next annual visit, commonly referred to as the Article IV consultation, which examines the overall health of the Kenyan economy. This is happening at a time when the final review of their most recent program was conspicuously abandoned early in 2025, leaving many questions unanswered about the previous commitments and their outcomes.

CNBC Africa, in their relentless pursuit of engagement, is pushing their newsletters, promising a deluge of business news, expert insights, and continental updates. They’re encouraging sign-ups for free newsletters, a standard digital bait-and-switch. One wonders what kind of "expert" insights are truly valuable when the core issues of governance and corruption, which impact everything from business to daily life, are subjected to such opaque processes.

The entire process feels like a carefully choreographed dance. The IMF provides a report, the government offers its carefully curated feedback, and the world is expected to believe that meaningful progress is being made. It’s a cycle that plays out repeatedly, often leaving the public with little concrete improvement. We’ve seen similar patterns of obfuscation and delay in other critical areas, like the deep-rooted issues surrounding [Kenya’s Road Carnage Industry](/posts/blood--billions--and-the-the-big-lie--kenya-s-road-carnage-industry), where the focus often shifts away from accountability.

Ultimately, the IMF sharing an assessment and waiting for comments is just another bureaucratic step in a long, often frustrating, process. The real impact on the ground, for the average Kenyan trying to navigate the complexities of the economy, remains to be seen. Until these assessments lead to tangible, transparent reforms, they will likely be perceived as just another paper exercise.
