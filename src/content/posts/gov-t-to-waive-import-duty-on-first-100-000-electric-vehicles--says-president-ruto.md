Gov't Waives Duty on First 100,000 Electric Cars, Ruto Says, But Don't Hold Your Breath

WARNING: Another grand announcement from the presidency promises a green dream, but don't be fooled. This is just another politician's ploy to look good while the real issues fester. Expect more talk than action, and more empty promises than actual relief for the average Kenyan struggling to make ends meet.

President Ruto’s latest pronouncement about duty-free electric vehicles for the first 100,000 imports sounds like a Christmas gift from Santa himself, doesn't it? He’s talking about a greener future, about becoming a regional hub, all while conveniently ignoring the fact that most Kenyans can barely afford a decent matatu ride, let alone a Ksh 544,000 Toyota C+Walk T. This is for the elite, the ones who can actually afford to import cars, not for the hustlers he claims to champion.

And let's not forget the "challenges surrounding charging infrastructure." That's political speak for "we haven't thought this through, and it’s probably going to be a mess." While they're busy dreaming up these grand schemes, thousands of borrowers are still locked out of the Hustler Fund due to a KSh 5 billion liquidity crunch. Seems like priorities are a bit skewed, wouldn't you say? It's like trying to convince people to buy solar panels when their roof is leaking - a bit premature, and definitely not addressing the immediate problem.

The government's own order of 3,000 electric vehicles for the Ministry of Interior and Defence - a move supposedly driven by rising fuel prices - also reeks of hypocrisy. While they’re splashing out on fancy new EVs, the common mwananchi is still battling with the fluctuating cost of petrol. It’s the same old story, really. The powerful get their shiny new toys, while the rest of us are left to deal with the fallout. It’s not unlike how some celebrities seem to think their wealth makes them immune to consequences, a cash grab that’s not so different from what we see here. [Amazon Buys Oprah Winfrey's Podcast - Another Celebrity Cash Grab](/posts/amazon-buys-oprah-winfrey-s-podcast---another-celebrity-cash-grab)

So, while Ruto is busy congratulating himself on his forward-thinking policies, remember that these grand gestures often come with hidden costs and are rarely as beneficial to the average Kenyan as they’re made out to be. The KSh 3.7 million Mitsubishi Minicab-MiEV Van CD isn't exactly within reach for most. This whole EV push feels more like a distraction, a way to make the government look good without actually tackling the deep-seated economic problems plaguing the country.

Instead of chasing after the latest global trends and promising duty waivers that will likely benefit a select few, perhaps the government should focus on ensuring that basic services are affordable and accessible for everyone. Until then, these EV announcements are just more hot air, another headline designed to impress, not to fix.
