Nairobi, Kenya - So, the geniuses at the Connected Africa Summit 2026 are having a powwow in Nairobi about building a "united strategy" for a "sovereign digital infrastructure." Apparently, our current fragmentation of policies and infrastructure has left a whole lot of us staring at blank screens. It's a real shocker, isn't it? We've got communities still begging for fibre, regions where the internet cuts out more often than a politician's promise, and citizens who are so digitally excluded they probably think 'cloud computing' is just a weather report. It’s like we’re still living in the dark ages, only with slightly better mobile reception - sometimes.

John Tanui, the ICT and Digital Economy Principal Secretary, went on a whole spiel about "massive strides" and how we're at a "critical inflection point." Look, Mr. Tanui, the only inflection point most of us are experiencing is when we’re trying to load a webpage and it just… doesn’t. GSMA data tells us that out of 1.4 billion people, a staggering 75% aren't even connected to mobile internet. And of the ones who *are* in coverage areas? A good chunk, 64% to be precise, are still not using it. What does that tell you? Maybe the strategy should involve making it actually work, not just exist.

They're touting the AfCFTA Digital Trade Protocol as the magic bullet, promising to unlock cross-border commerce and all that jazz. More likely, it's just another way for the suits to talk about business while the rest of us struggle with slow downloads and dropped calls. We're told investing in digital infrastructure, R&D, and AI skills for youth will "leapfrog traditional development." Sounds fancy, but I've seen more innovation in a Nairobi matatu tout's hustle than in these grand pronouncements. We need tangible results, not just more buzzwords and more meetings. It’s not that different from the scramble for foreign currency that plagues our borders, as seen when a [Foreign Suspect Busted With Heroin in Mombasa, DCI Claims Operation Success](/posts/foreign-suspect-busted-with-heroin-in-mombasa--dci-claims-operation-success).

The private sector is apparently ready to pour billions into this "sovereign digital infrastructure," but only if we get our act together with unified data governance and keep our critical infrastructure here. It’s a nice thought, but I’ve seen more promises from politicians than I’ve seen consistent internet speeds. They want us to host computational power on the continent, which sounds great until the power grid goes down. The choices we make today, they say, will shape Africa's digital future. Let’s hope those choices involve more than just another summit and a lot more actual fibre.
