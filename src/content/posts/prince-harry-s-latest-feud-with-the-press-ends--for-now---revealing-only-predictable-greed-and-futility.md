The 10-week legal slugfest featuring Prince Harry and a smattering of other public figures against Associated Newspapers has concluded, leaving behind a lingering scent of predictable anger and the cold, hard reality of a losing battle. Harry, predictably incensed, found himself flanked by a predictable cast of characters - Elizabeth Hurley, Sadie Frost, Elton John, David Furnish, Baroness Lawrence, and Sir Simon Hughes. Their collective grievance: the alleged misuse of private information by the Daily Mail and Mail on Sunday.

This was billed as the toughest newspaper court battle yet. The allegations, if true, would indeed paint a grim picture of journalistic malpractice, detailing phone tapping, computer hacking, and the shady procurement of private details - all criminal offenses, naturally. Associated Newspapers, however, remains steadfast in its denial of any unlawful conduct. One wonders what else they would say.

Unlike Harry’s previous forays into legal warfare against Mirror Group Newspapers and Rupert Murdoch’s News Group, where prior evidence of media malfeasance was already abundant, this trial was reportedly structured to be more manageable. Mr. Justice Nicklin, the judge, seemingly insisted on concrete proof linking specific stories to "unlawful information gathering" and demonstrated knowledge by journalists. This, as expected, made Harry's already uphill battle even more arduous. His barrister, David Sherborne, predictably tested the boundaries, leading to the expected shouting matches and theatrical exasperation from the bench.

The seven claimants, in their predictable display of outrage, offered emotional testimony. Harry, in particular, flew in to testify, lamenting an "endless pursuit" and an "obsession" with his life. He was, predictably, reminded to answer questions rather than pontificate. Elizabeth Hurley, also predictably, was reduced to tears discussing the paternity row surrounding her son. The underlying sentiment was clear: their private lives, it seems, are not for public consumption, especially not by the grubby hands of the Mail.

The dramatic turncoat was a private investigator, Gavin Burrows. Having initially signed a statement confirming his alleged involvement in serious wrongdoing against the Mail papers, he inexplicably switched allegiances. A dispute with a journalist, apparently, was enough to make him claim his signature was forged, forcing Harry’s team to treat him as a hostile witness. Jerry Yanover, a disgraced ex-police officer, further muddied the waters by claiming Burrows boasted about being commissioned by the Mail. The judge, however, seemed unimpressed, questioning the viability of the case without Burrows. Anthony White KC, representing Associated, was no doubt delighted.

The evidence presented, while voluminous, ultimately pointed to Associated Newspapers paying a network of individuals for information. Freelancers, a South Africa-based reporter, and an experienced UK freelancer all provided tips and details, with claimants arguing these methods were unlawful. The publisher, predictably, maintained they simply had "great sources." Payment records suggested the use of numerous private investigators, including Steve Whittamore, who admitted obtaining criminal records and driver details. Associated admitted commissioning some PIs, claiming they believed these individuals had access to legitimate databases for quick information retrieval. The Daily Mail’s editor, Paul Dacre, allegedly banned their use in 2007 after convictions for data protection offenses. White KC, predictably, pointed to the willingness of journalists to testify in defense of their reputations, implying their absence of guilt.

The best evidence, as is often the case, was on paper. However, Associated claimed to have found little in the 20,000 boxes of paperwork, citing the age of the allegations. The claimants presented a leaked "ledger," invoices, and cryptic reporter notes, but proving specific payments for unlawful information proved elusive. Sherborne’s complaint about "playing pin the tail on the donkey" with limited resources was apt. The judge’s persistent questioning about the single best piece of evidence for each article, and Sherborne’s admission of needing to "extrapolate," revealed the fundamental weakness of their case. The judge, predictably, suggested Sherborne was trying to shift the burden of proof.

Journalists, usually shielded from public scrutiny, were forced to explain their methods. Veteran reporters from the Daily Mail and Mail on Sunday were subjected to detailed cross-examination. Katie Nicholl and Rebecca English, Royal Editors, claimed their sources were on the "fringes" of Harry's social circle. Stephen Wright, a former crime editor, vehemently denied allegations of commissioning unlawful techniques, particularly concerning his relationship with a corrupt police officer. For many journalists, the biggest hurdle was recalling the minutiae of stories written over a decade ago.

This case, as the article rightly concludes, could very well be the last of its kind. The passage of time has eroded the claimants' ability to prove their cases, with privacy claims having time limits. The Duke of Sussex, seeking to mend fences with the Royal Family, likely understands that continued litigation is not conducive to reconciliation. In an era where celebrities are more likely to spill their own secrets on social media, these old-fashioned newspaper battles over ink-stained revelations seem increasingly anachronistic. Technology has, predictably, moved on, leaving these legal skirmishes as relics of a bygone era.
