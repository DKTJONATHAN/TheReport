So, President Ruto, bless his heart, finally noticed that Kenya is drowning in sexual and gender-based violence, especially femicide. A 42-member task force was assembled, presumably to brainstorm and drink chai while cases hit record highs. Now, to add to the spectacle, the National Police Service is adopting this fancy "Policare Training Curriculum." They say it's comprehensive, covers everything from survivor communication to digital abuse, and will even be accredited. Sounds impressive on paper, doesn't it? Just like that eCitizen platform that promised efficiency but turned into a whole other drama. [Senate Probes Sh9.4 Billion eCitizen Rip-off, Summons Big Fish](/posts/senate-probes-sh9-4-billion-ecitizen-rip-off--summons-big-fish)

The real kicker is the focus on technology-facilitated GBV. Apparently, our women and girls are being stalked, trafficked, and doxxed online faster than you can say "fake news." A study found X, WhatsApp, and Facebook are the new battlegrounds for this particular brand of misery. And guess who's bearing the brunt? The female university students, of course. They're getting defamed and having their private images spread like wildfire, leaving behind digital scars that are apparently "hard to erase." Senior Superintendent Zipporah Nderitu, the mastermind behind Policare, says this is the "new wave of sexual violence." Well, at least they're calling it what it is.

Now, they claim this Policare thing is more than just training; it's a "one-stop centre" with toll-free lines, interview rooms, trauma centres, and even pro-bono lawyers. Sounds like a utopia for victims, doesn't it? Almost too good to be true. We've seen government initiatives sprout up before, full of grand promises and shiny brochures, only to wither away like a neglected succulent. Let's just hope this isn't another case of putting a fresh coat of paint on a crumbling building.

The human rights component is also being trumpeted, with Eunice Nyong'a from the National Police Service Commission chattering about "highest standards of competency and integrity." It's all very well and good to talk about human rights and dignity, but the reality on the ground, where victims are often treated like criminals or inconvenienced, is a different story. The curriculum was developed with support from a whole list of NGOs, which is great, but the real test will be how effectively this knowledge translates into action by the very police force that has its own history of... well, let's just say questionable practices.

At the end of the day, a curriculum is just paper. It’s the people implementing it, the resources they are given, and the political will to truly crack down on perpetrators that will make the difference. Will this training actually lead to more arrests, more convictions, and a tangible reduction in the sickening rates of SGBV? Or will it just be another box ticked for a government that’s good at creating task forces and training modules, but perhaps less adept at delivering actual justice? We'll see. But don't hold your breath.
