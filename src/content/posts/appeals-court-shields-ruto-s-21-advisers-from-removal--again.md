So, the Court of Appeal has stepped in to "shield" President Ruto's 21 advisers from being kicked out. Apparently, removing them would make the Executive Office "inoperative." What a convenient excuse. It’s like saying you can't clean up a dirty room because you need the mess to live in it. This whole charade smells of the same old song and dance, where the powerful find ways to keep their gravy train rolling.

The High Court had the audacity to declare these advisers unconstitutional in the first place, noting they were created in secrecy, with zero public participation. But then, the Appeals Court, with Justices Weldon Korir, Hedwig Ong’udi, and Samson Okong’o at the helm, decided to put a hold on that decision. It's a classic move: when the law gets too inconvenient, find a loophole, or in this case, a whole appellate route to delay the inevitable.

The government, in its infinite wisdom, argued that the Executive would simply collapse without these unelected advisors. This is the same kind of logic that allows for endless corruption and backroom deals. It makes you wonder if the illusion of safety they engineer is just a precursor to the next financial trap, as we’ve seen before.

The High Court’s inability to "stay its finding of unconstitutionality" forced the state to seek this appellate relief. It's a legal tug of war, with the public's interest seemingly caught in the middle, tossed around like a political football. One court says "unconstitutional," the other says "hold on, let's talk." Meanwhile, the advisers, whoever they are and whatever they do, keep their jobs, funded by our taxes.

This isn't about making the government "inoperative." This is about protecting a system that benefits a select few. It’s about sidestepping accountability and public scrutiny. Just like Sifuna’s Kakamega tour, which was more about drama and less about genuine engagement, this ruling prioritizes the political theatre over the actual substance of good governance.

Ultimately, the question remains: When will the courts actually serve the public good, instead of becoming another arena for political maneuvering and the preservation of the status quo? It seems the "soft life" for some just got a little bit harder to disrupt.
