The Harambee Starlets are currently at Kasarani, sweating under the Nairobi sun as if a few residential training sessions can erase the reality of being ranked 133rd globally. We are told they are preparing for the 2026 Women’s Africa Cup of Nations in Morocco, but let’s be real. In this city, we know the difference between a serious campaign and a well-funded PR stunt. The Federation is busy patting itself on the back for naming a 34-player squad, while half the foreign-based players are still "expected" to arrive. It is the same old story of late arrivals and last-minute logistics that defines Kenyan sports.

Coach Beldine Odemba is trying her best to whittle down a squad of 34, but what is the point of a mini-camp if you only keep 10 players? It feels more like a lottery than a talent search. The local girls like Diana Shakava are talking about "learning from the best," but they are being set up for heartbreak. We are in Group A with Morocco, Senegal, and Algeria. These are teams that actually invest in their women, not teams that treat international friendlies like a surprise party they forgot to plan.

The Football Kenya Federation is acting like they have everything under control, yet they are yet to even name opponents for the upcoming FIFA window. It is the same flavor of institutional incompetence we see across the board. Just like [The Starlink War: Why Safaricom is Panic-Screaming](/posts/tech-daily-2026-01-31), our local giants only start moving when the threat is already at the doorstep. By the time they figure out who the Starlets are playing in February, the tactical advantage will be long gone, buried under layers of bureaucracy and "pending" budget approvals.

For players like Purity Akinyi, coming from the Super League, the experience is "rewarding," but rewards don't win matches against defending champions or North African powerhouses. Kenya hasn't been to Wafcon since 2016. That is a decade-long nap. To wake up now and think we can just walk into Morocco and compete is the kind of delusion that only thrives in Nairobi. We are celebrating "targeted training" while our opponents are likely analyzing our weaknesses over coffee in Rabat.

At the end of the day, the 2026 Wafcon will probably be another expensive trip for officials to enjoy Moroccan tea while the players struggle on the pitch. We want to believe in the "Harambee" spirit, but the spirit is tired, and the pockets of the fans are even emptier. If the Federation cannot even secure friendly matches before the tournament starts, we should stop calling it a preparation and start calling it what it really is: a prayer meeting for a miracle that isn't coming.
