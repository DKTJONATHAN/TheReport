Boniface Mwangi is back on the airwaves, shouting what every cynical Nairobi resident already knows in their bones. He is calling out President William Ruto for essentially turning the 254 into a personal kiosk. Between a housing levy that feels more like a heist and the mysterious billionaire status of a man who has spent his entire life on the government payroll, the math simply does not add up. It is just another chapter of [green dreams and dry pockets: ruto-s addis ababa fairytale](/posts/green-dreams-and-dry-pockets--ruto-s-addis-ababa-fairytale) where the leadership sells us a vision while they feast on the reality.

The activist is pointing fingers at the big industrial players like Bamburi and Devki, claiming they are the real winners of these state-backed projects. It is a classic move- take public land, tax the struggling mwananchi to build on it, and then let private actors reap the benefits. Mwangi is right to ask how a career politician becomes a billionaire while the rest of us are out here updating our CVs for jobs that do not exist. In this town, the only thing that grows faster than the cost of living is the net worth of the people telling us to tighten our belts.

Then there is the issue of the government being flat broke and desperate. They are hunting for coins in our empty pockets through automated traffic cameras and punitive fines, masquerading as road safety. It is not about order; it is about revenue collection. You cannot fine a population into being disciplined when the leaders themselves treat the constitution like a suggestion. Mwangi knows that "people power" sounds good in a speech, but on the ground, it usually just gets you a face full of teargas while the elite discuss their next merger over tea.

Mwangi has set his sights on 2027, promising a platform of free healthcare, education, and social services. It sounds like a dream, but in Nairobi, dreams are expensive and usually end in a headache. He talks about restoring dignity to citizens, but dignity is hard to find when you are stuck in a cycle of heavy taxation and zero accountability. He claims he is not a commercial activist, but in a country where everything is for sale, people are naturally skeptical of anyone claiming to be a servant of the people.

Ultimately, Mwangi argues that saving Kenya requires a coalition of values rather than individual ambition. That is a nice sentiment for a Sunday morning, but history shows us that today-s firebrand activist is often tomorrow-s silent beneficiary of the system. Whether it is youth-led protests or another round of promises from the pulpit, the reality remains the same- the small elite keeps eating, and the rest of us are just left holding the bill for a party we were never invited to.
