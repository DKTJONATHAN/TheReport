Just when you thought South African politics could not get any more dramatic, Showmax decides to turn the VBS Mutual Bank disaster into a four-part docuseries. Because nothing says "grab the popcorn" like billions of rand disappearing from rural villagers. "The People vs. VBS" is coming to your screens on March 25, and honestly, the trailer alone has more tea than a Sunday afternoon brunch in Sandton. Director Richard Finn Gregory is back at it again, proving that if there is a financial corpse to pick over, he is the man with the camera.

The guest list for this mess is actually wild. We have got Julius Malema, the EFF founder who is never one to shy away from a microphone, and the South African Reserve Bank governor, Lesetja Kganyago. They even dragged in former PIC CEO Dan Matjila. It is basically a "Who is Who" of people who should probably be spending more time in court and less time in makeup chairs for a Showmax original. The documentary promises stories of R5 million cash leaving in bags by helicopter and mayors asking for "Christmas presents" while the bank was essentially turning into a glorified pyramid scheme.

Let us talk about the real star of the show: the hypocrisy. This bank was supposed to be the "black-owned" model for the future, but instead, it became a personal piggy bank for the elite. It is the same brand of clout-chasing we see when [Zakayo the Peacemaker: Ruto Plays Global Policeman While Local Celebs Chase Clout](/posts/zakayo-the-peacemaker--ruto-plays-global-policeman-while-local-celebs-chase-clout) instead of fixing the kitchen sink at home. Everyone wants to be a hero on the global stage or a star in a documentary, but nobody wants to explain where the money went.

The series even touches on the 2016 drama when VBS loaned Jacob Zuma over R7 million for his Nkandla upgrades. That was more than the bank’s annual net profit at the time. Talk about a "friend with benefits" situation. While the bank’s executives were allegedly orchestrating fraud, they were still making sure the high-profile "investors" were kept happy. It is a cautionary tale, but let us be real: the only people learning a lesson here are the ones who lost their savings while the villains get to be TV stars.

If you are into true crime that feels a little too close to home, this one is for you. Between the assassinations of whistle-blowers and the absolute audacity of the "royal households" involved, it is a mess that even a high-budget production can barely contain. Catch it on Showmax or M-Net if you want to see how the other half steals. Just do not expect a happy ending; the court cases are still dragging on eight years later, which is just the cherry on top of this cynical sundae.
