Kalonzo Musyoka is back on his favorite soapbox, acting like he just discovered water is wet. By Tuesday, February 24, 2026, the Wiper leader was busy tweeting his shock that the police are being used to silence him. It is the same old Nairobi story: a politician gets a whiff of teargas and suddenly remembers that security agencies should serve citizens. We have seen this movie so many times that we can recite the dialogue from memory.

The script this time features President William Ruto as the villain, with Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen and PS Raymond Omollo playing the supporting roles of the frustrators. Kalonzo claims these men are working overtime to sabotage opposition meetings. He is promising a one-term presidency for Ruto, a threat that carries about as much weight as a paper bag in a July windstorm. In this town, unity is just a word leaders use when they cannot get a permit for a rally.

The irony is thick enough to choke on. Just a week ago, on February 16, Kalonzo and his team walked straight from the Inspector General’s office into a cloud of teargas at Tea Room. It is almost poetic. You go to demand accountability from the boss and get a face full of chemicals from the subordinates five minutes later. It is the same level of institutional incompetence we saw when [fake tickets and political hoarding](/posts/CHAN-tickets-Scam) locked genuine fans out of the stadium. The system is not broken; it is working exactly how it was designed to work: for the elite, by the elite.

Musyoka’s talk about healthcare and education working is a nice touch, but let us be real. These guys only care about service delivery when they are the ones being blocked from addressing a crowd. When they are in power, the police are maintaining order. When they are out, the police are tools of oppression. It is a revolving door of hypocrisy that keeps the Nairobi elite fed while the rest of us just try to avoid the canisters.

So, Kalonzo says the spirit of the people remains unshaken. That is politician-speak for please keep showing up so I look relevant. The message might be growing stronger everywhere, but so is the realization that whether it is Ruto or Kalonzo, the boots on the ground will always belong to whoever signs the checks. We are just the audience for a very expensive, very loud, and very teary theater production.
