So, George Barasa, a Kenyan musician and HIV-positive gay man, thought he’d found a safe haven in South Africa. Bless his heart. Turns out, South Africa’s "progressive constitution" meant squat when it came to actual protection. He was basically treated like a criminal, paraded around and mocked by immigration officials who demanded he "prove" he was gay. Two years of living in what he calls an "open-air prison," unable to get a proper job or even a bank account, all while xenophobic riots were kicking off. Sounds about right for our continent.

His Kenyan passport was confiscated, trapping him in a loop of temporary permits. Apparently, constitutional rights only apply to citizens, which is news to absolutely no one who’s actually lived here. He was about to be deported back to Kenya, where he’d face prison for being himself. Imagine that. A country that punishes people for who they are, and another that just shrugs its shoulders and kicks them out. This is the kind of "progress" we brag about.

Thankfully, some activists and the UNHCR finally stepped in, bypassing the completely useless South African system. They treated his case as an "emergency," which is funny, because for Barasa, it was an emergency for years. Amidst all the chaos and tear gas outside the immigration offices - the same chaos that makes it impossible for us to even do basic things like access services, much like the struggles faced by those dealing with [Duale’s Digital Dreams vs. The Reality of Empty Shelves](/posts/duale-s-digital-dreams-vs--the-reality-of-empty-shelves) - he got his papers and was flown to Canada.

This whole debacle is just another shining example of how broken the global refugee system is. The UN usually just shuttles people around in large groups, but when an individual’s life is on the line, they can, *if* they choose, step in. They’ve done it for other LGBTQ+ refugees in Kenya, and for dissidents. It’s a rare show of competence, proving that when the local systems fail so spectacularly, international intervention is the only lifeline. But it shouldn't have to get to that point.
