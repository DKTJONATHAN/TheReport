President William Ruto, ever the optimist, trotted out his familiar script at the G7 Summit in France. His plea? See Africa as a partner, not a beggar. He spoke of "vast potential" and the need for "genuine support." It's a line recycled more often than a bad meme, especially when our leaders are hobnobbing with the global elite. He challenged the world to rethink its perception, as if a few well-placed words will suddenly erase decades of skewed economic structures.

The President was quick to point out that Africa is "mispriced," forced to borrow at exorbitant interest rates, higher than "comparable economies." He called out the IMF, World Bank, and IFC for "exaggerating the risks" and undermining our growth. Fair points, for sure. But these are the same institutions that have been dictating terms for decades. Do we honestly expect them to suddenly change their tune because one African president gave a passionate speech? Hours later, the G7 leaders *generously* 'endorsed' a declaration on "mutually beneficial international partnerships" and identified "debt reform as priority." Sounds nice on paper.

This "debt reform" is another 'priority' for the G7, right up there with their last dozen. Remember Kananaskis in 2025? They talked about "stronger protections in debt contracts" and "repayment breaks for countries hurt by climatic disasters." Yet, only Canada has bothered to insert such clauses, and China, one of the biggest lenders, isn't even in the room. Africa is unfairly judged on risk and charged heavily on interest to secure credit. The impact of that, he has argued, is that Africans are perennially paying loans instead of utilising the monies to develop. It's a tale as old as time, a [DEBT FOR BREAKFAST: THE GOVERNMENT’S PLAN TO BORROW US INTO OBLIVION](/posts/debt-for-breakfast--the-government-s-plan-to-borrow-us-into-oblivion) scenario playing out on a continental stage.

While Ruto was busy pitching Africa's "positive light," the G7 was gathering against a backdrop of global chaos: fuel shortages, multiple wars, Ebola outbreaks. Our debt troubles, while very real for us, are likely just a footnote in their grand scheme of things. French President Emmanuel Macron extended an invitation to Ruto to "represent the African continent" - a polite gesture, perhaps, but it hardly guarantees a fundamental shift in power dynamics. We're still asking, not demanding.

Ruto also spoke of building "African multilateral financial institutions" to "de-risk" the continent and act as guarantors. A noble vision, but one that clashes with the powerful grip of the existing international lenders. And UN reforms? Another old chestnut, endlessly discussed but rarely acted upon. The G7 "recognise the value of the United Nations system... and encourage reform," but actions speak louder than platitudes. Until those actions materialise, it's just more talk in fancy halls, while everyday Kenyans continue to feel the pinch of unpayable debt.
