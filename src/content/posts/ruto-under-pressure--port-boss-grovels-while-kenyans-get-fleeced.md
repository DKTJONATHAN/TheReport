So, President Ruto's suddenly worried about what the locals think about the Mombasa port. Under local pressure, he's asked his teams to find a spot for some new infrastructure project. Fancy that, the man who’s always looking to impress his wealthy friends is now having to listen to the common mwananchi. It’s almost heartwarming, if you ignore the fact that this likely means more tenders for the connected and more headaches for everyone else. Nigeria’s Dangote probably dodged a bullet, or at least a Kenyan bribe.

And let’s not forget the ongoing saga of those substandard fuel imports. The police are crawling at a snail's pace, which, surprise surprise, has prompted the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission to jump in. Bless their hearts, trying to make a dent with their "limited powers." They’re not even afraid of clashing with cabinet members backed by the president. It’s a nice thought, but we’ve seen this movie before. The house always wins, especially when it comes to lining pockets.

Ruto’s grand plan for those gold mines in the west, supported by his coastal allies, is also proving to be a damp squib. Turns out, the public isn't too keen on seeing their land dug up for the benefit of a select few. It’s a predictable outcome, really, when "development" sounds suspiciously like "exploitation." It makes you wonder if any of these grand pronouncements are ever about more than just the next political manoeuvre, much like the predictable power grabs seen under the guise of humanity.

Then there’s the whole Kenya Ports Authority sale off scare. Uganda and Rwanda were worried their imports would get pricier. Ruto, the great compromiser, has apparently found a "solution." We'll see what kind of "compromise" it is. Usually, these compromises mean you get a slightly smaller slice of the bad deal. It’s all a performance, really, a carefully orchestrated dance of power and influence, where the common Kenyan is just a prop.
