So, Raphael Tuju, the man who used to jet around the world for Kenya, has decided to play hide-and-seek. His family claims he and his driver vanished on Saturday, supposedly en route to a radio chat. Now, the police are "investigating" - which usually means a lot of hand-wringing and very little actual action, unless the right people are involved. His fancy car was found, hazard lights blinking like a desperate plea, abandoned in Karen. Typical.

The whole thing smells fishy, and not just because Tuju's been in a drawn-out spat over his properties. Apparently, some big shots want his land, and he claims dozens of police officers - yes, *police officers* - raided his place and kicked out his staff. Powerful officials, he says. The government, of course, is silent as a tomb. They're waiting to see which way the wind blows before committing to anything, as usual.

This isn't some random street mugging. This is a man embroiled in a $15 million debt battle. He’s been fighting tooth and nail in court, losing at every turn, but somehow still managing to cling on. Now, poof, he’s gone. His lawyer admits Tuju had reported being followed by an unmarked car. Great foresight, wasn't it? Makes you wonder if this whole "disappearance" is more of a calculated exit.

The police are now asking for the public's help, urging everyone to report anything useful. They also want Tuju's family to "cooperate," conveniently forgetting they’ve apparently been denied access to his residence. It's the same old script: blame the victims, demand access, and hope everyone forgets the details. The opposition, naturally, is out in full force, crying foul and demanding answers. They’re quick to condemn abductions of "people of his stature," but I bet they’d be less vocal if it were someone from the streets. It’s all about perception, isn’t it? Just another day in Nairobi, where the powerful play games and the rest of us watch.
