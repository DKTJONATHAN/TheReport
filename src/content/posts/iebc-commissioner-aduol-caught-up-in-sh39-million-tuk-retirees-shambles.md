So, the esteemed Prof. Francis Aduol, now a commissioner at the very body meant to oversee our elections - the IEBC - finds himself in a familiar Kenyan drama. Apparently, Sh39 million meant for Technical University of Kenya (TUK) staff pensions has vanished. Poof. Gone. And who’s under the spotlight? Our very own professor, who conveniently also sits on the IEBC’s seven-commissioner panel, just as they kick off their six-year term. Makes you wonder what other "operations" they're supporting with the public’s hard-earned cash.

Aduol’s defence is a classic: the money was "used to support university operations" but will eventually land in the pension pot. Right. Because that’s how financial management works, isn't it? Just borrow from Peter to pay Paul, and hope nobody notices the growing hole in Peter's lifelong savings. The University Academic Staff Union (Uasu) is understandably screaming blue murder, pointing out that retirees and staff are now staring down the barrel of losing everything they’ve worked for. It’s the same old story, just a different pot of money being raided.

This whole kerfuffle is happening just as we’re gearing up for those Thursday by-elections, which are being touted as a "litmus test" for 2027. A litmus test for what, exactly? More of the same incompetent or corrupt management? It’s hard to have faith in election preparedness when the very people supposed to be above reproach are knee-deep in financial scandals. It’s not exactly inspiring confidence, is it? It’s almost as if integrity is a foreign concept in these circles, a bit like navigating the world of dubious travel influencers who might be using a fake passport.

And the National Assembly? They "dismissed memoranda" raised against nominees. Of course, they did. Why let a few pesky allegations of financial impropriety derail a good appointment? It’s all about moving forward, apparently, even if that forward motion is built on a foundation of missing millions and shattered trust. This latest scandal just adds another layer to the ongoing fight against high seas crime and financial recklessness, proving that even on dry land, the wolves are very much at the door.

It’s a shame, really. People work their entire lives, diligently putting aside funds for their retirement, only to see it treated like a convenient slush fund for "university operations." And then, when questioned, the response is a shrug and a promise that it will all be sorted out eventually. This isn't just about Sh39 million; it's about the blatant disregard for the security and futures of ordinary Kenyans. But hey, at least the IEBC commissioners are settled in for their six-year term. They’ve got plenty of time to make up for lost funds, presumably.
