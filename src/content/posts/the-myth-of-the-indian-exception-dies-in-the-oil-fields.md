While the RBI tries to project confidence by citing "resilient domestic fundamentals," this is little more than a desperate attempt to keep foreign capital from fleeing. Consumption cannot remain resilient when the cost of moving goods and powering cities is tied to a volatile energy market that India does not control. The narrative of self-sufficiency is a convenient mask for a nation that remains deeply tethered to the whims of global supply chains and geopolitical ego. As inflation forecasts rise, the purchasing power of the much-touted Indian middle class will be the first casualty.

The international impact is clear. As India lowers its growth projections, the global South loses one of its few remaining engines of growth. If the most promising emerging market is faltering under the weight of external shocks, it signals a broader collapse of the post-pandemic recovery narrative. Much like how the Antarctic "Danger Zone" expedition proves our global maps remain incomplete, the current economic crisis proves that central bank models are woefully unequipped for a world in permanent friction. You can read more about that sense of global uncertainty here: /posts/antarctic--danger-zone--expedition-stumbles-upon-yet-another-uncharted-island--proving-global-maps-remain-incomplete

Furthermore, the looming shadow of U.S. protectionism and the systemic risks highlighted by the Adani indictment suggest that India's "Goldilocks moment" was never about stability. It was a temporary lull before the inevitable return of structural chaos. When oil prices dictate the fate of a billion people, "resilience" is just another word for survival. The world is not entering a new phase of growth; it is merely managing its descent.
