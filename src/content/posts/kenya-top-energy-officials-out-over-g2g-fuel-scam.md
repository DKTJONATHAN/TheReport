Kenya's Top Energy Officials Out Over G2G Fuel Scam

Another day, another scam. This time it's fuel, where our top guys allegedly cooked the books to make a quick buck. Expect the usual fallout, more talk, and maybe a few heads rolling while the real problems fester.

The stench of corruption is thick enough to choke on. Three of Kenya's most senior petroleum sector officials have resigned, with others facing criminal and administrative proceedings, all thanks to a government investigation that apparently stumbled upon a scheme to fake a national fuel shortage. They say it was to exploit rising global prices and public anxiety. What else is new? These are the same folks who were supposed to be securing our supply, not lining their pockets. Joseph Wafula, Deputy Director of Petroleum, and Joel Mburu, KPC's Supply and Logistics Manager, are already in the hot seat.

The presidential notice paints a grim picture: primary duty bearers, entrusted with our fuel supply chain, allegedly manipulated data on our fuel stocks. Why? To trigger irregular procurement of an emergency fuel cargo. This wasn't just any procurement; it was a blatant breach of the G2G framework, bought at a price way above contracted rates, and to top it all off, the quality was suspect. The MV Paloma, carrying this dubious cargo, docked at Mombasa between March 27th and 29th, 2026. Detectives believe this fuel bypassed the official G2G channels, likely sourced from Saudi Aramco, rerouted through some international firm, and then allegedly funneled by a local Kenyan importer. It’s the same old Nairobi hustle, just on a grander scale.

The good news, if you can call it that, is that a KPC quality assurance manager had the guts to flag the consignment for elevated sulphur levels, making it non-compliant. This manager refused to authorize discharge, triggering internal pressure and disagreements, which eventually brought investigators into the picture. Their raid on officials' homes on Thursday night allegedly uncovered documents and cash. The government is calling it a serious breach of public trust, potentially economic crimes under our so-called Anti-Corruption and Economic Crimes Act and the Penal Code. They've directed all governance actors in the petroleum sector to cooperate, which in Kenya usually means "don't get caught doing the same thing."

Despite the arrests, KPC's board is assuring everyone that operations remain stable, with Pius Mwendwa stepping in as acting MD. They claim our fuel supply is secure, thanks to the contracted G2G suppliers who haven't blinked. We've got about 16 days of petrol and 19 days of diesel cover. The current pricing cycle isn't expected to be affected, but don't get too comfortable, as pump prices are predicted to rise from mid-April. The Energy Cabinet Secretary has even ordered oil marketing companies to release any hoarded fuel, threatening them with license breaches. This whole G2G arrangement, established after the 2022 fuel chaos, was meant to stabilize supply and ease forex pressure, but it seems it just created new avenues for graft. Maybe it’s time to revisit that idea of shifting some procurement to an open tender system, even though the government is considering a phased wind-down. After all, we saw how well the last tender system worked out, not so different from the fancy talk you hear at events like the Moi Forces Academy Career Day.

This scandal couldn't have landed at a more inconvenient time. With global oil prices climbing due to Middle East tensions and a looming fuel price review, public confidence in our institutions is at an all-time low. The investigators are still at it, promising to reverse irregular shipment requisitions. But let's be honest, the fuel might be rerouted, but the money? That's probably already gone.
