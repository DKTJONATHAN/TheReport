So, Pheelz has decided he is the self-appointed gatekeeper of the "musical soul." The Nigerian producer turned Toronto transplant is currently doing the rounds, clutching his pearls over the rise of Artificial Intelligence. Apparently, because his daddy was a pastor, he thinks every 808 drop is a religious experience. He is out here claiming that sound and spirit are inseparable. Give me a break. Most of these guys can barely finish a verse without five layers of pitch correction, but suddenly, when a computer does it, it is a "spiritual crisis."

Let's be real about what is actually happening on Spotify. The platform is flooded with generic tracks made by algorithms that are racking up millions of streams. These "synthetic artists" are even hitting the Billboard charts while real artists are struggling to pay their rent. Apple and Spotify are being predictably shady, hiding the "AI" disclosure in metadata where nobody looks. It is all about the bottom line, and Pheelz is right to be worried-not because of "spirituality," but because his royalties are about to get split with a server in Silicon Valley.

Pheelz argues that "human errors are part of perfection." That sounds like something you would find on a dusty Pinterest board or a handwritten plaque in a studio-oh wait, he actually has one of those. He wants us to believe that Afrobeats is "the spirit before it sounds." If that is true, then half the hits we are dancing to in the clubs are currently on life support. This desperate search for "authenticity" feels a bit forced, much like [The Brutal Truth: Why Kenyan Men Are Struggling to Get Laid in 2025](/posts/why-men-are-nolonger-getting-laid) when they realize that "vibes" and "soul" alone won't get them noticed in a world that demands actual effort.

The industry is currently "all babies" when it comes to AI, or so Pheelz claims. He is waiting for rules and checks that probably won't come until the labels have already figured out how to fire half their roster and replace them with holograms. He thinks the "soul will always prevail," but since when has the music industry cared about the soul? They care about TikTok trends and 15-second clips that go viral. If a robot can make a catchy hook, the labels will sign the robot and skip the "sacred exchange" altogether.

At the end of the day, Pheelz's crusade is cute, but it smells like a man trying to protect his territory. Afrobeats might be rooted in the legacy of Fela Kuti, but the modern version is a commercial machine. If the "spirit of the people" is just a data point for an algorithm to mimic, then we are all in trouble. But hey, as long as the beat drops and the checks clear, most of these artists will keep preaching about "art" while checking their bank balances. It is all a show, and we are just the audience paying for the tickets.
