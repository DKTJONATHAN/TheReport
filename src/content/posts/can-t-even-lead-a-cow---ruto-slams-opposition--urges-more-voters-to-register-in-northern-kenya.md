Ruto Throws Doles to North, Calls Opposition "Cow Herders"

President Ruto tours Northern Kenya, promising development and voter registration, while launching a verbal assault on the opposition.

President Ruto, a man who knows how to whip up a crowd, was in northern Kenya slinging promises and insults like a seasoned market vendor. He trotted out the usual lines about voter registration, dangling development goodies, and, of course, taking a good old swing at the opposition. Apparently, the "hatred and anger" of his rivals is so bad they "cannot even lead a cow." Nice one, Mr. President. Meanwhile, he’s busy fast-tracking ID cards, a constitutional right, mind you, but conveniently timed for a voter registration drive. Go figure.

The man’s got a plan, or so he says, a Sh100 billion National Infrastructure Fund, aiming for Sh300 billion to build roads and generate electricity. He’s even talking about correcting "past political injustices." Let's just hope this fund doesn't turn out to be another chapter in Kenya's road carnage industry, like we've seen before. The opposition, meanwhile, is painted as a bunch of lunatics needing SHA registration and hospital visits. It’s a classic case of deflection, isn't it? Accuse the other side of being sick while you're the one dishing out cash.

And the cash… oh, the cash. Sh10 million for three secondary schools, another Sh10 million for a mosque, Sh1 million for a beekeeping women's group, and Sh5 million for "locals to feast" for electing the UDA guy. It’s a veritable smorgasbord of development promises: Sh350 million for water, Sh330 million for roads, Sh600 million for housing in Banisa. All so we can, as the President so eloquently put it, "reach Singapore by building our own country." Sounds more like buying votes with taxpayer money, but what do I know? I'm just a cynical Nairobi realist.

He also made sure Mandera gets its fair share of "transformation" with nine generators for power and plans to connect to Ethiopia's grid. Plus, that 750km Isiolo-Mandera road, with eight contractors already on site. And to top it off, local teachers are being trained and hired locally. It’s all about stability and continuity, apparently. While they're busy talking about Madaraka Day celebrations in Wajir and ensuring Wajir Stadium is ready, one has to wonder if the real "development" is just for show, another piece in the grand political theatre.
