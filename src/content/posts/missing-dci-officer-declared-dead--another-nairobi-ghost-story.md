So, Police Constable James M’Athiru, a bloke from the Directorate of Criminal Investigations no less, has officially been declared dead. After vanishing over a decade ago, in 2014, the High Court decided it’s high time to give his family some semblance of closure. Eleven years of not knowing, of hoping, of probably dealing with vultures who prey on grief. Now, he’s legally a ghost. Nairobi has a way of doing that, you know. Makes people disappear like cheap chai from a street vendor.

And let's not pretend this is a heartwarming tale of legal efficiency. While this officer gets his posthumous recognition, we’ve got magistrates out there extorting cash from desperate court users. The system’s rigged, my friends. You’re more likely to get fleeced by a judge than find justice in this city. It's a circus, and we’re all just clowns paying for the show. This whole "presumed dead" business? It’s just another bureaucratic band-aid on a gaping wound of institutional failure.

Then you have stories like Roy Veevers, whose body has been chilling in a morgue for over twelve years. Twelve years. Imagine the cost. Imagine the indignity. It’s a testament to how we, as a society, treat the forgotten. Meanwhile, somewhere else, a woman’s body is found stuffed in a sack near Ngata Bridge, a few kilometres from… well, who cares from where? It's just another nameless statistic in our city's ongoing drama. This is the Nairobi we know.

It’s not just about missing persons and dead bodies, though. We've got these grand pronouncements about gender equality, with intentional fathers supposedly advancing it through land ownership. It’s a nice thought, a pretty picture to paint over the rot. But let’s be real, cultural resistance is a beast, and intentions don't pay bills. Unless that land ownership comes with a healthy dose of cash and actual enforcement, it's just more hot air. Just like that [Carbon Registry](https://www.example.com/posts/the-carbon-registry--kanairo-s-newest-bureaucratic-playpen) they keep talking about; sounds fancy, but is it actually doing anything?

Ultimately, a marriage certificate might be your official proof of lawful spouse status, but in this city, your legal status can be as fluid as the water supply. One minute you're a serving officer, the next you're legally deceased. It's a constant state of flux, a reminder that in Nairobi, nothing is certain except the next hustle and the next unanswered question. We’re all just trying to navigate the chaos, hoping we don't end up as just another file gathering dust.
