So, King Kaka had an album launch, and the Nairobi "elite" showed up to parade their questionable fashion choices. Awinja, bless her heart, "stunned" in a velvet maroon dress. Yes, a dress. Accentuating her "natural curves" - because apparently, that's a feat worth celebrating. She matched it with a maroon bag and heels, a whole coordinated effort that screams "look at me."

Then there's Mjaka Mfine, who apparently thought a red carpet was an invitation to a costume party. All red, a hat, a maxi dress, and enough gold jewelry to fund a small nation. While some were busy flaunting their wealth, Otile Brown decided jeans and a vest were the height of fashion, topped off with an overgrown coat. Real "elegance and opulence" right there.

Eddie Butita and his girlfriend Celeste at least managed to coordinate, a rare feat. He wore a vest suit, she wore a figure-hugging dress with a hat-thingy. King Kaka himself donned a checkered suit, which apparently had "tongues wagging." Probably about how much it cost, or how little it actually mattered.

Risper Faith made an appearance, and her baby bump apparently "stole the show." Because nothing says "elegance" like a pregnant woman at a music event. Amina Abdi and Shee Kemunto went for all-black, which is always safe, but Kemunto's dramatic hat just screams for attention. Oga Obinna, at least, remembered the dress code and wore a tuxedo. Finally, someone got the memo.

It's always the same old story with these events. A bunch of people showing off, pretending it's about the music. It's all about who wore what, who's dating whom, and who can get the most clicks. This whole scene reminds me of how politicians operate, all flash and no substance, like that time Matiang'i snapped back at Ruto's gym jab. It’s all just a big performance.

And let's not forget the constant push for engagement. "Search option is now available at TUKO!" they scream. Because you really need to search for more content about people you already see everywhere. It’s a relentless cycle of manufactured drama and superficial appearances, a perfect metaphor for how many things in this city operate.
