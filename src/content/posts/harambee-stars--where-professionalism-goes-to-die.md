In Nairobi, we are used to people disappearing when the bill comes, but doing it to international anti-doping officials is a special kind of "ujanja." Harambee Stars defender Ronney Onyango has managed to get himself provisionally suspended by the Anti-Doping Agency of Kenya (ADAK). Why? Because he could not be bothered to update his whereabouts. It is the classic Kenyan story: the talent is there, but the discipline is currently in the intensive care unit.

This "whereabouts failure" is not just a small clerical error. It is a sign of a player who thinks the rules are for other people. In a world where every move is tracked, trying to hide from testers is like thinking you can escape [the digital panopticon’s glitch](/posts/the-digital-panopticon-s-glitch--google-strips-pixel-features-after-exposing-user-privacy) that monitors our every move. You are a professional athlete, not a teenager sneaking out of a boarding school. If you cannot tell a tester where you are, why should a coach trust you on the pitch?

Benni McCarthy must be wondering what he walked into. The man is trying to prep for the 2026 FIFA Series in Kigali, and his "dependable" right-back decides to become a ghost. This is not just a setback; it is a middle finger to the entire technical bench. Now we have to pretend that Daniel Sakari or Alphonce Omija are ready to fill the gap, while the reality is we are just recycling the same problems and hoping for a different result.

The timing is almost comedic. Just as the rumors of a move to Europe or Tanzania were heating up, Onyango decides to go radio silent on the testers. Nothing kills a transfer value faster than a potential two-year ban. European clubs do not want "potential" that comes with a side dish of disciplinary hearings and anti-doping violations. It is a spectacular way to fumbled the bag while everyone is watching.

We keep talking about co-hosting the 2027 Africa Cup of Nations like it is some glorious destiny. But with players who cannot even fill out a location form, we will be lucky if we even have a squad that is not sitting in the stands serving bans. ADAK is just doing their job, but in this city, doing your job is often seen as an act of aggression against the "hustle." This is not a hustle; it is just a mess.
