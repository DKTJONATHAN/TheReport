With 70 to 80 percent of the budget already locked into mandatory spending, the upcoming national budget is little more than a work of theatrical fiction. When interest payments, a bloated civil service, and pensions are deducted, there is virtually nothing left for the actual population. This is the terminal stage of a mismanaged frontier market: a scenario where the state exists solely to pay back the money it borrowed to build infrastructure it cannot afford to operate.

The international implications are grim and far-reaching. Bangladesh is a critical node in the global apparel supply chain, yet it is currently trapped in a cycle of exporting low-cost labor while importing expensive high-level skills. It is a nation that built 30,000 megawatts of power capacity but failed to secure the fuel or the grid stability to run it. For global investors, this is a warning shot that mirrors the reality seen in The Great Indian Illusion Collapses Under Macro Pressure. If the garment factory of the world cannot keep the lights on because it is too busy servicing interest to the IMF, the regional instability will be profound.

Stagflation is no longer a risk; it is the resident reality. Printing money will ignite the already punishing 9 percent inflation into a full-scale bonfire, while domestic borrowing will choke out what remains of the private sector. The government is trapped in a room with a shrinking ceiling and a rising floor. There is no stimulus coming to save this. There is only the slow, cynical realization that the years of easy credit and superficial growth have reached their logical, dark conclusion.

Structural reforms are being touted as the last hope, but these are the same reforms that have been ignored for a decade. Separating the National Board of Revenue or fixing the banking sector requires political capital that the current administration cannot afford to spend while the public is being crushed by the cost of living. Without a radical shift away from borrowing-led growth, the economy will remain stuck in a loop of crisis management until the system finally snaps under its own weight.
