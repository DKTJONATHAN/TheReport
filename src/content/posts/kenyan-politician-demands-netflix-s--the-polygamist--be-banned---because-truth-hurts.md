Another day, another Kenyan politician discovering the internet and deciding it needs taming. This time, it's Geoffrey Mosiria, a man who presumably has exhausted all other pressing national issues, now waging war on Netflix. His target? The Polygamist, a show he claims inaccurately portrays a practice he holds dear. Never mind the millions globally who've watched it or its spot in Netflix's Top 10 non-English shows worldwide; clearly, Mr. Mosiria's personal perception is the only metric that matters.

His argument is a gem of self-referential logic: "If you say polygamy is bad and I'm a product of polygamy, then you mean that my father made a mistake bringing me into this world." The sheer audacity to equate a dramatic series' portrayal with the legitimacy of his own existence is a level of self-importance only a Kenyan politician can truly master. As if his personal experience somehow negates the varied, often complex, and sometimes painful realities of polygamous unions for others. It's a classic move: weaponize personal history to justify a worldview that conveniently benefits the speaker.

But the real spectacle is Mosiria's solution for infidelity: "If a man is cheating, look for ways of controlling that man... Let him bring that girl he's cheating with, sit down and agree. This is the person you're going to cheat with. Instead of cheating, marry her. That's where polygamy qualifies." This isn't just misguided; it's a throwback to an era where women's emotions and agency were afterthoughts, if considered at all. It's not control he's advocating for, but capitulation, dressed up as a "solution." It’s another paper shield for a leaking roof, offering superficial fixes instead of addressing deeper cracks in patriarchal structures. Of course, the grand finale is the threat: "This movie should be banned completely from Kenya. It should be suspended from Netflix, and we are going to sue." Because nothing says "rational critique" like an empty legal threat against a global streaming giant. It's the usual political song and dance, a performative outrage designed to drum up attention and appeal to a particular conservative base, all while conveniently sidestepping the actual issues that plague our society. The irony of a politician complaining about "inaccurate" portrayals is a comedy in itself, given the daily theatrics of our political landscape. Perhaps The Polygamist isn't inaccurate at all; maybe it just hits a little too close to home for those who benefit from keeping certain narratives neatly packaged.
