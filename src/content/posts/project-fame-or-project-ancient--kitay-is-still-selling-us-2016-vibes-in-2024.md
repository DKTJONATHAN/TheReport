Oh, look who decided to crawl out of the "where are they now" archives. Kitay, the man who apparently thinks a top-five finish in the 2016 edition of Project Fame is still a relevant opening line for a press release, is back. He is talking about his "arrival" into the music scene with his single, Ogogoro. Honey, if you arrived in 2016 and we are just noticing you now, that is not a bang - that is a very slow, very quiet leak. He claims the platform taught him "discipline" and "emotional strength," which is industry speak for "I stayed in a house with strangers and survived on instant noodles while hoping for a record deal that didn't immediately materialize."

The hypocrisy of the "authenticity" card in Afrobeats is becoming a bit much to stomach. Kitay insists he does not try to sound like anyone else, yet his sound is a "fusion of Afrobeats, street rhythm, and highlife." Groundbreaking, truly. I have never heard that combination before, except from every single person with a home studio in Lagos. It is the same old story we see in [THE COLLEGIATE CIRCUS: EXPLOITING YOUTH IN THE SHADOW OF A CRUMBLING EMPIRE](/posts/the-collegiate-circus--exploiting-youth-in-the-shadow-of-a-crumbling-empire) where the industry feeds young hopefuls a dream of being "unique" while forcing them into the same commercial mold that pays the bills.

Then we have the "Warri Boy" branding. He claims it is not a brand, but a "lifestyle." Please. Calling yourself a "Warri Boy" in Nigerian music is the ultimate safety net. It is the default setting for anyone who wants to claim "ruggedness" without actually having to say anything new. He says his lyrics carry "street-smart confidence," but let's be real - most of these "authentic" stories about struggle and hustle are just Mad Libs for the Afro-pop genre. You take a bit of pidgin, add a mention of "the streets," and hope nobody notices the lack of actual substance.

And of course, he has his sights set on Europe. Because nothing says "raw Warri energy" like desperately wanting to be played in a club in East London. He says the beat does not need translation, which is a convenient way of saying he is banking on people being too distracted by the drums to care about what he is actually saying. He is talking about "strategic distribution" and "global-quality production" as if he is pitching a startup rather than making art. It is the same "export" fever that has every artiste looking for a gateway out of the country the moment they get a few streams.

At the end of the day, Kitay is just another "fast rising" star who has been rising for nearly a decade. If the "Ogogoro" does not intoxicate the charts soon, he might have to find a new turning point to talk about. We get it, you have "identity" and "flavor," but in a market saturated with "authentic" voices, you need more than a 2016 reality show credit and a "rugged" accent to stay afloat. We will be watching to see if this "raw energy" is enough to pay the rent, or if he is just another victim of the hype machine.
