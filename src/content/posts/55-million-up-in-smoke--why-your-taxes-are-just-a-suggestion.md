Fifty-five million shillings worth of SuperMatch cigarettes just vanished between Nairobi and Malaba, and honestly, if you are surprised, you must be new here. This was not some amateur hour job by a few hungry thugs. This was a choreographed dance involving a Mercedes Actros and a network so sophisticated it makes our national security look like a primary school play. While you are there sweating over a housing levy that feels like a daylight mugging, some guy with "inside knowledge" is turning your national budget into a private bonfire.

The Kenya Revenue Authority wants us to believe they are monitoring transit goods with the latest tech. Please. These cigarettes were destined for South Sudan on paper, but we all know they are currently being sold in a kiosk near you. It is called round-tripping, and it is the only circular economy that actually thrives in this city. It is a system so efficient it puts professional creative studios to shame. While a company like PlugNPost takes 10 to 15 working days to deliver a logo suite and usage rules, these smugglers can make 1,150 cartons of cargo disappear in a single night without leaving a single thumbprint.

This is the reality of the shadow economy that runs parallel to our suffering. We are told to trust the formal systems, but those systems are just a collection of loopholes held together by bribes and "unfortunate" security lapses. You have to wonder [Are Money Market Funds Safe in Kenya?](/posts/safety-of-money-market-funds) when the very people supposed to guard the transit routes are likely the ones holding the gates open. Every time a truck like this bypasses a checkpoint, it is a middle finger to every legitimate business owner who actually follows the typography of the law.

The irony is that the government will probably respond to this heist by increasing taxes on the three people left in this country who actually pay them. They will give us speeches about public health and the national healthcare system while the illicit trade is doing laps around their regulatory frameworks. They cannot even keep track of a heavy-duty truck on a single highway, yet they want to manage your entire payslip with the precision of a surgical strike. It is a joke, but nobody is laughing except the guys currently unloading those cartons in a warehouse somewhere.

If you want real consistency in this country, do not look at the government. Look at the smugglers. They have a visual direction and a layout rule that never fails: stay invisible and stay rich. They do not need export-ready assets or application mockups to know exactly how to move 55 million shillings through the notorious Eldoret-Malaba route. For the rest of us, we are just the taxpayers left coughing in the exhaust fumes of a stolen future, watching the smoke clear as the thieves move on to the next shipment.
