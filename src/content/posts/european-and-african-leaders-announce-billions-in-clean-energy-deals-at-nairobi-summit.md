NEW ENERGY DEALS FOR AFRICA SEALED AT NAIROBI SUMMIT - SO THEY SAY

Those European bigwigs and their African counterparts flapping their gums in Nairobi about "clean energy" and "infrastructure investments." Forty companies, they claim, are splashing out a cool 27 billion euros across about 30 projects. The promise? A sweet 100 billion euros in revenue and jobs for over 600,000 people. Sounds like a fairytale spun by a slick PR department, doesn't it?

The grand plan is to "deepen industrial ties" and speed up Africa's "transition to low-carbon power." Energy is gobbling up the biggest chunk, a whopping 14 billion euros. Agriculture, human capital, finance, AI, industrialization, and the "blue economy" are also apparently on the menu. It’s all very ambitious, and frankly, the details often get lost in the corporate jargon. Remember when we were talking about [the oil noose tightening](/posts/the-oil-noose-tightens-around-a-delusional-global-economy)? This feels like another attempt to distract from the real issues.

Kenya and France, bless their colonial hearts, co-hosted this shindig. They’re calling it a "partnership of equals," which is rich, given history. French utilities are promising hydropower, and TotalEnergies, the same company that’s been busy with oil and gas, is now suddenly all about renewables and clean cooking. Talk about a convenient pivot. Then there's Meridiam doubling down on wind power in Kenya, and Global Telecom promising a solar farm in Zambia. Meanwhile, CMA CGM is upgrading Mombasa's port, likely so their giant ships can save a few drops of fuel while choking the skies.

Even the French development bank, Bpifrance, is teaming up with Morocco’s OCP Group for a seed fund for agro-industrial ventures. It’s all about "sustainable" and "renewable-powered" production lines. Kenyan President Ruto is waxing lyrical about the energy transition being an "industrial transition." Let's hope it doesn't end up like that [Kitengela family crying foul](/posts/kitengela-family-cries-foul--produce-him-in-court--cctv-shows-businessman-abducted) after being promised safety and security.

The summit document is calling for more commitments, more investments in hydrogen, hydropower, geothermal, waste-to-energy, and even nuclear power. The goal is to bring reliable electricity to the 600 million Africans who currently live without it. But let's be real, these are often promises whispered in boardrooms and on gala dinners. The true beneficiaries are rarely the common person on the ground.

So, we have a lot of big numbers and even bigger pronouncements. Whether these deals translate into tangible progress for the average African or just another round of profits for foreign corporations remains to be seen. Until then, it's just more ink on paper, another summit, another set of headlines.
