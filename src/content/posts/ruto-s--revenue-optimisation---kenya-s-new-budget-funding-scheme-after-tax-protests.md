So, two years after the streets ran red with protests over that ridiculous Finance Bill, President Ruto's administration is at it again, just quieter this time. They're "optimising revenue," which is fancy talk for finding new holes to plug the budget deficit without actually admitting they're taxing us more. It's the Kenyan way, isn't it? Sneaky.

Remember all that drama with the cookies? "Accept cookies for free access or pay up" - sounds familiar, doesn't it? This "revenue optimisation" is just another flavour of the same old tune. They dangle a carrot of "trusted, independent news" while the real deal is behind a paywall, or rather, behind a series of hidden fees and charges. It's like that time Substack got hacked; the illusion of digital safety is just another tax on existence, isn't it?

Instead of another blanket tax increase that would send everyone back to the streets, the Treasury is now playing a subtler game. "Revenue optimisation," they call it. It’s likely code for finding every nook and cranny where they can squeeze a few extra shillings out of us, or perhaps making those who *do* accept the cookies pay indirectly through more targeted advertising and data collection.

This isn't about improving your experience or supporting journalism; it's about filling the government's pockets. They know a direct tax hike will cause chaos, so they're opting for the slow bleed. Expect more fees, more levies, and more "partners" wanting your personal data for "audience measurement" and "advertising." It's the same old song, just with a new, polished chorus.

Don't be fooled by the "no advertising cookies" option for subscribers. Even if you pay, they're still collecting data. It's a lose-lose situation for the average Kenyan. You either give up your privacy for "free" access, or you pay for the privilege of *not* having your data mined quite so aggressively. Either way, your wallet and your personal information are on the chopping block.

They're masters of spin, these politicians. "Revenue optimisation" sounds so much better than "we failed to collect enough taxes, so we're going to find other ways to get your money." It’s a classic Nairobi manoeuvre: pretend to listen, then find a loophole. Just like how some courts say MP recall can't proceed until Parliament enacts an enabling law - a convenient delay, perhaps?
