TEA SECTOR ABUSERS SACKED AS COURTS BACK THEMSELVES

The Kenyan tea sector is drowning in its own filth, with managers caught with their hands in the cookie jar now facing the boot. Courts, bless their bureaucratic hearts, are finally backing sackings over sexual misconduct. It's a "decisive shift," they say. What it really means is that for a while, the big boys might think twice before treating junior staff like their personal playthings. Don't get too excited; this is Kenya.

It’s a predictable pattern: the powerful big shots who thought they were untouchable are losing their claims for millions in compensation. Multinational tea companies, the ones with actual lawyers on retainer, are having their "disciplinary processes" validated. Lipton, Unilever, James Finlay - names that sound fancy but are apparently just breeding grounds for entitled old men. After that BBC exposé, suddenly everyone's auditing and filing complaints. It's all about the fallout, darling, and who gets to clean up the mess.

At the heart of these cases are the usual suspects: senior managers abusing their power, mistaking their job titles for a license to grope and proposition. They're offering promotions for favours, the kind of transactional garbage that makes you sick. But the courts are saying "enough," backing dismissals when companies can actually prove something vaguely credible happened. Take that assistant field manager at Lipton Teas, demanding Sh50 million for being canned over "inappropriate relationships." The court, in its infinite wisdom, said no, there were witness statements, a "pattern of behaviour." Apparently, intent doesn't even matter.

The courts are even giving a nod to confidential complaints, because, you know, victims shouldn't have to face their tormentors in public. As if that's the biggest problem here. This echoes a case involving a Lipton plant manager who wanted Sh63 million after allegations of seeking favours. Twenty-seven years of service, and for what? To get sacked for being a creep. It’s the same old story playing out with James Finlay too, all spurred by that BBC investigation into the sexual exploitation of women on these estates. It’s a systemic problem, apparently, not just a few bad apples.

These rulings mean employers are getting a free pass to act on "credible complaints" without needing ironclad proof, and victims don't always need to testify. The courts are "lowering evidentiary barriers" and "raising expectations on employer processes." In simpler terms, the companies are winning, the managers are losing, and the women who actually suffered are just… well, they’re still there, working, probably. It's a "legal reckoning," they call it. I call it a slightly less rigged game for the powerful.

The bottom line is that these courts are finally drawing some lines, making sure that authority doesn't mean you can just exploit vulnerable people. They're backing internal investigations, witness statements, and reminding everyone that disciplinary hearings aren't criminal trials. These three managers, who collectively wanted over Sh195 million, got nothing. Zilch. Because, in the end, even in a system rigged for the powerful, sometimes, just sometimes, they get caught. And it’s about time.
