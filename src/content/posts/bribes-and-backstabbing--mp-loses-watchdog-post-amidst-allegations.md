So, Bumula MP Jack Wamboka is suddenly finding himself on the outs, suspended from chairing the Public Investments committee. The reason? Bribery allegations, apparently triggered by a complaint from a fellow MP, Peter Kaluma, and amplified by the National Cohesion and Integration Commission. Wamboka’s crying foul, claiming he was suspended unheard and without evidence. Classic. It’s almost as if due process only applies when it’s convenient.

This whole kerfuffle shines a harsh spotlight on how things really work in this country, doesn't it? We’ve got rules about campaign financing - the Constitution and the Election Campaign Financing Act, supposedly there to keep things clean. But then you hear whispers of governors being asked for Sh9 million, Sh12 million, by senators who are supposed to be holding them accountable. It makes you wonder if these trials are more about who can pay the most, not about actual governance.

And it’s not just the lower houses. Linturi’s claims have already cast a long shadow of doubt over the Senate’s integrity. It’s a merry-go-round of corruption, where power is traded for cash, and the public be damned. This isn't just about Wamboka losing his post; it’s about the systemic rot that seems to be a permanent fixture in our political landscape. It's a cycle as predictable as the sun rising, a little like the global cybercrime syndicates we’ve seen operating with impunity. Frankly, the idea that any of this is surprising is laughable. We’re talking about a system where allegations of bribery can get you suspended, but proving them, or even having a fair hearing, seems to be an optional extra. While the NCIC might be making noise, the real question is, how many more are getting away with it, hidden behind parliamentary privilege and a well-placed bribe? This isn’t just about one MP; it’s a symptom of a much larger disease.
