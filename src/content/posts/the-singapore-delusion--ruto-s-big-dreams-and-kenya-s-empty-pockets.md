President Ruto has started singing the Singapore song again, telling us to aspire to their standards as if discipline and meritocracy can be summoned by a press release. It is a bold move for a leader whose country is currently suffocating under a KSh 12 trillion debt mountain. Singapore rose because they had a zero-tolerance policy for thieves, whereas in Kenya, we give the biggest looters a front-row seat at the high table. We are told our GDP is US$120 billion, but when you look at the average Kenyan’s pocket, the US$2,100 per capita income feels like a cruel joke told by someone who has never had to choose between rent and unga.

The reality of our governance is not lean or mean; it is just hungry. We have 47 counties, a bicameral legislature, and a mountain of "representatives" whose only job is to consume recurrent expenditure like a swarm of locusts. While the President talks about structural reform, the taxman is busy inventing new ways to bleed the working class dry. Even [The CA’s 'Safety' Warning Is Just Another Tax Trap for the Broke](/posts/the-ca-s--safety--warning-is-just-another-tax-trap-for-the-broke) shows that the state’s only real "innovation" is finding clever ways to pick-pocket citizens who are already reeling from the high cost of living. 

Corruption is not just an obstacle here; it is the national sport. The source material suggests strengthening the EACC and chasing stolen wealth in Swiss accounts, but let us be real. We have a "Silicon Savannah" that is mostly a hub for digital betting and a mining sector where "transparency" is a foreign word. Mentioning the gold in Ikolomani or the oil in Turkana just reminds us of more resources that will likely be siphoned off long before they ever fix a single pothole in a village school. We have the Ministry of Mining and the Ministry of Energy led by career politicians, yet Tullow Oil’s activities remain as clear as mud.

Agriculture could be our backbone, but it is currently suffering from a broken spine. We have the potential to turn semi-arid counties like Mandera and Turkana into green zones using Israeli-style irrigation, yet we prefer to stay trapped in a maize monoculture that only benefits the subsidy cartels. The idea of North Rift farmers switching to Hass avocados sounds lovely in a policy paper, but in the real world, the roads are impassable and the middle-men are waiting to take eighty percent of the profit. We are a nation that imports food while our own fertile land sits idle or is subdivided into "plots for sale" by greedy developers.

If we want to be Singapore, we need to stop treating the national treasury like a personal ATM. Ambition without integrity is just a hallucination. You cannot build a global financial hub on a foundation of impunity and tribal appointments. Until public office is actually regarded as a trust rather than a "turn to eat," these comparisons to Asian Tigers are just bedtime stories to keep the donors happy. Kenya has the talent and the tech, but as long as we tolerate a bloated government and a culture of "kitu kidogo," we will stay exactly where we are: broke, cynical, and dreaming of a Singapore we are too greedy to build.
