So, the men in fancy robes are finally noticing what the rest of us in this dusty city have known for ages. Apparently, getting married legally in Kenya is now as easy as getting a passport on a Friday afternoon - impossible. The bishops are whining about a shortage of marriage certificates and a civil registration system that moves slower than a snail on its day off. Good. Maybe now they'll actually do something, or at least make enough noise to be heard over the usual political circus.

And speaking of the circus, these same bishops are tut-tutting at our dear leaders for their non-stop insults. "You are causing scandal to children and young people," they say. As if the kids aren't already desensitized by the constant drama and the fact that their parents can't even get a simple document without jumping through a dozen hoops. It's the same old song and dance, with politicians spewing venom and the clergy wringing their hands.

But the real kicker is their shock at the continued disregard for human life. They mention Shakahola and the recent grim discoveries in Kericho, lamenting the lack of social and state controls. How can such things happen without anyone noticing? Honestly, Bishop, have you seen the state of things? When the victims are poor and "insignificant," as they so delicately put it, who do you think is going to shed a tear? It's a well-known fact that in this country, some lives are more valuable than others, and the value seems to be directly proportional to your bank account. It's not surprising that this contempt for life fuels human trafficking, or that investigations into these atrocities [mysteriously fizzle out](/posts/riggy-g-s-new-gospel--same-wolves--different-sheepskin).

They also throw in a jab about "controlled criminal elements" running riot. You know, the goons who show up to disrupt meetings and, well, do worse. The bishops correctly point out that this thuggery only thrives when powerful people are pulling the strings. But again, this isn't news. It's the bedrock of Kenyan politics. We've seen it all before, and we’ll see it again. It’s just a shame the bishops have to spell it out for us every few years, as if we’ve all suffered a collective amnesia.
