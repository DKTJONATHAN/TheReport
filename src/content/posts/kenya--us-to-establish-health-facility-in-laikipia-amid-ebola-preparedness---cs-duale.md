Kenya, US Build Another Health Facility in Laikipia While Dubious Ebola Preparedness Looms - CS Duale's Grand Plans

Another day, another announcement from Afya House. This time, Health CS Aden Duale is parading a new health facility in Laikipia, courtesy of our friends in the US, all in the name of "Ebola preparedness." Because clearly, what we need is more bricks and mortar when our existing systems are already groaning under the weight of corruption and mismanagement. They talk about disease surveillance, diagnostic capacity, and emergency preparedness like it's a fresh idea, not the same old song and dance they trot out every time a foreign disease makes headlines.

The DRC's Ebola woes are, of course, the convenient trigger for this latest spending spree. Duale himself admits that "infectious diseases do not respect national borders," a profound observation that somehow escapes him when it comes to actual resource allocation that benefits the common mwananchi. He’s preaching a "comprehensive preparedness approach" - a fancy way of saying they'll probably just do a bit of screening at the airport and then hope for the best, while these new facilities gather dust.

Never mind that we've seen this movie before. Remember the COVID-19 response? They conveniently forget to mention how much of that "preparedness" ended up lining pockets and not actually reaching the frontline health workers. Now we're supposed to believe that a new facility, potentially another avenue for kickbacks, will magically fix everything? It's the same old script, just a different pathogen. We're told they've got isolation wards at KNH and MTRH, and now more in "high-risk border counties." High-risk for what, exactly? More opportunities for tenderpreneurs?

The CS chirps about strengthening our "health security architecture." This sounds suspiciously like the same promises made with the last ICT budget, which turned out to be [another digital mirage for a hungry nation](/posts/the-2026-ict-budget--another-digital-mirage-for-a-hungry-nation). We're meant to trust that investing in "laboratory systems, healthcare infrastructure, disease surveillance, emergency response mechanisms, supply chains and workforce readiness" will actually translate into tangible improvements. Given our track record, it's more likely to translate into more white elephant projects and inflated invoices.

Duale even has the audacity to cite the UNON facility at Nairobi Hospital as a "lasting example." Lasting example of what? How a temporary crisis can be milked for long-term international aid? This whole Ebola preparedness spiel feels less about genuine public health and more about securing more foreign funding and looking busy. Until they can prove these investments actually make a difference on the ground, for the nurses and doctors actually dealing with patients, it’s just another political stunt.
