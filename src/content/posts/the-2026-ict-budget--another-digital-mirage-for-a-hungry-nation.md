If you have lived in Nairobi long enough, you know that when a government official starts talking about a roadmap, it usually means they are looking for a new route to the bank. Eng. John Tanui was at it again on February 17, presenting the 2026 Budget Policy Statement to the parliamentary committee. They are promising a robust and secure digital economy, but for most of us, the only thing robust about this plan is the size of the budget they are trying to justify.

The ministry’s objectives are a collection of the usual buzzwords: ICT infrastructure, data privacy, and a secure cyberspace. It sounds great in a boardroom in Upper Hill, but on the streets, we are still dealing with overpriced bundles and government websites that go down whenever you actually need them. They claim to be aligning with the Kenya National Digital Master Plan, but these plans always seem to serve the planners more than the public.

Eng. Tanui brought the whole squad with him, including the heads of the ICT Authority and Konza Technopolis. Konza has been the city of the future for over a decade now, yet it remains a collection of shiny buildings in the middle of nowhere while our actual cities crumble. They are asking for expenditure ceilings that are already sky-high, yet they still have a list of activities that fall outside those limits. It is a classic move: ask for a lot, then ask for even more.

We are being told to trust this digital transformation while the Auditor General is busy showing us how the system actually works. It is hard to get excited about ICT-driven growth when you remember [Ruto's And Friends Ksh2.1 Trillion Theft: Auditor General Exposes Biggest Government Heist Since Independence](/posts/ruto-and-friends-ksh-2-trillion-theft-from-2022-to-2025) which happened right under our noses. If trillions can disappear from the main treasury, why should we believe these digital billions will be any safer?

The 2026 BPS is now under review by the National Assembly, but we already know how this movie ends. The committees will sit, the tea will be served, and the budget will be passed with very little change. They will talk about building a secure and inclusive digital economy while the average Kenyan is just trying to figure out how to pay for their next meal. In this country, the roadmap always looks beautiful on a PowerPoint, but the road itself is always full of potholes.
