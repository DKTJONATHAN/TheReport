Nairobi is a city that eats its heroes for breakfast and spits out influencers by lunchtime. Bradley Marongo, the so-called Gen Z Goliath who towered over the protests like a beacon of hope, has finally succumbed to the oldest trap in the 254. A photo is doing the rounds of him heading to his apartment with a slay queen in tow, proving that his transition from a Kawangware hustler to a corporate puppet is complete. We thought he was different, but he is just another man with a paycheck and a thirst for high-maintenance company.

It was not long ago that this man was a "kamagera" in Kawangware, fighting for every shilling in the dusty streets. Now, thanks to some height and a few viral videos, he has been packaged and sold to us as a symbol of aspiration. But aspiration for what, exactly? If the goal of the revolution was to get enough money to afford a woman who likely spends more on her skin than he used to earn in a month, then we have truly failed. This is the [Manners for the Elite, Bills for the Rest of Us](/posts/manners-for-the-elite--bills-for-the-rest-of-us) reality we live in, where the poor cheer for the very people who have forgotten them for a taste of the high life.

The corporate deals will dry up eventually. They always do. Right now, Bradley is the flavor of the month, the giant who can sell anything from soda to insurance. But Nairobi has a short memory. Once the novelty of his height wears off and the brands find a newer, shinier toy, he will be left with the bills for that fancy apartment and the demands of a woman who is only there for the clout. It is a predictable script that we have seen play out a thousand times in this city of illusions.

While we are busy celebrating his "growth," we ignore the rot underneath. Life is short and fame is shorter. You can go from celebrating a milestone like the late Johana Ng-eno to being a memory in a heartbeat. Bradley is wasting his peak years on fleeting thrills. Today he is being photographed with a beautiful woman; tomorrow he will be another cautionary tale about how fast you can fall when you let the bright lights blind you to the reality of the streets you left behind.
