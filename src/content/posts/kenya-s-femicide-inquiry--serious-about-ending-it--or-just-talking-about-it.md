Another year, another task force, another high-sounding report on something we already know is a crisis. President Ruto's grand gesture to investigate femicide and GBV has produced its recommendations, now being 'debated' in parliament. The big news? They might legally recognise femicide as a standalone crime. As if a new label on the death certificate will suddenly make women safer on these streets. We've seen this movie before, multiple times.

And then there's UN Women, ever busy with their "male engagement strategies" to shift perceptions. It sounds nice on paper, doesn't it? Changing hearts and minds while the bodies keep piling up. Their representative admits "challenges remain." That's a diplomatic way of saying, "We're throwing ideas at a wall, and nothing's sticking." Meanwhile, women are still marching, still angry, still fearing for their lives, because talk is cheap.

The country, they say, waits to see if Ruto will actually implement these recommendations. "Waits." Like we wait for anything meaningful to come out of these corridors of power. It's a national pastime, waiting for promises to materialise, much like [Health CS Aden Duale Plays Hide-and-Seek While SHA Sh11 Billion Vanishes](/posts/health-cs-aden-duale-plays-hide-and-seek-while-sha-sh11-billion-vanishes). The pattern is disturbingly consistent: a crisis erupts, a committee is formed, a report is published, and then… crickets.

So, is Kenya serious about ending femicide? Frankly, no. Not if "serious" means swift, decisive action backed by genuine political will and systemic change. What we're witnessing is a performance, a bureaucratic dance designed to appease international bodies and quiet public outrage, at least for a little while. The women who march know better. They know the fight isn't in parliament; it's on the ground, and it's far from over.
