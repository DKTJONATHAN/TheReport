The perennial question in British politics: how much trouble is Keir Starmer really in? This week's installment from BBC's Newscast, featuring Adam Fleming and Chris Gray, attempts to answer just that, by wading through the ever-present Peter Mandelson saga. They've even wheeled out the usual suspects: Lara Spirit, deputy political editor of The Sunday Times, and Helen MacNamara, a former deputy cabinet secretary - figures who are paid to offer an air of authority on the political theatre.

The core of the discussion, as ever, revolves around the persistent specter of Peter Mandelson and his potential ramifications for Starmer. This isn't about policy or vision; it's about the messy, backroom deals and past associations that can hobble a leader. Spirit and MacNamara, no doubt armed with the latest whispers from Westminster, will likely offer a measured, yet ultimately cynical, assessment of Starmer's precarious position. Expect talk of "damage," "perception," and the relentless grinding of internal party politics.

The accompanying promotional material for BBC Sounds and their online community is a predictable distraction. It’s a reminder that even when dissecting the rot, the BBC must still adhere to its broadcasting obligations, ensuring you know how to listen to more of their analysis, whether you want it or not. The inclusion of Ruai's Human Cargo as a "past story" feels like a desperate attempt to inject a modicum of grim reality, a stark contrast to the rarefied air of Westminster gossip. But then again, what else is new? Politics is, and always has been, a game of shadows and self preservation, and Keir Starmer is merely the latest player in a long, sordid drama. The danger is real, but the spectacle is all that truly matters to those who watch from the sidelines.
