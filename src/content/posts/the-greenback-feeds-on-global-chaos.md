Wall Street's sudden pivot from record underweight positions to a neutral stance reveals the mercenary nature of global capital. Speculators are no longer betting against the US currency because they realize the rest of the world is in far worse shape. For economies like Japan and the Eurozone, which are tethered to energy imports, the surge in crude prices past the 100-dollar mark is a slow-motion execution. The yen and the euro are being sacrificed at the altar of energy security, while the dollar harvests the "safe-haven" premium.

The Federal Reserve’s refusal to cut interest rates adds another layer of misery to this geopolitical theater. Persistent inflation-driven by the very energy spikes the war has triggered-means that borrowing costs will remain high for the foreseeable future. This creates a feedback loop of pain for developing nations and debt-burdened allies alike. The dream of a soft landing or a pivot toward cheaper money has evaporated, replaced by the cold math of a central bank that prefers a suffocatingly strong currency over global stability.

We are witnessing the reinforcement of dollar hegemony through chaos. The "drip, drip, drip" of negative headlines is a flood of instability that reinforces the status quo. There is no incentive for price stability when the world’s reserve currency derives its strength from the failure of every other alternative. As long as the Middle East remains a powder keg, the dollar will continue to function as the world’s most expensive insurance policy, sold by the very system that benefits most from the risk.
