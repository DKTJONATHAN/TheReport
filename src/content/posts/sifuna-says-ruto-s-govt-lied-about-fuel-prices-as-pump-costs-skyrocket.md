So, Senator Sifuna is having a go at the President, claiming the government has been feeding Kenyans a load of hogwash about fuel prices. Apparently, all those reassurances that things were "stabilized" were just hot air. Now, with the latest EPRA announcement, petrol is up a ridiculous Sh28.69 a litre, and diesel is practically bleeding us dry at Sh40.30 more. Kerosene stayed put, but who's buying kerosene these days anyway?

Sifuna’s not mincing words, calling it downright illegal to feed the public false information. He’s dragging President Ruto’s March 30 statement through the mud, where he apparently bragged about cushioning us from global oil shocks. Weeks later, what do we get? A kick in the teeth at the pump. It’s the same old story, isn't it? Promises made, promises broken, while we’re left to foot the bill.

EPRA, bless their bureaucratic hearts, blames it all on "rising global oil prices" and "exchange rate pressures." They’re quick to remind us how reliant we are on imported fuel, making us a global price ping-pong ball. They even reduced VAT and threw some billions from the Petroleum Development Levy at the problem, but lo and behold, it wasn't enough to stop the inevitable surge. It seems the "relief measures" were just a band-aid on a gaping wound.

And Sifuna is right - this isn’t just about the pump. This pain will ripple through the economy like a bad hangover. Transport costs will climb, and guess who pays for that? We do. When basic commodities become luxury items, you know something’s fundamentally broken. It’s another reminder of how we’re caught in the crossfire, perhaps even linked to claims of substandard fuel entering the market previously, as hinted at in past reports.

The whole situation is a mess, and it's piling on the pressure for clearer communication and actual solutions, not just platitudes. With petrol now a cool Sh206.70 a litre and diesel at Sh206.84 in Nairobi, it's hard not to feel like we’re being taken for a ride. And let's not forget the reports of lives lost during protests over this very issue. It’s a grim reminder of how much this affects ordinary Kenyans.
