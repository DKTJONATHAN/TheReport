The US government's penchant for spending vast sums on the latest gadgets for its security apparatus is a well worn narrative, and this latest venture by DARPA is no exception. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) helpfully points out that billions are already funneled into X-ray imaging for security purposes. Now, the Defense Advanced Research Project Agency (DARPA) is looking to push the boundaries further, awarding RTX BBN Technologies a contract to develop X-ray imaging algorithms that can "see" through objects from a kilometer away, or just over half a mile.

This isn't about cleaner, higher quality images. Oh no. BBN's approach, as reported by RTX, is to build algorithms that can make sense of a mountain of low quality, fragmented data. The justification? This, apparently, is what commanders will actually encounter on the battlefield. It's a tacit admission that the current methods are inadequate, and instead of fixing the fundamental issues of data acquisition in chaotic environments, they're opting to build a more sophisticated digital filter.

BBN Technologies, a subsidiary of the aerospace and defense giant RTX, has a long history of DARPA backed projects, particularly in networking. This new initiative, however, falls under DARPA's X-ray Extreme-range Non-imaging Analysis (XENA) program. The goal is to create "algorithmic toolsets" to ascertain the interior composition of distant objects, specifically to overcome the persistent problem of motion blur degrading intelligence data.

Historically, X-rays have been employed by law enforcement and military personnel to detect concealed items. However, their utility has been hampered by distance, with motion blur and noise corrupting transmissions as the range increases. The established challenge has always been acquiring sufficient high quality data to train these systems for practical use.

BBN's proposed solution is to invert this logic. Instead of focusing on pristine data, they're embracing the low quality, more easily obtainable information, to build an algorithm that can perform in less than ideal conditions, even at the ambitious range of 0.6 miles. Joshua Fasching, BBN's principal investigator, claims these algorithms will transform "a small number of grainy snapshots into enough detail for decision makers to act."

The company assures us that this will provide service members with "access to actionable information about concealed threats, potential weapons, or structural vulnerabilities from ranges previously beyond reach." One can only imagine the endless applications for such technology: spotting the next disgruntled individual with a hidden weapon, identifying structural weaknesses in enemy fortifications, or perhaps even, in a more dystopian turn, monitoring civilian populations from afar. It's another step in the perpetual arms race, a testament to the fact that when it comes to military spending, "enough" is a concept that simply doesn't exist. The question remains: will this expensive new toy actually make the world safer, or just provide more sophisticated ways to engage in conflict? History suggests the latter.
