So, the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission, in their infinite wisdom, finally stumbled upon a Nyeri campaigner who told them something we already knew: the system is rigged. This poor soul, sampled for some fancy survey, recounted a case of a 13-year-old girl violated by her father, a retired cop, and some other connected goon. When she tried to speak up, she was told to shut it. Surprise, surprise, only the father got nabbed. The rest, conveniently linked to someone "senior," were shielded. This is how corruption works, folks. It muffles the cries for justice, burying them in reports no one reads, except perhaps when they need to cook up some more "surveys."

The numbers are just a joke, aren't they? The 2026 Economic Survey chirps about defilement cases dipping slightly. "Slightly" in Kenya means we're still talking about thousands of children scarred for life. In 2021, over 6,000 men were accused of defiling girls. By 2025, it was a mere 5,000-plus. And we can't forget the 217 women accused of defiling boys back then, a number that fell to 105. These aren't just figures; they're screaming children whose stories get lost in the noise, much like how we've seen power dynamics often play out in broader political maneuvering, as hinted at in discussions around [Ruto's 2027 Problem: Why Kenya's President Faces Historic Electoral Backlash](/posts/ruto-2027-problem-electoral-backlash).

Globally, it's the same old song. A London firm found that misogyny coverage is at a decade low. Men's voices dominate, with 1.5 men quoted for every woman in these pathetic discussions. And then there's the Jeffrey Epstein circus. His entire sordid affair, dissected by researchers, shows a media more interested in power and money than the actual suffering of victims. His alleged pedophile network even had whispers of Kenyan connections, mentioning places like Malindi and hinting at ties with a former president. Makes you wonder how many more untouchables are out there, their crimes conveniently swept under the rug.

The researchers are calling for "survivor-centric reporting" and "solutions-based angles." Bless their naive hearts. They think a few feel-good paragraphs will dismantle a system built on impunity. They recommend signposting help for victims. That's rich. In a country where reporting a crime can get you threatened, where justice is a commodity for the rich and connected, where the powerful are always shielded, what good is a helpline? It's just another bureaucratic hoop to jump through, another way to tell victims, "We heard you, now go away."

The media, busy chasing their own tails and the next big scandal, barely touches these stories unless a VIP is involved. The Nyeri campaigner's story, buried in a report, is a stark reminder that for most survivors, justice isn't a destination; it's a mirage. They are left with nothing but scars and the bitter knowledge that their pain was deemed less newsworthy than the next political spat or celebrity gossip. This isn't just about sexual violence; it's about the rot that seeps from the top, poisoning everything it touches.
