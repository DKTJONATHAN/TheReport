So, here we are again. The same old song and dance every election season in Nairobi. Everyone’s whispering about "wait and see," which is just a fancy way of saying "hold onto your wallets, folks." Local and foreign investors? They’re already packing their bags, metaphorically speaking, waiting to see who’s going to win and if they’ll even bother with sanity. Liquidity will dry up faster than a political promise after the polls close.

And let's not forget the "ripple effect." That's the buzzword for when politicians start throwing tantrums and disrupting everything. Businesses will be caught in the crossfire, operations halted, and the economy will take a nosedive. It’s like watching a bunch of toddlers fight over a toy - except this toy is our livelihood. These "business moguls" are pleading with the government, as if it’s a new concept, to create a "conducive environment." As if they haven't seen this movie before.

Even at a fancy event celebrating women in business, the mood is a mixed bag. Some are chirping about tolerance and growth, bless their optimistic hearts. But the realists, like Perminus Kariuki from Nyota Njema Properties, are seeing the writing on the wall. He’s worried about the political "acrimony" threatening the peace businesses have enjoyed. Let's be clear: political fights mean businesses shut down, people lose jobs, and the economy takes a beating. It's not rocket science, it’s just Kenyan politics. And let's not even start on the government’s habit of not paying its bills on time - that’s a whole other disaster waiting to happen.

Then there's Rose Ntong’ondu of Make-up by Rose, who’s already feeling the pinch. The cost of doing business is "skyrocketing," apparently. How surprising. For small businesses, especially those run by women, it’s apparently "increasingly difficult to sustain operations." Quelle surprise. Lucy Njambi from Great Mwanzo Properties wants more roads, because clearly, that's the magic bullet for our economic woes. Jane Baiyu from Amara Realty is begging politicians to "tone down" and remember there's a country after the elections. Good luck with that. It's like asking a hyena to stop sniffing around a carcass.

Of course, you get the odd optimistic soul. Rosemary Njuguna of Ellen Tea is seeing "upward trajectory" in demand. And Rachael Wainaina is all about supporting women in creative arts. Apparently, there are "massive opportunities" in the digital workspace. Sure, if you're willing to work for peanuts and deal with endless online scams. It’s all very hopeful, isn't it? Just like hoping that [Open Borders, Closed Eyes: The Mandera Gamble](/posts/open-borders--closed-eyes--the-mandera-gamble) wasn't a sign of things to come.

But let's not get too carried away with the sunshine and rainbows. The reality is, elections in Kenya are usually a recipe for economic uncertainty and a scramble for survival. The "wait-and-see" approach isn't just a trend; it's a survival tactic. And the government's role in creating a "conducive environment" seems to be more about rhetoric than action. So, buckle up, Nairobi. It’s going to be a bumpy ride.
