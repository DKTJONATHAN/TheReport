Here we go again. Kisumu All Starlets, bless their struggling hearts, have managed to gift another three points to their opponents without even bothering to show up. This time, it's the defending champions, Kenya Police Bullets, who are laughing all the way to the bank - or rather, to the top of the league table. A third walkover? Honestly, it’s not even surprising anymore. It just confirms what we already know: this league is run on wishful thinking and empty promises for some.

This latest no-show at Kenya Police Sacco on Tuesday, March 24, 2026, means Police Bullets get their points and goals handed to them, pushing them to a comfortable 40 points. While they celebrate, Kisumu is sinking deeper into the mire at the bottom, a sure sign of what happens when the money runs dry. It’s a classic Nairobi narrative, really: the well-connected and funded shine, while the rest are left to scramble in the dust. You think this is about football? It’s about who has the shillings, pure and simple.

Meanwhile, the likes of Ulinzi Starlets, Vihiga Queens, and Zetech Sparks are quietly consolidating their positions, probably counting their own coins and making sure their players get fed. The race for continental spots and podium finishes is getting tighter, but down at the bottom, it’s a different kind of race - a race to see who can collapse the fastest due to sheer lack of funds. It’s a grim picture, and Kisumu is leading the pack in this particular, unenviable competition.

Head coach Juma Said’s admission that it’s all about "financial constraints" is just a polite way of saying they are broke. Can't afford to travel, can't pay allowances, can't even buy a decent meal. This isn't a football club; it's a charity case that's run its course. It's the same old story we see across many grassroots women's football structures. We've seen similar plays before, like the drama surrounding "The Milk Closing Festival," where the underlying issues get glossed over with noise.

So, Kisumu All Starlets are now staring at automatic relegation, a fitting end for a team that couldn't even manage to show up for their own matches. The Football Kenya Federation rules are clear, and repeated no-shows are a sure ticket to the lower division. They’ll be heading to the FKF Women's National Super League, which, let’s be honest, probably isn’t much better. The tough task of rebuilding both structurally and financially awaits, and knowing how things work around here, it’s a mountain they're unlikely to climb.

The widening gap in the FKF Women's Premier League is becoming painfully obvious. At the top, it's a gripping title race. At the bottom? It's a financial apocalypse. This is the reality of Kenyan football for many: a stark contrast between the haves and the have-nots, and Kisumu All Starlets are firmly on the wrong side of that divide.
