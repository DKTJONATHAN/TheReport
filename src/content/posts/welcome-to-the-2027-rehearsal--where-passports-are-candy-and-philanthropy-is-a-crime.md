Welcome to Nairobi, the city where "Karibu Kenya" now comes with a side of midnight interrogation and a one-way ticket back to wherever you came from. Last week, the state decided to flex its muscles on Brian Kagoro, a man who has spent three decades visiting this country. They held him for ten hours, grabbed his gadgets, and threw him out by dawn like a common criminal. Why? Because in the paranoid halls of the Ministry of Interior, a man from a philanthropic foundation is apparently more dangerous than the actual thugs roaming our streets.

The Open Society Foundations, with their billions in global funding, are now being painted as the big bad wolves trying to set the country on fire. The government claims Kagoro was here to fund another round of Gen Z protests, because apparently, Kenyans are too stupid to realize they are broke and angry without someone from Johannesburg paying them to say so. It is easier for the National Intelligence Service to invent a foreign conspiracy than to admit that the "Hustler" dream has become a high-speed nightmare for the people living it.

This is all part of a predictable [Strategic Evolution: How Multi-Tier Scoring is Redefining Gaelic Football Analytics](/posts/strategic-evolution--how-multi-tier-scoring-is-redefining-gaelic-football-analytics) in the state's playbook as we head toward the 2027 General Elections. We have seen this movie before. From the 1990 NGO Coordination Act to the Jubilee administration trying to snatch 85 percent of foreign funding in 2014, the goal is always the same: choke the life out of civil society so no one can call out the rot when it starts. They threatened the Ford Foundation earlier this year, and now they are coming for anyone who dares to fund human rights.

While they were busy harassing Kagoro under a section of the law meant for forged documents, news broke that our immigration department has been handing out Kenyan passports to individuals linked to RSF war crimes in Sudan and money-laundering schemes. It is a special kind of hypocrisy to deport a philanthropist while rolling out the red carpet for people who burn cities for a living. Our government is deeply embarrassed, or at least they should be, but shame is a luxury they clearly cannot afford right now.

We are currently sitting on the UN Human Rights Council, pretending to be a beacon of democracy while we violate every principle of freedom of association and movement. The Kenya Kwanza manifesto is looking more like a work of fiction with every passing day. As we slide toward the political crises seen in our neighbors, the question is not how low we can go, but how many people will be left to tell the story when the 2027 dust settles. Heads will not roll; they will just find new ways to hide the truth.
