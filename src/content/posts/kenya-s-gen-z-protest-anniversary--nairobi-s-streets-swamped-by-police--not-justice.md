Two years. Two years since Kenya's Gen Z dared to speak, dared to demand a future beyond the usual political theatre and poverty. And what do we get for an anniversary? A city choked by razor wire, armoured vehicles, and more police than mourners. Families whose children were gunned down trying to make a point are back at Parliament, not for justice, but to be reminded, with every barricade and grim-faced officer, exactly who holds the power in this country. The irony is as thick as the tear gas they didn't quite deploy today.

Caroline Mutisya, whose son Erickson Kyalo Mutisya fell just fifty metres from Parliament, came to remember, but couldn't even speak freely. Jacinta Anyango, a mother mourning her 12-year-old Kennedy Onyango, dares to ask, "Who will vote for the president if he continues killing us like this?" A valid question, perhaps, but one that falls on deaf ears. Two years on, the officers responsible walk free, and the government's only response is to deploy more of them, ensuring the grieving stay quiet. It's a calculated silence, a chilling reminder that some lives just don't matter enough for accountability.

Nairobi city centre was a ghost town today, not because people chose to stay home in solemn remembrance, but because the authorities made sure they couldn't get in. Roads blocked, public transport diverted, businesses shuttered-a perfectly executed lockdown to prevent any "criminality" from disrupting the state's carefully curated narrative of peace. It's a familiar playbook, isn't it? Control the movement, control the message. Make sure no spontaneous outpouring of grief or anger spoils the government's day.

What started as a protest against a Finance Bill in 2024 quickly morphed into something far bigger-a raw, furious cry against the rising cost of living, rampant unemployment, corruption, and the ever-present shadow of police brutality. Organised by young Kenyans who bypassed traditional political leadership, it was hailed as a "new era of youth civic activism." But what has this "new era" really delivered? More than 60 dead in 2024, another 16 in 2025 during subsequent commemorations. For all the talk of activism, the outcome remains the same: [the law enforcers win on the pitch while the rest of us lose on the streets](/posts/the-law-enforcers-win-on-the-pitch-while-the-rest-of-us-lose-on-the-streets).

The government, in its infinite wisdom, declared it would "facilitate peaceful demonstrations" while simultaneously warning it would act "firmly against criminality and violence." A classic double-speak, meaning: protest where we tell you to, say what we approve of, and don't you dare step out of line, or face the consequences. So here we are, two years later, laying flowers on barbed wire, chanting slogans under the watchful eyes of water cannons, and hoping for a justice that feels as distant as the promise of a better Kenya. It's not an anniversary; it's a stark, brutal reminder that nothing has changed, and likely, nothing ever will.
