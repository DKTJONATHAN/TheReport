PLAY READINGS: NAIROBI'S NEW WEEKEND HANGOUT? MORE LIKE A DESPERATION PLAY.
They're calling it a trend. Young Kenyans ditching clubs for dusty set books. What a joke.
This isn't cultural awakening, it's a symptom.
Set Books: Nairobi's New Weekend Hangout? Don't Hold Your Breath.
Young Kenyans are flocking to theatre halls, not for entertainment, but to dissect "Betrayal in the City" and other set books. Apparently, Francis Imbuga's words are more exciting than a night out. Goethe-Institut's lobby is overflowing, they say. Overflowing with what? Boredom? Or maybe just a desperate need for something, *anything*, to pass the time in this city that never sleeps but often just stares blankly at the ceiling.

Forget clubs, forget cinemas, forget video games. Now it's all about cramming into Maendeleo House to hear someone drone on about a play written decades ago. It’s less an awakening of the arts and more a collective sigh of resignation. In a year already flooded with content, deciding what to watch is a task in itself, but apparently, watching someone else read a set book is the pinnacle of intellectual pursuit. Give me a break.

What's really happening is that the usual avenues for youthful escapism are either too expensive or too… well, just not there anymore. Maybe it’s a genuine interest in literature, but let’s be real, it’s probably a scramble for cheap or free activities. This reminds me of those festivals that claim to be a "Celebration" of creativity but are just another cookie-cutter event, designed to look good on paper. [/posts/kulture-festival-2026--south-africa-s--celebration--of-african-creativity---or-just-another-cookie-cutter-event] The youth are just finding the cheapest way to feel something other than the daily grind.

And let’s not forget the "business side" of all this. Someone’s making a buck off these readings, I’m sure of it. Just like how others exploit vulnerability for profit. [/posts/your-pity-is-their-profit--the-sick-business-of-forced-begging] It’s a whole ecosystem of survival, and if reading set books is the current hustle, then so be it. But don’t dress it up as some grand cultural renaissance. It’s just Nairobi, adapting.

So, next time you hear about these "vibrant theatre scenes," remember it’s likely just a bunch of young people looking for a place to go that doesn’t cost an arm and a leg, and where they can at least pretend to be intellectually stimulated. It’s not the future of entertainment; it’s a reflection of limited options and a whole lot of Kenyan pragmatism.
