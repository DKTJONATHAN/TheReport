It seems that even in an era of heightened global tensions, the urge for self promotion and the mundane act of tracking one's exercise routine can override basic common sense. A French navy sailor, likely aboard the Charles de Gaulle or its escort, managed to publicly broadcast their location via the Strava fitness app, inadvertently revealing the near real time whereabouts of France's most powerful naval asset. The sailor’s seven kilometer run, logged in the middle of the Mediterranean Sea, northwest of Cyprus, was cross referenced by Le Monde with satellite imagery taken roughly an hour later, placing the aircraft carrier a mere six kilometers away.

This isn't a novel occurrence; it's merely the latest chapter in what Le Monde has aptly termed "StravaLeaks." Previous reports from the French publication detailed how the same sailor's digital breadcrumbs led them from the coast of France to Copenhagen, and identified at least one other sailor whose Strava activities inadvertently disclosed their ship's position. The pattern extends beyond naval vessels, with Le Monde having previously tracked the security details of the US, French, and Russian presidents, even pinpointing a San Francisco hotel frequented by Joe Biden.

The Pentagon, bless their bureaucratic hearts, attempted to address this glaring vulnerability back in 2018 by banning geolocation features on devices in operational areas. Yet, here we are, years later, and the fundamental problem persists: people are people, and the allure of social media sharing often trumps strategic prudence. France's Ministry of Armed Forces, while acknowledging the breach and stating it "does not comply with current guidelines," offers little reassurance beyond the promise of "appropriate measures." Strava, meanwhile, with its 200 million users, continues to offer privacy settings that few seem to bother employing, a testament to our collective, willful ignorance. The inconvenient truth is that while nations posture and prepare for war, the most significant security threats may well be walking, or running, amongst us, oblivious to the damage their digital vanity can inflict.
