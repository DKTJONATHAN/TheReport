States vs. Media: East African Councils Plead for an End to "Hostility"

The East African Press Councils are begging for a truce, calling for an end to the spat between governments and the media in the region. Apparently, shutting down TV stations and harassing journalists is just too much for some delicate egos.

NTV Uganda, Spark TV, and KFM FM are now off the air, courtesy of a crackdown ordered by the son of Uganda's president. Meanwhile, in Kenya, President Ruto is apparently having a little tiff with the Standard Group. The press councils are wringing their hands, worried about intimidation, profiling, and the terrifying prospect of self-censorship.

This whole song and dance is apparently a violation of human rights and endangers journalists. Who knew? The EAPC, signed off by David Omwoyo of the Media Council of Kenya, reminds us that access to information is a right, not a privilege. Meanwhile, Gen Muhoozi, the Ugandan heir apparent, is busy proving that "rule by iron fist" is still a thing, even if it makes for uncomfortable reading. It’s a familiar tune, really, like watching the [global export of American narcissism and the death of the hero](http://localhost:8000/posts/the-global-export-of-american-narcissism-and-the-death-of-the-hero) - all ego, no substance.

The press councils are urging "restraint" and "dialogue," which sounds lovely in theory. But when you’ve got state power being wielded like a blunt instrument, dialogue often gets trampled. They acknowledge the media’s economic investment and duty to report, even when it’s "uncomfortable, irritating, or inconvenient." It’s a tough gig, being the public watchdog when the public office is a fortress.

The EAPC statement, sounding like a broken record, warns that continued attacks on press freedom erode gains and undermine the rule of law. They call for respect for media freedom and protection from harassment, while simultaneously nudging the media to be "restrained and responsible." It’s a classic catch-22, designed to keep everyone in their place.
