So, the Kenya Police Service has decided their band, with all its "unmatched discipline" and "rich musical heritage," is now available for public hire. Forget chasing criminals; they're chasing your wedding budget, your graduation party funds, or your corporate shindig cash. For the low, low price of KSh 40,000 to KSh 60,000, you too can have the dulcet tones of our finest law enforcement serenading your guests. Because nothing screams "celebration" like the men and women who are supposed to be keeping us safe, playing background music for your cousin’s baby shower.

Apparently, there are tiers. For a measly KSh 40,000, you can get the "entry-level" package for your "regional conferences" and "networking cocktail parties." If your event is "complex" - like a scavenger hunt or building bikes for charity, which sounds suspiciously like more work than fun - you're looking at KSh 60,000. One wonders if the "unmatched discipline" extends to their punctuality, or if they’ll be doing a slow march to the venue an hour late. It’s a bold move, especially when you consider the general economic climate, where even basic necessities feel like a luxury. This whole endeavor just screams another reminder of the global monopoly of recycled mediocrity; why innovate when you can monetize the predictable?

They’ve even split the band into two: the Brass Band for your "parades" and "official ceremonies," which is code for "events where you want people to feel overly important." Then there’s the Jazz Band for "spiritual gatherings" and "entertainment-driven events." I'm picturing a gospel choir trying to get down to some jazz while the band leader, a stern-faced inspector, tries to keep them in line. It's a recipe for either absolute brilliance or a complete train wreck, and given our track record, I’m leaning towards the latter.

And let's not forget where this musical marvel is based: Kiganjo, Nyeri. So, you're not just paying for the music; you're subsidizing their travel expenses too. Because, as we all know, the cost of everything is going up, and the police band can't be expected to play for free while the rest of us are just trying to keep our heads above water. It’s a fascinating business model, turning a public service into a private enterprise, one wedding march at a time.
