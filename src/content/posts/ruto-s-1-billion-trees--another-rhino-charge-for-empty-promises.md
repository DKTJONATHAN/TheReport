So, President Ruto was out in Wamba, busy with the fancy Rhino Charge thing, pretending we're all going to plant a billion trees this year. One billion. Sounds good, right? Like when they promised us the 24-hour economy was some sort of miracle cure, and all we got was a headache and higher electricity bills. This whole "environmental agenda is the heart of economic sustainability" song and dance is getting old. Kenya's natural resources are the backbone, sure, and those water towers are worth Sh600 billion. We *depend* on them, he says. Funny how that dependence doesn't seem to translate into actual, tangible protection when it's inconvenient.

Then comes the grand plan: an Integrated Natural Resource Secretariat and a "comprehensive policy." Translation: more committees, more meetings, more jargon, and probably more corruption disguised as progress. And a nationwide mobilization campaign in September or October. A national tree-planting day. Just what we need, another public holiday where everyone pretends to care for a few hours before the real work gets ignored again. It’s the same old script, just with a greener coat of paint.

He keeps talking about synergy between the private and public sectors, using the Rhino Charge as a shining example. Sh2.8 billion raised over the years. That's commendable, I suppose, for their little off-road fun. But let's be real, while they're raising money for fences, the actual problems - the land grabbing, the unchecked pollution, the illegal logging - continue unabated. It’s like putting a Band-Aid on a gaping wound, a very expensive, off-road Band-Aid.

This whole 1 billion tree push is just the opening act for their 15 billion trees by 2032 goal. A generational climate strategy, they call it. We’ve seen these generational strategies before. Remember the grand promises about revamping the KPC? That ended up being a real KPC Board's Ego Trip, with more chaos brewing than actual progress. When will we learn that lofty targets and grand pronouncements are cheap, but actual, sustained action is what truly matters?

It's all about making noise and looking busy. The President is launching an endowment fund now, to sustain the fences. Sustaining the legacy. Legacy for whom? For the politicians who will eventually find a way to bypass those fences? For the cartels who will continue their business in the shadows? We've seen how these government evaluations and economic values play out in real life. It’s a lot of numbers on paper, while the ground reality remains the same for ordinary Kenyans.

And let's not forget the "foundational role" of ecosystems in the economy. Agriculture, fisheries, manufacturing, clean energy. All dependent on nature. Yet, we have floods wiping out thousands of families, and the response is… planting trees? It’s a good soundbite, but it doesn’t solve the immediate crisis. We need more than just a coordinated strategy and pretty speeches; we need actual accountability and a commitment that goes beyond the photo ops.
