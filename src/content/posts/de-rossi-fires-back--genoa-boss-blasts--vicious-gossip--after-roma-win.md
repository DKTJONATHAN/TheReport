Honestly, darling, it’s *exhausting*. Daniele De Rossi, bless his managerial heart, has apparently decided to get something “off his chest” after beating Roma. As if winning wasn't sweet enough, the man feels the need to address the peanut gallery. He’s apparently been reading all the “appalling” commentary about his lineup choices, and frankly, I’m not surprised. The minute a manager deviates from the script, suddenly everyone’s an expert. It’s like the football world collectively decided to become armchair psychologists and gossip columnists overnight.

De Rossi is practically wailing about how people are questioning a 20-year veteran's decisions. Oh, the horror! He's even gone so far as to quote Antonio Conte, a man who practically invented the "my way or the highway" coaching philosophy. Apparently, the advice was to "make a mistake using your own head and your own ideas." How utterly revolutionary. I mean, who would have thought a manager would actually, you know, *manage*? It's almost as if he's expected to operate in a vacuum, devoid of any public scrutiny. This whole "viciousness" he speaks of sounds suspiciously like the everyday noise of the sports world. It's not exactly high-level hallucinations, like what we see at those endless digital talkfests in Nairobi.

He’s also trying to explain away his player selection, talking about "attitude, tactics, and physical condition." Please, Daniele, we’re not children. Sometimes you pick players because they're fit, sometimes because you *think* they'll do the job, and sometimes, just sometimes, because you’re trying to avoid the feeding frenzy that comes from losing to your old club. Let's not pretend this is some groundbreaking revelation. Every manager does it, and the fact that he feels the need to defend it so vehemently just screams insecurity.

And then there’s the drama around his players. Apparently, Sabelli has gone from being underestimated to feeling "more important" since January. Good for him, I guess. And Leali, the "professional," is apparently being a good sport about not playing. It's all very heartwarming, isn't it? These little backroom dramas are the real stories, far more interesting than any public pronouncements about tactical genius. It's the behind the scenes stuff that truly fuels the gossip machine, much like the quiet desperation we often see in the Kenyan youth trying to find untapped jobs.

But let's get back to the juicy part: the "gossip." De Rossi is clearly bothered by the whispers of a swap deal with Roma. "People just love to gossip - it’s unbelievable," he huffed. Darling, that's literally your job description as a public figure in football. You’re supposed to be entertaining, not just winning. And frankly, a little bit of transfer speculation is the spice of life. If you can’t handle the heat, maybe stick to managing a Sunday league team where the biggest scandal is someone forgetting their shin guards.
