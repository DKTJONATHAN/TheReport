So, Debora nearly bought the farm - literally - a few months after becoming a wife. Stabbed in her own home by her in-laws in Homa Bay. Because, apparently, she was too old for their precious son and, surprise, surprise, the whole mess smells suspiciously like someone eyeing the land. This is Kenya, people. You think it’s about love? It’s about who gets what when the old man kicks the bucket.

Debora, bless her naive heart, thought marrying a man who accepted her and her three kids was a good start. She thought she'd be welcomed. Instead, she got the cold shoulder and then a knife. Her mother-in-law, apparently worried about the family lineage and a good old-fashioned land grab, decided Debora was persona non grata. And when words failed, they brought out the sharp objects. It’s a miracle she’s even alive, narrowly missing her vital organs. Lucky her.

And the justice system? Ha! After the bleeding stopped and the hospital bills were paid, Debora reported the assault. The police? More like the "Delayed Justice" department. Suspects arrested, then out on bail faster than you can say "corruption." They were practically scheduling court dates like playdates. It’s a classic Kenyan script: the victim becomes a nuisance, and the perpetrators are treated like VIPs. Honestly, at this point, I'm not surprised. It reminds me of how some of these cross-border crime discussions go; lots of talk, little action. It’s not just Debora. Mildred is branded a "gold digger" because her KRA husband can't fund his entire extended family *and* his new one. Then there's Jecinter, being pushed out because she hasn't produced grandchildren yet. Apparently, the matriarchs are more concerned with their lineage than actual human beings. And the land? Always the land. When resources are tight, it’s easier to demonize the woman than to share.

The experts say it's gender-based violence rooted in power imbalances. I say it’s greed and a twisted sense of entitlement. Widows get kicked out, women with kids from previous marriages are seen as future land-grabbers, and anyone who doesn't fit the mold is fair game. The State Department of Gender handles five cases like this a month in Homa Bay alone. Five. Every month. And these are just the ones that get reported.

So, women are left fleeing their homes, fighting for their lives and their dignity, while the system dithers and the land-hungry relatives plot. This isn't about marriage; it's about property disguised as family drama. And unless men start manning up and defending their wives instead of bowing to their mothers' every whim, this cycle of abuse will just keep on spinning.
