So, our Foreign Affairs PS, Korir Sing’oei, jets off to Prague this week, beaming about "deepening diplomatic ties" and "surging economic cooperation" with the Czechs. Apparently, our exports to them grew a whopping 24.3 percent over five years. That's a number that sounds impressive until you realise it’s probably from a base so small, it's practically microscopic. They're celebrating a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement too - you know, so it's easier to move money around. Sounds like a win for the folks who already have money to move.

And then there's the "Return of the Bongos." Four endangered mountain bongos shipped back to Mount Kenya. While it’s nice for the bongos, let's not pretend this is saving the planet. It’s a nice photo op, a bit of conservation theatre. Just like the "invaluable Czech medical scholarships" and the "eco-conscious Shanga Trails artisan initiative." All good feel-good stories, but are they truly transforming the lives of the masses? It feels more like window dressing for a grander strategy.

This whole "middle-power diplomacy" thing? It’s Nairobi’s latest buzzword. We're ditching the old Western heavyweights for countries like Belarus, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, and Croatia. Why? Because they’re "economically useful," "strategically flexible," and "politically less demanding." Translation: easier to deal with, less likely to point out our own internal mess. It’s about spreading the net wider, making sure we’re not putting all our eggs in one basket, especially when those baskets come with strings attached.

President Ruto’s pitch of Kenya as a "strategic gateway" for European investment into Africa? That’s the core of it. We want to be the middleman, the connector. It’s about economic opportunity, not ideology. We’re not about saving the world; we’re about making a buck. This pragmatic approach, focusing on trade, technology transfer, and climate financing, sounds good on paper. It’s the kind of thing that could lead to real change, but right now, it feels more like chasing the next deal. Just like the struggles we’ve seen with projects like the [Kipchoge Keino Stadium: A Decade of Dust and 33 Percent Progress](/posts/kipchoge-keino-stadium--a-decade-of-dust-and-33-percent-progress), promises are easily made.

The global order is fragmenting, and Nairobi is smart to play that game. By cultivating multiple partnerships, we gain room to manoeuvre. We're projecting ourselves as a "regional convening hub." It’s a sophisticated hustle, no doubt. But let's be clear, this isn't about profound ideological shifts. It's about diversifying our economic and political dependencies, making ourselves more resilient. It’s a smart play for survival and growth in a messy world, but don't expect the streets of Nairobi to suddenly transform overnight. This is about playing the long game, and for now, the game is about more friends, more deals, and hopefully, more money trickling down.
