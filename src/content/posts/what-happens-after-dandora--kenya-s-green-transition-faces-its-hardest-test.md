DANDORA CLOSURE: Kenya's "Green Transition" Is Just Another Dumpster Fire











So, Kenya is finally deciding to bury Dandora. About time, right? This place is a festering sore on the city, spewing poison into the air, water, and probably our breakfast cereal. The environmentalists are having a field day, and frankly, who can blame them? This mountain of garbage is a symbol of everything we get wrong as a city, a nation, and a species. But let's not pretend this is about saving the planet; it's about making Nairobi look less like a disaster zone to the tourists and the fat cats.

What nobody wants to talk about, of course, are the thousands of souls who call this dumpsite their office. These aren't just people sifting through rubbish; they're the backbone of Nairobi's recycling, a dirty, grimy, unpaid army making sure our plastic bottles and old phones don't just sit there adding to the mess. They are the real circular economy, long before the fancy folks in suits started using the term. For them, it's not rubbish, it's *kazi* - work. And for their troubles, they get a pittance, less than two euros a day, while breathing in toxic fumes and dodging falling debris.

Now, the government is planning to pave over their lives with a new facility in Ruai. Great. More concrete, more officialdom, and absolutely zero thought for what happens to the people. This isn't just about closing a landfill; it's about dismantling an entire informal economy built on desperation and resilience. They'll bulldoze homes, reroute access roads, and tell us it's for progress. Progress for whom, though? Certainly not for the women and men who have spent years navigating these mountains of waste, developing an intimate knowledge of what's valuable and how to get it. It's a familiar story in Nairobi, where progress often means displacing the poor. Just look at how the middle class has reasons to believe, while others are left behind.

The court ruling that finally acknowledged the hazardous conditions and awarded compensation was a drop in the ocean, a brief moment of visibility for the invisible workers. But will it change anything when the bulldozers arrive? The real challenge isn't closing Dandora, it's figuring out how to transition these livelihoods without crushing them. It's about recognising that these informal workers, with their deep understanding of waste streams and their established networks, are not a problem to be eliminated, but a vital part of the solution.

Nairobi’s urban regeneration might look good on the brochures, but history shows us these grand plans rarely benefit the people on the ground. It’s easy to close a landfill, but it’s a whole different ball game to dismantle the intricate web of human activity that has sprung up around it. Kenya’s "green transition" will truly be judged by whether these informal environmental workers are allowed to remain part of the picture, or if they're just another pile of discarded problems swept away to make room for the next shiny project.
