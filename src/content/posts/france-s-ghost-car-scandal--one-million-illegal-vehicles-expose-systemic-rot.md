France's grand experiment in modernizing its notoriously sluggish vehicle registration system has predictably devolved into a million-strong phantom fleet, a testament to the nation's enduring capacity for self inflicted idiocy. The state auditor, the Cour des Comptes, has laid bare the naked truth: privatizing access to the SIV (state vehicle licensing agency) in 2017, ostensibly to expedite paperwork, has become a goldmine for fraudsters and a liability for the public.

The core of the issue, as the report points out with weary inevitability, is the "excessive and mistaken" reliance on good faith. Of course. Because when has entrusting a critical government function to the good intentions of profit driven entities ever ended well? Hundreds of "unscrupulous operators" have, shocker, exploited this naive idealism. They've established "ghost dealerships," a fitting metaphor for the hollow promises of privatization, and for a fee, have been manipulating the registry.

The consequences are as predictable as they are dire. Hundreds of millions of euros in lost taxes and fines have vanished into the ether, a direct hit to the public purse. But more concerning, and frankly, more French, is the compromise of public safety. Unsafe cars and lorries, their legitimacy manufactured rather than earned, are now a common sight on the roads. And, as the report grimly notes, this vulnerability has become a veritable all you can eat buffet for criminals of every stripe, from the petty delinquent to the organized syndicates.

The shift of some 2,000 civil servants to "new tasks" sounds like progress, a modernizing gesture. In reality, it was a shuffling of deck chairs on the Titanic, as the system was handed over to those with the least incentive to uphold its integrity. The "digital salvation" promised by such initiatives, as we've seen before, often proves to be just another stagnant illusion, a convenient cover for deeper, more ingrained systemic rot. This ghost car scandal is not a glitch; it is the feature, the inevitable outcome of a system designed for convenience over control, and populated by those who understand that the state's good faith is merely an opportunity for exploitation.
