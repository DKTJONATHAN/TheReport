So, Tony Ndiema, an Ohangla star, is dead. And as usual, the whole internet is awash with "rest in peace" messages and tales of how great he was. But before he shuffled off this mortal coil, where was all this love? Akothee, bless her cynical heart, is pointing out the obvious hypocrisy. Apparently, the Luo community, like many others in this country, prefers to eulogize artists after they're gone, rather than actually support them when they're alive and kicking. Typical.

This whole drama plays out against the grim reality for regional artists in Kenya. Ndiema, bless his soul, passed away after a long illness. He battled financial hurdles his whole life, scraped together his BBR Band, and poured his talent into Ohangla. Yet, it seems his life's work wasn't enough to secure him adequate support. It’s a story we hear too often, isn't it? It reminds me of how sometimes the most obvious problems, like [Nairobi's Payroll Sham Exposed]( /posts/knh-and-moi-university--nairobi-s-payroll-sham-exposed-as-hundreds-paid-off-books), are the hardest to fix because people just prefer to look the other way until disaster strikes.

Akothee is getting flak for her "digital engagement," they say. But honestly, is it wrong to question the sincerity of grief when it's so conveniently timed? These artists, who bring so much joy and cultural richness to our lives, are often left to fend for themselves. Piracy, dodgy contracts, and a general lack of institutional backing means they’re always on the brink. They command huge followings, but that doesn't translate to a stable income or proper healthcare. It's a systemic failure, plain and simple.

While the rest of us are busy pretending to be heartbroken, perhaps it's time to think about what happens *after* the social media storm fades. How many more talented musicians have to die in obscurity and financial ruin before we, as a society, actually start valuing our cultural custodians? We need to move beyond performative mourning and offer tangible support - fair compensation, better healthcare access, and a system that celebrates artists during their lives, not just when they become a sad hashtag. But let's be real, that's a tall order in this country.

Meanwhile, in the land of the ridiculously wealthy, there's a place called 37ByIneza. Tucked behind Runda, promising a "nature-led residential enclave" with all the trimmings. Five-bedroom all-ensuite townhouses with DSQs, a clubhouse, heated pool, padel court, you name it. For a cool KShs 69.5 million. While artists like Ndiema struggle for basic care, this is where the money's flowing. It’s a stark reminder of the economic chasm in Nairobi, where one person's luxury dream is another's daily struggle for survival.
