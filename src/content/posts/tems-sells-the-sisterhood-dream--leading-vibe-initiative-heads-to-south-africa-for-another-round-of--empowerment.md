The initiative claims to address the "under-representation" of women in the music industry. Sure, because what the industry really needs is another masterclass on "creative development" instead of, you know, actual money or royalty checks that do not disappear into thin air. If you are a South Africa-based female artist aged 18-35, you are invited to apply for a chance to sit in a room with industry figures and learn about the "business of music." Translation: learn how to get played by the same system that Tems managed to escape.

We are told this edition will feature curated sessions on songwriting and production, with gear provided by Native Instruments. It is interesting how these "grassroots" movements always come with a heavy side of corporate sponsorship. Is it really about the art, or is it about making sure the "Leading Vibe" brand stays relevant across the continent? Tems claims she wants to help women "step into their power," but we all know the music industry power structure is more like a game of musical chairs where the music usually stops before you can even find a seat.

LVI has already done its rounds in Lagos and Nairobi, where Tems hosted intimate dinners and fireside chats. It is all very aesthetic, very curated in its presentation. But beneath the Instagram posts and the talk of "shaping the future," one has to wonder if these workshops actually produce stars or just more content for Tems' marketing team. The applications open today and close on February 28th, 2026. If you are ready to hustle for a spot in the spotlight, go ahead and apply, but do not be surprised if the "vibe" feels more like a well-oiled PR machine than a revolution.
