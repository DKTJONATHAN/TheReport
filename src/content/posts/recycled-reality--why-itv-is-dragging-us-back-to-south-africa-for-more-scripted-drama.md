ITV is back at it again, trying to convince us that watching C-list stars shiver in the South African bush is must-see TV. They just dropped a teaser during Britain-s Got Talent, featuring Ant and Dec doing their usual scripted "oops, a big cat" routine. It is the same tired slapstick we have seen for decades. If you think this is about survival or any kind of authentic experience, you have probably bought into the same kind of corporate hype seen in [Africa 2026: Navigating the New Frontiers of Energy, Finance, and Strategic Sovereignty](/posts/africa-2026--navigating-the-new-frontiers-of-energy--finance--and-strategic-sovereignty) regarding how the continent is constantly being rebranded for Western profit.

They are calling this series "Legends," which is a hilarious stretch of the imagination. Last year we had to endure Paul Burrell pulling faces and Myleene Klass somehow winning a crown for eating bugs. This year, the producers are promising "harder" trials and "relentless twists." Translation: they are desperate to stop you from changing the channel to something with actual substance. It is all pre-recorded filler, but they are throwing us a bone by letting viewers vote at a live finale in London. How generous of them to let us participate in their tax-deductible vacation.

Let us talk about that "big cat" stunt in the trailer. Ant jumps out of his seat because a sound effect played from a hidden speaker. It is the kind of high-budget acting that makes you miss the days when reality TV at least pretended to be real. The marketing team claims the campmates will face "bigger consequences" for their decisions. What consequences? A slightly smaller portion of rice? A stern look from a camera op? The only consequence is the inevitable decline of our collective IQ as we watch grown adults scream at shadows for a paycheck.

We still do not have a confirmed line-up, but expect the usual suspects: a soap star who needs to pay off a mortgage, a retired athlete looking for a redemption arc, and maybe an influencer who does not know how to use a kerosene lamp. ITV claims this is where "legends are made," but we all know it is where careers go to graze before they finally retire to the bargain bin of podcasting. It is a cycle of desperation that the British public laps up every single time.

And of course, they had to squeeze in a mention of a Taylor Swift fan magazine at the end of the press release. Because why not? If you are gullible enough to believe Ant is truly scared of a roar in a controlled environment, you might as well drop nine pounds on a 100-page picture book of a billionaire. It is all part of the same corporate machine designed to keep you distracted while they recycle the same three ideas until the end of time. Stay cynical, people.
