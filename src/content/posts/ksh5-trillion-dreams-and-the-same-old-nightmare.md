Parliament is at it again, patting itself on the back for passing the National Infrastructure Fund Bill, 2026. Majority Leader Kimani Ichung’wah is out here comparing it to the 1965 Sessional Paper No. 10, as if nostalgia can fill the potholes on Mombasa Road. They want us to believe this is a "big step" toward a new financing model, but to anyone who has lived through more than two election cycles in Nairobi, it sounds like another sophisticated way to move public money into private pockets.

The plan is to "mobilise" Ksh5 trillion over the next decade. Let that sink in. Five trillion shillings. They claim this will move us away from a debt-driven model to an investment-led approach. It sounds lovely on paper, but in this country, "mobilise" is usually code for "we have run out of people to borrow from, so we are coming for your savings directly." It is the same old script, just with a more expensive font and a brand new name to confuse the taxpayers.

While they talk about transport, energy, and digital connectivity, we know the drill. [In Kenya, Even Getting Robbed Is A Taxable Event](/posts/in-kenya--even-getting-robbed-is-a-taxable-event), and this fund is just the latest tax-adjacent scheme to keep the wheels of the state machinery greased. They call it "strategic projects," but we call it a Wednesday. By the time these "investments" trickle down to the person on the street, they will be as dry as the taps in Lang'ata.

They tried to fix the optics by creating a "Governing Council" to ensure transparency. This council includes the Treasury CS, the CBK Governor, and six "professionals" handpicked by the President. If you think a board appointed by the executive is going to provide "checks and balances" against the executive, I have a bridge in Kisauni to sell you. It is a closed-loop system designed to give the illusion of oversight while the keys to the vault stay firmly in the usual hands.

The bill even includes "tough" penalties for misuse - a Ksh10 million fine or five years in prison for eating billions. In a city where a decent SUV costs more than that fine, that is not a punishment; it is a service charge. It is almost insulting how low they have set the bar for accountability. They are basically telling the looters to make sure they steal enough to cover the legal fees and still have change for a retirement in Dubai.

Now, the whole thing sits on William Ruto’s desk, waiting for a signature to become law. We are told this will lead to "rapid economic transformation," but we have been transforming since 1963 and we are still stuck in the same mud. Another fund, another council, and another decade of paying for "world-class" infrastructure that usually ends up being a two-lane road with a fancy name and a collapsing bridge. Welcome to the new era, same as the old one.
