Grace Nyokabi, a twenty-year-old mama with a month-old baby, is perched on a cold mabati verandah in Kosovo, Mathare. The polluted Mathare River, stinking of decay, is her neighbour. Her back aches, her abdomen throbs - the lingering souvenirs of a C-section. Rest? A fairy tale for Grace, whose reality is serving chapati at dawn, prepared over a smoky jiko while her infant slept. Survival is the only agenda, because without those chapati, there's no food, no diapers, no roof. She resumed work three weeks after leaving the hospital; the Sh100 her mother could spare wasn't going to feed a growing family.

Then there's Caroline Kemunto, another survivor of this new Kenyan healthcare gamble. She remembers the good old days under Uhuru, when she walked out of a hospital with nothing but a basin and slippers, no bill in sight. Now? Her second child needed an incubator for three weeks after a C-section at Pumwani, racking up a Sh30,000 bill. To clear it, she’s washing clothes for Sh100 a *karai*. You think she can save for SHIF premiums with that kind of income? It’s enough to make you laugh, if it wasn’t so tragically pathetic. It’s a cruel joke, not unlike some of the political acrobatics we see, where leaders seem to operate in a different reality, as seen with Ruto Storms Matiang'i Turf in Gusii Land.

Grace’s own birth story is a testament to sheer luck, or as she calls it, divine intervention. Turned away from Pumwani, she ended up at a Muslim-run hospital for a C-section. Her baby needed an incubator for 11 days, and the bill? A cool Sh50,000. Again, she’d been washing clothes for a hundred bob a day. The only reason she walked out without paying was because it coincided with the end of Ramadan. Talk about timing. The new Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF) demands upfront annual contributions for casual workers like Grace and Caroline. Try saving Sh30,000 for a C-section when you're barely scraping by. It’s a guaranteed loss.

The numbers don't lie, of course. A 2023 ILO report showed a pathetic 6.5% of pregnant women were in the formal sector. For the rest, nearly 93.5% got no income replacement during confinement. And a staggering 33.5% didn't even benefit from free maternity care. This is Kenya, where maternity leave is shorter than the international minimum, and even then, it's a privilege for the few in formal employment. While some companies like Safaricom are trying to do better, offering extended paid leave, for the Graces and Carolines of this world, that’s just another story from a different planet.

Grace, despite the constant pain, dreams of a government that actually cares. She suggests a monthly stipend of at least Sh30,000 for the first three months to cover basic costs, followed by a lump sum to start a business. It’s not an outlandish request, given the Kenya Social Protection Policy (2023) has plans for similar support. But implementation? That’s where the government's commitment seems to evaporate faster than water in the Nairobi heat. Other countries, like India and Peru, have functional cash transfer programs; even Murang'a County has its own maternal health support. But here? Well, we're still waiting for the budget allocations that never seem to materialize. It’s a classic case of talking the talk, but never walking the walk, much like the discussions around revolutionizing emergency care versus just another expensive gimmick.
