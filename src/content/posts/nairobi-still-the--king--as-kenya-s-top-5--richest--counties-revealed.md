THE TOP 5 "RICHEST" COUNTIES IN KENYA: MORE LIKE THE TOP 5 COUNTIES STILL PRETENDING

So, the Kenya Bureau of National Statistics is out here telling us Nairobi, Mombasa, Nyeri, Nakuru, and Meru are the "wealthiest" counties. Big surprise, right? Nairobi, the city that eats ambition for breakfast and spits out broken dreams on Haile Sellassie Avenue, is apparently sitting pretty with Ksh4.1 trillion. Kiambu, Nakuru, and Mombasa are trailing behind like hopeful sidekicks. They all chipped in about 42.9 percent of the "national economy." Sounds impressive, until you remember that a good chunk of that is probably just the rent money from all the concrete jungles they’re building.

Nairobi's economic engine? Wholesale and retail trade, manufacturing, fancy financial services, real estate, and construction. Translation: it's where the deals are made, the goods are moved, and the lucky few build their empires while the rest of us scramble for crumbs. Kiambu's not far behind, also deep in manufacturing, construction, and retail. Nakuru is bragging about agriculture, manufacturing, and electricity - because nothing says prosperity like growing food and keeping the lights on for the few who can afford it. Mombasa’s port is still its cash cow, propped up by transport and trade. And Meru? Agriculture, trade, real estate, and construction. They're all playing the same game, just at different speeds.

The real kicker is the GCP per capita. Nairobi’s Ksh850,332 is "nearly three times the national average." So, while Nairobians are supposedly swimming in cash, the rest of Kenya is struggling to keep its head above water. Mombasa clocks in second, followed by Nakuru, Meru, and Kiambu. This data just screams inequality louder than a matatu driver on Waiyaki Way. Only seven counties are even close to the national average. The rest are probably wondering what this "GCP" thing even means while they’re still trying to figure out how to pay for basic necessities. It's a classic story of the few benefiting while the many are left in the dust, a situation we've seen play out countless times, even in discussions about [the IMF debt trap killing Kenyan dreams](/posts/test-article).

This whole "wealthiest counties" narrative is just a shiny distraction. It papers over the cracks of a system that’s designed to concentrate wealth in a few hands and a few locations. These numbers are just statistics; they don't reflect the reality for the average Kenyan trying to make ends meet. It's the same old song and dance: the haves getting richer, and the have-nots just hoping they don't get pushed further down the ladder. The stark disparities between these "rich" counties and the arid, semi-arid regions paint a grim picture of where Kenya is actually heading.
