So, the UN boys are dreaming up a Nairobi for 2035. More concrete, more steel, more shiny things. Apparently, we’ll have trains zipping us into town instead of the usual metal boxes spewing exhaust. And these fancy BRT buses? They might even start giving the matatus a run for their money. Right. Because the same people who can’t even fix the potholes will suddenly conjure up public transport that actually works. Don’t hold your breath.

They’re talking about 7.2 million people crammed into this city by 2035. That’s 1.5 million extra souls needing homes, jobs, and clean water - something we’re already struggling with. The grand Nairobi Railway City, a sprawling 425 acres, is meant to be the solution, a modern hub linking everything. They say it’ll handle half a million passengers a day. Sounds impressive, until you remember the current chaos. More platforms, more retail space - more places for the few to prosper while the rest of us navigate the same old grind.

And then there's the "smarter mobility" talk. Synchronised traffic lights and digital surveillance. It’s just another way to squeeze more money out of us, I reckon. They want us living closer to these new transport arteries, turning entire neighbourhoods into dormitory towns. Meanwhile, the CBD is supposedly spreading its wings to Westlands, Upper Hill, Gigiri. Basically, more concentrated areas for the chosen few to conduct their business while the masses are left to figure out how to get from A to B without losing their sanity. It reminds me of how even seemingly solid business strategies can go sideways, like that Sasini Avocado Processing Plant saga.

The airport is getting a facelift too, a new terminal and an "Airport City". All to make Nairobi a regional aviation hub. More flights, more tourists, more business. Great. Maybe they’ll finally fix that Nairobi River they keep promising to clean up by January 2027. Last I checked, sanitation and flood mitigation are still more of a wish list than a reality for many parts of this city.

And the tech capital dreams? More CCTV, more digital services, more data centres. They’re hoping for AI and cloud computing to save the day. It’s all very shiny and futuristic, but let’s be honest, while they’re busy building these digital empires, the basic infrastructure for the average Kenyan is still stuck in the analogue age. We'll see if all this fancy tech can actually deliver better services or just more digital red tape.
