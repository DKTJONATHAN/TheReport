ABBAS KUBBAFF KES 10 MILLION STATE PAYDAY: NAIROBI'S REALITY CHECK











So, Abbas Kubbaf, our esteemed pioneer of "Booba" rap, pulls a stunt worthy of a magician, pulling a cool KES 10 million out of thin air for a few songs at a presidential do. Suddenly, the rest of us struggling artists, who are lucky to make KES 100,000 a month from gigs, are supposed to feel inspired. This isn't inspiration, people, it's a flashing neon sign pointing to how the real money flows - straight from the public purse to a select few, leaving the rest of us to scramble for crumbs.

Let's not forget the KRA and all the other 'statutory deductions' Abbas conveniently glossed over. That KES 10 million gross is more like a KES 5 million net, if we're being charitable. It's a classic Nairobi trick: big numbers thrown around to impress, but the actual takeaway is a lot less glamorous. This is the same government that can find millions for a handshake ceremony but struggles to fund basic grassroots arts programs. It's a performance, alright, but not the kind that benefits the majority.

This whole "winner-takes-all" scenario isn't new. We see it everywhere. While Abbas lives it up, the everyday musician is stuck in a cycle of uncertainty. It's like watching those [Police on floods: 49 dead, over 2,600 families displaced across Kenya - hiiraan.com](/posts/police-on-floods--49-dead--over-2-600-families-displaced-across-kenya---hiiraan-com) stories - massive events that grab headlines, while the real suffering in the background goes largely ignored. The creative economy's 'talanta hela' initiative sounds good on paper, but until the distribution is fairer, it's just another way for the elite to get richer.

And this "longevity premium" argument? It’s just another way to say that the system rewards those who play the game long enough, often by being "safe" and palatable to the powers that be. It’s not about pure talent; it’s about being the right kind of brand for a photo op. While Abbas jets between Europe and Kenya, lecturing us about owning masters, the reality for most is just trying to keep the lights on. Maybe Light It Solutions should offer a discount for musicians struggling to pay their bills, instead of just decorating the homes of the already well-off.

The cultural historian might talk about the state "validating local culture," but what validation does an artist get when they're barely surviving? This KES 10 million isn't a rising tide lifting all boats; it's a yacht sailing past a fleet of dinghies. It's a good story for Abbas, a cautionary tale for everyone else about who truly benefits when art meets government.
