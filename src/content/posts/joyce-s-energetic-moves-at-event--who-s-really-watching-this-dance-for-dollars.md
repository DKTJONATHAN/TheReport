Joyce Omondi, bless her heart, is still at it. Her "EPIC Women" thing, supposedly about faith and sisterhood, seems to be more about keeping her name in the spotlight. And of course, Waihiga Mwaura, the ever-supportive husband, is right there, clapping from the sidelines. It's all very convenient, isn't it? This "faith-based movement" sounds suspiciously like another avenue for content creation, which, according to TUKO's helpful guide, is all about making money from your creativity.

Apparently, these assemblies are "vibey" and feature "discipleship classes." I'm sure those classes are about as deep as a puddle after a light shower. Still, it’s a good way to gather a following, isn't it? And when Joyce busts out her energetic dance moves, it’s not just "entertaining," it’s a calculated effort to go viral. Because, as we all know, a good dance clip can rack up those views and, subsequently, those dollars. It's a well worn path, this blend of spirituality and showmanship.

The whole "older millennial can still bust a move" bit is just cringe. And the internet, being the fickle beast it is, lapped it up. Praise for the "woman of God." Honestly, it's exhausting. While she's busy dancing and collecting praise, remember that behind all this curated authenticity, there's a brand being built, a following being cultivated. It’s the same old story, just with a different gospel.

And speaking of leaving shows, Joyce’s departure from Citizen TV's Rauka is hardly a surprise. Five years is a decent run, and a tearful farewell complete with husband surprises and motherly pronouncements? Very theatrical. It’s all part of the narrative, designed to evoke sympathy and solidify her image as a relatable, beloved personality. It’s a calculated exit, leading into whatever comes next for her burgeoning content empire. Just remember, it’s all a show, and the real prize is often the income stream.

It’s funny how these "movements" always seem to align with career shifts. While Joyce is dancing and preaching, let’s not forget that the digital media landscape is a brutal place. Making money from content is the name of the game, and sometimes that means leaning into what gets clicks, even if it means blurring the lines between genuine faith and calculated performance. It’s a strategy as old as time, just repackaged for the 21st century.

We've seen this play out before with other media personalities, chasing the next big thing, the next revenue stream. Whether it's about England-based duos getting call-ups or the baffling spectacle of celebrity offspring launching careers amid family drama, it’s all about visibility and, ultimately, monetization. Joyce's dancing is just another tool in her ever-expanding promotional arsenal.
