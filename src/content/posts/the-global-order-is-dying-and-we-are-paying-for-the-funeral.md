In the United States, the second Trump administration has moved with a speed that suggests a premeditated execution of democratic norms. Within months of the 2025 inauguration, the machinery of the state was repurposed for personal grievance and institutional arson. The pardoning of political extremists was merely the opening act. By removing protections for civil servants and targeting the Federal Reserve, the U.S. has effectively signaled to the world that it is no longer a reliable partner. It is now a source of systemic risk. The shootings by immigration agents and the open hostility toward the courts are not glitches; they are the new operating system of a collapsing empire.

The international fallout is catastrophic for anyone relying on global trade. We have entered an era of international financial anarchy. The surge in gold prices is not a sign of a healthy alternative market, but rather a desperate flight to safety in a world where the adults have left the room. For countries like the U.K., this means higher defense costs, fractured supply chains, and a permanent reduction in living standards. The populist fire that began with Brexit has now consumed the primary engine of the global economy, and there is no fire department coming to save us.

Simultaneously, the digital frontier is undergoing its own version of a controlled demolition. Artificial intelligence, once touted as the ultimate productivity booster, has instead become a source of massive market dislocation. We are seeing a cannibalistic cycle where legacy software companies are destroyed by AI insurgents, while the tech giants spend billions on infrastructure that yields no clear path to profit. It is an everyone-loses scenario. The current market highs are a thin veneer over a hollowed-out core of uncertainty.

The parallel to the dotcom bubble is obvious, yet the stakes are higher now because the geopolitical safety nets have been removed. In 2000, we had a functioning global order to catch us when the bubble burst. In 2026, we have a polarized, autocratic superpower and a world where even the most seasoned investors are considering total retreat. The hippies of the 1960s might have been excitable, but at least they were dreaming of a future. Today, we are simply trying to survive the wreckage of a system that decided to commit suicide in broad daylight.
