In an immediate response to the judicial setback, the executive branch has moved to invoke Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act. This maneuver implements a 10-percent global tariff on all imports into the US for a 150-day window, effective February 24, 2026. This represents the inaugural use of Section 122 authority for such purposes. Under this provision, the administration can apply temporary surcharges of up to 15 percent to address balance-of-payment issues, though the measure will expire in July unless legislative action is taken by Congress to extend or ratify the mandate.

The business sector is currently assessing the implications of these shifting legal grounds. While the new 10-percent tariff includes exemptions for goods from Canada and Mexico that align with USMCA standards, the broader impact on global supply chains remains a primary concern for multinational corporations. SBI Research suggests that during this 150-day interim, the administration will likely complete investigations under Section 301 and Section 232 to establish more permanent tariff structures based on national security or unfair trade practices.

The invalidation of IEEPA-based tariffs poses a direct challenge to existing trade agreements. These frameworks have historically facilitated trillions of dollars in commerce with major economies, including China, the United Kingdom, and Japan. The sudden removal of the legal basis for these tariffs could destabilize long-standing deals and complicate future diplomatic engagements, as the interaction between inter-sovereign treaties and the legal actions of private entities creates a more litigious environment.

Market analysts suggest that the interaction between sovereign policy and the actions of juristic persons could lead to further disruption. As the US Congress weighs its role in ratifying these trade measures, corporations must prepare for a volatile regulatory environment. This period of transition mirrors broader global instabilities where domestic policy and international obligations frequently clash, similar to the geopolitical tensions explored in the report on why [Opposition Leaders Demand Recall of Kenyan Youth from Russia](/posts/opposition-leaders-demand-recall-of-kenyan-youth-from-russia).

The coming months will be critical for determining whether the US can maintain its trade relationships while navigating this new judicial reality. The reliance on Section 122 provides a temporary buffer, but the long-term resolution will depend on the legislative appetite for protectionist measures and the outcome of pending trade investigations. Businesses are advised to prepare for counter-intuitive negotiation environments as jurisdictions position themselves strategically ahead of the July deadline.
