The international fallout from this scandal will be devastating. If 133 matches are officially wiped from the records, the Dutch league loses all credibility as a stable environment for investment. Global broadcasters and sponsors do not pay for a product that can be deleted by a courtroom gavel because a left-back forgot to file a residence permit. It serves as a grim warning to other European leagues that the era of turning a blind eye to nationality loopholes is coming to a litigious end.

There is a biting irony in the fact that players from former Dutch colonies are the central figures in this disaster. The Netherlands continues to benefit from its historical reach while maintaining protectionist salary floors for non-EU workers. By forcing a 600,000-euro minimum wage on these players the moment they switch national teams, the state has created a financial trap that the clubs were too arrogant to avoid. This is the dark side of modern globalization. You can have the heritage, but you cannot have the rights without a massive price tag.

This Passportgate situation highlights the fragility of the footballing spectacle when confronted by the cold, unyielding machinery of the state. If the season is abandoned, it will be a self-inflicted wound born of sheer laziness. The league governing body is frustrated by the lawsuits, yet they are the ones who allowed these compliance failures to fester. Dutch football is now a case study in how a lack of foresight can turn a multi-million-euro industry into a legal ruin overnight.
