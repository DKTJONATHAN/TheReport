According to recent data, the disconnect between corporate rhetoric and economic reality is widening. While 374 companies in the S&P 500 highlighted AI as a positive driver during earnings calls between late 2024 and 2025, these sentiments have yet to manifest in broader macroeconomic data. A study by the National Bureau of Economic Research, surveying 6,000 executives across the U.S., U.K., Germany, and Australia, revealed that nearly 90% of firms have seen no impact on employment or productivity over the last three years. Despite the hype, the average executive reports using AI for only 90 minutes per week.

Torsten Slok, chief economist at Apollo, recently invoked Solow’s legacy, noting that outside of the "Magnificent Seven" tech giants, there are no discernible signs of AI-driven expansion in profit margins or inflation data. This stagnation stands in stark contrast to the 2023 projections from institutions like MIT, which suggested AI could boost individual worker performance by as much as 40%. Instead, more conservative estimates, such as those from economist Daron Acemoglu, suggest a modest 0.5% increase in productivity over the next decade - a figure he describes as "disappointing" relative to industry promises.

The lag in results may be attributed to what economists call the "J-curve." This theory suggests that major technological shifts initially cause a dip in performance as companies navigate the complexities of implementation, followed by an eventual exponential surge. We saw this in the late 1990s when the IT investments of the 70s and 80s finally yielded a 1.5% productivity jump. Some analysts, including Erik Brynjolfsson of Stanford’s Digital Economy Lab, argue the tide is already turning, citing a 2.7% rise in U.S. productivity last year as evidence that firms are transitioning from investment to realization.

However, human capital remains a significant hurdle. Data from ManpowerGroup indicates that while AI adoption is rising, worker confidence in the technology’s utility fell by 18% in 2025. Furthermore, tech leaders like IBM are recalibrating their approach to automation; the company recently announced plans to triple its intake of young hires to ensure a stable leadership pipeline, acknowledging that over-automating entry-level roles could hollow out future management tiers.

The current era differs from the 1980s in one critical aspect: accessibility. Unlike the proprietary hardware of the past, modern large language models are subject to fierce competition that is rapidly driving down costs. This suggests that the ultimate economic value of AI will not be found in the software itself, but in how effectively different sectors integrate these tools into their specific operational frameworks. For now, the global economy remains in a period of expensive anticipation, waiting for the digital revolution to finally show up in the ledger.
