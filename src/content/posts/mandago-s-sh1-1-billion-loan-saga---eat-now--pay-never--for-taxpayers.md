So, our Auditor-General Nancy Gathungu has dropped another bombshell, revealing that former governor Jackson Mandago and his merry band of administrators managed to hand out a cool Sh1.1 billion in housing, car, and student loans without a single shred of collateral. And who benefited? Well, some of those loans, shocker, went to Mandago himself and his cronies back when he was governor between 2013 and 2017. It’s almost like they saw public funds as their personal piggy bank.

And the drama doesn't end there. We're hearing that a bank manager is set to testify in a fraud case involving our man Mandago, who is now a Senator. This is getting messier than a matatu tout’s lunch. Meanwhile, the ghost of past mismanagement still haunts Uasin Gishu, with former officials like the Baringo County Chief of Staff allegedly using their influence to get their daughters enrolled. Because why not add nepotism to the list of grievances?

The former Uasin Gishu County Secretary claims these shady deals were all under the "youth department." How convenient. Victims of this alleged "botched program" are now crying foul, saying the governor was all smiles and promises months before everything went south. It’s the same old song and dance: a grand project, a quick payout, and then… poof.

The report cites corruption and a gaping lack of proper oversight for this alleged misappropriation. In other words, the people meant to be watching the cookie jar were either asleep at the wheel or actively helping themselves. It’s a classic Nairobi realist's nightmare, where promises of progress crumble under the weight of greed. It makes you wonder if any of these public funds ever truly serve the public good, or if it’s just a cycle of "eat now, pay never" for the connected few, leaving the rest of us to pick up the pieces, much like navigating the volatile world of commodity trading where prices can shift faster than you can blink, as we saw with [jet fuel costs in Africa](posts/in-africa--the-cost-of-jet-fuel-is-changing-faster-than-you-can-fly).

This whole affair is a stark reminder of how easily public money can disappear when accountability is a foreign concept. We’re left with a gaping hole in our finances and a bitter taste in our mouths. It's the kind of systemic rot that makes you question the very foundations of our governance, especially when you consider that even central banks admit to [flying blind in the face of financial instability](posts/the-shadow-banking-apocalypse--central-banks-admit-they-are-flying-blind).

At the end of the day, this isn't just about Sh1.1 billion. It's about the erosion of trust and the blatant disregard for the taxpayer's hard-earned money. It’s a story we’ve heard a thousand times, and sadly, it's a story that seems destined to repeat itself.
