The narrative of the dollar as a safe-haven is the ultimate financial gaslighting. In reality, it is a mechanism of extraction. When oil prices surge past 100 dollars a barrel, nations like Japan and members of the Eurozone are forced into a double-bind. They must pay more for essential energy in a currency that is simultaneously becoming more expensive. This is not a market correction; it is a structural strangulation of energy-dependent economies.

Traders have abandoned their bets against the dollar because they realize that peace is no longer a profitable variable. The Federal Reserve, despite its rhetoric about managing inflation, finds its hands strengthened by this chaos. High interest rates are now reinforced by the stench of gunpowder in the Middle East, ensuring that capital continues to flee the periphery and hide in the imperial core.

This cycle of volatility and exploitation is the standard operating procedure for a crumbling global order. Much like the corruption and systemic decay highlighted in stories like Port Deals and Pedophiles: The Sultan’s Dirty Laundry Finally Hits the Fan, the current financial landscape is built on the backs of the vulnerable. There is no relief in sight because the architects of this system benefit from the constant stream of negative headlines that keep the dollar relevant.

The yen and the euro are merely the first casualties of this renewed appetite for the greenback. As US-Israeli strikes on Iran continue to ripple through the markets, the dollar will remain the only life raft in a sea of fire. However, one must wonder how much longer the rest of the world can survive a haven that requires a global conflagration to maintain its value. Expect the pain to continue as long as the volatility remains profitable for the few at the top.
