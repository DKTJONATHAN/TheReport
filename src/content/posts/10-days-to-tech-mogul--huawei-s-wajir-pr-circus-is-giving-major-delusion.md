Darling, if you thought your two-week "juice detox" was a scam, wait until you hear about what just went down in Wajir. Huawei Kenya rolled their solar-powered DigiTruck into the dust and somehow convinced 200 hopeful souls that they are now tech titans. After a mere ten days of poking at screens, these "graduates" are being told the world is their oyster. It is giving very much "participation trophy" energy, but with more solar panels and better lighting for the press photos.

Of course, the politicians could not resist a good photo-op. PS Ummi Bashir and Wajir East MP Aden Daudi were front and center, looking like they just discovered the internet themselves. They were busy preaching about "economic opportunities" and "global participation" to a crowd that probably just wants a stable job and a working network. It is the same old story: give them a certificate, take a selfie, and pretend you have solved poverty before the next election cycle kicks off.

The audacity of the claims is what really gets me. One graduate actually claimed the training "transformed" their skills in just ten days. Honey, in ten days, most of us can barely decide on a Netflix show to binge, let alone master data privacy and entrepreneurship. This whole spectacle feels like a chapter straight out of [The 2026 ICT Budget: Another Digital Mirage for a Hungry Nation](/posts/the-2026-ict-budget--another-digital-mirage-for-a-hungry-nation), where we are sold high-tech dreams while our stomachs are doing the talking.

Let us be real about why Huawei is doing this. It is not out of the goodness of their corporate heart. It is about optics and keeping the government happy so they can keep those juicy infrastructure contracts rolling in. They have reached 40 counties now, which means they have 9,000 photos of smiling youth to show their shareholders. Meanwhile, the "digital divide" remains as wide as the gap between a celebrity’s Instagram face and their real one.

At the end of the day, 200 kids in Wajir have a new piece of paper to hang on walls that might not even have power outlets. If digital literacy is the "foundation for empowerment," then why does it feel like we are just building houses of cards on social media? Stay cynical, folks, because in the world of Kenyan PR, the only thing "high-speed" is how fast the politicians disappear once the cameras stop clicking.
