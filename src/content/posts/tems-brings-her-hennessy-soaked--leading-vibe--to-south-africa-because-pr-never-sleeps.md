Oh look, another initiative wrapped in the shiny foil of female empowerment and sponsored by a liquor brand. Tems is taking her Leading Vibe Initiative (LVI) to South Africa this March, because apparently, she has solved all the music industry problems in Lagos and Nairobi already. It is the usual script: grab 20 emerging girls, put them in a room with some microphones, and act like you are dismantling the patriarchy one Hennessy cocktail at a time. I find it hilarious how these brands love to preach about professional growth while making sure everyone stays sufficiently tipsy.

The South African edition, set for March 21, 2026, is targeting women aged 18-35. Because, as we all know in the entertainment business, if you are 36 and still trying to produce a beat, you might as well go back to the village. The program promises masterclasses and curated sessions on the business of music, but let us be real: it is a high-budget networking mixer where the chosen few get to bask in the reflected glow of the GRAMMY winner. Tems says she wants to help women find their voices, but I suspect she really just wants to expand her brand footprint into the Southern market.

It is all very convenient, is it not? Launched in August 2025, this initiative has been moving across the continent like a localized circus of optimism. While the press releases talk about the ongoing underrepresentation of women, they conveniently ignore how the industry mostly rewards women who fit a very specific, marketable aesthetic. We are told these workshops provide tools and opportunity, but most participants end up with a certificate and a few photos for the 'Gram rather than a actual distribution deal or a royalty check that clears the rent.

This constant push for emerging talent feels less like genuine mentorship and more like [the mirage of emerging markets](/posts/the-global-guillotine--wealth-taxes-and-the-mirage-of-emerging-markets) where the promise of a golden future is used to distract from the messy financial reality of the present. While Tems shares her personal journey in fireside chats, most female creatives are still struggling with the basic logistics of an industry that treats them as decorative accessories. South Africa has its own set of industry sharks, and a two-day workshop with Native Instruments tech is not going to change the fact that the gatekeepers are still mostly men in suits.

Applications close on February 28, 2026, for those who want to try their luck at being the next big thing. If you are a South Africa-based producer or songwriter, feel free to apply and hope that the mentorship lasts longer than the Hennessy bottle. I will be here, sipping my tea and waiting to see if any of these twenty participants actually get a hit song out of this, or if we are just setting the stage for another glossy recap video to keep the sponsors happy. After all, the vibe might be leading, but the money is always following.
