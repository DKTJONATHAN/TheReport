Rigathi Gachagua has finally realized that being the Villager-in-Chief has its limits, especially when you have been kicked out of the palatial office you fought so hard to occupy. Now he is roaming the country, trying to convince us that he has magically transformed into a national leader who cares about everyone from Kisii to Nandi. It is the classic Kenyan political rebrand: take a bitter man, give him a new party symbol, and hope the voters have the memory of a goldfish. He is targeting the Rift Valley now, telling them they have been shortchanged by the very man he spent years praising as a godsend.

The irony is so thick you could cut it with a blunt panga. This is the same man who told us that the government is a company with shareholders where some people have a bigger say than others. Now that he has been fired from the board, he suddenly cares about the informal business structures being demolished in Nairobi. He is even promising to scrap the housing levy, a tax he defended with his whole chest just a few months ago. It is hard to take him seriously when his convictions change faster than a matatu switching lanes on Thika Road during rush hour.

Gachagua is now preaching about spreading political risks to the people of Rift Valley, essentially telling them not to trust the President he helped put in power. It is a desperate move from a man who knows his political survival depends on chaos. While he complains about youthful, inexperienced politicians making money, he is conveniently forgetting that the entire system is designed for that exact purpose. [The Vultures Are Fed and the Rest of Us Are for Sale](/posts/the-vultures-are-fed-and-the-rest-of-us-are-for-sale) as these elites reshuffle their alliances while the rest of us struggle to buy a packet of unga.

He claims he turned down a 2 billion shilling bribe to resign, which sounds more like a tall tale told in a smoky bar than a political reality. Whether it is true or not, it just shows how detached these people are from the reality of a Kenyan who cannot even afford a packet of milk. They throw around billions like they are pocket change while arguing about who gets to fix the economy they helped break. His United Opposition is just a collection of familiar faces who are only united by their shared thirst for power and their sudden unemployment status.

As we head toward the 2027 circus, expect more of this theatre. Gachagua will tour the country, shed a few tears for the poor, and promise us heaven on earth. But we know the script by now. The faces might change, and the party colors might get a fresh coat of paint, but the game remains the same. They will teargas each other for the cameras, call it public participation, and then go back to their mansions to plan the next way to tax our very existence. Don't be fooled by the new DCP tag; it is just the same old politics in a cheaper suit.
