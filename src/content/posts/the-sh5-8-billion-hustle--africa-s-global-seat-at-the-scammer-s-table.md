Kenyans and our obsession with shortcuts will be the death of us. Interpol just rounded up 27 suspects in Nairobi who were busy selling the dream of global corporate investments to anyone with a smartphone and a lack of common sense. The script is tired: a few fake testimonials, a shiny dashboard showing fabricated profits, and a request for a small $50 deposit. We are so desperate for a win that we will hand over Sh6,451 to a stranger on a messaging app and act surprised when the withdrawal button suddenly stops working.

This was not just a local small-time scam; we are part of a Sh5.8 billion continental disaster. Interpol’s eight-week operation across 16 African countries exposed just how deep the rot goes, identifying over 1,200 victims who thought they were outsmarting the system. While you are struggling to pay for basic data bundles, these syndicates are out here building residential hubs for their criminal activities. It really makes you think about [the development mirage: Nigeria as the guinea pig for a collapsing global order](/posts/the-development-mirage--nigeria-as-the-guinea-pig-for-a-collapsing-global-order) where the only thing actually developing is the sophistication of the thieves.

In Nigeria, the police found a ringleader who actually built a house to serve as an operational hub for his cyber-enabled crimes. That is the new African Dream, apparently: phishing, identity theft, and social engineering from the comfort of a custom-built headquarters. They recruited young people to run fake digital asset schemes, proving that our greatest export these days is probably just highly organized fraud. They took down 1,000 accounts, but for every one they delete, five more pop up with a new investment opportunity.

Côte d’Ivoire is not doing any better, with 58 arrests linked to those predatory mobile loan apps. These guys target the most vulnerable people, promising quick cash and then turning around to harvest personal data and use abusive debt-collection tactics. They seized 300 SIM cards and hundreds of devices, but the psychological damage is already done. It is the same story everywhere: if it looks like a quick way out of poverty, it is probably just a faster way to lose the little you have left.

We can celebrate the 2,341 seized devices all we want, but the reality is that the infrastructure of fraud is permanent. These syndicates know exactly how to play on our greed and our desperation. Neal Jetton from Interpol can talk about devastating financial harm, but in Nairobi, we just call it another Tuesday. Until we stop believing that a global corporation needs our fifty dollars to survive, we will keep funding the lifestyles of the people Interpol is trying to catch.
