The call for a renewed social contract in Nigeria is less a blueprint for prosperity and more a desperate plea for stability in a region where institutions are routinely hollowed out by corruption and neglect. Mohammed speaks of trust being built through visible action, citing cash transfers to eight million households. In a nation of over 200 million people facing rampant inflation and currency devaluation, such measures are mere crumbs intended to prevent a total collapse of the state apparatus. This is the cynical reality of modern governance in the Global South: providing just enough relief to keep the population from the gates of the presidential palace while the structural rot continues unabated.

On the climate front, the UN's demands for 300 billion dollars per year in financing for developing countries are essentially a fantasy. Africa receives a pathetic two percent of global clean energy investment despite possessing the world's best solar resources. The West’s refusal to fund the Loss and Damage Fund adequately proves that the climate transition is a gated community. Nigeria is being told to lead a green revolution while 86 million of its citizens lack basic electricity and its states are decimated by annual floods. The global north has no intention of paying its climate debts; it is merely waiting for the global south to become a captive market for its green technologies.

The financial discourse remains the most grim aspect of this roadmap. Nigeria’s debt-to-GDP ratio is touted as a sign of fiscal discipline, but this metric ignores the crushing reality of debt service costs that cannibalize the national budget. As explored in THE DEBT TRAP’S NEW TEETH: HOW TARIFFS ARE STRANGLING THE DOLLAR’S DOMINANCE, the global financial system is rigged to ensure that every external shock forces the most vulnerable into deeper cycles of borrowing. The proposed Borrowers Club is a last-ditch effort at collective bargaining by the bankrupt, a union of the desperate trying to negotiate with a global financial architecture that was never designed for their survival.

Finally, the push for trade-enabled growth through the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) ignores the fact that the rules-based trading system is currently under direct assault by its own creators. While Nigeria is urged to stay open and avoid turning inward, the world's major powers are retreating into protectionism and trade wars. Nigeria is being encouraged to play by a set of rules that the rest of the world has already discarded. This roadmap is not a path to development; it is a set of instructions on how to remain a polite passenger while the global economic vehicle heads toward the cliff.
