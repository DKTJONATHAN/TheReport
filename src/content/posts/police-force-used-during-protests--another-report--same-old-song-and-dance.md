So, Odipo Dev and Amnesty International Kenya have dropped another report, this time meticulously detailing how our beloved police force has been doling out "crowd control" between 2020 and 2025. Guess what? Tear gas canisters were the star of the show, apparently deployed more times than you can count on your fingers and toes, and then some. One thousand and two protests, they say, and 230 instances of choking the life out of dissent. Groundbreaking.

This gem of an analysis comes hot on the heels of the transport sector strikes, which saw people getting roughed up, injured, and, of course, some ending up dead. Hussein Khalid from VOCAL Africa chirped in about 17 Kenyans biting the dust in just two days over fuel prices. Seventeen. But hey, at least the police had their trusty tear gas, their second-favorite being actual bullets. Eighty-seven times they decided live ammunition was the answer, with water cannons and rubber bullets rounding out the top four. It’s almost like they're collecting a full set of "dispersal methods."

The report paints a grim picture, detailing how tear gas in enclosed spaces or at close range can ruin someone's day, and their lungs. Live rounds? Well, they do what they do - they kill. Water cannons at full blast? Apparently, they can break bones. Who knew? Every weapon deployed is a "decision by an officer, authorised by a command structure." So, when you're choking on fumes or dodging bullets, remember there’s a whole chain of command to thank. It's this kind of systemic disregard that makes you wonder if our leaders, like Ruto admitting his Mt Kenya headache, truly have any control, or if they just hand it all over to the "command structure" and pray.

And the human cost? A neat little 159 deaths linked to protests, 1,245 injuries, and a staggering 2,603 arrests over those five years. Youth-led protests were apparently the most "affected" - which is a polite way of saying they bore the brunt of the official "response." It’s a tired narrative, really. Protests happen, people get hurt, arrests are made, and then we get a report to tell us what we already know. It’s like the story of Tory Lanez’s father marrying a Kenyan woman; something to talk about for a bit, then we move on, waiting for the next disaster.

With fuel protests still simmering and the Finance Bill under scrutiny, the report couldn't be more timely. Add in the second anniversary of the Gen Z protests and the looming elections, and it’s a recipe for more of the same. Because why would anything change when the "command structure" has such a reliable arsenal? It's not just about the weapons; it's about the decision to use them, a decision that routinely prioritizes silencing dissent over protecting lives.

The report is clear: batons and physical force are used "routinely, often in situations where the protest itself had been peaceful." Peaceful. So, the next time you hear about a protest, remember that the "freedom" you might expect comes with a high price tag, often paid in pain, injury, or worse, courtesy of the very people meant to protect you.
