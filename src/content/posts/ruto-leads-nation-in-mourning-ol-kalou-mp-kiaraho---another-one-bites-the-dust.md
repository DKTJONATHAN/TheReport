Ruto Leads Mourning for Ol Kalou MP Kiaraho - Another One Bites the Dust

The predictable script unfolds as President Ruto and his crew offer platitudes for the departed Ol Kalou MP, David Njuguna Kiaraho. Another year, another politician shuffling off this mortal coil, leaving behind a trail of well-worn phrases about humility and vision. Sixty-two is hardly ancient, but in Kenyan politics, it might as well be the twilight.

Speaker Wetangula announced the MP's passing at Nairobi Hospital, a place that's seen its fair share of high-profile treatments, often for ailments that seem to catch leaders by surprise, despite the lavish benefits they enjoy. Ruto, ever the showman, called Kiaraho "humble, steadfast, and visionary." These are the same adjectives trotted out for any public servant who manages to stay out of jail for a respectable period.

The President waxed lyrical about Kiaraho's contributions to education and his service in the National Assembly. He even quoted a slogan, "Kazi Kwanza, Siasa Baadaye," which sounds nice on paper, but we all know how that usually plays out. It’s the same old song and dance, isn't it? Leaders talk about focusing on issues, but the political games never stop. It reminds me of the convoluted deals Ruto strikes, like that China trade agreement - duty-free access, but for whose benefit, really? [/posts/ruto-s-china-trade-deal--duty-free-access--but-for-whose-benefit]

Deputy President Kindiki chimed in, describing Kiaraho as polite and respectful, a brilliant architect gone too soon. "Fickle and fleeting life on Earth," he lamented. Yes, the cycle of life and death is indeed a constant, especially when you're in a profession that seems to shorten lifespans considerably, or at least drain the joy out of it. Another accomplished son, another pillar lost. The constituents of Ol Kalou will undoubtedly miss their "steady representative."

The official narrative is all about inspiration and rising above rivalry. But let's be real, the political arena is a battlefield, and Kiaraho’s passing is just another casualty. We’re left with the empty seats, the solemn pronouncements, and the enduring question of whether any of this "service" truly trickles down to the mwananchi.

So, here's to David Njuguna Kiaraho. May his soul find peace, and may the remaining public servants remember that their "Kazi Kwanza" might actually mean something beyond a catchy slogan. But I won't hold my breath.
