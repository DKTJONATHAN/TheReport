Restore Britain, the brainchild of former Reform UK MP Rupert Lowe, has found itself in the uncomfortable spotlight, forced to return donations from a cryptocurrency project named Britain Token. The party's scramble to distance itself from the digital currency comes after Labour MP Phil Brickell flagged concerns to the Electoral Commission, urging an investigation into the anonymous source of the funds.

While the Electoral Commission is "considering the information," it has not yet launched a formal probe. Nevertheless, Restore Britain has been quick to state that "every single penny" has been returned, a move they claim goes "above and beyond any necessary rules." This defensive posture suggests an awareness of the precariousness of their position.

Britain Token, described as a memecoin created by anonymous developers, purportedly donated around £26,000 to Lowe's party. The project claims no affiliation but expressed support for Restore Britain's policies, which include low taxes, small government, and a hardline stance on immigration. The party, which has garnered an endorsement from cryptocurrency enthusiast Elon Musk, is polling at a modest 3%.

The timing of these donations is particularly noteworthy. They appear to predate the UK government's recent announcement of plans to legislate a ban on crypto donations to political parties. Communities Secretary Steve Reed cited "the anonymity inherent in crypto transactions" as a key reason for the proposed ban, highlighting the potential for masking the origin of funds and evading robust checks.

The fact that Britain Token's apparent donations were made in British pounds, potentially converted from crypto before reaching Restore Britain's online portal, only adds to the murkiness. Labour MP Phil Brickell, chair of the all-party Parliamentary group on Anti-Corruption and Responsible Tax, argues that Britain Token "does not appear to have any legal status or recognised existence," and therefore cannot constitute a permissible donor under UK law. Parties are legally obliged to verify the source of donations over £500, ensuring they come from identifiable and permissible entities.

The Electoral Commission's statement that this is "not the subject of an investigation" is unlikely to quell suspicion. Meanwhile, Restore Britain has accused the "impartial" regulator of "leaking supposed damaging information in order to smear us," signaling a combative stance amidst this unfolding controversy.

The value of the Britain Token memecoin has since reportedly collapsed, with a new version of the cryptocurrency emerging. The ultimate beneficiaries and uses of investments in this new iteration remain as opaque as the original. This episode serves as a stark reminder of the wild west nature of cryptocurrency and its increasing entanglement with the less transparent aspects of political finance, a situation ripe for exploitation and ripe for regulatory headaches.
