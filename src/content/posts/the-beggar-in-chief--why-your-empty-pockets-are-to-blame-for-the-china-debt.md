President Ruto is back from his luxury jet tours and he has a message for the Kenyan people: everything is your fault. Apparently, the reason we are drowning in debt is not because of runaway corruption or inflated infrastructure projects, but because you did not save your last fifty shillings. He looks at us from the podium and says we only save 12 percent of our GDP while the Chinese save 55 percent. It is a classic political move - shift the blame to the victim so you can justify reaching deeper into their already empty pockets.

The President finally admitted that those frequent, expensive flights to Beijing were not just for the cameras and the handshakes. He was there to beg. When the pomp and colour fades, our leader is behind closed doors asking for mercy because we are paying $1 billion a year just to keep the debt collectors at bay. This is the reality of the "hustler" government. While the elite discuss these heavy burdens inside [The Stone Sentinel: A History of the Kenyan Parliament Building](/posts/The-Kenyan-Parlianment-building), the rest of us are left wondering where the promised bottom-up prosperity went.

The comparison with our neighbors is the ultimate insult to a struggling Nairobian. Ruto claims Uganda and Tanzania are doing better because they made "right decisions" about social security funds earlier. He conveniently forgets to mention that those countries do not have a new, creative tax appearing every Tuesday. He says he wants to grow domestic savings, which we all know is just code for more mandatory deductions from your meager salary. If you cannot afford unga today, how are you supposed to save for a "self-reliant" Kenya in 2035?

We are told these reforms are "necessary" and that the President does not care about being popular. It is very easy to say popularity is irrelevant when you have a motorcade, a state house chef, and a guaranteed pension. For the average Kenyan trying to survive the high cost of living, these "unpopular decisions" feel less like leadership and more like a slow strangulation. He promises we will stop borrowing in a decade, but we have heard these fairy tales since the days of the old KANU regime.

In the end, the script remains the same regardless of who is in power. The government spends what it does not have, signs bad deals with foreign powers, and then turns around to tell the mwananchi to "save more." They want us to believe that if we just suffer a little longer, the debt will vanish. But in this city, we know that the only thing that vanishes is the money meant for the very roads he says he cannot afford to build. Don't be fooled by the humility; the begging bowl is still out, and you are the one expected to fill it.
