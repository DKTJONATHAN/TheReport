Everyone is acting like Afrobeats just fell from the sky because Wizkid and Burna Boy finally got some Westerners to pay attention. They love to cite Fela Kuti like he was a saint, conveniently forgetting he was actually fighting the system these modern stars now worship. Today, "challenging conventions" just means seeing how many luxury logos you can fit into a three-minute music video while the rest of us struggle to buy fuel. It is a manufactured revolution where the jazz and funk roots have been replaced by auto-tune and a desperate need for a Billboard mention.

Then we have The Headies, supposedly Africa’s most prestigious platform. Please. It is basically a yearly reminder of who has the most aggressive PR team and the loudest, most toxic fan base on Twitter. We watch these "artistes" act like a plastic plaque is the Holy Grail, only for them to ditch the local scene the moment a Grammy nomination whispers their name. It is all about international traction now, which is just code for "I am too big for Lagos but not big enough for Coachella." The sound of modern Nigerian music is being defined by whatever will play well in a London elevator.

The industry loves to brag about billions of streams and global influence, but [Gravity Always Wins While The Elite Fly Over Our Mess](/posts/gravity-always-wins-while-the-elite-fly-over-our-mess) as these celebrities jet-set across the globe while their fans deal with the reality of a crumbling economy. Olamide just hit 1.5 billion streams on Spotify, which is impressive until you realize how much of that digital "wealth" actually stays in the hands of the people who built the genre. We are the ones buying the data to keep them trending, but they are the ones reaping the "global influence" from the comfort of a private lounge.

And do not even get me started on the desperation for attention. We have DJ Pretty Play trying to spin records for 288 hours just to get into a book of records that nobody actually reads. Why? Is the music not enough anymore? Now we need marathons and "world-firsts" just to stay relevant in a news cycle that moves faster than a Rema remix. Even Fido is announcing a European tour for April-May like he is the second coming, but we all know half these "tours" are just glorified club appearances in front of twenty cousins and a promoter who still owes them money.

We are told Afrobeats represents our cultural identity, but it feels more like a commercial product packaged for the highest bidder. The foundations were built by groups like The Remedies and Plantashun Boiz, who actually had to work for their fame without the help of a viral TikTok dance or a Chinese-owned streaming farm. Now, anyone with a catchy hook and a good ring light can "conquer new frontiers." It is not a revolution; it is just a very loud, very expensive business meeting that we were never invited to attend.
