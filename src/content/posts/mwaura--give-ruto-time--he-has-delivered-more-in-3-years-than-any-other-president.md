Mwaura: Give Ruto Time, He's "Delivered More" Than Any Other President - Or So He Says

Don't expect any miracles from this lot. Government Spokesperson Isaac Mwaura, fresh from defending the latest Budget Policy Statement, is out here singing President Ruto's praises, claiming he's achieved more in three years than any president before. Meanwhile, the opposition leaders, many of whom were in government themselves just yesterday, are apparently just being "nostalgic" for their old jobs. It's the same old song and dance, folks.

Mwaura's defending the administration against criticism by accusing the opposition of thinking they're the only ones who can unite the country. Apparently, they're all vying for the top spot, pretending to be better suited to lead than Ruto. He even names names - former President Uhuru Kenyatta and former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua - as if that’s news. These are the same people who were running things not too long ago.

But hold on, Mwaura wants us to give Ruto "time to deliver." He points to a stadium in Wajir, some roads, and a runway as proof of "accomplishments." Sounds like standard government projects to me, the kind that always get trotted out when the petrol prices are high and the cost of living is through the roof. And let's not forget the new pension system - supposed to eliminate paperwork and human interference. We'll see how that plays out.

He also mentions that pensions will now be tax-free, a move to "encourage a stronger savings culture." Right. Because what Kenyans really need right now is more pressure to save, not relief from the crushing debt they're already drowning in. It's all about "digitization and improved service delivery," apparently. It’s as if these fancy tech buzzwords can fix the fundamental economic rot. Remember how we talked about [The Engineering of Influence](/posts/the-engineering-of-influence--how-advanced-packaging-design-is-reshaping-the-global-tech-and-retail-sectors)? This feels like a similar kind of repackaging of the same old promises.

Mwaura's message is clear: stop complaining, support the President, and maybe invest in post-retirement medical insurance. Because, you know, quality healthcare is just a click away with the new e-system. It’s a real step forward, he says, into a more digitized future. A future where we can all be assured of timely pension payments and tax-free earnings, while the cost of basic commodities continues to climb. Keep dreaming, Nairobi.






Government Spokesperson Isaac Mwaura, appearing before a Parliamentary committee, decided it was time to lecture the opposition on their criticisms of President William Ruto’s administration. From his perch at Harambee Annex, Mwaura accused these opposition leaders, many of whom were once part of the government themselves, of acting like they’re the only ones capable of uniting Kenya and positioning themselves as superior leaders. It’s the usual script, isn’t it?

Mwaura specifically called out former President Uhuru Kenyatta and former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua, suggesting they’re just "nostalgic" for their time in office. He’s basically telling them to get over it and let Ruto work. His argument? Give the President "time to deliver," because apparently, in his three years, Ruto has accomplished more than any other president. We’ll see if that holds water when the bills come due.

To back up his claims, Mwaura pointed to ongoing development projects, like the construction of a stadium in Wajir - where this year’s Madaraka Day celebrations will be held - along with new roads and a runway. He also highlighted the rollout of a new Information Management System for pensions, set to benefit over 100,000 pensioners. This system, he claims, will eliminate paperwork, reduce human interference, and ensure timely payments.

He went on to say that pensions will now be tax-free, a move intended to foster a "stronger savings culture." Meanwhile, many Kenyans are struggling to afford food, let alone save for retirement. It's a classic case of telling people to eat cake when they're starving. This push for digitization and improved service delivery sounds nice, but let’s not forget how these grand plans often leave the common mwananchi behind. It feels like another instance of [The Engineering of Influence](/posts/the-engineering-of-influence--how-advanced-packaging-design-is-reshaping-the-global-tech-and-retail-sectors) where the packaging looks good, but the contents are questionable.

Mwaura also advised Kenyans to invest in post-retirement medical insurance schemes, framing it as a significant shift toward digitization and better service delivery in the pension sector. It’s all about securing your future, apparently. But for most of us in Nairobi, the immediate future looks less about medical insurance and more about how to survive the next price hike. We're just waiting for the next grand announcement that promises the moon and delivers a dust cloud.
