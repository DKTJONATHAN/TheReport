President William Ruto has stepped in to defend a planned U.S.-backed Ebola quarantine facility at a military air base in central Kenya, painting it as a crucial part of a broader national preparedness strategy and a continuation of a long-standing health partnership with Washington. Last week, Kenya gave the green light to a U.S. request to establish this facility at a military air base in central Kenya for Americans potentially exposed to Ebola.

However, residents and local leaders in Nanyuki, near the base, voiced their dissent with a protest on Monday. Their primary concerns revolve around the risk of community exposure to Ebola and a general questioning of why Kenya should host a quarantine center specifically for U.S. citizens. This local outcry follows a wave of public criticism and legal action aimed at halting the project.

Speaking out for the first time about the facility, Ruto asserted that it's not an outlier and is comparable to other similar facilities already operational within Kenya. "The facility that is at Laikipia Air Base is not a facility different from all the other facilities that we have across Kenya," Ruto told reporters late on Monday, imploring Kenyans to have faith in the government's preparedness. This comes as the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda are grappling with the rare Bundibugyo strain of the Ebola virus, with 48 deaths reported and the WHO declaring it a public health emergency of international concern, a crisis the global response has been slow to contain.

Ruto revealed that he approved the facility after a request from U.S. President Donald Trump, citing decades of cooperation with Washington on health programs spanning HIV/AIDS, Ebola, and COVID-19. He further elaborated that Kenya has prepared isolation, surveillance, and treatment facilities in 23 counties, and this new facility would serve both Kenyans and foreign partners, including Americans, should the need arise. The president dismissed the criticism, framing it as Kenya's duty to prepare for any potential Ebola cases, including those among Kenyans working in the Democratic Republic of Congo.

Adding to the controversy, a court last week issued a temporary suspension of the plan following a lawsuit arguing the site could pose a public health risk. Ruto, however, did not acknowledge the existence of this court order. Adding fuel to the fire, a U.S. military C-130 transport plane was observed flying into Nanyuki as recently as Friday afternoon, and two Nanyuki residents reported seeing military aircraft heading towards the base over the weekend. While Reuters couldn't confirm if these were U.S. aircraft, the sightings have undoubtedly heightened anxieties. "We are a responsible government. We know what we are doing," he concluded, a statement that, given the ongoing protests and legal challenges, might resonate more with a healthy dose of cynicism than with reassurance for many Kenyans.
