The pursuit of Olympic gold is the most expensive form of national vanity, and Charlotte Bankes has just provided the latest invoice. Despite 26 World Cup gold medals and a resume padded with world titles, Bankes proved once again that high-performance funding cannot buy mental fortitude. Her quarter-final exit in the snowboard cross at the Milan-Cortina Games is not a "hoodoo" or a string of bad luck; it is a recurring failure to perform when the stakes move beyond the insulated circuit of professional snowboarding.

Bankes’ excuse of "struggling with the track" is the standard rhetoric of an athlete who has been found out. We are told she is back to full fitness following a collarbone injury, yet she looked off the pace from the start. This is the reality of the British snow sport program: a series of expensive "almosts" and "nearly-theres." Two fourth-place finishes for Kirsty Muir and Mia Brookes are celebrated as triumphs of spirit, but in the cold math of global athletics, fourth place is simply the first loser. 

This pattern of underperformance extends to the ice. The British curling rinks, supposedly the world’s top-ranked specialists, are currently engaged in a slow-motion collapse. Bruce Mouat’s rink lost to Italy, a team they should have controlled, while the women’s team is winless after two matches. Their upcoming schedule against Canada, Sweden, and Switzerland suggests that their Olympic journey will end in a quiet flight home long before the medal rounds.

The public is expected to swallow these defeats with a smile, ignoring the massive state investment required to keep these athletes in the "world-class" bracket. As noted in the analysis of [Buying Loyalty with Our Grandchildren's Taxes](/posts/buying-loyalty-with-our-grandchildren-s-taxes), the diversion of public funds into the vanity projects of the elite is a hallmark of modern governance. We are mortgaging the future to pay for the "feel-good factor" of a gold medal that never arrives.

Bankes says she feels "lost." It is a fitting sentiment for a program that prioritizes the aesthetics of participation over the brutal necessity of winning. The Livigno Snow Park is not a "cruel" venue; it is a neutral auditor of talent and nerve. Thus far, Team GB’s accounts are deep in the red. The wait for a snow medal continues, not because of bad luck, but because the hype of the British sports machine has once again outpaced the reality of its results.
