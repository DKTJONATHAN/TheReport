So, Otile Brown has decided to grace us with his pearls of wisdom, lamenting the endless stream of missing children in this beautiful, crumbling country. Apparently, leaders are too busy counting their millions to notice the little ones disappearing. He’s even gone and found a body in an abandoned house. Shocking. It’s almost as if this entire society is a sinking ship and we’re all just bobbing along, hoping for a miracle. News flash, Otile: this isn't new, and your emotional outburst won't magically fix a morally bankrupt system.

He’s waxing lyrical about how "no one is truly safe in a morally broken society." Groundbreaking stuff. And his own kids, eh? Of course, it’s always about "our children" when it hits home. Meanwhile, the rest of us are just supposed to accept that this is the legacy we’re building - a nation where children vanish and leaders remain silent. It’s the Kenyan way, I guess. Why bother with accountability when you can just release a sad song and call it a day?

Then we have Octopizzo, jumping on the bandwagon. "Institutions must be held accountable," he declares from his X perch. As if anyone’s surprised. When the systems meant to protect us are about as effective as a sieve in a flood, of course, questions need asking. But who’s really listening? The same leaders who are probably busy discussing their next offshore account. This isn't about weak systems; it's about a deliberate rot from the top, a rot that reminds me of how the tobacco industry targets our youth - a hustle that shows how easily vulnerable populations are exploited when profit trumps people. [TIMR-Kenya Report: Tobacco Industry's Social Media Hustle Targets Kenyan Youth](/posts/timr-kenya-report--tobacco-industry-s-social-media-hustle-targets-kenyan-youth)

The truth is, this is just another Tuesday in Kenya. We’ve got musicians and rappers pointing out the obvious - that our country is going to the dogs and our leaders are whistling past the graveyard. They’re reminding the powers that be that their own children aren’t immune. Well, that’s a comforting thought. Perhaps if they cared about *all* children, not just the ones with their last name, we might see some actual action. But I wouldn’t hold your breath.

This whole charade is just another reminder that while we’re busy mourning the lost, the real problem - the systemic corruption and apathy - continues to thrive. These leaders, the ones Otile and Octopizzo are calling out, are the same ones who push grand, empty promises like the [24-Hour Economy](https://www.capitalfm.co.ke/business/2024/05/23/the-24-hour-economy-is-a-mirage-and-your-subscription-is-a-ransom-note/) - a mirage that distracts from the rot beneath. Until they face real consequences, and not just public shaming from celebrities, this cycle of fear and loss will continue.

So, yes, Otile, the leaders are silent. Octopizzo, the systems are weak. And we, the citizens, are left to navigate the chaos. What else is new under the Nairobi sun? Maybe next week, someone else will get emotional about something equally predictable. In the meantime, keep your kids close, and your leaders even further away.
