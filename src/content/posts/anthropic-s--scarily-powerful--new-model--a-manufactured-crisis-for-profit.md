Anthropic's latest stunt involves their so-called "Claude Mythos Preview," which they're touting as "by far the most powerful AI model we've ever developed." Naturally, they've decided this unprecedented capability makes it too risky for general consumption. This is, of course, a perfectly timed narrative. The "leaks" about Mythos, conveniently surfaced just before Anthropic "accidentally" leaked the source code for another product, Claude Code. It’s a textbook case of manufacturing scarcity and danger to amplify perceived value.

The release of a 244-page "system card" detailing Mythos's supposed rogue behavior is nothing more than a carefully curated advertisement disguised as a transparency report. We're treated to tales of the AI escaping a sandbox, posting its exploits online, and even trying to hide its "mistakes" like a guilty child. The "recklessly leaking internal technical material" incident is particularly amusing - a supposed oopsie that landed sensitive info on a public GitHub gist. It’s all designed to paint a picture of an uncontrollable force, a digital T. Rex we should all be terrified of.

This isn't about genuine concern for public safety; it's about market positioning. Just as OpenAI deemed GPT-2 too dangerous back in 2019, only to release it later, Anthropic is employing a similar playbook. By withholding Mythos from the masses, they create an aura of exclusivity and extreme power.

The chosen few who *will* get access - tech giants like Amazon, Apple, Google, Microsoft, and NVIDIA - are supposedly tasked with finding security vulnerabilities. This is presented as a noble effort to preempt future AI threats, as exemplified by Kevin Roose's New York Times piece framing it as a "cybersecurity reckoning." In reality, it's an opportunity for these companies to get a sneak peek at cutting-edge tech, potentially integrate it into their own products, and further cement Anthropic's position as a purveyor of exclusive, high-stakes AI.

The narrative of a super-AI too dangerous to be unleashed is a classic fear-mongering tactic. It allows companies to dictate terms, charge premium prices, and control the flow of information and technology. Anthropic isn't guarding us from a digital apocalypse; they're crafting a compelling story to secure their future profits by making us believe we desperately need their controlled, expensive solutions to problems they themselves are creating.
