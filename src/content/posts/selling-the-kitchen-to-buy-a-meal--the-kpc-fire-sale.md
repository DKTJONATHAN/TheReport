The courts have spoken, and as usual, the little man is left holding a bag of empty promises. Justice Lawrence Mugambi decided he could not stop the train because it had already left the station, or rather, the privatisation was already listed for mention. It is the classic Nairobi shuffle: by the time you realize your pockets are being picked, the thief is already halfway to the bus station. Okiya Omtatah and his team tried to argue that this whole KPC sale is just a puppet show directed by the IMF, but the law seems more interested in jurisdiction than in who actually owns our infrastructure.

We have seen this script before, where the state pretends to listen while already signing the checks in backrooms. This latest move feels like [The Sh80 Billion Trojan Horse: How Nairobi Just Got Sold Back to the State](/posts/the-sh80-billion-trojan-horse--how-nairobi-just-got-sold-back-to-the-state) all over again, where development is just a fancy word for selling the country back to ourselves or to the highest bidder. Omtatah's claim that this is anti-sovereign is probably the only honest thing said in a courtroom all week. But in a country where the cost of living is a permanent headache, sovereignty is a luxury we apparently cannot afford.

Even more hilarious is the fact that Uganda has already jumped on the bandwagon before the ink on the court ruling was even dry. Minister Ruth Ssentamu is already celebrating securing a strategic stake in our pipeline. It is quite the irony: while we are told we need to sell KPC to survive, our neighbors are rushing in to buy it because they know it is a gold mine. We are basically selling our kitchen to the neighbor and then paying them to cook our dinner. It is a brilliant strategy if your goal is to be a permanent tenant in your own house.

The government wants to offload 65 percent of KPC for a measly nine shillings a share. They are calling it an opportunity for Kenyans, but we all know how these public offers go. The big fish will swallow the ocean, and the rest of us will be left with the salt. They expect to raise 106 billion shillings to fix a hole in the budget that they probably dug themselves. It is not an investment; it is a fire sale.

At the end of the day, the March 18 hearing is just a formality. The Privatisation Act has already been cleared by other judges, ensuring that the government can keep auctioning off state-owned enterprises without that pesky thing called parliamentary authority. We are watching the slow-motion dismantling of the state, one pipeline at a time, while being told it is for our own good. Welcome to the new Kenya, where everything is for sale and the public is just an audience watching the auction.
