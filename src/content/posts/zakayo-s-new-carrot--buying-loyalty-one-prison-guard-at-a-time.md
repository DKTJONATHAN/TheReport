So, the man from Sugoi went to Ruiru to play Santa Claus again. While the rest of us are wondering if we will even afford unga tomorrow, President William Ruto was busy promising a 36 percent salary hike to the guys who hold the keys to the cells. It is the classic Kenyan political move: feed the hounds so they do not bite the hand that keeps them on a leash. The Maraga Taskforce report is the new holy grail, apparently, cited every time the state needs to look like it cares about its "indispensable" enablers of peace.

This whole charade about "welfare and motivation" is just a fancy way of saying the government is scared. They know the ground is thin, and the last thing they need is a disgruntled prison service. It reminds me of [Boni’s big reveal: as if we didn't know the state was already in our pockets](/posts/boni-s-big-reveal--as-if-we-didn-t-know-the-state-was-already-in-our-pockets), because every shilling they "give" to these officers is just another shilling squeezed out of a frustrated mwananchi. We pay for the handcuffs, the bread, and now the increased salaries of the people who might eventually have to process us for "incitement."

The math looks good on paper, I will give them that. A 36 percent jump for entry-level constables and a 24 percent overall scale growth sounds like a dream. But in this economy? By the time that July 1 paycheck hits the account, the price of sugar and fuel will have already done a marathon. A 36 percent raise in a country where the currency is doing gymnastics is like bringing a spoon to a flood. It feels nice for a second, but you are still going to drown.

Then there is the talk of "Phase Three" coming in July 2026. 2026! That is practically a lifetime away in this "hustler" kingdom. Promising money two years from now is the ultimate gaslight. It is a carrot dangled so far in the distance that you need a telescope to see it. It is designed to keep the officers hopeful and compliant through whatever chaos the next two years will bring. It is not a policy; it is a retention strategy for a regime that is losing friends fast.

Ruto calls these officers a "key pillar" of national security. I call them the ultimate insurance policy. As long as the people in uniform are fed, the people in suits can sleep soundly in Karen. They talk about "humane and secure custody," but we all know the drill. This is about making sure the gates stay locked and the guards stay loyal while the rest of the country wonders when the "bottom-up" miracle is actually going to reach the bottom.
