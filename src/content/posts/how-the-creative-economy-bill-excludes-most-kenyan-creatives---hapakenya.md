So, They Think They Can Pull a Fast One on Kenyan Creatives Again?

This so-called "Creative Economy Bill" is nothing but a slap in the face, a greasy handshake disguised as a helping hand. They promise the moon, but deliver a dusty film studio to the chosen few while the rest of us get the scraps.

Another Shoddy Piece of Legislation That Excludes Most Kenyan Creatives

BELINDA ACHIENG: Creative Economy Bill 2026 is a sham, all flash and no substance for most Kenyan artists.

SCREEN

The twilight of Kenya’s legislative calendar is apparently the perfect time for politicians to pull their usual stunts. They’ve thrown this Creative Economy Bill, 2026, at Parliament, all dressed up in fancy words about liberation and cultural powerhouses. But let’s get real, Nairobi. This isn't about liberating anyone; it's a calculated move to funnel resources into one sector, leaving the rest of us to fend for ourselves. They promise a feast for every artist - musicians, painters, writers, designers - but when you peel back the layers, it’s just an audiovisual buffet, and everyone else is told to find their own table.

Clause 2 and Clause 5(1) might sound inclusive on paper, listing all the arts under the sun. But don't be fooled by the theatrical language. The real story is in Part II, where they’ve conjured up two shiny new bodies: the Kenya Audio-Visual and Cinema Commission and the Kenya Audio-Visual Regulatory Authority. And then there’s the Kenya School of Film and Creative Arts. Notice a pattern? It’s all about the screen. Every budget line, every job, every regulation is pointed squarely at film. What about the musicians packing out stadiums? The theatre groups that keep our communities alive? The fashion designers whose work puts Kenya on the global map? They’re left high and dry, with no institutional champion to speak for them in this so-called law. It’s just like how they tried to control what we saw and heard before.

This whole mess smells suspiciously like the colonial laws they claim to be replacing. The old Films and Stage Plays Act was already too narrow, and this new bill just modernizes that narrow-mindedness. It creates an institutional structure so focused on film that it actively sidelines everyone else. Kenya’s creative economy is huge, employing hundreds of thousands across countless disciplines. The music industry alone pulls in billions, yet it gets no dedicated support here. Theatre, the heartbeat of our towns, is lumped in with a movie production. Visual arts, crafts, fashion - sectors that could genuinely boost rural economies and create jobs for our youth - are treated like afterthoughts in a bill that claims to champion *the* creative economy. We've seen this playbook before, from how they guard cement gains to how they talk about progress.

Kenyan artists aren't fools. We can spot legislative poetry from a mile away - the grand pronouncements, the vague promises. But we also know legislative practice: the narrow mandates, the sector-specific institutions, the cold, hard reality of where the money actually goes. This Creative Economy Bill is no different. Its promise of a real creative economy is a lie. Its promise of support for all creatives is a lie. Its promise of a framework that truly embraces Kenya’s cultural diversity is a blatant lie. The question for Parliament isn't about tweaking this disaster; it's about whether they're going to fall for the same old deception one more time. We deserve better than this patronizing charade. This bill, in its current form, needs to be dumped, not tinkered with.
