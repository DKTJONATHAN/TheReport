So, the fuel prices are up. Again. Up to Sh40 a litre, they’re saying. And shocker, businesses, the ones that have been telling us they’re struggling, are suddenly finding the energy to pass on the pain to us. Maize flour, matatus, cabs - everything’s getting a price hike. It’s like clockwork in Nairobi; one government screw-up, and the common mwananchi gets it in the neck.

And what’s President Ruto’s take on this? Blame it on global forces, of course. Because admitting that maybe, just maybe, some decisions made right here at home contribute to this mess would be too much to ask. It’s always the world’s fault, isn't it? Never mind that this diesel price jump is going to make everything, including the air we breathe, feel more expensive.

Suppliers are already whispering that May to August consignments will be even pricier. This isn’t just a temporary sting; it’s a long-term economic assault. And while we’re all scrambling to figure out how to stretch our meagre earnings, there’s talk of scandals threatening the Sh106 billion Kenya Pipeline Company privatisation. Sounds about right. When things get tough for us, they get profitable for someone else, maybe even linked to the very people claiming it’s just “global forces.” It’s a familiar dance, and we always end up on the wrong beat.

This constant cycle of economic pain is exhausting. It makes you wonder if any of the grand plans, like the Sasini Avocado Processing Plant Sale signalling Nairobi Strategy Chaos, are actually for the benefit of anyone but a select few. The promises of progress feel hollow when the daily reality is just about survival. It’s like we’re stuck in a loop, and the only thing that changes is how much less we can afford.
