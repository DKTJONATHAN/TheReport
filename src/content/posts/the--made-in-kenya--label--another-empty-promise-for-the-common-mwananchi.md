So, we've got this "Made in Kenya" label. A packet of tea sits there, looking innocent. Outside, a bus built right here grinds through the Nairobi traffic, probably belching fumes and taking twice as long as it should. We’re meant to feel proud, right? Like we’re building something great. But let’s be real, when you look around at all these "Made in Kenya" items - the juice, the shoes, the steel rod for some half-finished building - what do you see? You see prices that make your eyes water and quality that makes you wonder if it was made by someone who’s never actually used the product.

CS Mbadi is out here defending some new tax plan, saying it’s to "ease traders' burden." Ease whose burden? Certainly not mine, when I’m trying to stretch my salary to cover rent and ugali. It sounds like another way to shuffle money around, making promises about simplifying things while ensuring the same old middlemen get their cut. This whole song and dance about taxes and trade feels like a broken record, much like the promises of prosperity we’ve heard before. It makes me think of that time we were sold a vision of a trillion-shilling pipe dream, and where did that get us? Nowhere fast.

Then there's this Sh1.4 billion award handed to some Peter Nthei Muoki in a copyright dispute over Safaricom’s M-Pesa. So, someone's getting rich off an idea that's meant to help ordinary Kenyans. Brilliant. Meanwhile, Rebecca White’s low-cost system that could actually save lives is gathering dust. Apparently, potential to save lives isn't as profitable as legal battles over mobile money wallets. Classic.

And let's not forget the entertainment industry. Ronnie Nyauchi, rubbing shoulders with Hollywood bigwigs who’ve worked on "Iron Man" and "Think Like a Man." It’s a nice story for him, I guess. But for the rest of us, the "Made in Kenya" label on our own creative output often feels like an excuse. CKay himself pointed out how "Afrobeats" can be a lazy label, exposing the industry's sound snobbery. Maybe we're just good at slapping labels on things, both our products and our music, without really understanding what's underneath.

Ultimately, this "Made in Kenya" label is supposed to be a badge of honour, a sign of progress. But when you’re staring at the cost of living and the endless cycle of empty promises, it starts to feel like just another marketing ploy. It’s a nice thought, but in reality, it often translates to more expenses for us and more profits for the few. We’re just expected to nod along, buy the local stuff, and hope for the best, while the real issues remain conveniently ignored.
