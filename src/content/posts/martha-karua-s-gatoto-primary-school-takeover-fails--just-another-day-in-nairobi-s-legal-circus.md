Well, isn't this a shocker? Another day in Nairobi, another attempt to privatize a public good goes sideways, leaving 'dignitaries' scrambling for their vehicles. Gatoto Community Primary School in Mukuru kwa Reuben was the latest battleground, where a 'rowdy crowd' - read: angry locals - decided a High Court order wasn't quite as powerful as their collective will. Martha Karua, bless her heart, lasted a grand total of two minutes before finding herself on the receiving end of a very clear message. It's almost poetic, isn't it? One minute you're a big shot, the next you're fleeing a mob like some [street hero reduced to an apartment puppet](/posts/from-street-hero-to-apartment-puppet--the-short-reign-of-bradley-marongo).

The whole charade was supposed to be a 'handover ceremony,' meticulously planned, even with a judge's directive. Justice Gregory Mutai, from the comfort of his chambers, had ordered the school be given back to 'private non-profit' owners, complete with police security paid for by the petitioners. Because nothing says 'non-profit' like needing a court order and a police escort to claim your noble institution. Felix Mwangani, a former board chairperson, whined that 'people who are not parents said that they were paid to come and make sure that the takeover is not successful.' Imagine that - ordinary folk having their own agenda, or perhaps, being used as pawns, just like always.

The private owners, naturally, were up in arms, accusing the police of being 'lenient.' As if officers are supposed to start a riot just to enforce a paper decree against a determined community. Makadara OCPD Judy Nyongesa, ever the pragmatist, dismissed the claims, stating the school was 'in operation as usual' and they didn't want 'commotion with learners.' Conveniently, she also mentioned the school was 'commissioned by President William Ruto' as a public school. So, one government branch makes it public, another court orders it private. Pure Kenyan bureaucracy at its finest - a beautiful mess designed to keep everyone guessing, and the lawyers busy.

At the heart of this predictable circus are the actual parents, the ones who actually *need* the school. They've filed an appeal, stating plainly that 'Parents of these children from the vast Mukuru kwa Reuben slums cannot afford to pay fees charged by private schools.' No surprises there. While the 'non-profit' private owners promise to 'continue pushing' and swear converting it to public would 'jeopardise education,' we all know whose education is truly jeopardized when a free school becomes a for-profit venture, no matter how many fancy labels they slap on it. This isn't about education; it's about ownership, power, and another piece of land in a city that's run out of it.
