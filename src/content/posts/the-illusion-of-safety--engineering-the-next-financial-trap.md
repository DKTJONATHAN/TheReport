The rhetoric of "grit" and "resilience" that Chang espouses is the standard liturgy of the modern financial elite. By ranking intelligence last in his list of success factors, he inadvertently reveals the nature of the current economic era. Success is no longer about deep understanding or systemic stability. It is about the "influence" required to sell a dream and the "grit" to keep selling it when the underlying reality begins to crumble. This is not the wisdom of a builder, but the survival manual of a middleman.

Vest specializes in buffered ETFs, products designed to protect investors from a certain percentage of market losses while capping their potential gains. On the surface, this looks like a prudent hedge for a nervous global middle class. In practice, it is the financialization of fear. These products rely on complex derivatives that link the fortunes of retail investors even more tightly to the health of the options market. When the next systemic shock arrives, these "buffers" may prove to be nothing more than psychological placebos that fail exactly when the "grit" of the market is tested.

Chang’s experience during the 2008 crash, where he watched the repo markets freeze and the "hubris of finance" reveal itself, should have been a warning. Instead, it served as a blueprint for how to profit from the next wave of instability. By wrapping derivatives in the user-friendly skin of an ETF, firms like Vest are exporting Wall Street’s complexity to the global masses. They are building a world where no one actually owns an asset; they only own a "defined outcome" managed by a specialist who takes a fee regardless of whether the shield holds.
