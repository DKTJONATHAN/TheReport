The modern romantic relationship is a fragile social contract constantly undermined by the pressure of individual fulfillment. When journalist Eve Simmons describes being blindsided by a divorce after nine years of investment, she is not describing an anomaly. She is describing the logical 

Relationships fail because the participants are encouraged to treat them as consumption goods rather than rigid institutions. The suggestion to use "I" statements and schedule "time outs" treats domestic life like a corporate mediation session. These methods do not fix underlying structural incompatibilities; they merely provide a polite vocabulary for the eventual dissolution of the household. If you have to use a script to talk to your partner, the authenticity of the bond has already evaporated.

The concept of the "tipping point" mentioned by psychotherapists is particularly revealing. It suggests that people do not leave because of a moral or ethical breach, but because they have performed a subconscious cost-benefit analysis. Margot Davis, cited in the source material, left her partner of three years because his personality did not fit her "vision" of parenthood. This is not a tragedy of the heart; it is a strategic pivot. It mirrors the trend seen in other regions where the economic cost of splitting is too high, leading to the phenomenon of /posts/unspoken-divorce-middle-class-kenyan-couples-splitting-living-together where the contract is dead but the cohabitation continues for survival.

Furthermore, the obsession with "not settling" is a byproduct of a dating market that promises infinite upgrades. When experts warn against being "lonely in a relationship," they are weaponizing the fear of missing out. The advice to "let yourself deserve the love you want" is a consumerist mantra. It ignores the reality that long-term stability requires the sacrifice of the very "spark" and "intellectual challenge" that people are told to demand. 

Ultimately, the "blindsided" partner is usually the one who believed in the permanence of the contract, while the one who leaves is the one who realized the market value of their freedom had finally surpassed the comfort of the status quo. These are not failures of communication; they are the inevitable results of a culture that prioritizes the "flying" individual over the grounded pair. In a world of total autonomy, every relationship is "sub-par" compared to the idealized fantasy of the next one.
