So, the 2027 presidential contest is already starting to look like a bad rerun, isn't it? President Ruto and his former deputy, Gachagua, are at each other's throats, and it’s not about policies or fixing this broken economy. No, it’s all about personal grievances, broadcasted on every screen like a cheap soap opera. They call it “political engagement.” I call it a spectacular waste of time when people are struggling to put food on the table and wondering if their country is actually going to stay afloat.

The way these two are going at it is unsettling, not because they disagree - that’s how democracy is supposed to work, in theory. But these public spats are less about national direction and more about settling scores. For a country still reeling from the Gen Z protests that exposed just how fed up people are with governance and economic pressure, this kind of noise is like pouring petrol on a smoldering fire. We haven't forgotten the violence of 2024, and nobody needs a reminder of how quickly things can go south.

And let’s not even get started on the regional implications. The shadows of 2007-2008 are long, a stark reminder of how political disputes can erupt into something far uglier. The Rwandan genocide is still a chilling lesson about the dangers of rhetoric that divides and dehumanizes. These aren't abstract history lessons; they're woven into our collective consciousness, and our leaders ought to remember that when they open their mouths. Instead, they're busy with petty squabbles, making it easier for the truly extreme voices to get louder.

This is a massive missed opportunity. Kenya is at a critical juncture. We're drowning in debt, youth unemployment is sky-high, and public trust in institutions is lower than a snake’s belly. Citizens need concrete answers, not endless drama. They're looking for a clear path forward, a sense of "bankable hope," as they say, not more uncertainty. It’s a shame, because leaders have shown in the past that they can rise above the fray for the good of the nation.

But history repeats itself, and often, it's just as bad, if not worse. It feels like we're stuck in a loop, watching the same old script play out. Remember when we talked about the mismanagement at JKIA? [Welcome to JKIA: The World’s Most Expensive Parking Lot](/posts/welcome-to-jkia--the-world-s-most-expensive-parking-lot). It’s the same old story of incompetence and misplaced priorities. This current political spat is just another symptom of the rot.

Ultimately, the responsibility lies with those who want to lead. They need to elevate the conversation, not drag it through the mud. The stakes are too high for anything less. This isn't just about individual ambitions; it's about the very cohesion of our nation and the stability of a region that desperately needs better leadership than this.
