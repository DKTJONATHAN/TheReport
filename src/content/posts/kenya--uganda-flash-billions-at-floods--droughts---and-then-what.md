So, Kenya and Uganda have suddenly remembered that the sky isn't always blue and decided to splash out billions on climate-related budgets for the 2026/27 financial year. Kenya's throwing Ksh124.8 billion ($960 million) at the problem, and Uganda's chipping in Ush494.08 billion ($133.5 million) plus a disaster fund. It’s a nice headline, isn’t it? "Pouring billions!" sounds impressive, like they're actually doing something substantial. But let's be real, this is just rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic.

Remember last year when Kenya actually *cut* its climate spending, even as the drought got worse and the floods went wild? Now they're suddenly "restoring climate spending to a higher priority." Mbadi, the Treasury Cabinet Secretary, is talking a big game about climate change being "embedded across development sectors," which is just fancy talk for spreading the blame. He’s allocating a chunk to water infrastructure - Ksh51.5 billion for water and sewerage, Ksh6.3 billion for resource management, and Ksh2.5 billion for flood control. It sounds good on paper, but Nairobi’s informal settlements still flood every other rainy season. We've seen this movie before, and it doesn't end well.

Uganda's also getting in on the act, focusing on restoring forests and wetlands. They've even got a massive Ush361.88 billion contingency fund. Minister Musasizi is all about "restoration and resilience," promising to protect forests and demarcate riverbanks. But then you’ve got experts pointing out the obvious: having all the climate data in the world doesn't mean squat if you can't actually *act* on it. This is the same old song we hear across Africa. We know the problems, but the capacity to actually *do* something before disaster strikes? That's where things always fall apart. It’s like these leaders are more interested in the announcements than the actual implementation, a problem we’ve seen surface even in discussions about whether [Kenya's New Bangkok & Jakarta Envoys are more than just pretty faces amidst scam scandals?](/posts/kenya-s-new-bangkok---jakarta-envoys--more-than-just-pretty-faces-amidst-scam-scandals).

And then there's Kenya's grand experiment, the Financing Locally-Led Climate Action (FLLoCA) programme. Ksh8.9 billion directed to counties for adaptation projects. Mbadi calls Kenya a "leader in designing innovative," benefiting "more than 2.5 million people." But the cracks are already showing. Governance and accountability issues are rampant, and counties are bogged down by capacity gaps and delays. Counties have to prove they’ve done the work *before* they get the money. It’s a classic case of wanting to look good without the actual groundwork.

Even with all these billions, the reality on the ground is grim. Droughts persist in Kenya’s arid regions, floods drown urban settlements, and human-wildlife conflict is still a nightmare, pushing animals into communities and leading to more losses. The Wildlife Research and Training Institute is practically screaming about habitat loss and the desperate search for resources. In Uganda, people are still being displaced by floods. They're investing in climate science and space technology, but experts warn that without better infrastructure and resilient housing, all that fancy monitoring is just a high-tech distraction. The IPCC reports are crystal clear: vulnerable regions are hitting the limits of adaptation, and Africa, heavily reliant on rain-fed agriculture, is right in the firing line.

The most we can say is that climate change is no longer a side issue; it's "embedded" everywhere. But this integration means responsibilities are scattered across so many agencies that it's a miracle anything gets done at all. As Pascal Murasira from the African Food Fellowship put it, "Climate action is happening across government, but not always in a coordinated way." And guess what? Both countries are still heavily focused on reacting - cleaning up the mess after the fact - rather than preventing it. They're institutionalizing recovery as a permanent part of governance. They talk a good game about changing the system, but the execution? That's where it all breaks down.
