So, the fuel prices went up. Big surprise. And just as predictably, our beloved matatu Saccos and even the boda bodas are already doing what they do best: making your wallet cry. Capital Business’s little ‘spot check’ found fares on key routes nearly doubling. Rwaka to the city, which used to cost you a measly Sh70, is now Sh100, or if you’re unlucky, a whopping Sh120. This isn't a surprise party, people; it’s just Nairobi being Nairobi.

And it's not just the urban chaos. The long-distance folks are getting it too. ENA Coach has already slapped on new prices - Sh2,000 for Nairobi to Mombasa. That’s an extra Sh200 to Sh300 you’ll be digging out of your pockets. Transline's even more honest, admitting prices to Kisii might change by evening, but you can bet your last shilling they’ll be higher by tomorrow. It’s a constant game of “how much can we squeeze before they walk?”

Even the boda bodas are feeling the heat, with drivers complaining clients are opting to walk. Good for them, maybe. But for the operators, it’s a sign of what’s coming. They have to raise their rates, of course. Because why should they be the only ones feeling the pinch? This whole system feels like that one time Gor Mahia couldn't even hit a million in gate collections, but somehow the fans always end up paying more for everything else. [K’Ogalo’s Broke Era? Gor Mahia Fails to Hit KSh 1M Gate Target as Fans Ghost Nyayo Stadium]

The Matatu Owners Association President, Albert Karakacha, confirmed it all. Operators nationwide are adjusting. It’s all thanks to EPRA, who decided petrol needed to cost Sh206.70 a litre and diesel Sh206.84. Apparently, global oil prices and tensions in the Middle East are the perfect excuse to fleece us. Never mind the government’s ‘measures’ like VAT reductions and subsidies; those are just window dressing for the inevitable.

Karakacha even admits the burden falls on us, the commuters. And it’s not just transport. Diesel and petrol are the blood of this economy. So, expect everything else to go up too. It’s a domino effect, and we’re the first ones to fall. The global supply disruptions around the Strait of Hormuz? Just another chapter in the same old story.

Honestly, what did you expect? They’ll keep finding reasons, and we’ll keep paying. It’s the Nairobi way. The government throws us a tiny bone, the global markets do their thing, and then the matatu guys and bus companies look at us with those innocent eyes and tell us, "Sorry, but you have to pay more." It's a cycle, and we’re stuck on the hamster wheel.
