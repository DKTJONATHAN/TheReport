So, Justice Njoki Ndung'u is off to the International Criminal Court. Nominated by Kenya, no less. This little maneuver by CJ Koome, which will eventually land on President Ruto's desk for a rubber stamp, conveniently opens up yet another seat at our esteemed Supreme Court. And we all know how smoothly things run when there's a quorum problem, don't we? Apparently, the death of Justice Mohamed Ibrahim wasn't enough drama. Now we're looking at a potential third vacancy before the 2027 elections. Because what Kenya needs is more uncertainty, especially when it comes to the highest court in the land.

The ICC elections are in December 2026, so she's got some time. But the implications are immediate. With an uneven number required for rulings to avoid those awkward deadlocks, one judge has to recuse themselves from *every single case* until this is sorted. It's a classic Kenyan solution: create a problem, then marvel at the "innovative" way we manage the fallout. They say there's no crisis, that the law "prescribes what to do." I say the law is being used to shuffle deck chairs on a sinking ship.

This whole exercise smacks of the same old song and dance. We’ve seen how easily things can devolve into chaos, whether it’s mid-air brawls on planes or the endless pursuit of profits over genuine progress, as seen in places like the collegiate meat grinder. The Supreme Court, meant to be the pinnacle of our legal system, is turning into a revolving door. And for what? A bit of international prestige? Meanwhile, back home, the wheels of justice are grinding, perhaps a little slower now.

And let's not forget the other hopefuls from the High Court and Appellate Court. They're all vying for a shot at this ICC gig, or perhaps the vacant seat back home. It’s a bit like a reality show, isn't it? Who will get the golden ticket? Who will be left holding the bag? The focus is always on the next appointment, the next potential move, rather than on the actual functioning of the institution itself. It's all about the optics, the appearances. Just like a flashy photo-op can hide a much grimmer reality, a judicial nomination can mask deeper systemic issues.

The law might say there's no crisis, but the reality is that a Supreme Court constantly in flux, with judges leaving and new ones being considered, doesn't inspire much confidence. We're rearranging the furniture while the house is showing cracks. Before the 2027 elections, this bench will have shifted so much, it'll be unrecognizable. And the irony of it all? We’ll probably call it progress.
