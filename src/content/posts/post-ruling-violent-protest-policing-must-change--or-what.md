POST RULING VIOLENT PROTEST POLICING MUST CHANGE, OR WHAT?

The Kisumu High Court's ruling, doling out Sh38.6 million to victims of police brutality during the "Sufuria" protests, is being hailed as a victory. But let's be real, a splash of cash doesn't erase the trauma of being beaten senseless or watching your neighbours bleed out in the street. It confirms what we already knew, what human rights groups and even your local imbazi vendor have been whispering about for ages: the police went on a rampage, and it was as brutal as it was unlawful.

Imagine this: you're minding your own business, maybe heading for a plate of ugali, when the riot squad decides your gate needs kicking down. They storm in, drag you out, and proceed to introduce your ribs to their boots. In the streets, it's the same story - unarmed civilians, kids, everyone getting a taste of live bullets or batons. People you know are collapsing, and there's nowhere to go, no one to help. Later, you're left scrambling through overcrowded wards or identifying cold bodies in the morgue. Shattered jaws, torn intestines, limbs amputated - all on an ordinary day.

So, 28 people, some lucky enough to survive with gruesome injuries and others grieving lost loved ones, decided enough was enough. They dragged the Police Inspector General to court, proving that the force under his command, despite his attempts to claim ignorance by not producing operational documents, acted with extreme prejudice. The Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA), which is supposed to be the watchdog, also apparently had its systems "down," because that's a common excuse for inaction. Years later, these memories are still fresh, a constant reminder that the people paid to protect you turned into your tormentors.

Now, the court orders the Attorney General to cough up Sh38.6 million. Families get a paltry Sh910,000 to Sh1.4 million, and the survivors get up to Sh4.87 million. It's a pat on the back, I suppose, but does it really make up for a shattered life? The Interior Ministry is being told to "develop clear regulations" for managing assemblies and police force. This is like telling a raging inferno to "be a bit more mindful of the surroundings." The court will check up on them in July, no doubt to another round of excuses. It’s all a bit like that baffling scientific distraction about Antarctica’s ‘Blood Falls’ - a lot of noise, little substance in the end.

Justice Alfred Mabeya deserves a nod for wrapping this up in two years, a feat that probably felt like trying to [achieve twelve guns and a trillion shilling pipe dream](posts/twelve-guns-and-a-trillion-shilling-pipe-dream) in this country. The Law Society of Kenya, KNCHR, Katiba Institute, and Amnesty International also deserve credit for their affidavits. The ruling itself, which points out that the working-class neighbourhoods of Nyalenda, Nyamasaria, and Kondele deserve the same protection as the wealthier ones, is a crucial statement. It confirms that dignity isn't reserved for those living in Milimani or Riat.

But here's the kicker: compensation is still pending, and frankly, it's less than what you'd get for a fender bender. This judgment might set a precedent, but it's a weak one. Will settlements be delayed? Will investigations into specific officers and commanders magically get buried? We’ll see. As we head towards the 2027 elections, the police are already gearing up. Let's hope this ruling acts as more than just a stiff warning, because history shows us that the "change" promised after such incidents often evaporates faster than a puddle in the August sun.
