Ruto: Itare Dam Issues Resolved, Again. Or So He Says.

Here we go again. President Ruto, fresh from hobnobbing with the Italians in Rome, is flapping his gums about the Itare Dam project. Apparently, the "issues" are "resolved." This is the same song and dance we've heard before, a familiar tune played every time someone needs to look like they're fixing things. It's all about strengthening ties and boosting trade, naturally. Because what screams "economic cooperation" more than a decades-old water project riddled with graft and stalled more times than a broken-down matatu?

After a chat with his Italian counterparts, Ruto declared the "long-running standoff" settled. This "standoff," mind you, is code for a colossal financial mess, a black hole where billions of Kenyan shillings vanished. They've apparently "settled" with the contractor, CMC di Ravenna, and pulled out of arbitration. You know, instead of actually digging into the mess and finding out where all that money went, they just decided to wave a magic wand and make it disappear. This is the same old playbook, just with a different coat of paint. Remember when we talked about how Kenyans pay twice for basic services? This is a prime example. The State House spokesperson, Hussein Mohamed, chimed in about "Memoranda of Understanding" and "cross-sector agreements." Itare, Arror, and Kimwarer are now being framed as part of a grand irrigation strategy. Expansion to 2.5 million acres? Food security? Agricultural exports? Sounds lovely, doesn't it? It's a nice story, but the reality is these dams are the lingering ghosts of Uhuru Kenyatta's presidency, a financial scandal that just won't quit. They paid billions upfront, and what do we have to show for it? A half-finished dam and a whole lot of unanswered questions.

The Sh45.5 billion Itare package, arranged way back in 2014, was supposed to be a marvel. Then came the arrests of Treasury CS Henry Rotich and others over alleged Sh55.8 billion losses. CMC di Ravenna, the contractor, went bankrupt and then tried to squeeze Kenya for another Sh12 billion. Ruto's administration has now opted for settlement, a nice way of saying they've swept it all under the rug to avoid a proper investigation. Itare is reportedly only 27 percent complete. It's a classic Kenyan situation: promises made, money spent, and the public left holding the empty bag.

So, construction on Itare and Arror is supposedly resuming, while Kimwarer remains a question mark. The talks in Rome were all about securing financing and structures robust enough to move these projects forward and, crucially, "outlast the shadow of the scandal." Good luck with that. Italy is a "strategic partner," and Kenya is the "gateway of choice" for Africa's growth. We're investing in AI and digital infrastructure. It’s all very grand and forward-thinking. But until these dams actually hold water and benefit the people, it's just more hot air from politicians on a foreign jaunt.







President William Ruto has declared that the persistent problems surrounding the Itare Dam project have finally been ironed out. This announcement came hot on the heels of a high-level meeting in Rome, aimed at bolstering bilateral relations and expanding trade and investment between Kenya and Italy. Following discussions with Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni and President Sergio Mattarella, Ruto stated that the long-standing impasse, fueled by legal and financial disputes that had stalled the project for years, has been settled. The agreement includes a commitment to revive cooperation on the project, with reports indicating the Kenyan government has reached an accord with contractor CMC di Ravenna, leading to the withdrawal of arbitration cases.

"I’m also very happy that finally we have resolved the issues that were outstanding on some of our projects, Itare project for one. Now we have agreed on how we are going to implement this project going into the future," Ruto announced during his three-day visit to Italy. State House Spokesperson Hussein Mohamed confirmed that several Memoranda of Understanding and cross-sector agreements are anticipated, with discussions focusing on accelerating the Arror, Kimwarer, and Itare dam projects. These are now being positioned by the Ruto administration as integral to its broader irrigation strategy, designed to increase irrigated land, enhance food security, and boost agricultural exports.

However, these dams are far more than mere items on a diplomatic agenda; they represent unfinished business from one of the most significant financial scandals of Uhuru Kenyatta's presidency. They are also a litmus test for whether Italian-backed financing can return to large-scale Kenyan public works without unearthing past malfeasance. The contracts were awarded to a joint venture involving CMC di Ravenna and Itinera. The Sh45.588 billion (€306) million Itare package, arranged in 2014, involved SACE, Intesa Sanpaolo, and BNP Paribas. Arror and Kimwarer in Elgeyo-Marakwet County followed shortly after.

By September 2019, the project had imploded. Kimwarer was abandoned and Arror scaled back following a technical review that highlighted irregularities. Subsequent investigations led to the arrest of then Treasury Cabinet Secretary Henry Rotich and 15 others over alleged Sh55.8 billion losses. CMC di Ravenna subsequently filed for bankruptcy protection in Italy and initiated arbitration proceedings at the International Court of Arbitration in The Hague, claiming approximately Sh12 billion in damages. Ruto’s administration has opted for a settlement over a prolonged legal battle, approving terms that resolve both the commercial contracts and financing agreements, with the contractor withdrawing its case in return.

This settlement is the focal point of the current visit. Kenyan reports preceding the Rome trip indicated that Itare was only 27 percent complete, while Arror and Kimwarer had seen minimal substantive work despite substantial upfront payments. Now, construction on Itare and Arror is slated to resume, though Kimwarer remains stalled due to technical concerns. The talks in Rome are expected to secure financing, implementation, and insurance structures robust enough to advance these projects and, critically, to transcend the shadow of the scandal. It's a familiar narrative of grand promises and potential pitfalls.

Italy, a key EU member, remains a crucial partner for Kenya in renewable energy, security cooperation, and development financing. Italian Prime Minister Meloni stated that the two nations have agreed to consolidate their cooperation under a three-year action plan, describing Kenya as "one of the most dynamic economies" in Africa. She noted increasing interest from Italian businesses in Kenya's technology, industry, tourism, and agro-processing sectors. President Ruto emphasized that the visit builds on over six decades of diplomatic ties, prioritizing value addition, job creation, and digital transformation, positioning Kenya as a "gateway of choice" for Africa's growth. He highlighted eight agreements signed during the visit, including grants for climate adaptation, forest restoration, peace training, and research and innovation, alongside deals in the leather sector, ICT, defense, and transfer of sentenced persons.
