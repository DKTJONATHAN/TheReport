For the players who chased the blood money, the reckoning will be particularly bitter. Men like Jon Rahm and Bryson DeChambeau, who traded their reputations for generational wealth, now find themselves holding tickets for a sinking ship. The international impact is clear: the disruption of the global golf calendar was a temporary fever dream. While markets in Korea, Australia, and South Africa may have enjoyed a fleeting moment of relevance, they were merely pawns in a larger game of soft power. As the money dries up, these regions will likely be abandoned as the elite seek refuge back in the protective arms of the PGA Tour.

Reintegration will not be the warm embrace some defectors expect. The PGA Tour leadership and its remaining loyalists have a long memory and a thin skin. The legal battles and the public insults have created a rift that a simple apology cannot fix. Those who led the charge to sue their former colleagues will find the road back paved with heavy fines and professional humiliation. It is a classic tale of corporate warfare where the foot soldiers are the ones who pay the ultimate price when the generals decide to call a truce.

Ultimately, the LIV experiment proved that golf is not immune to the laws of supply and demand. The world did not want more professional golf; it wanted the prestige and history that the Saudis tried to bypass with a checkbook. The real winners are the few dozen players who managed to pillage the PIF coffers before the gates closed. The losers are the fans who watched their sport fractured for three years, and the international venues that were promised a new era of global competition that was nothing more than a mirage in the desert.

This collapse mirrors the broader trend of society discarding its high-cost experiments once they no longer serve the elite.
