So, our President William Ruto, bless his well-intentioned heart, has decided to grace us with a Sh10 reduction on diesel for the next two months. Ten bob. In a country where a matatu fare can jump by more than that with a stern look, this is meant to be a lifeline? It’s less a stabilisation programme and more a polite nudge to the cliff edge. While he’s busy patting himself on the back for saving us from a hypothetical Sh278 per litre, let’s not forget that the cost of living is still biting harder than a hungry mosquito at a lakeside resort.

The big man’s solution to our escalating global fuel woes? Electric vehicles. Apparently, the 3,000 electric vehicles ordered for government officials will somehow shield the entire economy from oil shocks. It sounds like selling the kitchen to buy a fancy new kettle, a strategy that never ends well. The president’s explanation about Iran and global oil shocks feels like yesterday’s news; we’ve heard it all before. What we haven't heard is a concrete plan that addresses the immediate pain of millions.

Ruto claims the government has spent Sh28.19 billion on subsidies and tax relief. That's a lot of money, supposedly cushioning households and businesses. Yet, every time I fill up my tank, it feels like the cash is evaporating faster than a mirage in the Turkana desert. The government-to-government fuel import framework, touted as a dollar-saver, seems to have done little to stop the relentless march of prices.

And now, we’re also getting regulations on digital taxi fares. Because the real problem in Nairobi traffic isn’t the gridlock or the shoddy roads, it’s those Uber drivers charging a few extra shillings. It’s almost as if they’re trying to distract us from the fact that our fuel import costs have skyrocketed by over 100% for diesel and kerosene. This whole EV push feels like another shiny object, a way to look proactive while the real issues fester. We need more than just a Sh10 discount and a fleet of Teslas for the VIPs to fix this mess.
