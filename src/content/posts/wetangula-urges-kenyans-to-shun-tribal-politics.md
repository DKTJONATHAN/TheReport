Wetangula Tells Kenyans to Ditch Tribal Politics, Or Else











So, Moses Wetangula, the National Assembly Speaker, was in Narok, doing the usual politician thing: telling us to stop being tribal. He says our future hinges on leaders and citizens - yes, *citizens* - remembering the national interest over who our parents are. He was at a fundraiser, apparently, where Sh5 million was magically raised. Imagine that. Progress, he claims, comes from peaceful coexistence and respecting democratic competition. Funny, because what I see more often is shouting matches and rigged elections.

He’s worried about divisive, ethnic maneuvering fracturing cohesion. Duh. And he wants leaders to focus on development, service delivery, and youth empowerment instead of all this "political noise." Bless his heart. He actually thinks our ethnic diversity should be a strength, not a weakness. True leadership, according to him, is uniting the country. I’d settle for a leader who just delivers on basic services without stealing us blind. He even told us not to be confused and drawn into tribal politics. As if we have a choice.

Then he went and defended the Kenya Kwanza administration's "development record." And, because no speech is complete without the "Singapore comparison," he brought that up too. Apparently, Singapore left us behind due to "consistent policies, development, hard work, and a positive attitude." Right. Because Kenya's problems are solely due to our lack of a positive attitude and not, say, endemic corruption and the kind of political squabbles that make even the most basic business ventures seem like navigating Slaps, Screams, and Shoddy Scripts.

Wetangula also wants us to believe that President Ruto deserves a second term to fulfill his promises. And he’s all praises for the government's "empowerment programmes" for businesses, urging us to ignore the critics. He’s adamant that Kenya isn’t for one community and politicians pushing tribal agendas should be ignored. He's also proud of this "broad-based government" that includes opposition folks. Apparently, this is meant to make every Kenyan feel "seen and valued." It’s a shame this feeling doesn't translate into lower cost of living or fewer potholes.

He even had the audacity to condemn politicians "hiring goons" to disrupt meetings. He thinks they should use their own sons and daughters, not "innocent children." It’s a nice thought, but let's be real, those "innocent children" are probably the same ones benefiting from the "empowerment programmes." He's confident about Kenya’s economic future, crediting Ruto for laying the foundation. And he reminded us that Kenya belongs to all of us, and every community has an equal right to produce leaders. Which is great, but it doesn't pay the bills, does it?

And of course, he couldn't forget the women. He praised them for being "catalysts for positive change." The Rift Valley leaders echoed his sentiments, saying they won't support ethnic division. They even thanked Ruto for "inclusive leadership." It's all a beautiful song and dance, isn't it? But while they're busy singing about unity and progress, the rest of us are just trying to survive.
