Christine Keni, a 22-year-old computer scientist by degree, is loudly proclaiming she's "betting everything" on becoming a great musician. "No in between," she declares, as if life ever works in such clean, dramatic lines. It's a bold statement, especially from someone who just secured a degree in computer science - a field Business Insider projects offers a median annual pay of over $135,000. Most Kenyans would kill for that kind of fallback, but Keni insists money had "nothing to do" with her choice. Convenient, isn't it, when you have a cushy option waiting if the music thing doesn't quite pan out?

She admits external pressures pushed her into a "reasonable degree," and being good at math and physics made computer science an easy pick. "Almost made me lose my mind but it was fun," she says, which sounds like most people's work experience, not some grand passion. Her musical awakening, fueled by Taylor Swift and Sauti Sol, came with the usual lament about "colonisation" making Kenyans believe local music isn't good. It's a popular excuse for a lack of exposure, and Sauti Sol, apparently, had to "legitimize African artistry" for her to even consider it. The search for "tangible evidence" of success from other Kenyan artists sounds less like a burning passion and more like a calculated market analysis.

Her big "break" wasn't some organic discovery of raw talent; it was born out of boredom during COVID-19 lockdown, making YouTube vlogs because she "didn't have anything else to do." One viral video later, she's a "freelance content creator" and applying digital marketing strategies to her music. She's quick to point out that "being an artist" means doing the "boring stuff" - distribution, marketing, performances. It's a very pragmatic, almost corporate, view of art, stripped of any real romance. "Willing to do the work," she says. Well, if you want to make money from it, of course you are.

Keni claims money isn't a motivator, yet she's quick to point out that she's "seen some of the best cars in this country drive into that music studio." So much for the romantic "starving artist" trope. Of course, the lack of it is a "hindrance" - her "big ideas" always have to fit the "shape of my budget." Welcome to the real world, dear. Despite this glaring reality, she's convinced "the best thing you can do is chase your dreams." It's a common refrain, especially from those who, unlike the majority struggling daily, have the luxury of choice. For many, chasing dreams is simply a pipe dream when the immediate concern is putting food on the table, a reality starkly highlighted in how [the Constitution frees us, or more like it chains us back to old ways](/posts/constitution-frees-us--more-like-it-chains-us-back-to-old-ways).

She's now working on an EP, promising "authenticity" and songs that "provoke" emotion. If you don't "feel anything," there's a problem, she says. Perhaps the problem isn't always with the listener, but with the artist's ability to connect beyond the marketing hype. Her advice to fellow creatives - "willing to put yourself out there, willing to learn and willing to fail" - sounds good on paper. But it rings a bit hollow when you know she's got a highly employable computer science degree tucked away, ready to catch her if she actually *does* fail. It’s easy to be fearless when you have a safety net.
