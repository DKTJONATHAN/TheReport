President William Ruto is back at it again, standing in the marble halls of State House and telling us that the European Union is our "strategic partner." He is touting the new Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA) as a "new chapter," but for anyone who has survived more than one election cycle in Nairobi, it sounds like the same old story. We are being told that duty-free access to European markets is a gift, when in reality, it is just a way to ensure their supermarkets stay stocked with cheap roses and tea while our own people struggle with the cost of unga.

The catch, as always, is hidden in the fine print of "stringent quality requirements." Ruto talks about value addition and standards compliance like they are easy hurdles, but these are the same moving goalposts the West uses to reject our goods whenever they feel like it. It is a rigged game where the rules are written in Brussels. This reminds me of how [The Semaglutide Shakedown: Why Corporate Monopolies Always Win](/posts/the-semaglutide-shakedown--why-corporate-monopolies-always-win) proves that the big players always hold the cards while the rest of us are left to scramble for the crumbs they drop from the table.

We are obsessed with "broadening global markets" for our farmers, but we never ask why those farmers are still living in poverty after decades of exporting to the EU. We export raw coffee and tea so that Europeans can roast them, package them, and sell them back to us at a premium. Ruto's talk of "aligning with global value chains" is just fancy talk for staying at the bottom of the food chain. We provide the soil and the sweat, and they provide the profit margins.

The promise of jobs for the youth is the most tired trope in the government's playbook. Unless "jobs" means working in pesticide-heavy flower farms for a wage that cannot even cover a matatu ride to the city, there is nothing here for the young people of this country. They want tech hubs and manufacturing, not more opportunities to be manual laborers for a continent that views us as a backyard garden.

While the EU provides "funding and technical support," let us not pretend it is charity. Every cent of that "development cooperation" is designed to keep us dependent and compliant. While Ruto and Ambassador Geiger review "ongoing cooperation programmes," the average Kenyan is wondering how a trade deal with Europe is supposed to lower the price of electricity or fix the broken healthcare system. It is all optics and no substance for the man on the street.
