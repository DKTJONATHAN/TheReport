THE "MIDDLE CLASSNESS" Mirage: Nairobi Realist Believes Kenya Is Stuck











The road from Kiganjo to Nairobi used to be a journey. Now, thanks to the endless tarmac and the new towns popping up like mushrooms after rain, it’s just a longer, more congested crawl into the city. They talk about a "middle classness" blooming everywhere, from Mandera to Mombasa, but let’s be real. It’s mostly just more people scrambling to keep their heads above water, clinging to digital loans and saccos just to afford school fees. The numbers might look pretty, but the reality on the ground is a lot less rosy.

Look at the household budgets. You've got the bottom tier spending nearly all their cash on food, one slip and they're in the ditch. Then there's the middle tier, the ones supposedly "making it." They’re living hand to mouth, one unexpected expense away from disaster. This isn't "middle classness," it’s a tightrope walk, and most of them are one gust of wind away from falling. The "upper income" folks? They're the ones actually benefiting, insulated from the daily grind, while the rest of us are left feeling like we're running on a treadmill that’s speeding up.

And let's not even start on the "informal" economy. Sh50 trillion moving through mobile money annually, yet we still label millions of hustlers as "employed" when they're just trying to survive. It’s a joke. This isn't some quaint "informal" sector; it's the engine room for many, and it's largely untaxed, leaving the formal sector to carry the nation's financial dead weight. It makes you wonder if this whole system is just designed to keep the powerful comfortable while the rest of us chase shadows. It’s a similar feeling to when you see politicians promising the moon and then delivering dust, like Orwoba accusing Ruto of being a smart con man on campaign pledges.

Inflation is the real thief in the night, eating away at any meager gains. Food, transport, housing - these are the basic necessities that are now a luxury for too many. They talk about economic revival and growth figures, but for most Kenyans, per capita income has been stagnant or declining for decades. The global health crisis might have reversed things temporarily, but the underlying problems of inequality and a struggling economy were always there, just waiting to pounce.

Yes, the per capita income has technically gone up since the dark days of the 80s and 90s. From $102 in 1960 to a whopping $2,363 now. But that average is a lie. It’s skewed by the few at the top who are raking it in, while the majority are barely treading water, if they’re not drowning. This isn't a reason to believe; it's a reason to be angry, to demand real change, not just prettier roads and more "middle class" illusions. We need to see the crude reality stinging the sensex, not just a pretty headline.
