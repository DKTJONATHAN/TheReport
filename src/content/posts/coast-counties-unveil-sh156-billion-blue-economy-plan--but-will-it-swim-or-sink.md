Coast Counties' Sh156 Billion Blue Economy Plan: More Hot Air Than Ocean Breeze?

This plan is the latest attempt by the six coastal counties under the Jumuiya ya Kaunti za Pwani (JKP) to charm investors and citizens with a Sh156 billion ($1.2 billion) "blue economy" initiative. Touted as a leap towards sustainable growth by 2030, it dangles promises of investment in fisheries, ports, maritime transport, tourism, and green energy. Launched at the Our Ocean Conference in Mombasa, it's being billed as a grand orchestration of national and county governments, regulators, private players, and development partners. But let's be real, how much of this is genuine commitment and how much is just another grand pronouncement to fill airtime?

The JKP chair, Major (Rtd) Dhadho Godhana, claims this is a product of "wide community and institutional engagement." Dr. Edwin Odera, an economist and technical advisor, elaborates on the financing: a hefty 35% from the national government, 20% from county budgets, a mere 20% from development partners, and the rest - well, that's for the private sector to fill. It's a classic Kenyan script, really. We lay out a big plan, get the government to cough up some money, and then hope the big boys with deep pockets will swoop in and do the heavy lifting. It’s like [Selling the Kitchen to Buy a Meal: The KPC Fire Sale](/posts/selling-the-kitchen-to-buy-a-meal--the-kpc-fire-sale) - a lot of talk about restructuring while the real problems fester.

To sweeten the pot, they’ve even conjured a "blue-green bond" instrument targeting $400 million. The Capital Markets Authority and the Nairobi Securities Exchange have apparently given it their blessing. Dr. Emmanuel Nzai, the JKP CEO, assures us that global finance "always trusts a bond framework." That’s a convenient narrative, isn't it? Make it sound official, add some fancy financial jargon, and suddenly everyone's supposed to believe the money will flow in. We’ve heard this song and dance before, especially when it comes to grand projects.

The plan itself is a laundry list of buzzwords: sustainable fisheries, maritime transport, coastal tourism, environmental sustainability, renewable energy, and industrialisation. It covers all the usual suspects from Mombasa down to Taita Taveta, including the prized Mombasa port and the LAPSSET corridor. Nzai proudly states it's timed to conclude with Kenya Vision 2030, putting them in "countdown mode." The International Maritime Organisation's push for net-zero emissions is also a convenient hook, with Kenya already exploring green hydrogen production. It’s always about aligning with global trends, isn't it?

Dr. Odera boasts that this model avoids the "pitfalls of externally-driven development projects," where plans are made without local input and failures are blamed on the locals. He claims this is a "real transformation of the bottom-up economic transformation agenda." Let's see if this "people's plan" actually trickles down or just ends up lining the pockets of the usual suspects. The ultimate goal, apparently, is to consolidate these regional plans into a single national framework. More bureaucracy, more committees, more meetings - the Kenyan way.

So, here we have a Sh156 billion plan for the coast. It’s got all the right ingredients: government buy-in, private sector allure, international finance aspirations, and a healthy dose of buzzwords. Whether it translates into actual tangible progress for the coastal communities or just becomes another chapter in Kenya's long history of ambitious but ultimately underperforming development projects remains to be seen. Until then, it’s just another multi-billion shilling promise on the horizon.
