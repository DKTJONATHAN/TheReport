Welcome to Nairobi, where your identity is apparently a negotiable asset. The latest scandal at the immigration department is not a bug; it is a feature of a system designed to serve the highest bidder. We love to act surprised when we find out foreign nationals and criminals are walking around with Kenyan passports, but let's be real. If you have enough cash and the right connections, you can become a citizen before your lunch order arrives. It is not an administrative failure - it is a thriving business model.

The international community is watching, and they are definitely not impressed. When our state documents become as reliable as a street-side watch, the big players like the US and UK start tightening the noose. This entire mess feels like a localized version of [The Clinton Contract: A Transactional Blueprint for Power](/posts/the-clinton-contract--a-transactional-blueprint-for-power), where institutional integrity is traded for short-term gain. We are selling our global mobility for a few pieces of silver, and the bill is going to be delivered to the regular mwananchi.

Of course, the people who will actually suffer are not the ones selling the booklets or the criminals buying them. It is the student trying to get to London for a degree or the entrepreneur heading to Dubai. They will be the ones stuck in secondary screening, treated like suspects because their document is now a massive red flag. The privilege of a Kenyan passport is fast becoming a liability that requires a five-hour interrogation at every border crossing.

We keep shouting about being a regional hub and a gateway to Africa. But who wants to enter a gateway where the security guards are selling duplicate keys to anyone with a bribe? Security cooperation is built on trust, not just handshakes and press releases. If our identity management system is compromised, we are not a strategic partner; we are a security risk. No amount of diplomatic PR is going to fix the look of suspicion on an immigration officer's face at Heathrow.

In the end, this is just Nairobi being Nairobi. We have institutionalized graft to the point where national security is just a buzzword used to justify more budget allocations that will also be stolen. We do not need reforms or task forces that go nowhere. We need to admit that the system is working exactly how the corrupt intended it to work. Until then, keep your bags packed and your explanations ready, because your passport is currently worth less than the paper it is printed on.
