If you thought the Sh3.5 billion Anglo-Leasing ghost was finally being laid to rest, you clearly haven't lived in Nairobi long enough. The Court of Appeal just hit the pause button for the Kamani brothers, proving once again that if you have enough zeros in your bank account, the wheels of justice don't just turn slowly - they stop for a lunch break that lasts a decade. It is the same tired routine we see every time a major graft case gets too close to the sun.

While the big fish swim free, the rest of us are treated to the usual political theater. We are stuck in a cycle of [Same Script, Different Date: Nairobi’s Annual Near-Miss with Disaster](/posts/same-script--different-date--nairobi-s-annual-near-miss-with-disaster) where the actors change but the plot remains depressingly predictable. Whether it is a multi-billion shilling scandal or a flooded highway, the response is always a committee meeting and a promise that things will be different next time.

Speaking of meetings, the Mt Kenya leaders are gathering on Friday to "discuss the issue." We all know what that means. It is never about the common person; it is about who sits at the high table and who gets to hold the knife. Meanwhile, Fred Matiang’i is out here guarding votes that haven't even been cast yet, acting like his word is gospel. It is funny how everyone becomes a saint of democracy the moment they are out of the immediate inner circle.

President Ruto is making noise about internal rivalries and government sabotage, trying to sound like the captain of a ship that isn't leaking from every corner. But how can you talk about order when six governors can simply ignore a summons from the County Public Accounts Committee? In this city, a summons is basically a "save the date" card that you can RSVP "no" to without any real consequences.

The reality is that the system isn't broken; it is working exactly how it was designed to. It protects the elite, frustrates the taxpayer, and keeps the scandals on a loop until we all get bored and move on to the next outrage. From the Kamanis to the governors dodging questions, the message is clear: if you are going to steal, steal enough to buy the delay. Justice is just a commodity, and right now, the price is too high for the likes of you and me.
