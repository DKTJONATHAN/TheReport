So, Nairobi is still "booming" according to some. Skylines are rising, estates are expanding, and the sound of mixers is the soundtrack to our lives. We're told this is progress, this concrete and steel symphony. It’s all built on cement, of course, and the not-so-secret ingredient, clinker. Apparently, for years, our local clinker production was a joke, barely covering half our needs, forcing us to beg for imports. Every shipment was a drain on our precious forex, leaving us at the mercy of global price hikes. Classic Kenya.

Then came the Finance Act of 2023, with its Export and Investment Promotion Levy (EIPL). Suddenly, we’re meant to be producing our own clinker, becoming some sort of regional supplier. We’re told installed capacity has grown, drawing in cash, creating jobs, and even bringing in revenue. We’ve supposedly gone from begging for imports to exporting to Uganda, South Sudan, Rwanda, and Tanzania. Sounds nice, doesn’t it? A real feel-good story for the papers.

But now, some bright spark wants to remove that 17.5 percent cement levy. The argument? To lower costs. Sounds good, right? But let’s be real. This is Kenya. This is about who gets what. Removing the levy means we're wide open to dumping from countries that subsidise their energy and production. It’s a race to the bottom, and local investors, who sank real money into those clinker plants with their long payback periods, will be left holding the bag. Stalled expansion, job losses, and a return to begging for imports are the likely outcomes. It's like we’re addicted to repeating the same mistakes. Remember the Great Shamba Scam? [The Great Shamba Scam: Why Kenyan Farmers are Destined to Fail](/posts/the-great-shamba-scam--why-kenyan-farmers-are-destined-to-fail), same playbook, different sector.

If we go back to importing clinker, our precious foreign currency will disappear faster than a politician’s promise. Reserves will be pressured, the exchange rate will take a beating, and our contribution to GDP will shrink. The "price stability" they crow about will vanish, replaced by the whims of global shipping costs, fuel prices, and whatever geopolitical nonsense is happening elsewhere. We’ll be back to being puppets of the global market, and don’t think for a second the price at the pump will actually drop for the common mwananchi.

The choice is simple, really. We either keep our value, our skilled jobs, and our supply chains at home by producing clinker locally, or we send it all abroad by relying on imports. The US and South Africa, bless their sensible hearts, have protections in place. They don't let everyone stomp all over their local industries. We, on the other hand, seem determined to invite the wolves in, just so someone can make a quick buck by trading in imported dust.

This whole thing is about protecting gains. Gains for who? For the people who are already lining their pockets? For the government that collects its cut? Or for the wananchi who just want a decent roof over their heads without breaking the bank? If we lose this, it’s not just an industry that suffers; it’s everyone. But hey, what do I know? I'm just a cynical realist in Nairobi.
