Papa Wa Roma himself, Moses Wetang-ula, was at the Supreme Court on Monday looking very solemn. He was there to "close the judicial file" of the late Justice Mohamed Ibrahim. It is the kind of theatre Nairobi elites love - long robes, expensive suits, and a lot of English words that mean absolutely nothing to a Kenyan trying to survive on a casual wage. They called Ibrahim a protector of the Constitution and a man of integrity, which is funny coming from a political class that treats the law like a buffet where they only pick what they like.

Wetang-ula had the audacity to quote Martin Luther King Jr., saying that injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere. Someone should tell him that in this city, we know the truth better than anyone else. You can talk about the law being a fortress for the vulnerable all you want, but the reality is that [30 Years to the First World? We Can’t Even Cross the Road Without a Bribe](/posts/30-years-to-the-first-world--we-can-t-even-cross-the-road-without-a-bribe) is our daily bread. Closing a file does not mean the system is working; it just means one more person who actually knew the law is out of the way.

The late Justice Ibrahim actually paid his dues, unlike many of the people who sat in the front row of his memorial. He was detained without trial back in the day for standing up for democratic governance and reform. Now, the same types of people who would have signed his detention order if they were in power back then are the ones singing his praises. It is the classic Kenyan way - we kill your spirit while you are alive and then build you a monument once you are safely in the ground. They celebrate his "composed temper" because a quiet judge is a judge they can easily ignore.

They made a big deal about him being the first representative of the Somali community to be admitted as an advocate in 1983. It is a nice milestone for the history books, but let us be real for a second. Inclusivity in the legal profession is just a shiny sticker on a rusty kanjo bus. We have many "firsts" in high places now, yet the average person from a minority group still gets profiled at every roadblock from Eastleigh to Elwak. Representation does not equal results when the entire structure is designed to serve the few at the top.

So, the file is officially closed and the dignitaries have gone back to their tinted V8s and security details. Chief Justice Martha Koome talked about the "symbolic reminder" of their responsibility, but symbols do not feed the hungry or jail the corrupt. They will keep calling the Constitution a "living document" until they finally manage to choke the life out of it for good. Justice Ibrahim has left the stage, and honestly, looking at the state of our courts today, I cannot say I blame him for checking out.
