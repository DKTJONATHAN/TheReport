So, Érick Valencia Salazar, a co-founder of the ever-so-charming Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG), has apparently decided to play ball with Uncle Sam. He’s pleaded guilty to the thrilling charge of conspiring to distribute a whopping 5 kilograms or more of cocaine. The US Department of Justice, beaming with pride, announced this monumental victory. Valencia Salazar, or "El 85" as he’s fondly known on the streets, was nabbed by the Mexican army last year and then, with much fanfare, bundled off to the United States along with 28 other alleged luminaries of organized crime.

The Drug Enforcement Administration, in its infinite wisdom, declared that Valencia Salazar helped mold CJNG into a "ruthless organization" that weaponizes violence, turning Mexico into a slaughterhouse for territorial gains and America into a dumping ground for narcotics. Riveting stuff. His moment of reckoning, or rather his sentencing, is set for July 31st, where he’ll likely face a minimum of 10 years behind bars. A harsh punishment, no doubt, for a man who simply facilitated the flow of poison that decimates communities.

Let’s not get too excited. The CJNG, as we all know, is hardly a one man show. It’s a hydra, a sprawling, adaptable beast that thrives on the very system designed to contain it. The recent chaos unleashed across 20 Mexican states after rumors of its leader, "El Mencho," meeting his demise, merely underscores the cartel’s resilience and the widespread panic its operations can instigate. One arrest, one guilty plea, is a drop in the ocean of illicit commerce. It’s a convenient narrative for the DOJ, a tangible win to present to a public demanding action. But will it fundamentally alter the landscape? Will it stem the tide of drugs? Unlikely. This is merely the rotation of personnel in a business that has proven remarkably resistant to disruption, a testament to the enduring power of greed and the inherent failures in combating such deeply ingrained criminal economies.
