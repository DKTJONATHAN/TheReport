Omondi Tells Governors: Stop Crying, Go to DCI or EACC, Not the Cameras

Another day, another drama from our esteemed governors. This time, Suba South MP Caroli Omondi, bless his sensible heart, is telling these county chiefs to stop their public wailing and actually take their "extortion" claims to the people who are supposed to investigate them. You know, the DCI, the EACC. Apparently, just shouting accusations in front of cameras isn't quite cutting it. Who would have thought?

Now, the Council of Governors (CoG) has decided to get official about their little spat with the Senate. They've named four senators - Moses Kajwang, Edwin Sifuna, Samson Cherargei, and Johnes Mwaruma - claiming these folks are extorting and bullying them during those oh-so-important oversight sessions. It's a classic Nairobi show, isn't it? Accusations fly, reputations get dragged through the mud, and meanwhile, the actual work of governing probably takes a backseat to all this theatre. It's enough to make you wonder if we're living in a political soap opera, a topic we've touched on before regarding [the global distraction factory](/posts/the-global-distraction-factory--why-modern-media-is-a-terminal-regression).

The governors are so fed up, they've suspended appearances before Senate committees. They want these four senators out, claiming the sessions have become "political witch-hunts." One governor, Muthomi Njuki, tried to clarify that it's not the Senate as an institution they have a problem with, but rather these specific four senators. Right. Because a whole committee, filled with people who are supposed to be checking on public funds, suddenly becoming a den of extortionists. Sounds about plausible in this city.

And it's not just the Public Accounts Committee. The governors are also scaling back their attendance at the County Public Investment Committee, complaining about too many summons for the same old projects. Honestly, who has the time to explain their every move when there's fundraising and photo ops to attend to? It's a tough life, being a governor. They even threw in a complaint about bloggers covering Senate proceedings instead of official channels. Apparently, the sanctity of the "august House" is at risk when the wrong people are tweeting.

So, while the governors are busy pointing fingers and boycotting meetings, Omondi is suggesting a more direct approach. Submit evidence, he says. Don't just make noise. It's a novel idea, I know. But if these governors truly have evidence of extortion, then yes, the DCI and EACC are the places to go. Otherwise, it's just another political circus, and frankly, Nairobi has enough of those. We've seen this kind of messy legal and regulatory shakeup before, and it rarely ends with anyone truly held accountable.
