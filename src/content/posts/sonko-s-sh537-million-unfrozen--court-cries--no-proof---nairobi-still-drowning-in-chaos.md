So, the Tax Appeals Tribunal has decided that our dear Mike Sonko, the former governor who’s been facing charges of abuse of office, money laundering, and acquisition of proceeds of crime, can now access his Sh537 million. Apparently, after six years of being frozen, the Kenya Revenue Authority suddenly can’t find enough proof to keep the money locked up. “No proof of crime,” they say. Typical. Meanwhile, the rest of us are left to deal with the very real, very present crimes happening on our streets.

It’s funny, isn’t it? While Sonko’s millions are being unfrozen because the legal system couldn’t pin him down, the illegal parking syndicates are tightening their grip on Nairobi streets, extorting motorists left, right, and centre. That’s a tangible crime, a daily grind for ordinary Kenyans, but somehow it’s the politician’s millions that get the swift, albeit reversed, judicial attention. It makes you wonder where the real priorities lie.

This whole saga feels like a familiar tune. We’re constantly fed grand narratives about continental dreams and economic miracles, like the supposed $3 Trillion Mirage that promises prosperity while Nairobi suffers the ground reality. It’s a dizzying disconnect between the pronouncements from on high and the grimy, everyday struggles we face.

The High Court is busy extending orders to stop the state from selling Safaricom shares, another sign of the intricate, often opaque financial dances happening behind closed doors. While these high stakes games are played out, the everyday citizen is left to navigate a city where even finding a legal place to park can feel like a criminal undertaking, a direct consequence of the very rot that allows millions to be unfrozen with a wave of a judicial hand. It's a cycle that never seems to break.
