Darrien Luke is back with a new single called WOZA, and if you listen to his PR team, it is a life-changing moment for South African culture. Born in Chatsworth and raised on the dreams of being a triple-threat, Darrien is now trying his hand at the Amapiano-meets-3-Step wave. It is the classic Durban formula: find a scenic beach, hire a pretty actress like Phindile Ntuli to look interested, and pray the algorithm gods find your dance moves catchy enough for a TikTok challenge. 

The track is supposedly an "invitation" to call love and good energy closer. Honestly, it sounds more like an invitation for brands to notice he can fill a frame. While he claims to be proud of representing his roots within the mainstream Amapiano movement, one cannot help but notice how everyone suddenly becomes a "cultural fusionist" the moment a genre starts printing money. It is a bold move for a South African Indian artist to step into this arena, but let us be real: the space is already crowded with people doing the exact same thing.

Director Keveshan Govender is talking about "capturing a feeling" and "celebrating rhythm in a modern way." This is industry speak for "we did not have a script, so we just filmed Darrien looking soulful against the North Coast." It is all very polished and very safe. In an industry where everyone is desperate for that instant glow-up and a shortcut to the top, it reminds me of how people treat their bodies and reputations these days. Much like [the Ozempic cartels: why your pursuit of a cheap miracle just hit a legal wall](/posts/the-ozempic-cartels--why-your-pursuit-of-a-cheap-miracle-just-hit-a-legal-wall), the music industry is obsessed with the fast track to perfection without wanting to do the heavy lifting of actual innovation.

Darrien says this project was a "labour of love" and that they stayed up late to push boundaries. If "pushing boundaries" means making a dance track that sounds exactly like what is playing in every club from Sandton to Durban, then mission accomplished. He is thanking the crew for braving the heat, but the real heat will be seeing if this track actually survives more than two weeks on a playlist. We will be watching, Darrien, but mostly to see if the "authenticity" you mentioned is real or just good lighting.
