Kenya has finally decided to "digitize" the way it eats our taxes. They call it electronic Government Procurement (eGP), a fancy name for moving the boardroom deals into a cloud where nobody can see the weather. They want us to believe that by replacing dusty files with PDFs, the cartels who have been breastfeeding on public funds for decades will suddenly develop a conscience. It is a classic Kenyan move: if you cannot stop the theft, at least make the interface look world-class so the donors stay happy while the looting continues at high speed.

They are selling a dream to the so-called "hustlers" in Garissa and Turkana, telling them they can now compete with the big boys in Nairobi from the comfort of a cyber cafe. It is a lie. Knowing about a tender is not the same as winning it. The system claims to empower youth and women, but we have seen this script before. Whether it is in business or the corridors of power, the reality remains that [Siasa Ni Vurugu: Why Kenyan Politics Is A Death Trap For Disabled Women](/posts/siasa-ni-vurugu--why-kenyan-politics-is-a-death-trap-for-disabled-women) and those structural barriers do not vanish just because you have a stable internet connection and a login password.

The real joke is the talk about "data exhaust" and "X-rays of spending." They want us to imagine dashboards where red flags blink whenever a tender is rigged. In this country, a red flag is just an invitation for a bigger bribe to turn it green. If the same people who are supposed to be watched are the ones holding the admin passwords, the only "data" we will see is whatever makes them look like saints. A corrupt process conducted at the speed of fiber-optic cables is just more efficient looting, not a revolution.

Then there is the "human layer." They talk about training procurement officers and suppliers like they are teaching children how to read. These guys do not need manuals; they are already PhD holders in the art of the "system bypass." The moment you build a digital wall, they will find a digital ladder or just pay the guy at the gate to leave the back door open. If the system is built by the elite for the elite, it will always be an exclusive club where the entrance fee is a political connection rather than a competitive bid.

Looking ahead, they want to link eGP with IFMIS and the company registry to catch "politically exposed persons." It sounds like a grand plan, but it is just a bigger web for the same spiders. We are being told this is a "living public ledger," but in Nairobi, we know that ledgers can be edited, deleted, or "lost" during a convenient server migration. Until the people behind the screens change, the only thing eGP will deliver is a faster way to empty the national basket before the citizens even realize it is open.
