This is not a localized Canadian problem; it is a global contagion. The Middle East remains the world's primary gas station, and when the pumps are threatened, the cost of existence rises for everyone. We have seen this play out before, most recently in Kenya where energy executives were forced to step down amid a fuel manipulation probe. The script is predictable: chaos in one region justifies price gouging in another, and the consumer is left to foot the bill while being told it is a matter of "supply and demand."

The cynicism of the current era is best reflected in how corporations and governments respond to these crises. While households report cutting back on travel and major spending, tech giants like Alphabet are selling rare 100-year bonds to fund their expansion into artificial intelligence. The message is clear: the elite are planning for a century of dominance while the working class is worried about the price of bread next Tuesday. The shift toward AI is not a promise of efficiency, but a threat to labor power, ensuring that even if inflation stabilizes, the worker's share of the pie continues to shrink.

Governments are also sharpening their tools for domestic suppression as economic discontent grows. In places like Kenya, the state has been exposed for using taxpayer money to fund "keyboard warriors" to silence dissent. This is the dark underside of the global realist's world-view: when the economy fails and prices soar due to war or mismanagement, the state does not fix the problem; it fixes the narrative. They use digital bots and restrictive cybercrime bills to ensure that the frustration of the masses never reaches a boiling point.

The Bank of Canada's hope that the public will view rising prices as a "one-off event" is a desperate gamble. Inflation is not just a mathematical fluctuation; it is a transfer of wealth. As long as the global order relies on volatile regions for energy and central banks continue to print away their debts, the "war on inflation" will remain a permanent, unwinnable conflict. Peace is bad for the bottom line, and a comfortable population is too difficult to control. Expect the fires to keep burning.
