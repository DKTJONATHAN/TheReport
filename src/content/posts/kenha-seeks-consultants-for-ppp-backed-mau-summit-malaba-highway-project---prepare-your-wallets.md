So, KeNHA is once again inviting "consulting firms" - read: well-connected friends - to bid for a study on expanding that dusty stretch from Mau Summit to Malaba. Apparently, it's a "significant step" towards upgrading a "strategic transport corridor." Translation: it's a route where billions of shillings in cargo disappear annually, and the government wants to look like they're doing something about it. This time, they've got the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) footing the bill for the "technical feasibility study." Because that’s exactly what we need - more studies, more money flowing out, and a whole lot of waiting.

The whole thing is supposed to be a Public-Private Partnership (PPP) model. Which, in Kenya, usually means the public gets to shoulder the burden while private entities rake in the profits. They’ll be assessing "technical, environmental, social and financial viability." You know, the usual jargon to justify more spending before they eventually slap on tolls that will make fuel prices look like pocket change. Remember when fuel prices were squeezing everyone? [Transport Operators vs. Govt: Who Will Crumble First? Fuel Prices Continue to Squeeze Kenyans.](/posts/transport-operators-vs--govt--who-will-crumble-first--fuel-prices-continue-to-squeeze-kenyans) This highway expansion is just another chapter in that same old story.

They’re talking about Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS) and improved traffic management. Sounds fancy, doesn't it? What it really means is more cameras, more data collection, and probably more ways to fine you when you're just trying to get to work. And all this talk of environmental and social impacts? Let’s just hope they remember to actually consider them, unlike some other "developments" we've seen around Nyayo House. The whole assignment is slated for 12 months, kicking off in September 2026. So, don't expect any changes anytime soon.

The firms bidding need to prove they've done this kind of "large-scale highway engineering" and "PPP transaction advisory" before. Conveniently, they also mention projects financed by multilateral development banks. This smells like a well-rehearsed play, where the same players get to move their pieces around the board, get paid handsomely for "studies," and we, the ordinary Kenyans, are left with the crumbs and the eventual toll fees. It's the art of the shakedown, Kenyan style.
