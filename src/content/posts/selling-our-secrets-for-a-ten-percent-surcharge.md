So, the usual suspects have flown to Washington to "negotiate" our future. While you are struggling to buy unga, Regina Ombam and her team are sitting in air-conditioned rooms in D.C. discussing a Reciprocal Trade Framework. They call it strengthening bilateral cooperation, but let us be real. Donald Trump already slapped a 10 percent global tariff on everything we try to sell them. We are essentially paying for the privilege of being ignored by the Big Brother of the West while pretending we have a seat at the table.

This whole circus is happening while the stench of that shady health deal still lingers in the Nairobi air. Okiya Omtatah, the only man in this country who seems to read the fine print, is shouting about how our HIV status and mental health records are being auctioned off without any public participation. The government says they are only sharing de-identified data. If you believe that, I have a bridge in Nyali to sell you. This is the same administration that loves [THE BEGGAR IN CHIEF: WHY YOUR EMPTY POCKETS ARE TO BLAME FOR THE CHINA DEBT](/posts/the-beggar-in-chief--why-your-empty-pockets-are-to-blame-for-the-china-debt) approach to diplomacy, where we give up our sovereignty just to keep the foreign aid taps dripping.

President Ruto claims the Attorney General reviewed the deal with a fine-tooth comb. We all know how those combs work in this city. They usually miss the giant lice of corruption and the total lack of Parliamentary approval. Omtatah is right to point out that this deal operates outside of IFMIS, creating a parallel governance structure that ignores our own laws. It is a classic move - sign the papers first in a fancy hotel, then figure out how to explain why the national interest is in the gutters later.

Let us look at the numbers they are bragging about to distract us. Ksh 425 billion in trade sounds great on a PowerPoint slide until you realize we are exporting flowers and macadamia nuts while they are exporting high-value services and technology. They want to talk about Intellectual Property rights because they want to make sure we keep paying top shilling for their innovations while our farmers continue to sweat for pennies. It is not a fair trade environment - it is a landlord-tenant relationship where we are three months behind on rent and the landlord just increased the deposit.

In the end, these talks are just another way to keep the elite flying high while we stay grounded in reality. Whether it is digital trade or agricultural commodities, the result is always the same. The Americans get the data and the profits, and we get a 10 percent surcharge and a "thank you for coming" note. Nairobi realists know the drill - if you are not at the table, you are on the menu, and right now, Kenya is looking like a very cheap snack for the D.C. power players.
