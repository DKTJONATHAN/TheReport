Another day, another rollover. This time it's AIC Lomut Secondary School's bus, tumbling down a "sharp corner" near Kabarnet with 60 souls on board. The good news, according to MP Joshua Kandie, is "no serious casualties." Just what you want to hear after a bus full of kids goes for a spin. We're told they're all in "stable condition" and the emergency responders, healthcare workers, and "good Samaritans" did their thing. Bless their hearts.

Of course, the powers-that-be are probing. NTSA and KeNHA are getting their usual grilling, as if they haven't heard this song and dance before. The bus is at the police station, evidence of another close call. This comes barely 24 hours after a lorry decided to join a school compound in Nairobi, killing a student. And let's not forget the multi-vehicle pile-up on the Nairobi-Nakuru Highway. It’s becoming a rolling news cycle of chaos, and frankly, it feels like another digital mirage when you think about the actual infrastructure and safety measures that are supposed to be in place.

MP Kandie is busy with his "speedy and complete recovery" wishes and prayers. Such heartfelt sentiments. Meanwhile, the rest of us are left wondering about the actual cause. Was it speed? A faulty bus? Or just the general disdain for road safety that seems to permeate this country? It’s the same old story, isn't it? A bit of drama, a few concerned statements, and then we all move on until the next disaster.

It’s a miracle, they’ll say. God’s grace. But let’s be real. It’s sheer luck that we’re not talking about body bags and grieving families today. This isn’t about divine intervention; it’s about a system that consistently falls short. We’re so focused on the immediate aftermath, the "stable condition," that we forget to ask the uncomfortable questions about prevention. This whole charade feels as predictable as the fallout from someone catching their partner cheating - a lot of drama, a little bit of band-aid fixing, and then back to the same old patterns.

And the irony? This happens while the country is supposedly looking for a bigger stake in the global AI economy. You'd think with all that talk of progress and innovation, basic road safety wouldn't be such a monumental challenge. But here we are, celebrating the absence of fatalities as if it's a grand victory, rather than the bare minimum expectation. We'll keep hearing about these "incidents," and NTSA and KeNHA will keep promising investigations, while the cycle of near-tragedies continues.
