The oil industry, facing the existential threat of cheaper renewable energy sources, has a new, grim strategy: plastics. Journalists and authors like Beth Gardiner are exposing how fossil fuel behemoths, rather than transitioning to sustainable alternatives, are doubling down on petrochemicals. This isn't a move towards innovation; it's a cynical bet on the enduring profitability of plastic, a material inextricably linked to their declining oil and gas empires.

Gardiner’s research highlights a disturbing trend: as individuals and governments grapple with the overwhelming plastic pollution crisis, companies like ExxonMobil, Shell, and Saudi Aramco are pouring billions into increasing plastic production. The rationale is brutally simple: when oil and gas become less lucrative, plastic becomes their fallback. This is not about meeting consumer demand for convenience, as these corporations often claim. Instead, it’s about creating demand by flooding the market with an artificially cheap product that externalizes its true costs onto society and the environment.

The narrative pushed by these companies-that plastic is a waste management problem, not a production problem-is a deliberate misdirection. They lobby heavily at international forums like the global plastics pollution treaty negotiations, not to find solutions, but to block any measures that might limit production or hold them accountable for the toxic chemicals embedded in their products. Their "green language" of sustainability and innovation at industry conferences is merely a veneer, a desperate attempt to mask the reality that their core business model remains unchanged.

The cheapness of plastic is not a natural market phenomenon; it is a consequence of industries not accounting for the vast expenses associated with its lifecycle. Taxpayers, not the corporations profiting from plastic, are left to shoulder the burden of managing the mountains of waste generated. This creates a self perpetuating cycle where the very entities responsible for the crisis actively resist meaningful change, all while continuing to reap enormous profits. The future, as painted by this industry, is one where plastic continues to proliferate, a testament to their unwavering commitment to profit, regardless of the devastating environmental and health consequences.
