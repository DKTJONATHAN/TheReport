So, Brian from The Kenyan Wall Street is back with more tales from the land of broken promises. This transport strike, a week-long charade over fuel costs, is *suspended*. Emergency talks, they say. As if talking ever filled anyone's pockets or lowered the price of that vital litre of diesel. The roads were empty, commuters stranded, and I bet the air was a little cleaner for a hot minute. Don't get too comfortable, though. This is Kenya.

EPRA, in its infinite wisdom, has "urgently revised" fuel prices. Nairobi diesel is down by a princely KSh10.06, now a steal at KSh232.86 a litre. But hold onto your hats, kerosene is up. Why? To stop people from cooking up their own "diesel" with cheap kerosene. This whole mess is a clear sign of just how much the government is bleeding us dry with taxes and levies. The operators wanted a bigger cut, of course. Their demands are simple: don't bankrupt us. But the government's got its own money problems, blaming "global oil market disruptions" and the Iran conflict. Classic.

The protests themselves were a beautiful, chaotic symphony of matatus, truckers, boda bodas, and ride-hailing drivers united in their misery. Sadly, it wasn't all peaceful barricades. Clashes with police, people feared dead, scores injured. Just another Monday, right? It’s a stark reminder that sometimes the loudest calls for change come with the most violent consequences. This whole situation feels as predictable as Nairobi Hospital officials getting arrested amidst escalating leadership wrangles.

Now, the real drama begins. Who will crack first? The government, drowning in debt and trying to look like it's in control, or the transport operators, whose margins are thinner than a rumour in the CBD? The Finance Bill 2026 is also on the horizon, promising to squeeze multinationals even harder with more reporting requirements. They’re tightening the screws on how global firms move their profits, and I’m sure Kenyan subsidiaries will be thrilled to file more paperwork.

On a slightly less depressing note, Equity Group Holdings has crossed the KSh2 trillion asset mark. Good for them. Their regional expansion seems to be working, with more than half their assets and profits coming from outside Kenya. CEO James Mwangi is aiming for 15 countries and 100 million customers by 2030. Meanwhile, Sidian Bank's profit is up 9%, and I&M Bank managed to raise KSh13 billion. Seems the banks are doing just fine while the rest of us are worrying about fuel.

And then there's HACO Industries, which nearly went belly up after losing a deal with BIC. They're now focusing on homegrown brands and manufacturing in Ghana, banking on the AfCFTA. But even their turnaround story is laced with the grim reality of African manufacturing: punishing electricity costs, border delays, and logistics nightmares. It’s a constant battle. Oh, and Kenya imported over 3 million litres of South African wine last year. Cheers to that, I guess.
