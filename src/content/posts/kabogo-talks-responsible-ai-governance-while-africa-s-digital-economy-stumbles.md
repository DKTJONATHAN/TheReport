So, William Kabogo, our ICT Cabinet Secretary, is out here talking about how Artificial Intelligence is going to save Africa. Millions of jobs, economic growth - the usual song and dance. He’s calling for “inclusive and responsible AI governance frameworks,” which sounds nice and all, but let's be real. This is Nairobi. We’ve heard these promises before, usually before another tender gets mysteriously awarded to someone’s cousin.

Kabogo’s pitch is that AI can help farmers with weather patterns and seed selection. Great. So, while we’re busy with fancy AI apps for agriculture, what about the actual land dispossessions and environmental degradation that KNCHR keeps banging on about? Seems like a bit of a disconnect, doesn't it? We're talking about tech solving problems while basic human rights are being trampled.

He’s also harping on about investing in digital skills and infrastructure. Fine, but where’s the money coming from? And who is actually going to get these skills? It’s always women and youth this, women and youth that. Noble words, but when have they ever truly been at the forefront of these so-called opportunities, beyond being the cheap labour? It’s easy to talk about inclusion when you’re not the one being excluded.

Look, the idea of AI creating jobs isn’t entirely far-fetched, especially if you squint hard enough and ignore the current realities. We've seen how tech giants are reshaping infrastructure in other contexts, like when Meta expands its Nvidia partnership, but here, it feels like we're always playing catch-up. The focus should be on building a foundation that actually benefits the average Kenyan, not just the technocrats and their foreign buddies. Until then, Kabogo’s AI utopia sounds like another pipedream in a city full of them.
