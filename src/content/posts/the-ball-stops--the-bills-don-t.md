Nairobi is a city of illusions, and the Ulinzi Complex is just another stage for the theatre of the absurd. Kenya Police FC, or "The Enforcers" as they like to be called, managed to scrape a 2-0 win against Bandari, but let us not pretend this changes the price of unga. While Abud Omar was busy having his eighth-minute effort pushed wide, the rest of us were just wondering if the bus fare home would spike before the final whistle. It is a predictable cycle of temporary highs followed by the inevitable trek back to reality.

The first goal came in the 14th minute, a tap-in by Yves Koutiama after Erick Zakayo did the heavy lifting. The crowd roared as if a few goals could wash away the stench of a failing economy. They kept their 11th clean sheet of the season, which is impressive until you realize that "clean" is a word rarely used to describe anything else in this town. While the defenders stayed solid, the fans stayed broke, distracted by a ball moving across grass while their own lives remain stationary.

By the time the substitutions started in the 70th minute, the script was already written. Clinton Kinanga and his cohorts came on to refresh a game that was already decided by the sheer weight of institutional backing. Jacques Ndecket headed home a corner in the 76th minute to seal the deal. It is easy to look like a champion when you have the resources, but for the average person on the street, life is more like [the terminal trap: another 'investor' lands in the cells](/posts/the-terminal-trap--another--investor--lands-in-the-cells) than a victory lap under stadium lights.

The most hilarious part of this whole circus is the confusion in the records. One minute it is Ulinzi Stars on the photo caption, the next it is Bandari in the match report. It is the classic Kenyan mix-up, where nobody knows who is actually playing whom as long as the gate collections are made. We are told to focus on the "positive start" and the "impressive scoring run" while the actual structures of our society are as flimsy as a corner flag in a gale.

Even the talk of Islamic finance being "not charity" is just another reminder that nobody is giving you a hand-out. Whether it is on the pitch or in the bank, you are on your own. The reigning FKF Premier League champions can celebrate all they want at the Ulinzi Complex, but when the floodlights go out, we are all still stumbling in the dark, looking for a way to survive until the next payday that never seems to be enough.
