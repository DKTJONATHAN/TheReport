Kenya's "Road to Singapore": Another Dose of Rhetorical Exoticism, Another Kenyan Dream Sold Down the River

President Ruto wants to take Kenya to Singapore. He talks big about a "First World" future, but let's be real, it's just another politician peddling illusions. Singapore’s success is built on foundations Kenya doesn't have, and trying to copy their roadmap is like asking a miraa farmer to grow mangoes in the Sahara. This whole "Singapore obsession" is just a distraction from the real problems here at home.

The numbers don't lie. Singapore has a GDP per capita that makes Kenya's look like pocket change. While both were British colonies once, their paths diverged decades ago. Singapore is a tiny island, a global port driven by its strategic location and a manufacturing sector that boomed decades ago. Kenya, on the other hand, is still stuck with agriculture as its backbone and a manufacturing sector that's been sputtering for years, despite promises from every administration. We can't even get our ports fully operational, let alone compete with Singapore's freight volume. It’s like comparing a bicycle to a Boeing 747.

Then there's the financial sector. Nairobi might be East Africa's financial hub, but Kenya is on the Grey list thanks to a serious lack of oversight. Singapore? Never been there. And let's not even start on corruption. Singapore built strong institutions, while Kenya is drowning in it. Ruto’s focus on energy, education, and infrastructure is fine, but those are universal investments. The real Singaporean "blueprint" he seems to admire is Lee Kuan Yew's authoritarian grip. It's no coincidence this talk started when there were whispers of extending presidential terms, a classic playbook for concentrated power.

This obsession with strongmen like Lee Kuan Yew, and even Kagame in Rwanda, is a Western fetish for finding a single leader to blame or credit for a nation's fate. While leaders matter, so does the context. Other Asian countries grew without this "Great Man" narrative. And even Singapore's supposed authoritarianism had an independent anti-corruption body that didn't spare the ruling party. Here? The EACC can investigate, but it can’t prosecute. Cases get bogged down, and officials accused of scandals are merely shuffled out, not convicted. We’ve seen scandals around fertilizer, but no trials. Ruto himself was even named a "person of the year" by a corruption watchdog, right behind Bashar al-Assad. This isn’t fighting corruption; it’s just political theatre.

The article points out that countries like Uruguay, Namibia, and Botswana have tackled corruption and built strong institutions without sacrificing democracy. They've focused on independent bodies and press freedom. But for some Kenyan leaders, it's easier to point to a far-off authoritarian success story than to implement real, non-authoritarian reforms. This "rhetorical exoticism" is a convenient way to avoid the hard work of fixing our own mess. We need to stop chasing phantom blueprints and focus on building Kenya with its own strengths. We can chart our own path, one that doesn't involve sacrificing our democracy for a pipe dream.

This isn't about invalidating Singapore's success. It's about recognizing that their path isn't our path. Kenya has a progressive constitution, even if it's not fully enforced. Its relatively open democratic system is a strength, not a weakness. Sacrificing it under the guise of emulating a distant city-state is a dangerous game, especially with the global rise of authoritarianism. We need to remember that, despite the current political climate, Kenya's democracy is a valuable asset that needs protection.
