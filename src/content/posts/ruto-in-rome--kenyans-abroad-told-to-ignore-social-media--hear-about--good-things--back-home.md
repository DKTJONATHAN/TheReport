So, our President William Ruto is in Rome, lecturing Kenyans abroad about social media. Apparently, the internet is full of "fake news" and "manipulated content" designed to make you think "there is nothing good happening in Kenya." He wants you to stop scrolling and start believing what he tells you, because obviously, your own eyes and ears are too easily fooled by "political and selfish ends." He's worried you'll see the real picture and not the shiny one he's painting.

He then launched into his usual song and dance about how Kenya is transforming into a "first-world economy," despite "stiff resistance." According to him, his tough decisions have saved us from debt default, unlike five other African countries. It’s a real miracle, apparently, that we’re not in the same hole. He’s so focused on "transforming Kenya" that the next election is just a minor detail, like a fly on his designer suit.

And the "achievements"? Universal health coverage, they say. Thirty million Kenyans supposedly signed up for SHA in 18 months, compared to eight million in 60 years of the old NHIF. Sounds impressive, until you realise that SHA will be paying out KSh13 billion to hospitals for March 2026 services, which is apparently what NHIF collected in half a year. And then there's the housing, with a quarter of a million units magically appearing and creating jobs. Never mind if they’re actually affordable or livable.

Agriculture is booming, too, with subsidised fertiliser boosting maize production from 44 million bags to 70 million. Hunger is history, apparently. Coffee farmers are raking it in, getting KSh110 to KSh160 per kilo, up from KSh60. And sugar cane farmers are finally getting paid on time and receiving bonuses. It’s a sweet deal for everyone, except maybe those who can’t afford the "increased commodity prices" that come with all this prosperity. The President even claims Kenya will be a net sugar exporter soon, which will be a first.

He's also got the diaspora covered, with a whole department dedicated to their problems. Fifty-four thousand Kenyans have found work abroad thanks to his "intentional decisions," and 600 rogue recruitment agencies have been deregulated. It's all about protecting Kenyans from exploitation, harassment, and poor pay, while simultaneously boosting diaspora remittances from $4 billion to $5.2 billion. They're our biggest source of foreign exchange, so it's only right they contribute. Just remember to respect the laws of your host countries, or you might end up like that Ugandan opposition figure Kizza Besigye, whose rendition was apparently a "high-profile incident." And if you’re wondering about the rising cost of transport back home, well, that’s a whole other story, as Kenya's Truckers Eye 14% Hike As Diesel Prices Reach New Heights.
