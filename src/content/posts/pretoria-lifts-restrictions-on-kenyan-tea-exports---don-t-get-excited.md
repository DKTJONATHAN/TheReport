Relief? Pretoria Lifts Tea Restrictions - For Now.

South Africa finally decided to stop being petty with Kenyan tea, but don't break out the champagne just yet. This is just tit for tat, and we've got a massive trade deficit to show for it.

Pretoria is finally allowing Kenyan tea to flow freely after Kenya slapped tariffs on their steel. Big deal. It’s a move announced during President Ruto’s visit, part of a package of six trade "facilitation instruments." Sounds impressive, doesn't it? Probably just a lot of handshakes and photo ops.

Let’s talk numbers, because that’s where the real story is. In 2025, Kenya imported a staggering $585 million worth of goods from South Africa, while our exports barely scraped $54.4 million. So, when they talk about reducing the trade deficit, it’s mostly them telling us to buy less of their stuff. President Ramaphosa practically admitted it: "Because Kenya had imposed a tariff on our steel, we in South Africa decided to reciprocate." It’s a game of economic ping pong, and we’re losing.

Then you have the Kenyan business leaders falling over themselves, calling it a "major milestone." Joshua Oigara from Stanbic Bank is busy talking about financing farmers, as if that magically fixes the fundamental imbalance. While he’s at it, perhaps he can explain how we ended up in this mess in the first place, maybe by looking at how [global economic cannibalization masks a looming systemic collapse](/posts/global-economic-cannibalization-masks-a-looming-systemic-collapse). The tea is good, yes, but it’s not going to pay for the mountains of goods we’re importing.

This whole tea saga is happening while Kenya is desperately trying to find new markets for its exports because the Middle East is a mess. So, lifting restrictions on tea is less about genuine partnership and more about South Africa playing a calculated game. They need us as a partner outside their SADC club, and we’re grateful for any crumbs they throw our way.

So, yes, the tea restrictions are lifted. For now. Let’s see how long this "relief" lasts before the next trade spat erupts. This is Nairobi realism, not some fairy tale.
