The main complaint is taxation, specifically on raw materials. The government wants us to manufacture locally, but they tax the components so heavily that it is cheaper to import a finished product from a country three oceans away. These CEOs are warning about "reduced consumer demand." No kidding. When people are choosing between a bag of maize flour and paying the bus fare to work, they are not exactly lining up to buy whatever "innovative product" these firms are churning out.

Then there is the talk of technology. They say 86 percent of firms have automated their processes. That sounds great on paper until you remember that our digital infrastructure is as stable as a three-legged stool. They are worried about "cyber risks" and "expertise gaps," and they should be. We already saw how Russian state hackers exploit Microsoft Office vulnerabilities (/posts/russian-state-hackers-exploit-microsoft-office-vulnerability), yet our government acts like "innovation" is just a buzzword to put on a billboard. You cannot run a digital economy on a physical system that is falling apart.

Credit is supposedly getting easier to find because bank rates dropped. That is a joke. If you are a big player with a "cloud-based operation," maybe you get a seat at the table. If you are an SME in downtown Nairobi, you are still facing "high collateral requirements" and "bureaucratic delays." In this city, "bureaucracy" is just the code word for the gatekeeper waiting for his cut. Over a quarter of these leaders are still hitting a wall, and that wall is built of red tape and greed.

The survey ends with the same tired demands: reduce taxes, fix the roads, and pay the suppliers. It is the same script every year, just with a different date on the header. The government will take these recommendations, file them in a drawer that never opens, and then announce a new levy on digital oxygen. Growth is something that happens to other countries; in Kenya, we just find new ways to stay underwater without drowning.
