Here we go again. Another Wednesday, another "major breakthrough" by the Counter Terrorism Policing Unit. They want us to clap because they found a stash of assault rifles and explosives before the city went up in flames. It is the same old song and dance. They track "logistical networks" for months, letting the danger simmer right under our noses, only to swoop in at the last minute to justify their budget. Meanwhile, the rest of us are just trying to get through the day without getting caught in the crossfire of a war we did not sign up for.

The official statement says they recovered 600 rounds of ammunition. Think about that number for a second. That is not a "small cell" operation; that is a full-scale massacre waiting to happen. Apparently, the plan was to turn the holy month of Ramadan into a bloodbath. It is a grim reminder that while we are busy arguing over fuel prices, there are people in the shadows counting bullets and mapping out our favorite malls. The "intelligence assessments" suggest they were also looking into kidnapping foreigners and hijacking cars. It is just another day in the green city in the sun.

They are pointing the finger at the Dadaab refugee camps again, claiming the arms were funneled through the usual cracks in our porous borders. It is convenient, is it not? Blame the camps, increase the surveillance, and ignore the fact that these weapons traveled hundreds of kilometers through "secure" checkpoints to reach the capital. If you have enough Shillings, you can move a tank through this country without anyone noticing until the authorities need a press release to look busy.

As we watch [Nairobi's Rising Towers, Human Stories](/posts/nairobi-s-rising-towers--human-stories) redefine the skyline, it is hard to ignore the rot at the base. We are building glass monuments to progress while the basement is packed with TNT. The contrast is peak Nairobi. One minute you are sipping an overpriced latte in a rooftop bar, and the next, you are reading about a foiled plot to level the very building you are standing in. The city grows taller, but the shadows only get longer and more dangerous.

Now comes the inevitable "security surge." Expect more roadblocks, more bored officers poking your car floor mats with sticks, and more "random" searches that mostly target people who look like they cannot afford a bribe. They tell us Nairobi remains secure due to "sustained counterterrorism operations," but "secure" is a relative term when you live in a place where peace is just the silence between explosions.

We are told to stay alert, as if we have a choice. The reality is that we are all just spectators in a high-stakes game of cat and mouse where the mouse has an AK-47 and the cat is busy checking its reflection. Al-Shabab has been at this for a decade, and despite all the "foiled" plots, the threat remains as permanent as the potholes on Mombasa Road. We will keep breathing until the next "intelligence-led" operation fails to happen in time.
