So, it seems Embu Governor Cecily Mbarire decided to play the role of the inconvenient truth-teller at a recent parliamentary group meeting. While President Ruto was busy congratulating himself on whatever he was talking about regarding the NADCO report and his grand ten-point agenda, Mbarire, bless her bold heart, snatched the microphone to point out the elephant in the room - or rather, the absence of it. The two-thirds gender rule, a promise whispered in the wind for years, is apparently as silent in this NADCO report as a politician admitting they were wrong.

President Ruto, of course, immediately clutched his pearls and declared that the rule is "there, it is there, Cess. We cannot make such a fatal mistake." Oh, the drama! Interrupting the Head of State? Unheard of! But Mbarire, apparently unfazed by presidential pronouncements, doubled down, reminding him how critical this is for the women of this country. You know, the ones who are supposed to be represented, not just tokenized. It’s almost like these political agreements are less about genuine commitment and more about checking boxes on a list of talking points.

This little spat between the Governor and the President is just a small crack in the facade, revealing the simmering unease that always brews beneath the surface when these grand plans are rolled out. The NADCO report, with its ten-point agenda, sounds impressive on paper, doesn't it? But when the rubber meets the road, and critical issues like fair representation are conveniently overlooked or vaguely “included,” you have to wonder what the real agenda is. Is it about progress, or just about maintaining the status quo while throwing a few scraps to the masses?

It’s the same old song and dance, isn't it? Leaders gather, make grand pronouncements, sign documents, and then conveniently forget the details when it’s time for implementation. We've seen it time and time again, from promises of better infrastructure to the ever-elusive fight against corruption. It makes you wonder if we're just stuck in a loop, forever debating the same issues while the real problems fester. It's enough to make you feel like you're living through a particularly drawn-out episode of [Clout, Chaos, and Corpses: Why Kenya’s Social Media Feed Is a Crime Scene](/posts/clout--chaos--and-corpses--why-kenya-s-social-media-feed-is-a-crime-scene), where the plot never really advances.

Mbarire’s insistence that the gender rule hasn't been implemented and hasn't received sufficient attention, despite Ruto's claims, just underscores the perpetual disconnect between promises and reality. It’s not about whether the words are in the report; it's about whether they translate into tangible change. And judging by the governor's public frustration, the answer, for many women in Kenya, is a resounding no. This is what happens when political convenience trumps genuine action.
