So, Vladislav Liukov, or "Yaytseslav Truhov" if you prefer your online villains with an extra syllable, is back at it. Apparently, the Kenyan authorities and his previous stint of being a wanted man didn't stick. Now he's re-releasing old footage of a Kenyan woman, Choice Kinoti, apparently accusing her of stealing his headphones. It’s all very dramatic, with her crying and emptying her bag. Honestly, the sheer desperation to stay relevant online is exhausting.

But don't let the headphone drama fool you. This is just a rehash of his old playbook: befriend women, convince them they're in some kind of relationship, promise them the moon, and then secretly film them for TikTok and YouTube. He was even selling longer versions of these "intimate videos" on some shady Telegram channel. It’s a whole operation, really. You'd think by now people would learn, but then again, Kenya's seen its fair share of [foreign nationals trading truth for tea](https://www.tuko.co.ke/posts/dashboard-drama--tanzania-s-officials-get-a-reality-check-after-trading-truth-for-tea), so perhaps naivety is a national sport.

The latest clips, filmed months ago, are being pushed now because, well, why not? Choice confirms it’s old footage, and she’s telling people not to read too much into it. Smart move, honestly. Deny, deny, deny, and hope the internet moves on to the next outrage. But the damage is done. She claims it ruined her marriage and her lover threatened to leave her. And let’s not forget the rumour mill about her HIV status, which she had to publicly shut down. All thanks to this creeps' obsession with his "rizz" challenges.

And it’s not just him. Apparently, another Russian bloke, Alex Ananasik, was doing similar things. It’s like Nairobi’s become a free-for-all filming ground for these guys. They strut around with their smart glasses, charming unsuspecting women, and then broadcast their private lives to the world. The authorities were supposedly on his case, coordinating with international law enforcement, but here he is, back in the news, circulating more videos. The wheels of justice grind slowly, especially when there's viral content to be made.

The whole thing is a sad commentary on consent, privacy, and the ever-growing reach of social media. These foreigners see Kenya as some exotic playground where they can do as they please, filming women without their knowledge and then profiting from it. And some Kenyans, caught up in the allure of attention or perhaps just plain desperate for a better life, fall right into their traps. It's a cycle of exploitation disguised as entertainment.

So, what’s next? More stolen headphones? More tearful confessions? More videos of women who thought they were falling for a romantic foreigner, only to find themselves victims of a voyeuristic hustle? Given the track record, it's safe to assume this isn't the last we'll hear of our Russian "content creators" and their Kenyan subjects. Just another day in the digital jungle.
