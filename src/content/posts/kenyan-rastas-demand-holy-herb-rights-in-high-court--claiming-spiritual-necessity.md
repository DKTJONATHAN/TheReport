Rastas Want Their Weed, High Court Says "Hold My Holy Herb"

The Rastafarian community is trotting into the High Court, wailing about how Kenya's ban on Cannabis Sativa is basically a religious felony. Apparently, their "holy sacrament" is crucial for spiritual worship and meditation - you know, those sessions where they discuss societal issues and home prayers. They’re claiming it’s a constitutional right violation, and honestly, who are we to argue with their "reasoning" sessions? They insist it's strictly for meditation, not procreation, and that only adults with IDs who understand the spiritual purpose get a toke. Because, you know, rules.

They're acting like this herb is a divine gift from God, yet they’re crying about family isolation, arrests, and being slapped with drug trafficking charges for personal use. It’s a real shame, isn't it? Meanwhile, lawyers are out here citing global cases of legalisation, probably hoping to make a few bob while the Rastas get their spiritual groove back. This whole kerfuffle reminds me of how some self-proclaimed prophets seem to get away with financial shenanigans, like that whole [Prophet David Owuor saga](/posts/prophet-david-owuor-false-miracles-billion-shilling-fraud), proving that sometimes, the law seems to have a rather selective memory.

The petitioners are adamant that their usage is never recreational, just meditative. They’ve got their own internal constitution, apparently, to guide their daily lives and protect their members. It’s all very spiritual and organized, isn't it? They're not looking for a party; they're looking for enlightenment, one puff at a time. It’s almost charming in its earnestness, if you ignore the fact that in Kenya, "bhags" will still land you in jail for up to eight years.

So, while the rest of the world is slowly waking up to the idea that maybe, just maybe, cannabis isn't the devil's lettuce, Kenya is still holding firm. The Rastas are pleading their case for religious freedom, and honestly, after hearing about the alleged repercussions, you have to wonder if there’s a bit of hypocrisy lurking in the shadows. Is it a spiritual sacrament or just a really good way to chill out? The High Court will decide, I suppose.
