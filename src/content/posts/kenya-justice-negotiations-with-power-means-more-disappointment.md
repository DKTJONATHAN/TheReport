"Leo wacheni niseme kinitoke." Allow me to just say it all, because for too long we've been chasing shadows, believing elections are the magic wand. They are not. They are just a revolving door for faces, a cosmetic change that leaves the rot underneath untouched. We've been fed this lie that a new president will magically fix everything, but every single one walks into office already beholden to the financiers and wheeler-dealers. By the time they're sworn in, they're already navigating a labyrinth of obligations. It's not the individuals, it's the cage they're put in.

And Parliament? Don't even get me started. We expect them to be the saviours, but the incentives there are just as twisted. You have a few brave souls speaking up, sure, but their fight for national duty is constantly overshadowed by the desperate scramble for political survival. Debate turns into performance art, and real reform gets bogged down in endless negotiations. Kenya hasn't lacked leaders; it's lacked a system that shields good intentions from being perverted. It's a familiar script, this constant recycling of hope.

Look at our glorious constitution, the envy of many, yet justice is delayed, development is a snail's pace, and accountability is a foreign concept. We have the laws, the institutions, but the systems just dilute everything. This gap, this yawning chasm between promise and reality, is where our future has been quietly strangled. We've been distracted by the shiny baubles of elections while the real machinery of disappointment grinds on, much like how capital flees when the "predatory state" makes its move. The real power, the one we've criminally underutilised, is the Judiciary. It doesn't campaign, it doesn't fundraise, it doesn't negotiate for its survival. It stands on the Constitution. And when it acts, it does so with an authority that no politician can easily dismiss. We've seen it nullify elections, halt unconstitutional moves - these aren't minor interventions, they're proof of a system that can, and has, corrected power itself.

But here's the kicker, the deeper truth we conveniently ignore: the courts can't act on their own. They need us, the citizens and institutions, to bring real matters before them. We need to shift our perspective, to see the court not as a last resort but as a proactive tool for national progress. Imagine demanding timelines for projects, enforcing transparency in procurement, protecting public resources not as favours but as constitutional mandates. Power trembles when the citizenry understands the Constitution, and that's the line Kenya needs to cross.

The Judiciary, with its quiet heroes safeguarding our legal soul, has been an unsung force. This isn't about fighting the government; it's about strengthening the nation. The Executive must lead, Parliament must legislate, but the Judiciary must be activated to keep them honest. Counties must respect court orders, national leadership must protect judicial independence and resource it properly. As we inch towards another election cycle, let's stop the endless rallies and the disguised favours. We need stronger systems, not just new faces. Nimesema na kimenitoka. I've said it all, and I'm at peace. What you do with it is your problem.
