So, 215 of our finest have finally crawled back home from Haiti. Touched down at midnight, probably reeking of desperation and gunpowder. They're calling it a "landmark mission," a "demonstration of global peace." Right. More like another Kenyan taxpayer-funded escapade into a country that's been a dumpster fire for years. At least they're back, and the politicians can pat themselves on the back for a job… well, a job that's now being handed over to another bunch of international troubleshooters. Because obviously, we solved everything.

These officers were wading through Port-au-Prince, a place so volatile it makes Eastleigh on a Saturday night look like a quiet Sunday service. They were supposed to be securing installations and reopening roads. Did they? Maybe. But let's not forget the "hard lessons" we were supposed to learn, because apparently, we're slow learners. And let's not forget the three officers who *didn't* make it back, casualties of a war that wasn't ours to fight, in a place that probably wishes we'd never shown up. It’s always the same script, different date, as we stumble from one disaster to the next.

Now we're transitioning to this "GSF" thing, a "hybrid security formation." Sounds fancy, doesn't it? More acronyms, more money being thrown around. The US is apparently stepping up its involvement, bringing in warships to create a "maritime security ring." Because, of course, the solution to complex political and social breakdown is more military might. Meanwhile, our own institutions back home are struggling, but hey, at least we're projecting strength abroad. It’s almost as if our leaders are trying to distract us from the problems right here, the ones that make Nairobi’s annual swamp edition look like a minor inconvenience.

Interior Principal Secretary Raymond Omollo is out here praising their "courage and professionalism." He’s talking about how Kenya’s reputation is enhanced. Enhanced how? By sending our police to be cannon fodder in a failed state? And to the families of the fallen, "the Government will continue to stand with you." We've heard that one before. It's the same empty words trotted out after every botched operation, after every policy failure. They did their duty, sure, but what did it actually achieve beyond costing lives and money, and giving Mudavadi a nice speech at Chatham House?

The UN is still pushing for elections in Haiti, urging everyone to "come to an agreement." You know, like how we're all in agreement about how to fix Kenya? Yeah, not so much. This "National Pact for Stability" sounds like another pipe dream, another attempt to paper over the cracks. The insecurity, the weak institutions, the political fragmentation - these aren't problems you solve with more police contingents or fancy new security frameworks. This entire mission, from start to finish, is a testament to a global system that’s more interested in appearances than real solutions, and a Kenyan leadership that’s happy to play along, as long as the photo ops are good and the global partners are pleased.

So, these officers are back, with their "valuable operational experience." Let's hope that experience translates into something useful for Kenya, instead of just being a footnote in another costly foreign policy gamble. The drawdown continues, more are expected home. And then what? We'll be left with the memories, the debts, and the lingering question: was any of it worth it? Don't hold your breath for a straight answer.
