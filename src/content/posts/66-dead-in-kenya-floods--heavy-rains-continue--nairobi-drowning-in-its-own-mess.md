So, another four souls have been washed away by the sky's tantrum. That's 66 people gone in the last week, most of them from Nairobi. And surprise, surprise, the capital got another drenching overnight. No new deaths reported, which is about as much of a silver lining as you'll find in this mess. The Red Cross managed to fish some people out of a stuck matatu and a flooded house, but honestly, this is just Nairobi doing what Nairobi does best - drowning in its own underdevelopment.

The usual story: rivers bursting their banks, homes underwater, roads looking like aquatic obstacle courses, and the power lines… well, they're probably having a nice swim too. Bridges have decided to take a holiday, and schools are acting more like swimming pools. The interior ministry's warning about more rain? Groundbreaking. It's almost like they're surprised gravity still works. Meanwhile, thousands are being told to "seek safer ground," which I assume means finding a slightly less flooded spot to wait for the next downpour.

Half of the fatalities, 33 of them, are from Nairobi. Why? Because "poor drainage" and "unregulated development" are apparently the city's official motto. People like Kareem Hassan Ali in Parklands were staring at two meters of water, though his posh flat was spared. His underground parking, however, became an impromptu car wash. Another resident, Deenesh Patel, wisely bailed and spent the night elsewhere. He points out the obvious: other low-lying areas aren't as bad because they have "proper infrastructure." Revolutionary stuff.

Both Ali and Patel are calling for officials to actually *do* something about the drainage and the building that blocks our waterways. You know, the same thing residents have been screaming about for years. President Ruto claims they're clearing drains, but Ali says nobody's shown up, and the residents had to sort out the debris themselves. Sounds about right. Emergency food and medical aid are supposedly on the way, so at least there's a chance of surviving the flood only to be starved or infected.

This whole disaster isn't just a Nairobi problem. Ethiopia's also having a rough time, with over 100 dead. And the scientists will tell you it's all down to "climate change," a warming atmosphere making extreme rain more likely. We've already warmed up by 1.1 degrees Celsius, and unless governments stop fiddling around, it's only going to get hotter and wetter.
