So, Kenya wants to criminalize match fixing. Bless their cotton socks. Apparently, when Martin Munga and his international pals got nabbed for allegedly fixing games, the cops realised they had no actual law to charge them with. So now, we have a bill. Because that’s how we do things here, right? Draft a bill, pat yourselves on the back, and then watch it gather dust while the real business continues.

It’s almost laughable. They’re charging these characters with "conspiracy to commit a felony namely cheating," which, surprise surprise, is what match fixing is everywhere else. It’s like trying to arrest someone for jaywalking when there’s no law against it, then slapping them with a “conspiracy to obstruct pedestrian traffic” charge. The match officials are out there insisting sanctioning referees isn't the answer. Oh, really? What a revelation. Maybe they should try *not* letting the foxes guard the hen house.

The whole charade around whether those "silly mistakes" by goalkeepers are genuine errors or something more sinister is just a distraction. It's the Nairobi version of the "soft life" suddenly getting harder, isn't it? We're supposed to believe these guys are just having a bad day, while the evidence of blatant manipulation is staring us in the face. It's the same old song and dance, just with a different sporting arena. We've seen this PR machine working overtime before, trying to sell us manufactured authenticity. [The PR Machine is Smoking: Why We Are Sick of Manufactured 'Authenticity'](/posts/the-pr-machine-is-smoking--why-we-are-sick-of-manufactured--authenticity)

Let's be clear: this bill is probably just another exercise in optics. It's a way for politicians to look like they're doing something while the deep-seated corruption continues its merry march. Don't expect any sudden influx of honest referees or clean games. This is Kenya, after all. We specialize in high-flying rhetoric and empty pockets, and this bill is just the latest addition to that illustrious tradition.
