So, Boniface Mwangi, the man who’s always shouting from the rooftops, has decided to get nostalgic. If he wasn’t busy documenting our country’s slow slide into the abyss with his camera, he reckons he’d be up front of a classroom, droning on about history. Apparently, his passion for the past, specifically the gory bits like lynchings and ‘weka taya,’ would have made him a history teacher. One wonders if his students would have learned more about historical atrocities or the current ones happening right under our noses.

His real point, buried under the sentimental fluff, is the same old song and dance: Kenya is a mess. While some poor sod gets his tires lit for stealing a chicken, the real thieves, the ones pilfering millions in grand corruption, are probably sipping expensive scotch and planning their next heist. Mwangi’s calling for judicial execution for these economic vampires. It’s a nice thought, a real fantasy for most of us tired of seeing the same faces enrich themselves. We’ve seen whispers of this kind of scrutiny elsewhere, like with the [Prison Time for Banknote Mutilators? Ex-Tycoon's Downfall Proves Scrutiny Bites.](/posts/prison-time-for-banknote-mutilators--ex-tycoon-s-downfall-proves-scrutiny-bites) but that was a different story, wasn't it?

And then there's the passport scandal. Oh, the drama. Apparently, our precious travel documents are being handed out like flyers to people who might, you know, blow things up or cause trouble. Mwangi’s rightly pointing fingers at the government officials who clearly see this country as their personal ATM and playground. It’s hardly surprising, is it? This whole setup feels like a perpetual human warehouse, with [Ruai is the New Human Warehouse and Nobody is Surprised](/posts/ruai-is-the-new-human-warehouse-and-nobody-is-surprised), just on a national scale.

He’s also got his sights set on the presidency, naturally. He’s out there, like a street preacher, telling Kenyans to get their voter cards and actually cast a ballot in 2027. He’s worried about apathy, about the millions who sat out 2022 and the millions more who don’t even have the means to participate. He wants us to find a reason, someone to vote for. It’s a noble sentiment, coming from someone with a platform. But frankly, who are we kidding? The system is rigged, and finding a reason to believe anything has gotten harder than finding clean water in some parts of Nairobi.

Mwangi’s call to action, while delivered with his usual fervor, feels like a desperate plea into the void. He wants us to wake up, to resist apathy, to believe our vote matters. But when the choices are this grim and the promises so hollow, it’s hard to muster the energy. Maybe he should have just stuck to teaching history. At least then, he could have shown us how we ended up in this perpetual cycle of disappointment, instead of asking us to participate in creating more of it.
