It’s hardly surprising. Meta, the behemoth that brought us endless scrolling and carefully curated realities, is now facing a lawsuit for its alleged role in profiting from scam ads on Facebook and Instagram. The Consumer Federation of America (CFA) has lodged a complaint, arguing that Meta’s handling of these fraudulent advertisements violates Washington, DC’s consumer protection laws. Apparently, Meta isn't just passively hosting these scams; it's allegedly letting them "proliferate" while simultaneously making promises about cracking down on fraud. The irony is as thick as a poorly rendered metaverse avatar.

The CFA's lawsuit points to specific examples found in Meta's own ad library - ads peddling phantom $1,400 checks and free government iPhones. These aren't sophisticated operations; they're the digital equivalent of snake oil salesmen, preying on the gullible and desperate. And Meta, it seems, is happy to collect its commission.

Meta's spokesperson, Chris Sgro, predictably issued a statement claiming the allegations "misrepresent the reality of our work" and that they "will fight them." This is standard corporate playbook: deny, deflect, and hope the noise dies down. Meanwhile, Ben Winters, the CFA's director of AI and data privacy, suggests a quick search of Meta's ad library with terms like "free phone" or "stimulus check" will reveal a veritable buffet of dubious advertisements, many of which are still live. A quick look by WIRED even uncovered ads for "secret tax checks" promising "Wall Street’s recession-proof investing strategy." When pressed, Meta remained conspicuously silent on whether these ads actually comply with their own policies. One can only assume the answer is a resounding "it depends on how much they pay."

The CFA isn't just looking for an apology; they want damages and to claw back what they claim are illegal profits. They also want business reforms, which, in the world of big tech, usually translates to a polite request for the fox to guard the henhouse.

Meta's ubiquity is precisely why this matters. Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp are the digital town squares, and apparently, those squares are being overrun by con artists, with Meta's tacit approval and financial benefit. Internal Meta documents, leaked to Reuters, paint a grim picture. One presentation estimated that Meta's platforms were involved in a third of all successful scams in the US. Another gem revealed that it's "easier to advertise scams on Meta platforms than Google." This isn't an accident; it's a business model.

The financial implications are staggering. One internal document from 2024 estimated that Meta would rake in a staggering 10.1 percent of its revenue - roughly $16 billion - from ads that were actually scams or other prohibited content. To put that in perspective, the FBI estimated that Americans lost a comparable $16 billion to all internet crimes in the same year. Meta dismisses these figures as "rough and overly inclusive," but conveniently fails to provide any alternative numbers. The truth, as always, is likely somewhere in the murky, profitable middle.

Meta spokesperson Sgro insists they "aggressively combat scams," claiming they removed over 159 million scam ads last year, with 92 percent taken down before being reported, and shut down 10.9 million scam-associated accounts. These numbers, while superficially impressive, are designed to distract from the core issue: the ongoing and profitable proliferation of scams on their platforms. It’s like a fire department boasting about how many fires they've put out while simultaneously providing the accelerant.

Even state attorneys general have gotten involved, urging Meta to crack down on ads leading users to WhatsApp groups used for investment scams. Their complaints, like those from New York AG Letitia James, highlight that Meta's "solutions" are clearly not working, with investigators continuously seeing the same scam ads months after reporting them.

This lawsuit is less about justice and more about the inevitable consequence of a system designed to prioritize profit over protection. Meta, like many digital giants, has built an empire on user attention, and if attracting that attention involves turning a blind eye to scams, then so be it. The algorithms are designed for engagement, not ethics, and until the financial incentives change, these scams will continue to be the unwelcome, yet profitable, guests at the digital feast.
