So, VukaPay is all puffed up because their Canadian outfit, Wirpaid Limited, got itself registered as a Payment Service Provider over there. Big deal. They’re calling it a “strengthened global payment infrastructure strategy.” Sounds fancy, right? Basically, they’ve got a shiny new stamp of approval that lets them pretend they’re serious players in connecting Africa to the rest of the world. Because, you know, Africa is just a bunch of fragmented markets needing serious infrastructure.

This whole song and dance is about building "trusted infrastructure." They talk about collections, payouts, cross-border payments, the whole nine yards. And of course, they throw in the projected trillions for Africa's digital economy. What they conveniently gloss over is the reality. Sending money into or across Africa is still a nightmare of high fees, delays, and currency chaos. Sub-Saharan Africa is the most expensive place to send money to globally, remember that. It’s like trying to navigate a maze blindfolded while juggling hot coals.

They want to be the guys who simplify this mess by connecting everyone to multiple payment rails. For banks and businesses, this is supposed to be a “stronger foundation.” But let’s be real, most of these initiatives sound good on paper. We've seen plenty of "innovators" come and go, promising the moon and delivering dust. It’s the same old song: big ambitions, big talk, and the everyday person still waiting for their money to arrive. It’s almost as predictable as Kenya's [12 Million Silent Voters getting ready for the usual circus](/posts/kenya-s-12-million-silent-voters--get-ready-for-the-usual-circus).

Sam Malonza, the CEO, is quoted saying this is a "strategic step in building African payment infrastructure with global credibility." Credibility? That’s earned by actually making payments work, not by getting a registration in Canada. They're looking for "serious corridors, serious partners, and serious scale." But until they can prove they can fix the fundamental problems that plague cross-border payments for ordinary Kenyans and businesses, this sounds like more hot air. We’ll see if this "milestone" actually translates into anything other than another press release.
