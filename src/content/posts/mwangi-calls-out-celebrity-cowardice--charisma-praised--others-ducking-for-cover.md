Boniface Mwangi, bless his idealistic heart, is out here calling for artists to actually use their "platforms" for something other than selling teeth whitening kits. He's basically saying, "Hey, you big shots making bank off your songs and faces, when are you going to stop being scared of losing the bag and actually stand for something?" It's a novel concept, I know.

He's got a point though, doesn't he? During that whole Macron visit circus, you saw the usual scramble. Suddenly, everyone's got a sudden bout of laryngitis when it comes to anything remotely controversial. Mwangi likens it to those American athletes who suddenly go mute, afraid to upset the advertisers who keep their champagne flowing. We're talking about people who can afford to speak truth to power, but would rather sip champagne and pretend the world's problems are just background noise, much like the constant hum of unpaid bills at places like KNH, where [18,000 patients have seemingly vanished](posts/knh--sh867m-unpaid-bills---18-000-patients-vanish--what-s-new).

Mwangi's gone and done the historical deep dive, reminding us that art used to be about more than just pretty pictures and catchy tunes. It was about challenging the status quo, holding leaders accountable. He brings up Muhammad Ali, a man who actually put his career on the line for his beliefs. Can you imagine that happening here? Someone refusing a lucrative gig because it conflicts with their values? Unlikely. They'd probably just Photoshop themselves into the campaign ad and call it a day.

But of course, there are exceptions. Mwangi actually gives a nod to Charisma for, you know, actually *supporting* someone he believes in, David Maraga. Wearing the T-shirts and all. That's rare, apparently. Most of our artists are busy singing about corruption one minute and then posing for selfies with the very people they're supposedly criticising the next. Talk about a mixed message. It’s a level of cognitive dissonance that would make your head spin.

The kicker? Mwangi isn't just pointing fingers at our local talent. He's broadening his attack to the global stage, calling out the likes of Beyoncé and Jay-Z for their perceived silence on issues like Palestine and, dare I say it, Black Lives Matter. He's essentially saying that if you're going to have a microphone, you should be willing to use it for more than just your own fame and fortune. It's a tough pill to swallow when your entire career is built on carefully curated optics.

Mwangi keeps circling back to the same idea: stop pretending to be one thing in your art and another in your life. Be consistent. Be brave. Stand for what you believe in, even if it means losing a few endorsement deals or having to explain your political leanings to your Auntie Mildred at Christmas. It's a call to arms for creatives who've gotten too comfortable in their gilded cages, and frankly, it’s about time someone said it.
