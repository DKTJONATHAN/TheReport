Welcome to the internet in Nairobi, where posting a video of yourself is basically an invitation for strangers to tear your self-esteem to pieces. Branice Okanga thought she was just sharing content from Vihiga, but the TikTok mob decided her height was a public crime. These people sit in their rooms, probably in some dusty bedsitter, typing insults about her physical appearance while they struggle to buy their own airtime. It is the typical Kenyan story: you try to make an honest living online, and the trolls treat you like a communal punching bag.

Now, the Kenya Police Service is trying to act like they have a plan. They have launched this fancy module on technology-facilitated gender-based violence under the name Policare. It sounds like a new brand of detergent, and honestly, it might be just as thin. They have got occupational standards, curriculum content, and trainer guides. It is all very impressive on paper, but we know how the system works. While they are busy training and launching modules, women are already carrying digital scars that no police manual can heal.

This whole initiative feels like [THE PR FIRESHIELD: WILLIAM’S STRATEGIC 'CONCERN' OVER THE EPSTEIN STENCH](/posts/the-pr-fireshield--william-s-strategic--concern--over-the-epstein-stench) in the sense that it is a lot of institutional noise to cover up a massive, systemic failure. Inspector General Douglas Kanja says security is a shared responsibility, which is just police-speak for "we cannot actually protect you." They talk about victim-centered policing frameworks while a Russian national is allegedly out here recording Kenyan women in secret and uploading the videos for the whole world to see.

The Gender Cabinet Secretary and the DCI are finally "pursuing" this Russian suspect after the damage is already done. They are talking about transnational crimes and international bodies, but the videos have already finished their rounds on Telegram. The DCI warned us not to share the footage, threatening us with criminal liability, which is classic. They cannot stop the predator in real-time, so they threaten the public with the law instead. It is always easier to police the bystanders than to actually catch the guy behind the keyboard.

Let us be real: a new module is not going to stop some bored guy from stalking women on Facebook. Technology has moved faster than the police ever will. AI and GPS are being used to amplify abuse, and while the police are reading their new guides, the predators are already three updates ahead. Branice says she will not let them derail her dreams, and that is brave, but in this city, bravery is often the only thing the law allows you to keep. The digital wild west is still won by those with the thickest skin, not the best-trained police.
