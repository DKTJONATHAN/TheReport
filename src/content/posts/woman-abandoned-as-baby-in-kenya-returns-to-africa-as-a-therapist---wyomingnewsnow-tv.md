Woman Abandoned In Kenya Bag Now "Therapist" For Africa - Another Foreign Fix.

This is the story of Becky Chaplin, dumped like yesterday's rubbish on a Kenyan roadside, only to be scooped up by some do-gooding mzungus. Now, she's back on the continent, playing the benevolent saviour as an occupational therapist. Because apparently, after a taste of poverty, the best way to “help” is to fly in with your fancy degrees and lecture the locals.

Chaplin, a 30-year-old who apparently feels a divine calling to Africa, was discovered as a newborn with her umbilical cord still attached - a charming start to life, no doubt. Rescued by British missionaries, she was shuffled through an orphanage before being adopted and whisked away to the UK. Now, armed with her skills in hand therapy and burns, she's volunteering on a Mercy Ship, which sounds suspiciously like a floating advertisement for Western guilt.

She claims exposure to Kenya's "extreme poverty" as a child motivated her. Funny, most people just get depressed. Chaplin, however, saw opportunity - the opportunity to acquire expensive education and then return to Africa to "help." She left a perfectly good job in the UK, because what else is a white saviour to do? Her heart, she says, was always to return to an African country once she had the "skill or experience." So, for years she was just… waiting? Waiting to be qualified enough to swoop in and fix things, I suppose. It’s the classic narrative: the privileged outsider returns to “uplift” the less fortunate. Reminds me a bit of how some politicians try to sell Kenya as an investment hub while conveniently ignoring the rot beneath; you can read about that here: [Ruto Tasks Diplomats: Sell Kenya as Investment Hub and Global Partner, But Don't Forget the Home Truths](/posts/ruto-tasks-diplomats--sell-kenya-as-investment-hub-and-global-partner--but-don-t-forget-the-home-truths).

Now she’s mentoring physiotherapists in Sierra Leone, a country that, until recently, had a laughable number of qualified practitioners. She’s proud of the new graduates, yet focuses on the children, specifically those under five, suffering burns from boiling water. It’s tragic, yes, but the implication is that these local physios, with their newly minted degrees, will somehow magically prevent such accidents. This isn't a fairytale where everyone suddenly becomes an expert overnight; it’s a complex issue requiring deep societal change, not just a visiting therapist with a good heart.

Chaplin talks about hoping to see "less contractures come to the ship" as Sierra Leone develops. It’s a nice sentiment, but let’s be real. Development is slow, and the reliance on external aid, however well-intentioned, can create its own set of problems. This whole story is a well-packaged narrative of redemption and altruism, but it conveniently glosses over the systemic issues that lead to such abandonment and hardship in the first place. It’s easy to be a hero when you can fly in, play savior, and then report back to your comfortable life.
