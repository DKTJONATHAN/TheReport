So, the East African Community has finally decided to stop pretending it is a functional family and admit it is a glorified WhatsApp group where half the members are on mute and nobody wants to pay the admin fees. At the Arusha summit, we watched the predictable handover as President William Ruto stepped aside for Yoweri Museveni to take the chair. Trading Ruto for Museveni is like swapping a high-speed motivational speaker for a landlord who has owned the building since the colonial era. It is the same old faces, just sitting in different seats while the Arusha secretariat struggles to keep the lights on because certain member states treat membership dues like a polite suggestion they can ignore.

The real news isn't the leadership swap, but the fact that they have finally realized the "joyride" is over. They have skipped the tradition of rotation to install Tanzanian bureaucrat Stephen Mbundi as Secretary General, effectively ghosting South Sudan because they haven't paid their bills. It is a classic Arusha move: if you don't bring anything to the potluck, you don't get to choose the music. The bloc is drowning in a financial mess so deep that staffers have gone months without pay. This desperate scramble for a new funding model is just a way to squeeze the "big boys" for more cash while acknowledging that [The Great Political Con: Why 'Friendly Fire' is Just Code for More Eating](/posts/the-great-political-con--why--friendly-fire--is-just-code-for-more-eating) is the only real policy that survives across these borders.

They have also decided that EALA MPs will now have their salaries paid by their home countries. Arusha will only handle the "allowances," which we all know is where the real "eating" happens anyway. This is a brilliant bit of buck-passing. If your country is too broke to pay its teachers, good luck getting them to prioritize a salary for an MP sitting in a plush office in Tanzania. It is a cynical attempt to downsize a bureaucracy that has grown too fat on a diet of empty promises and unpaid invoices. They are finally admitting that "integration" is an expensive hobby that most of these regimes can't actually afford.

Ruto, playing the role of the departing disciplinarian, announced that no senior employee will come from non-paying members. It is about time. You cannot expect to sit at the high table when you haven't even paid for your chair. They have even changed the quorum rules to 65 percent because waiting for all eight members to agree on anything was like waiting for a Nairobi bus in the rain - it never happens when you need it to. The fact that the presidents of South Sudan, DRC, and Rwanda skipped the meeting tells you everything you need to know about the "political will" they keep bragging about in their press releases.

Ultimately, the EAC is expanding its borders while its foundation is turning to dust. Adding Somalia and the DRC to a bloc that can't even pay its own secretariat is like adding more rooms to a house that has a leaking roof and no foundation. They are waiving 50 percent of the arrears for defaulters, which basically tells every member that if you ignore your bills long enough, you get a "loyalty" discount. It is a cynical, desperate cycle. We are being told this is "certainty," but for those of us watching from the streets of Nairobi, it just looks like the same old circus with a slightly different seating chart.
