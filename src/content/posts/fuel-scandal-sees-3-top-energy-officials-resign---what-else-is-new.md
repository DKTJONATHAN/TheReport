So, the big bosses in our petroleum supply chain are finally jumping ship. Mohamed Liban, Joe Sang, and Daniel Kiptoo - sounds like a cast of characters in a bad telenovela, doesn't it? Apparently, they’ve been caught with their hands in the cookie jar, fiddling with fuel stock data and lining their pockets with irregular procurement. The Kenya Pipeline Company MD even got the boot before he could hand in his resignation. Classic move.

This whole song and dance comes as the government is supposedly doing a "full inquiry" into "serious breaches." Like buying substandard emergency fuel at inflated prices, all while pretending to follow some fancy G2G framework. It's the same old story, just with more expensive fuel. You'd think after all these years, they'd at least try to be original with their scams, but no. It's always about the money, and who can get the biggest slice.

It's funny, isn't it? We're constantly lectured about tightening our belts, about the cost of living, while these people are busy orchestrating what are essentially daylight robberies. It makes you wonder if anyone is actually surprised. This is Kenya, after all. We've seen watchdogs forget to bark before, haven't we? [The Watchdog Who Forgot to Bark at His Own Poverty](/posts/the-watchdog-who-forgot-to-bark-at-his-own-poverty) is just the tip of the iceberg.

Now they're promising "more details" and "beefing up the story." Let's be real, this is just PR. The real story is that the system is rigged, and these resignations are just a temporary deflection. The public gets a show, the officials get a soft landing, and we, the common mwananchi, get to continue paying the price. Maybe next time, they'll try to steal our crypto instead. [The Sunday Morning Slaughter: Crypto Turns Geopolitical Chaos Into a 24-Hour Casino](/posts/the-sunday-morning-slaughter--crypto-turns-geopolitical-chaos-into-a-24-hour-casino) is a good primer on how money moves, or disappears.

And who's writing all this up? A young fellow named Kenneth Mwenda, who apparently joined People Daily last year. He's got a finance degree and a course in digital investigation techniques. Let's hope he's got the stomach for digging into this mess, or maybe he's just the latest recruit to polish the narrative. We'll see if he sticks around long enough to see the real consequences, or if he, too, will be persuaded by the "digital diet and the growing cost of AI energy use" - or rather, the cost of keeping quiet.

One thing's for sure, the energy sector in this country is a perpetual drama. From inflated prices to questionable procurement, it's a constant reminder of who's really in charge and who's paying the bill. These resignations are just a fleeting footnote in a much longer, much more corrupt saga.
