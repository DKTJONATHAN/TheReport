This whole Mandelson ambassador fiasco is a masterclass in how not to run a country, or even a local parish council. Apparently, Sir Keir Starmer would have "blocked" the appointment if he'd known about the security vetting issues. Well, isn't that convenient? The reality is, the Foreign Office, under the purview of those in power, managed to bungle this so badly that even the Opposition leader is claiming to be blindsided.

Liz Kendall, bless her heart, trotted out the line that Starmer was only told Mandelson had "developed vetting status," not that he'd actually failed it. So, we have a situation where critical security information was either deliberately withheld or so poorly managed that it never reached the people who were supposed to make the final call. It’s a classic case of finger pointing and plausible deniability, a well-worn playbook in this game.

David Lammy, who was Foreign Secretary at the time, claims he and his advisors were kept in the dark. Shocking, isn't it? This despite the fact that the Foreign Office's top civil servant, Sir Olly Robbins, was apparently ousted over this very mess. Lammy’s bewilderment over Robbins’ departure, while pointing out Robbins was only in the job for a few weeks when the vetting report came back, reeks of a calculated effort to deflect blame. There were "time pressures," you see, to get Mandelson in place post-Trump. National security, or political expediency? Take your pick.

Yvette Cooper confirms the vetting was a "priority clearance," but insists "full checks" were done. This is like saying a quick once-over of a building's foundation is a "full structural integrity check." When it comes to security clearances, "priority" and "full" often translate to "we'll get around to it if we have to."

Sir Keir himself calls it "staggering" he wasn't informed sooner. Indeed, the sheer incompetence or deliberate obstruction required to keep such a significant security failure under wraps is staggering. Helen MacNamara, a former senior civil servant, nails it when she says the government is desperately trying to find "new processes to blame, new people to blame." She even speculates that Foreign Office officials might have decided the risks were "priced in" and that their job was simply to "do what the prime minister wanted," with any necessary mitigations being an afterthought. This is not governance; it's a high-stakes gamble with taxpayer money and national safety.

The usual suspects are already circling. Conservative shadow minister Alex Burqhart, predictably, blames the Prime Minister. Robert Jenrick, from the Reform Party, is having a field day questioning Starmer’s competence, or lack thereof. Liberal Democrat leader Ed Davey chimes in with "catastrophic misjudgment." All of them are playing their parts in the political theatre, using the Mandelson vetting row as ammunition.

The real kicker is that Dame Emily Thornberry points out that Sir Olly Robbins' previous evidence to MPs now "called into question" his previous testimony. He apparently didn't disclose that the security vetting agency advised *against* granting Mandelson clearance. His allies claim he was bound by confidentiality. Confidentiality, or a convenient excuse to avoid admitting the mess until it blew up in their faces?

Sir Olly is expected to face the Foreign Affairs Committee again, presumably armed with a new set of carefully crafted statements. The whole affair is a testament to the cynical dance of power, where accountability is a rare commodity and competence is often sacrificed on the altar of political expediency. The public, as always, is left to clean up the mess and wonder how many other "red flags" are being quietly swept under the rug.
