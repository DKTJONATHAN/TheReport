Darling, we have all been there. You are trying to secure the bag, looking all professional in your Sunday best, only for someone to bring up that one unhinged rant you posted at 2 AM. University of Nairobi law lecturer Migai Akech learned this the hard way while auditioning for a spot as a Court of Appeal judge. Imagine trying to fill one of those 15 lucrative vacancies and suddenly the panel starts acting like your bitter ex, scrolling through your social media history just to ruin your vibe. It is honestly the kind of digital karma that makes my cynical heart skip a beat.

It is hilarious how these intellectual types think they can play the revolutionary on X and then pivot to being "Your Lordship" without any friction. Akech was out here talking about how Ruto's government needs to initiate an inclusive process of reforming the system for everyone, not just the chosen few. That is cute and all, but in the world of high-stakes recruitment, those "inclusive" vibes usually translate to "unreliable narrator" for the people holding the checkbook. You cannot scream for reform in the morning and expect the establishment to hand you the keys to the kingdom in the afternoon.

This whole situation reeks of the same manufactured authenticity we see in the political arena every day. It reminds me of [Lydia Wanyoto’s 'Clean' Campaign: The Ultimate Political PR Stunt](/posts/lydia-wanyoto-s--clean--campaign--the-ultimate-political-pr-stunt) where the public face never quite matches the behind-the-scenes reality. Whether you are a politician or a law lecturer, trying to scrub your digital footprint to fit a specific narrative is the oldest trick in the book. Unfortunately for Akech, the JSC has better internet access than he probably anticipated. They were not just interested in his knowledge of the Supreme Court's power over state of emergency declarations; they wanted to know why he was being so loud online.

Even his takes on the IEBC were fair game. Sure, he might have claimed the election was credible despite "various challenges," but that kind of middle-of-the-road fence-sitting is exactly what gets you grilled. In the era of clout-chasing, even the most prestigious legal minds find themselves trapped in the same cycle of performance as your average Instagram influencer. The only difference is that when an influencer fails, they lose a brand deal. When a judge fails, they lose a lifetime of power and a very comfortable pension.

So, let this be a lesson to all the aspiring elites out there. If you want to sit in the second-highest court in the land, maybe keep your opinions on government reform in the drafts. The hypocrisy of the system is real, but so is the delete button. Until these guys realize that the internet is forever, I will be right here with my popcorn, watching their career aspirations go up in smoke one tweet at a time. It is messy, it is petty, and it is exactly what we deserve.
