ACTIVISTS STILL BEING ABDUCTED IN KENYA: THE SAME OLD STORY, DIFFERENT YEAR

WARNING: Another year, another presidential promise. Human rights groups are screaming that activists and ordinary citizens are still disappearing in Kenya. Forget the slick PR; the reality on the ground is grim, with families still desperately searching for loved ones who vanished, especially after protests.

This isn't new. It's the same song and dance we've seen before. Remember the Gen Z protests against that ridiculous finance bill? Families are *still* waiting for answers. Now, even after President Ruto said he'd put a stop to it, the disappearances continue. It's almost as if saying "we'll stop it" doesn't actually mean anything.

Activists like Davis Lichuma are proving that point. He got "reported missing" and then conveniently turned up injured in a hospital. And Dennis Chege? Still gone, swallowed up by the system since those 2024 protests. Human rights organizations point fingers at the police, saying they're not doing their job, or worse. The authorities, of course, deny everything. It’s a classic case of "not us, never us."

You’d think after all this, things would change. But here we are. It makes you wonder if the cost of keeping the cops happy [Blood Money Or A Bonus? The Cost Of Keeping The Cops Happy](/posts/blood-money-or-a-bonus--the-cost-of-keeping-the-cops-happy) is more important than basic human rights. This isn't a surprise; it's just Kenya being Kenya.

This relentless cycle of disappearances, denials, and broken promises is a chilling reminder that some shadows never truly lift. For the families left behind, it's not just a headline; it's a gaping wound that refuses to heal.

Activists are still being snatched off the streets in Kenya, and the government's promises to end these enforced disappearances are starting to sound as hollow as a politician's apology. Two years after the Gen Z protests against that cursed finance bill, the search for missing loved ones continues. It’s a stark reminder that while some might be busy with the ‘Baddie’ Industrial Complex, others are just trying to find their family members.

Human rights groups are sounding the alarm, saying abductions and disappearances are a “serious concern” despite President William Ruto’s 2024 pledge to put a stop to them. Families are still left holding empty spaces where their brothers, sisters, or parents used to be, especially after the protests against the finance bill and subsequent demonstrations. It's a tragedy repeating itself, with a new date stamped on the same old pain.

Take activist Davis Lichuma, who was conveniently "reported missing" only to be found later, injured, in a hospital. Then there's Dennis Chege, who hasn't been seen since the 2024 protests. Human rights organizations are accusing the police of failing to prevent these abuses, but the authorities are, predictably, denying any involvement and rejecting the allegations of enforced disappearances. It’s the usual playbook: deflect, deny, and hope everyone forgets.

This isn't just about activists; it's about the entire atmosphere of fear that descends whenever dissent bubbles up. It makes you question the real priorities. Is it worth the risk to speak out, when the potential price could be your very existence? You wonder if the system that allows this sort of thing to fester is as broken as the promises made to fix it. But for the families grappling with the agonizing uncertainty of a missing person, these denials offer little comfort. They are left to navigate a labyrinth of bureaucracy and unanswered questions, their hope dwindling with each passing day.

Ultimately, the message is clear: if you dare to challenge the status quo, especially in the wake of public outcry, you might just find yourself another statistic in Kenya's grim tally of the disappeared. The cycle continues, and the whispers of disappearances cast a long, dark shadow.
