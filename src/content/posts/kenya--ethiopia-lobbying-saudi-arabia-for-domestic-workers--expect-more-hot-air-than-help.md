So, Kenya and Ethiopia are having a chat with Saudi Arabia about how they treat the poor souls sent to work there as domestic staff. Apparently, the Gulf kingdom, which has a reputation for treating foreign workers like disposable tissues, is suddenly feeling a bit of pressure. With the 2034 World Cup coming up, they're making all sorts of noises about reform. Kenya even signed some fancy "Labour Recruitment and Employment Agreement" in July 2026. Sounds official, doesn't it? Like it'll actually change anything.

Barely a week after that parchment was signed, we hear about Justin Mugendi, a 33-year-old Kenyan, allegedly held captive by his former employer in Riyadh for over three months. His documents confiscated, confined to an empty house, all because he dared to find a new job. His village is scrambling to raise a pittance for his release while our government is busy signing more MOUs. Faith Odhiambo, formerly of the Law Society of Kenya, nails it: this is "a diplomatic and economic instrument," not a protective one. It's about converting "strong political relationships" into cash, not about keeping our citizens safe. Reminds me of that time [Ghana Acts While Kenya Naps: The Cowardice of Our Diplomacy](/posts/ghana-acts-while-kenya-naps--the-cowardice-of-our-diplomacy) - same old story of reactive, ineffective posturing.

The truth is, these agreements are about as binding as a New Year's resolution made after too much chang'aa. The Kafala system, where workers are tied to employers, might be officially out, but the practices persist. It’s always been about the employer's hold, the limited capacity of our own consular officers to do anything meaningful, and the fact that Saudi courts are the only ones with any real power. We’ve seen the reports: unexplained deaths, mysterious falls, rapes, and abuse that often go unpunished. Hundreds of Kenyans have died in Saudi Arabia alone. Is this the "practical economic opportunity" Mudavadi was so excited about?

And it’s not just Kenyans. Ethiopians are in the same mess, with over 750,000 of them in Saudi Arabia facing similar struggles. While Ethiopia’s ministry drones on about "constructive cooperation" and "humanitarian outcomes" from royal amnesties, the grim reality of executions and continued legal challenges for their citizens remains. It’s a PR exercise, pure and simple. Both governments are pushing these labour export schemes - Kenya’s "Kazi Majuu," Uganda’s programme - to tackle unemployment. They’re sending our youth to a place where they're "rendered invisible, being excluded from the labour law that governs protections for other workers."

Amnesty International points out that Saudi Arabia hosts nearly four million migrant domestic workers, and many live "hidden, isolated and completely segregated lives." Behind closed doors, it's abuse, exploitation, and discrimination. Ugandan groups are tracking over 40 worker deaths between January and mid-2026, with Saudi Arabia topping the list. Their chief executive laments the lack of a "comprehensive real-time tracking system." In other words, we have no idea what’s really happening until it’s too late, and by then, it’s usually a body bag arriving home.

So, while Kenya and Ethiopia "lobby" and sign more papers, the fundamental issues remain unaddressed. It's a rigged game where the promises are cheap, and the cost is paid by the most vulnerable. We should all be asking ourselves if this is the price we're willing to pay for a few dollars in remittances and some feel-good headlines about diplomatic breakthroughs. Because right now, it looks like a raw deal, with a high human cost and very little actual protection.
