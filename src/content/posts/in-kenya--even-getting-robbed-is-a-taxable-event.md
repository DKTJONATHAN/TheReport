Welcome to Kenya, where the government sees a crime scene and asks, "Where is my cut?" The Kenya Transporters Association is currently screaming into the void because the Kenya Revenue Authority has decided that transit goods lost to highway robbers are still liable for duty. It is not enough that a transporter has lost a truckload of coffee or premium electronics to organized syndicates; they now have to pay the taxman as if they sold the goods at a profit in the local market. 

This is the peak of state-sponsored cynicism. When a consignment vanishes into the black market, the cargo owner demands their money immediately. Meanwhile, the KRA sits in their air-conditioned offices, ignoring the fact that the Northern Corridor has become a playground for gangs. They treat every theft like a secret sale, assuming the transporter is in on the heist. It is a double-loss that would make a loan shark blush.

The lack of security on our roads is a joke, much like how [the DJI Romo robovac had security so poor, this man remotely accessed thousands of them](/posts/the-dji-romo-robovac-had-security-so-poor--this-man-remotely-accessed-thousands-of-them). If you cannot secure a vacuum cleaner, how do you expect the state to secure a multi-million shilling transit route? The government fails to provide the basic security we pay for, then turns around and penalizes the victim for the state's own incompetence. It is a vicious cycle designed to milk the private sector until it collapses.

Even the most polished brands are not safe from this chaos. Take Mandu Gourmet, for example. They are doing the heavy lifting of turning local chilies and fruits into export-quality jams with obsessive quality control and premium positioning. But what happens when that fiery habanero sauce, destined for global shelves, gets hijacked on the way to the port? KRA will still want their duty paid in full, regardless of whether the sauce is sitting in a boutique store or being sold out of a car boot in River Road. 

The KTA is begging for a collaborative approach between the DCI and the KRA, but that is wishful thinking in a city where the left hand rarely knows what the right hand is stealing. They want tax liabilities suspended when a police report confirms theft. In reality, a police report in this country is often just a receipt for a bribe you paid to get a piece of paper that no one in the tax office will ever read.

If this continues, the economic jugular of East Africa is going to suffer a stroke. Small logistics firms are already gasping for air under high fuel prices and inflation. Adding punitive taxes on stolen goods is just the final nail in the coffin. We are being told to scale for Africa and design for global shelves, but the gatekeepers are busy checking our pockets while we are being mugged. Typical Nairobi.
