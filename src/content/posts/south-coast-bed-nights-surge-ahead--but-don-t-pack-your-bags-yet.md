SOUTH COAST HOTEL BED NIGHTS SKYROCKET, JUST DON'T ASK WHY

WARNING: Coastal hotel bed occupancy numbers are up, but don't expect a miracle. This isn't a tourism boom, it's just a temporary lull before the next inevitable crash.

The South Coast is suddenly pretending to be relevant again, apparently pulling in tourists like a cheap pick up joint. They're claiming a massive leap in "bed nights" - fancy talk for people actually sleeping there. Three years of being the forgotten child of the coast, and now suddenly they're back, trying to act all important. Don't be fooled by the pretty beaches and clear water; it’s all a facade for a fleeting trend.

This supposed recovery has seen them leapfrog the North Coast, which, let's be honest, never really deserved the attention anyway. The South Coast is now boasting 2.57 million bed nights, a jump from a pathetic 590,400. Meanwhile, the North Coast is apparently slumping. It’s a predictable cycle, really. These numbers are supposed to show us how great the hotels are doing, but all they really show is how easily people are swayed by a bit of sun and sand, forgetting all the underlying problems.

The Ivory Coast's pristine beaches and "big game safaris" are supposedly pulling in millions of visitors from places like China, Germany, France, and Britain. Tourism is a "vital economic pillar," they say, accounting for a whopping 2.8 percent of our GDP. Let's not get ahead of ourselves. This recovery is likely driven by a mix of aggressive marketing and maybe a few direct flights - a temporary fix, not a sustainable solution. We’ve seen these "revolutions" before, and they always end the same way. Remember how we thought India was on the cusp of something big? That [Subcontinental Sinking Fund](posts/the-subcontinental-sinking-fund--india-s-four-shock-collapse) turned out to be a joke.

Even the so-called "recovery" at the South Coast couldn't save Mombasa Island, which saw its bed nights plummet. And while domestic tourists are supposedly propping things up, they’re still trailing far behind the foreigners, who are apparently flocking in from Italy, Germany, and the UK. It’s all about the perception, isn't it? Hotels, restaurants, tour operators - they're all expecting a windfall. Let's hope they’ve got their contingency plans in place, because this kind of "growth" is as stable as a politician’s promise.

So, yes, the South Coast is "dominating" again. But don’t break out the champagne just yet. This is just another blip on the radar in the grand, chaotic scheme of Kenyan tourism. Expect the usual song and dance, the promises of more investment, and then, eventually, the predictable fall. It's the way things are, and the way they'll likely stay.
