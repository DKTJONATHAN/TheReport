So, Konshens, the dancehall star who apparently has too much time on his hands, wants Kenyans to stop comparing him to Vybz Kartel. He took to Instagram, probably after checking his "likes" count, to explain how these comparisons are like the never-ending Ronaldo versus Messi debate. Apparently, it's taking away from his "own accomplishments." Bless his heart.

It's fascinating how these guys equate constant comparison to a sign of their own importance, rather than a reflection of how deeply entrenched certain artists are in the culture. For over a decade, Kenyans have been trying to figure out who’s the king of the riddim, and frankly, it’s a debate that’s more entertaining than half the music they’ve released lately. This whole saga reminds me of how discussions around development and progress can often get sidetracked by political grandstanding, much like how we're seeing in the [Ruto Cries '60 Years of Neglect' Over Northern Kenya Development Woes](/posts/ruto-cries--60-years-of-neglect--over-northern-kenya-development-woes).

Konshens goes on to say we should "love me for me," as if we're in a romantic relationship with his music. He’s clearly forgotten that in this city, and in this country, we’re busy trying to survive. Worrying about whether Konshens is better than Vybz Kartel is a luxury most of us can’t afford. We're more concerned with the rising cost of living, the dodgy deals happening behind closed doors, and whether the matatu conductor will actually give us our change.

The reality is, Vybz Kartel is a monumental figure in dancehall. He’s a lyricist, a businessman, a phenomenon. Konshens has his hits, sure, "Gal a Bubble" and all that. But to ask us to ignore the shadow of "di teacha" is like asking a Nairobi landlord to ignore the rent increase. It’s not happening. We appreciate what we appreciate, and sometimes, that means acknowledging the giants who paved the way, even if it means a popular singer gets a bit grumpy.

So, Konshens, while you're busy comparing yourself to Cristiano Ronaldo and lamenting the unfairness of it all, maybe take a step back. Your fans in Kenya are probably more focused on making rent and avoiding traffic jams than dissecting your lyrical prowess against a legend. Just make good music, and if people compare you, well, that’s the price of fame, isn't it? Or perhaps you're just trying to distract us from the fact that [The Petro-Illusion: Why Global Conflict Only Enriches the Few while Starving the Many](/posts/the-petro-illusion--why-global-conflict-only-enriches-the-few-while-starving-the-many) is a much bigger issue.
