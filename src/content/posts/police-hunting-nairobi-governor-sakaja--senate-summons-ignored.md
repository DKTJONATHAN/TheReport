So, the great Johnson Sakaja has apparently gone AWOL. The Nairobi Governor, who seems to think parliamentary summons are optional suggestions, is now on the police's wanted list. Regional Police Commander Issa Mohamud is out there, "looking for the governor all day," like some sort of lost sheep, all because Sakaja decided his time is more valuable than accounting for public funds. Typical.

This isn't exactly groundbreaking behaviour, is it? We’ve seen this script before. Governors deciding they’re above the rest of us, especially when it comes to explaining where our money goes. It’s a whole standoff, this Senate versus governors thing, and frankly, it’s getting tiresome. They’re so busy playing power games, accountability is just a dirty word. It’s like watching a bunch of toddlers squabbling over toys, except these toddlers are managing our cities and counties.

CPAC chairperson Moses Kajwang’ has had enough, apparently reading the governors "the riot act." As if fines and arrest warrants are going to magically make them responsible. They're threatening to declare these non-compliant leaders unfit for office. Good luck with that. These guys have more layers of protection than a presidential limousine, and they’ve probably already got their escape routes planned. It reminds me of how these so-called influencers peddle their "soft life" dreams, but their entire existence is probably built on shaky foundations, [much like some of the schemes we've seen before](/posts/the--soft-life--is-a-scam--your-favorite-influencer-s-airbnb-is-probably-illegal).

And it's not just Sakaja. Lati Lelelit, Muthomi Njuki, Simba Arati, Ahmed Abdullahi, Fernandes Barasa, Gladys Wanga, Erick Mutai, Joseph ole Lenku - the whole gang is under fire. It’s a parade of shame, really. These are the people we entrust with our taxes, and their priority is dodging questions. The Senate is trying to do its job, but it seems a lot of these governors are more interested in their own comfortable "soft life" than in the welfare of the citizens they swore to serve.

The police are "here to present the governor before the Senate. It's a legal obligation under the Constitution." Oh, the sweet irony. A legal obligation that’s proving rather difficult to fulfil because the man in question has seemingly mastered the art of disappearing. Let’s hope they find him before he finds another excuse to dodge his responsibilities. This whole circus, however, is just another testament to the enduring reality of political theatre in Nairobi.
