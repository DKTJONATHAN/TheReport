So, President Ruto is out in Kisii, telling everyone how the government-to-government fuel deal is making Kenya the "competitive fuel market" of the region. He’s going on about how other countries are running dry, but here, we’ve got enough. He even claims they've got a "subsidy" to keep high costs from reaching the "common citizen." Please. It sounds like the same old song and dance we always hear when fuel prices start to bite harder than a hungry hyena.

He's promising to "cushion Kenyans from high fuel prices" and keep an eye on "global and domestic fuel trends." This G2G framework, he says, has somehow ironed out supply stability and stopped market manipulation. Meanwhile, the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) is admitting that "global price shocks continue to exert pressure on local pump prices." Landed costs for petrol and diesel have jumped by over 40 and 50 percent respectively, with kerosene doing a ridiculous 100 percent leap. And let's not forget, Kenya relies entirely on imports, so we're always at the mercy of exchange rates and whoever's holding the oil barrels.

Then there's the "relief" they've handed out: a measly 3 percent VAT cut from 16 to 13 percent, and a Sh6.2 billion dibble from the Petroleum Development Levy. They’re trying to make it sound like a grand gesture, but considering the massive price hikes, this is like offering a single drop of water to someone dying of thirst in the desert. EPRA itself acknowledges these are "broader measures to stabilise pump prices amid sharp increases." It’s a patch on a gaping wound.

It’s the same old story, isn't it? When global tech giants are facing their own fiscal backlashes over things like AI power demand, you can bet the ripple effect will eventually hit our already strained pockets. [The Energy Ultimatum: Global Tech Faces Fiscal Backlash Over AI Power Demand](/posts/the-energy-ultimatum--global-tech-faces-fiscal-backlash-over-ai-power-demand) is a good reminder that these "external economic pressures" aren't just theoretical; they translate into real pain at the pump.

The opposition is already pointing fingers at "systemic failures and vested interests" in the petroleum supply chain. And honestly, after seeing how these prices are still climbing despite the so-called interventions, it’s hard not to agree. This isn't about global shocks; it's about a broken system that seems designed to make sure we always pay more, no matter what promises are made.

So, yes, President Ruto can claim Kenya is a competitive fuel market. But for the average Kenyan watching their hard-earned cash disappear faster than a mirage in the Turkana desert, that competitiveness feels like a cruel joke. We’re still footing the bill, and that 3 percent VAT cut is hardly enough to make us forget the real cost of living.
