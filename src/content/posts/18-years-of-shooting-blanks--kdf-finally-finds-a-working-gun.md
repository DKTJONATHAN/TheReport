It took 18 years for the Kenya Defence Forces to remember how to win a shooting competition. Let that sink in. Since 2008, our supposed protectors have been outclassed by the National Police Service, mostly because the KDF was busy lugging around inferior equipment that probably belonged in a museum. Sergeant Michael Gitonga finally broke the dry spell at the 87th Kenya Open Fullbore Shooting Championship in Nanyuki, but only after someone had the bright idea to give him a "refurbished" gun. In Nairobi terms, that is like winning a drag race in a 1998 Matatu because you finally fixed the side mirror.

Gitonga managed to scrape together 140.007 points to beat the police shooters who have treated this trophy like a family heirloom for two decades. The fact that the KDF admits to using "inferior equipment" for years while their police counterparts lived the high life is the most Kenyan thing you will hear today. It is the same pattern of institutional rot we see everywhere, just like the way [why private schools quietly sabotaged Kenya's Grade 10 transition](/posts/why-kenyas-private-schools-are-quietly-sabotaging-the-grade-10-transition) to protect their own interests while the public system choked. Here, the military was basically bringing knives to a gunfight and wondering why they kept losing to Chief Inspectors.

The 48-year-old sergeant credited a "well-fitting jacket" and a recycled telescope for his victory. It is pathetic, really. We are celebrating a man who had to "tap into the knowledge" of retired legends just to figure out how to gauge the wind. If our elite soldiers need nearly twenty years and a second-hand telescope to hit a target at 900 metres, we should all be very worried about who is guarding the borders. Gitonga is now aiming for the UK Bisley in July, but unless he finds a better tailor and some more scrap metal to fix his rifle, he is just going there to see the sights.

The organizers even tried to sugar-coat the event by claiming it was about raising awareness for gender-based violence. Because nothing says "peace and safety" like a bunch of men in uniform firing high-powered rounds at a range in Laikipia. It is the classic PR move: take a story about military incompetence and equipment neglect, then wrap it in a "noble cause" so nobody asks why it took 18 years to win a home-grown tournament.

Gitonga calls Major (Retired) Peter Mathenge a "living legend," but that is just a polite way of saying the KDF has not produced anyone relevant since the Kibaki era. While the police dominated post-Covid-19 events with clinical precision, the KDF was essentially playing with toys. Now that they have "turned the tables" with refurbished junk, we are supposed to clap. I will wait to see if they can hold onto the trophy for more than five minutes before the police reclaim their territory and the KDF goes back to finishing fifth with a jammed bolt.
