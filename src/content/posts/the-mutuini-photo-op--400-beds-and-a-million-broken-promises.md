The duo is back at it again, parading through Dagoretti South like they have just discovered fire. This time, it is the Mutuini Hospital expansion, a 400-bed facility that is supposed to save us from the death traps we call public hospitals. They are calling it a "Level 5" facility, but in Nairobi, we know that the level usually refers to the number of hours you will wait before a tired nurse tells you there is no Panadol. President Ruto is promising 400 million shillings worth of equipment, which sounds great until you realize that "promises" are the only thing this administration produces in abundance.

They claim patients under the Social Health Authority (SHA) will get free services. That would be hilarious if it was not so insulting. Most of us are still trying to figure out why the transition from NHIF feels like jumping out of a frying pan and into a bonfire. It is the same old story of political optics. While they talk about decongesting Mbagathi and Mama Lucy, the reality on the ground is that these "pacts" are often just [The Broad-Based Buffet: When the Vultures Start Biting Each Other](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-the-vultures-start-biting-each-other) where the elite divide the spoils while we fight for a single bed in a crowded ward.

Sakaja is busy shouting about an 80 billion shilling investment framework for roads and drainage. Look around you. One afternoon of rain and the city becomes a giant swimming pool for frogs. If they had invested even half of that "framework" into actual drainage instead of colorful posters and PR tours, we might not need an amphibious vehicle to get to work in the morning. This "Ruto-Sakaja pact" feels less like a service delivery agreement and more like a survival strategy for 2027.

The tour did not stop at hospitals. They moved to PC Kinyanjui to commission a workshop and promise more hostels. It is the classic Nairobi trap: build the classrooms, employ the teachers, and then leave the graduates to roam the streets with degrees that no one wants. They call education the "greatest equalizer," but in this city, the only thing that equalizes us is the shared frustration of watching our tax money fund high-profile inspections while the cost of unga remains a vertical climb.

Nairobi is supposed to "rise to the level of other great cities," according to the Governor. That is a bold statement from a man whose city still struggles with basic waste management and a water billing system that is essentially a lottery. We are being sold a dream of "modernized trading spaces" while hawkers are chased like criminals from the very streets these leaders claim to be fixing. It is all a performance, and as usual, the citizens are the ones paying for the front-row seats.

By the time they finished their tour of 17 classroom blocks and handed over "tuition blocks" in Kawangware, the message was clear. They want us to focus on the bricks and mortar so we do not ask where the rest of the 80 billion shillings is hiding. We have seen these ribbon-cutting ceremonies before. They look good on the evening news, but once the motorcade leaves and the dust settles, we are left with the same old Nairobi: expensive, exhausting, and governed by people who think a hospital bed is a substitute for a functioning economy.
