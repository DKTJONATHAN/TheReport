So, the boys in blue finally decided to earn their salt in Ruai. They found 70 souls - 66 Ethiopians and 4 Eritreans - packed into a house like cheap charcoal. They call it a rescue, but let us be real: in this city, being "rescued" often just means moving from one type of cage to another. The police claim it was a tip-off from the public, which is Nairobi code for "someone didn't get their cut of the bribe this month."

They arrested one Kenyan national in connection with the whole thing. Just one. You really think one guy is running a cross-border logistics operation involving 70 people? Please. The big fish are likely sitting in air-conditioned offices, probably the same ones drafting those fancy "inter-agency" press releases. It reminds me of the usual police theater where they [Arrest Now, Invent the Crime Later: The Mwabili Mwagodi Special](/posts/arrest-now--invent-the-crime-later--the-mwabili-mwagodi-special) just to look busy for the cameras while the real syndicates keep the wheels turning.

This comes hot on the heels of the JKIA drama where they caught people with forged papers. It is a booming business, after all. Whether it is Ethiopians trying to get south or Kenyans being lured to Russia with the promise of "jobs" only to end up as cannon fodder in a military role, the desperation is the same. People are so tired of starving at home that they are willing to gamble their lives on a stranger's lie. The cost of living is so high that even a war zone looks like a career move.

The government is now bragging about AIRCOP and the Better Migration Management programme. More acronyms, more meetings, and more donor funding to keep the bureaucrats happy. They talk about "coordinated intelligence sharing" like they are in a Hollywood movie, but the reality is a dusty room in Ruai with no ventilation and dozens of terrified foreigners. If these programs worked as well as the brochures say, we would not be finding human warehouses in residential suburbs every other week.

The police are telling us to remain "vigilant" and report suspicious activities. That is rich, considering most of us are too busy trying to figure out how to pay for unga to play detective for a service that rarely shows up unless there is a bribe or a photo-op involved. Human trafficking is a "serious crime," they say. Sure, it is serious for the victims, but for the syndicates and their facilitators, it is just another profitable Tuesday in the Nairobi economy. Don't hold your breath for the "big fish" to see the inside of a cell.
