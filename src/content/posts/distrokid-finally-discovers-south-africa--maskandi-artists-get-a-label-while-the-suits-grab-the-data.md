The music industry is patting itself on the back again, and honestly, I am exhausted. This week, DistroKid decided to act like they have discovered fire because they finally added "Maskandi" and "Xigaza" to their genre dropdown menu. It is 2024, but apparently, the executives in New York just realized that South Africa is not one giant, homogenous drum circle. They are calling it a "significant milestone," but for the artists who have been buried under the "World Music" label for decades, it feels more like a late apology than a revolution.

We are being told by market consultants like Unathi Memela that this is a "technological breakthrough." It is a database update, honey, let us not get carried away. While the corporate elite love to claim that [Data Infrastructure Efficiency Becomes Critical Pillar for Global Enterprise Scalability](/posts/data-infrastructure-efficiency-becomes-critical-pillar-for-global-enterprise-scalability), the reality for local musicians is usually much less glamorous. This move is less about "cultural triumph" and more about ensuring the digital machine can track every cent with surgical precision. If they cannot categorize you, they cannot monetize you effectively, and we know DistroKid loves their subscription fees.

For years, the "World Music" tag has been the industry’s favorite trash can for anything that does not sound like a generic pop synth from Los Angeles. It was a lazy way of "Othering" brilliant musicians by pretending their heritage was just a niche curiosity. Now, suddenly, these sounds are "cultural archives" and "vibrant art forms." The hypocrisy is loud. They want the prestige of "preserving heritage" without acknowledging that they are the ones who ignored the heritage until it became a "trending" market opportunity.

The press release is making big promises about better royalty tracking through SAMRO and SAMPRA. I will believe that when I see a Maskandi legend actually getting a check that covers more than a round of drinks at the local tavern. We have heard the "algorithmic visibility" speech before. In a world where a fifteen-second TikTok dance determines your career, having a specific genre label is just a tiny life jacket in a very crowded, very cynical ocean.

So, let us give a half-hearted clap for DistroKid for finally doing the bare minimum. Maskandi and Xigaza artists can now upload their music without feeling like they are hiding their identity. It is a win, I suppose, but let us not pretend the industry has suddenly developed a soul. They just realized that South African culture is a "dynamic contributor" that they can finally put into a neat, taxable little box.

Don't get it twisted-the music is legendary, but the suits are just looking for the next stream. Stay woke, because the moment these genres stop being "vibrant," the industry will find a new "milestone" to sell us. For now, enjoy the "recognition" while it lasts, before they find a way to automate the Zulu guitar and replace the artists with AI.
