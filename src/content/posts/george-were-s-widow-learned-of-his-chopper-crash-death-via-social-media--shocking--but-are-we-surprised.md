Oh, darling, the sheer *audacity* of social media to deliver devastating news. Mama Emily Were, bless her heart, found out her husband, Captain George Were, was gone via her phone screen. While she was grappling with the shock, I was busy scrolling through TikTok trends. Honestly, it’s a miracle anyone gets any real news anymore without it being curated by algorithms and filtered through a million memes.

And let's talk about the immediate aftermath. Her first thought? "Gotta tell the kids before they see it online!" Because, of course, in this day and age, a family tragedy is just another piece of content waiting to go viral. She even had to direct her son to break the news to his brother, and then *another* son had to inform his sister. The drama of it all! Apparently, her daughter Stacy was so miffed about hearing it from her brother that she ghosted her own mother for two whole days. Talk about a spoiled brat moment, even in mourning. It’s almost as dramatic as the Runda Bubbles drama we covered last month; same script, different tragedy.

Now, Emily paints a picture of her late husband, George, as this peaceful rhumba-loving family man. "Mpenda Amani," they called him. Adorable. She even admitted that marrying a military man wasn't exactly a walk in the park back in '93. Took her brother some convincing too. Honestly, who marries a pilot when they know these things happen? It’s like dating a reality TV star and then being surprised when they’re on camera all the time.

The whole crash, which also claimed Emurua Dikirr MP Johana Ng’eno and four others, is just a grim reminder that the rich and famous (and those associated with them) often live on the edge. Six people gone, a helicopter in flames, and all we get is a behind-the-scenes look at grief through the lens of a smartphone. It’s enough to make you want to delete all your apps and go live in a cave.

Friends and family at the burial are all talking about how gentle and disciplined George was. Of course they are. It's the obligatory eulogy script. But the real story here, the one that actually makes the gossip columns, is how we’re all so plugged in that we can’t even have a private tragedy anymore. It's all broadcast, hashtagged, and, dare I say, monetized by clicks.

So, Mama Emily, while we extend our deepest, most performative condolences, let's not pretend this is some groundbreaking revelation. Social media is a beast, and it devours everything, even the most sacred moments. Next time, maybe just unplug the Wi Fi for a day. Just a thought.
