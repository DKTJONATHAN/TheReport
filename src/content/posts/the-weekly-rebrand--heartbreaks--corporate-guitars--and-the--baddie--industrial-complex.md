Welcome back to the circus, where every artist is suddenly a 'visionary' and every breakup is a 'spiritual deconstruction.' Zoë Modiga is leading the pack this week with "Something New," a track she claims is about shifting the needle. She is busy inspecting and reassembling her 'yard,' which is just fancy talk for crying over an ex while trying to make cabaret-amapiano a thing. She claims hate isn't in her 'pedigree,' yet she spends the whole song praying her ex finds anyone but a clone of themselves. It is the kind of passive-aggressive energy we usually reserve for Twitter threads, but I guess when you call it 'The Vault,' people think it is art instead of just a messy voice note.

Then we have Fatoumata Diawara, who is now the face of a signature Epiphone guitar. It is a historic moment for women of color, sure, but let us look at the optics. She is draped in Zulu beadwork and isicholo hats despite being the pride of Mali. It is giving 'Pan-Africanism for the Pinterest board' aesthetic. While she shreds through West African traditions to satisfy the world music critics, the corporate machine is busy slapping her name on wood and strings. It is almost as cynical as [Boni’s big reveal: as if we didn't know the state was already in our pockets](/posts/boni-s-big-reveal--as-if-we-didn-t-know-the-state-was-already-in-our-pockets) when it comes to who really profits from these 'historic' cultural milestones.

The heartbreak math this week is also getting out of hand. Tower is out here singing about thinking of someone 27 out of 31 days a month. Honestly, if you are doing that much arithmetic on your grief, you do not need a recording studio, you need a spreadsheet and a therapist. Meanwhile, RAPHA is shouting about being the 'happiest ever' on a track called "goodbye." If you were actually happy, you would be at a brunch in Brussels, not leaning on a pop-rock sonic palette to tell us how over it you are. We see through the 'healing' narrative; it is just a road trip playlist filler.

Moliy is still trying to make the 'baddie' lifestyle her entire personality with "Partygyal." We get it, you are committed to the cause. But when you are taking cues from Amaarae’s songbook and hiring the same producers who worked for Tyla, it starts to feel less like a movement and more like a factory line. Everyone wants to be 'charged' and 'spiritual' on the dance floor until the lights come on and the streaming numbers don't match the ego. It is the baddie industrial complex at work, turning 'spiritual' vibes into marketable commodities for people who think wearing sunglasses indoors is a personality trait.

Finally, we have the 'creative endeavors' of Shabaka and the recycling project that is Joeboy’s "Solace II." Shabaka decided to 'rap' because he had some beats lying around, which is the musical equivalent of a chef deciding to perform surgery because they are good with a knife. And Joeboy? Re-releasing a song from January just to add Kunmie is a classic move when the first version didn't give the 'aura' the label promised. They call it 'future-proofed,' but in this town, we just call it a desperate bid for a second chance at going viral. Stay cynical, kids.
