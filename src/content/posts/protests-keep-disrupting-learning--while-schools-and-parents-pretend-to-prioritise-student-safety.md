Here we go again. Another June 25th, another anniversary of 'protests' that means nothing more than predictable chaos for ordinary Kenyans. The source material talks about "uncertainty" for families, "concerns over safety," and "difficult decisions." Please. This isn't uncertainty; it's the usual song and dance. Every time the streets heat up, our schools become collateral damage, and parents are left weighing the risks of sending their kids out to face whatever fresh hell awaits.

This year, some lucky CBE learners got a reprieve, shielded by a perfectly timed half-term break that started just before the main event. Good for them, I suppose. But for those in other curricula, it was business as usual- meaning, business disrupted. Mr. Abdi Noor from KESSHA states the bleeding obvious: "violent protests always affect the education sector because students and teachers are caught up in the melee while commuting." Yes, we know. When roads are barricaded and tear gas is flying, getting to class becomes a dangerous adventure, not an academic pursuit. It's the same old story, year in, year out.

And let's not forget the impression it leaves on the kids themselves. Willie Kuria, KESSHA Chairman, points out that students feel "Gen Zs are one of them" and want to join the "noise." So, our children aren't just missing lessons; they're getting a masterclass in street politics and rebellion. They watch the 'demonstrators,' see them as peers, and suddenly, the classroom feels like a prison. Why sit in a stuffy room when there's a spectacle outside? It fuels a rebellious streak, making teachers out to be the bad guys for simply trying to enforce some semblance of order while the city burns.

This disruption isn't just about kids missing a few hours of math. Elias Cheruiyot, Dagoretti High School principal, reminds us it inhibits movement for everything- teachers, day scholars, even the delivery of essential supplies. School programs are interrupted, especially for day schools which often just shut down. Remember May, when schools in Nairobi, Mombasa, and Machakos suspended classes over "safety fears" linked to [Workers' Pain Deepens: Fuel Prices Ignite 28-Month Inflation High](/posts/workers--pain-deepens--fuel-prices-ignite-28-month-inflation-high)? It's a recurring nightmare. The entire logistical backbone of education buckles under the weight of political street fights.

The National Parents Association expresses "concern," of course. Mr. Silas Obuhatsa says day scholars are "always affected" due to transport issues and insecurity. It's a nice sentiment, but what's the solution? We're stuck in a loop. Every time our leaders decide to ignore the pleas of the common mwananchi, the streets become battlegrounds, and education, that fragile promise of a better future, is the first thing we sacrifice. Don't pretend to be shocked when the next generation thinks chaos is the only way forward; we're teaching them.
