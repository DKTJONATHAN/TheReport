So, the Missing Voices Coalition has dropped their 2025 report. Big surprise. More statistics, more "verified cases," more tales of lives cut short and families left to pick up the pieces. It's the same Kenyan movie, different year, just with a slightly higher body count and the same deafening silence from those who are supposed to enforce the law. They talk about a "crisis," but for those of us living in Nairobi, this isn't a crisis, it's just Tuesday.

They've been doing this since 2018, diligently documenting the disappearing acts of our supposed protectors. "Data-driven advocacy," they call it. "Survivor-centered storytelling." Sounds nice and fluffy, doesn't it? It's all about pushing for "justice" and "systemic reform." Meanwhile, out here, the system keeps on reforming itself by continuing to swallow people whole. Remember those 27 'entrepreneurs' Interpol nabbed recently? That’s the kind of flashy headline we get. This report, however, dives into the shadows where the real work, and the real tragedies, happen.

This 2025 edition, they claim, is packed with "verified data, trends, survivor testimonies, and concrete recommendations." We'll see how "concrete" those recommendations are when the next body washes up on shore or a family wakes up to an empty house. It’s a nice gesture, this whole reporting thing, a way to acknowledge that people are indeed going missing. But acknowledgment doesn't bring anyone back, and it certainly doesn't put the fear of God into the men with badges who seem to operate with impunity.

The coalition is out there, pushing and pulling, hoping to make a dent. They're talking about holding people accountable. But in Kenya, accountability is a word whispered in hushed tones, a myth for the hopeful. We've seen plenty of grand pronouncements and public relations exercises, like that Sh2 billion "pity payment" for KeMU. It’s easy to throw money around when it’s not your child who’s gone missing, or your brother who’s been silenced forever. This report is just another reminder that while some are busy collecting data, others are busy disappearing.
