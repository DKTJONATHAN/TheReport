Welcome to Nairobi, the only city where you can be a former Deputy President and still get treated like a common prop for a viral video. The Pharmaceutical Society of Kenya (PSK) has finally crawled out of its cave to address the footage of Rigathi Gachagua buying medicine. Apparently, the guy behind the counter wasn't just a privacy-invading clout chaser; he wasn't even a licensed pharmacist. This city is truly a ghetto with high-speed internet.

It is just another chapter in the [Silicon Savannah or Digital Slaughterhouse?](/posts/silicon-savannah-or-digital-slaughterhouse) narrative where your medical history is less secure than a twenty-shilling padlock on a mabati gate. PSK is now "deeply concerned" that a rogue element was dispensing drugs and filming clients without consent. It is funny how they only notice these "compliance issues" when a big fish gets caught in the net. For the rest of us, we have been buying Panadol from suspicious characters who look like they failed chemistry since the 1990s.

The statement from PSK claims that recording a client is a serious breach of healthcare confidentiality. No kidding, Einstein. But in a country where everyone is a content creator first and a human being second, professional ethics are a foreign language. They are calling on the Pharmacy and Poisons Board to investigate, which in Nairobi terms usually means a lot of paperwork and a few "tea" sessions before everyone forgets until the next scandal hits the timeline.

PSK wants to remind us that real pharmacists undergo extensive university training and take professional oaths. That sounds great on paper, but on the ground, the "Death of Honor" is real. We live in an era where the [The Death of Honor: How Professionalism Killed the Myth of the 'Gentleman’s Sport'](/posts/the-death-of-honor--how-professionalism-killed-the-myth-of-the--gentleman-s-sport) applies to every sector, including medicine. If a whole former DP can be turned into a meme while buying his prescription, what makes you think your local chemist isn't gossiping about your "special" cream to the whole estate?

At the end of the day, PSK’s "regret" is just corporate speak for "we are embarrassed we got caught sleeping." Every Kenyan apparently has the right to confidential healthcare, but try exercising that right in a shop where the attendant is more interested in his Ring Light than your dosage. If you are looking for privacy in this town, you might as well look for a honest politician. Both are extinct.
