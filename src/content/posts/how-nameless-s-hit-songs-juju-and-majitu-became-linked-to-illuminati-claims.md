Nameless, Illuminati, and the Githeri Delusion: Kenya's Oldest Conspiracy Theory Gets a Laugh

Nameless, the evergreen Kenyan artiste, has decided to grace us with his presence and a good dose of self-deprecating humour, all while clinging to the preposterous notion that his old hits somehow affiliated him with devil worship and the Illuminati. As if Nairobi wasn't already a circus of its own, we're now revisiting the days when parents genuinely warned their kids about "Majitu" and "Juju" being gateway drugs to eternal damnation. Honestly, the imagination on some folks back then, and apparently still now, is more terrifying than any dark force.

The veteran singer is touting his silver jubilee concert by, you guessed it, laughing off claims that have followed him for decades. He claims his songs were just "art," a concept seemingly lost on a generation easily spooked by anything that wasn't a benga tune played at a funeral. He’s out here saying, "Musipanic it is called art, just art." Yeah, right. Because dramatic visuals, dark costumes, and lyrics that sound like they were scribbled by a madman are just everyday artistic expressions for your average Kenyan mum. It's less "art," more "convenient scapegoat."

Back in the early 2000s, Kenyan pop culture was a fertile ground for conspiracy theories, especially with the global obsession over the Illuminati. Any artist who dared to step outside the bland box of predictable music videos became an instant suspect. Nameless's "Majitu" and "Juju" were prime examples of this - unique, yes, but also perfectly crafted fodder for parental panic. It's a wonder more musicians weren't accused of orchestrating the next [The Boardroom Coup](/posts/the-boardroom-coup--how-you-got-scammed-by-a-political-prenup) or some other grand scheme.

Nameless is now inviting people to identify themselves if their parents warned them off his music. He's bragging about not being Illuminati and instead "nakunywanga chai na Githeri." How original. This whole "it's just art" defence is convenient, but let's be real, it's also a testament to how easily people will believe anything if it’s sensational enough. It highlights the gullibility that fueled these rumours, not the artistic genius of the singer.

The singer’s lighthearted approach is a smart move, turning old anxieties into nostalgic comedy. It’s a shame, though, that the conversation has to revolve around how easily audiences were fooled. While it's true Kenyan entertainment has evolved, this whole affair is a stark reminder of how prone we are to chasing shadows and attributing our own mundane realities to some grand, sinister plot. At least the songs are still remembered, not for the supposed dark forces, but because, apparently, they were actually good.
