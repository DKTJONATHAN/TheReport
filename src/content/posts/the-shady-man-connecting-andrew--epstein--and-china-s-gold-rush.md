The latest dump of US Department of Justice documents has shed a harsh, unflattering light on David Stern, a German businessman who served as a crucial, albeit low profile, link between Prince Andrew and the convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein. Stern, operating from the background, meticulously facilitated connections and deals, particularly in China, a nation then perceived as a boundless frontier for wealth and influence. His involvement, revealed through thousands of unearthed emails, underscores a cynical brand of transactional networking where personal gain and access trumped ethical considerations.

Stern's trajectory, from a self described "student" eager to impress Epstein to a director within Prince Andrew's Pitch@Palace initiative and a trustee of a royal charity, highlights a calculated ascent. He presented himself as a gateway to China, a land ripe for exploitation by those with the right connections. The documents reveal a disturbing pattern of behavior: Stern's frequent, demeaning remarks about women, often using crude abbreviations, and his willingness to engage in discussions that mirrored Epstein's own depravity. His purported role in sorting out Sarah Ferguson's substantial debts, brokered through Epstein, further illustrates the intertwined financial and personal entanglements.

The article details how Stern, claiming to be "hidden" and merely making arrangements, acted as a key intermediary. He facilitated introductions, passed documents, and pitched deals, all while maintaining a carefully managed public persona. His claim of selling a stake in his Beijing based IT healthcare business, Asia Gateway, to Informa, which later expired without product launch, raises questions about the substance of his business acumen and the true value of his ventures.

The genesis of this unholy trinity, as detailed in the documents, lies with Epstein, who introduced Stern to the Duchess of York, Sarah Ferguson, around 2010. Ferguson, in an email to an unnamed "A," likely Prince Andrew, described Stern as someone who had "been helping me a great deal with an ear and advice." She noted his impressive "roladex for China" and his company, Asia Gateway. Stern then, with Epstein's backing, approached "Your Royal Highness" offering assistance. This overture occurred amidst Ferguson's significant financial difficulties, with Stern and Epstein engaging in disparaging private conversations about her, demonstrating a profound lack of respect.

Stern's proposed ventures, such as a London based business targeting high net worth individuals with a focus on China, explicitly aimed to leverage Prince Andrew's "aura and access." While many of Stern's ambitious proposals seemingly never materialized, his relationship with Andrew deepened. He reportedly accompanied the Prince on taxpayer funded trade trips to China, utilizing his alleged connections to "open doors." His appointment to the board of Pitch@Palace in 2016, and his presence at a royal event alongside the Queen, illustrates his successful infiltration of the upper echelons. The subsequent controversy surrounding the co founder of Pitch@Palace's China arm, Yang Tengbo, a man suspected of espionage, further taints the narrative of Stern's China connections.

Stern's appointment to the board of St George's House Trust, despite reservations from some members regarding his opaque background, indicates a pattern of powerful individuals pushing through appointments despite red flags. His request for Epstein to be godfather to his child, though declined, underscores the disturbing closeness of their relationship. Stern's appeal to both Epstein and Andrew was clearly rooted in his perceived mastery of the Chinese market, a land of "opportunity and potential riches" during a period of global economic recovery. His pitches for investment offices, designed to be "extremely China focused" with no "obvious" outward link, exemplify the discreet, perhaps even clandestine, nature of their dealings. Claims of friendships with influential figures like Louis Cheung and Alvin Jiang, grandson of a former president, further solidify his image as a man with unparalleled access, a narrative that proved irresistible to those seeking to capitalize on China's burgeoning economy.
