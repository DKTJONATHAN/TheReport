So, KRG The Don has decided to play savior for the foreign workers in Kenya, spurred on by some MP Beatrice Elachi who apparently woke up and chose to state the obvious. The whole spiel is about "inciting citizens" and not letting people "fight over daily bread." Yeah, right. It’s easy to talk about xenophobia when you’re not the one watching your own countrymen get undercut for the same work, or worse, lose their jobs entirely.

The comments from the peanut gallery - badgirlcashina, tobysoloby, likkymaffiaso - they echo the raw frustration you hear on the streets every day. Kenyans are tired of seeing their own people struggling while foreigners, often undocumented and working for peanuts, fill the gaps. It’s not about hating anyone; it’s about basic survival and fairness. We’re not saying the work itself is "upumbavu," as some MP so eloquently put it, but the wages are a joke, leaving everyone, Kenyan and foreigner alike, in a race to the bottom.

And let's not forget the tax man. tobysoloby hit the nail on the head: illegal immigrants don’t pay taxes. So who’s really benefiting? Certainly not the Kenyan economy. It’s just more burden on the system that’s already teetering. This isn't a complex issue; it's about simple economics and national interest, something that seems to elude some of our esteemed leaders who would rather focus on virtue signaling.

The idea that Kenyans didn't study "from primary to university" for these jobs is just insulting. People are desperate. They take what they can get. The problem isn't a lack of ambition; it's a lack of opportunity, and then seeing those meager opportunities snatched away. This whole debate is a distraction from the real crisis of unemployment and underemployment plaguing Kenya. It’s a familiar tune, much like how the city’s unofficial red-light districts have shifted, showing us that underlying social issues rarely disappear, they just morph. Instead of defending foreign workers with grand pronouncements, maybe KRG should be talking about how to uplift Kenyan workers. How do we ensure fair wages? How do we create jobs that pay a living wage? How do we implement proper work permit systems that actually benefit Kenya? These are the questions that matter, not this performative defense that conveniently sidesteps the core problem.

Ultimately, this is about Kenyan jobs for Kenyans first. If we can't even ensure basic dignity and fair compensation for our own people, then what are we even doing? It’s time we stopped pretending this is about xenophobia and started addressing the economic realities that are leaving many Kenyans behind.
