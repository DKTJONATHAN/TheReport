Oh, darling, grab your finest linen and prepare to look busy because the Montreux Jazz Festival is finally gracing Africa with its "boutique" presence. Hosted in Franschhoek this March, because apparently, nothing says "African creativity" like a Swiss export setting up shop in South Africa's most expensive wine valley. Mark Goedvolk is talking about "re-imagining" the platform, but we all know that is just PR-speak for making sure the guest list is exclusive enough to keep the riff-raff at the gate. If you are not holding a glass of premium vintage while nodding along to a time signature you do not understand, do you even exist in the eyes of the Nedbank sponsors?

The lineup is a chaotic fever dream that smells of mid-life crises and desperate corporate networking. We have the legendary Thandiswa Mazwai and Salif Keita sharing the stage with... Wet Wet Wet? Yes, because nothing screams "cutting-edge jazz" like 1980s soft rock from Scotland. It is clear the organizers are trying to please everyone and ending up with a musical salad that nobody actually asked for. I suppose if you are drunk enough on Franschhoek's finest vignerons, even Róisín Murphy's art-pop will start to sound like a spiritual experience rather than a confusing art project.

While they claim this is about "world-class artistry" and "cultural exchange," it feels more like another chapter in the story of how global brands use local culture to mask their bottom line. It reminds me of the broader trend of [how private equity is cannibalizing the African commons](/posts/the-ghost-in-the-ledger-how-private-equity-is-cannibalizing-the-african-commons) under the guise of "development" and "opportunity." They are using a historic church and a monument as backdrops for a festival presented by a major bank, yet they want us to believe this is a soulful, grassroots exchange of stories. It is corporate branding with a saxophone filter, and we are all expected to clap.

And do not get me started on the "Sunday Slowdown." After two days of pretending to understand complex drum solos, the festival ends with un-ticketed performances and public bars on Huguenot Street. It is basically a sanctioned hangover for the elite who spent their mortgage on "hospitality-focused" VIP packages. If you are looking for real jazz and not just a networking event for people who wear fedoras unironically, you might want to look elsewhere. But hey, at least the wine will be good while the "experts" debate whether Robert Glasper is "too mainstream" for a festival that is literally being held at a monument.
