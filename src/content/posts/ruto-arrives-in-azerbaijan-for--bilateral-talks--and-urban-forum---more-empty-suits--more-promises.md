So, William Ruto has touched down in Baku, Azerbaijan. Apparently, he's there for the World Urban Forum, a place where Kenya's "progress" in affordable housing and sustainable urban development is being lauded. Affordable housing, you say? Meanwhile, back home, EPRA is being dragged to court, likely over the same old song and dance of fuel prices. Funny how the world celebrates our 'progress' when the reality on the ground is far more… pungent.

He's not alone, of course. The First Lady is there to grace the occasion, and they’ve been greeted by some local dignitaries. The real agenda, though, is the "series of bilateral meetings" with Heads of State and "international investors." They're talking energy, ICT, and infrastructure. Basically, more deals to be signed, more handshakes, and more opportunities for someone to explain why we’re still paying through the nose for basic necessities. Don't forget the expanded export opportunities for Kenyan agricultural products - because that's always the carrot dangled, isn't it?

The big one is the chat with Ilham Aliyev, Azerbaijan's president. They'll be "strengthening diplomatic and economic ties." This sounds suspiciously like another round of "Selling the Family Silver," where we trade our future for short-term gains. It’s all about cooperation, they say. But whose cooperation? And for whose benefit? Meanwhile, Deputy President Gachagua is back home, apparently reflecting on political developments and his party’s progress. I wonder if that includes the progress of convincing us that things are getting better, especially when we see news about household air pollution and the high-frequency hustle that makes everything cost a fortune.

And let's not forget the undercurrents. While Ruto is busy making friends abroad, back home, calls for Energy CS Opiyo Wandayi to resign over the fuel crisis continue. Someone's even circulating videos of officers, sparking debates about their conduct. It’s a classic Kenyan narrative: the leaders are off discussing grand plans and global partnerships, while the rest of us are left dealing with the fallout of questionable decisions and the ever-present struggle for survival. This visit, like many before it, will likely be a dazzling display of diplomacy, followed by a quiet return to the status quo.
