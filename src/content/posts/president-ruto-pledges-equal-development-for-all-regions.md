Ruto's "Fair Share" Promise: Development for All, or Just More Hot Air?

President Ruto talks a big game about equitable development for every corner of Kenya, but let's see if his actions match his Sunday sermon. Promises of dams and roads are cheap until the bulldozers actually start moving.

Kitui County, Kenya.

Politics, Development, Promises

Politics

President William Ruto, fresh from a Sunday service and fundraiser in Kitui, has declared that every part of Kenya, regardless of voting patterns, deserves and will get its "fair share" of development. He's calling for unity, painting the usual picture of a nation held back by "exclusion, division and ethnicity." Apparently, all Kenyans pay taxes, and therefore, all Kenyans deserve… well, something. It’s the same song and dance we’ve heard before, promising a transformative change while conveniently glossing over the nitty-gritty of how it all gets funded and, more importantly, delivered.

The President rattled off a list of projects for Kitui: stalled dams that have been "talked about for 40 years" are apparently now being revived, thanks to a new National Infrastructure Fund fueled by billions from Safaricom and Kenya Pipeline. We're also promised affordable housing, modern markets with amenities like digital hubs for Mama Mboga, and a significant boost to the education budget, including hiring more teachers than "previous administrations" - because that’s always the benchmark, isn't it? He even mentioned the Chuluni-Zombe road, a project launched in late 2025, supposedly to be completed by mid-2027. Fascinatingly, this is happening while digital drivers are feeling the squeeze from fuel hikes. [Digital Drivers' Pockets Get Squeezed After Fuel Hikes](/posts/digital-drivers--pockets-get-squeezed-after-fuel-hikes).

However, let's not forget the context. Ruto's administration, like many before it, thrives on grand pronouncements. While the Sh350 billion National Infrastructure Fund sounds impressive on paper, the proof is always in the pudding. We’ve heard this “transformation” narrative many times, often followed by the same old stories of stalled projects and unmet promises. The mention of the Thwake Dam resuming after a decade-long hiatus, and Umaa Dam being revived after 15 years, highlights a persistent problem: either incompetence or corruption has consistently derailed progress in the past. Now, suddenly, with a new fund and a new vision, these behemoths will be completed? Color me skeptical.

The President also touted the expanded Social Health Authority (SHA), boasting of 31 million registered Kenyans and promising that outpatient services are free for registered members. He’s essentially framing healthcare as a constitutional right, which it is, but the reality of healthcare access and quality across the country remains a far cry from these optimistic pronouncements, especially in underserved regions. The claims of hiring 124,000 teachers in four years are also noteworthy, a stark contrast to previous governments, but whether this translates to better learning environments and qualified educators in every classroom is another question entirely.

The accompanying delegation of Cabinet Secretaries and MPs, many of whom are from the Ukambani region, naturally chimed in with praise, echoing the President’s message and commending him for not sidelining them due to voting numbers. This is standard political theatre, a carefully orchestrated show of support designed to solidify a narrative. The underlying truth, as always, will be revealed not in the Sunday pronouncements, but in the tangible development that actually reaches the ground, long after the cameras have packed up and left.
