President Ruto, fresh from a photo-op with a football club, took the opportunity to lecture Kenyans on what he calls "courageous" leadership. He proudly declared he deliberately ignored critics and political pressure to implement his development projects, all aimed at transforming Kenya into a First World economy. Because, of course, *his* vision for a First World future doesn't require *your* consent, or even *your* input, does it? It’s easy to dismiss "populist politics" when you're not the one feeling the pinch of those "difficult decisions."

He then posed the perennial question - "Makosa ilitokea wapi?" - wondering why Kenya isn't like Malaysia or Singapore after sixty years. He attributes our slow pace to a failure to make bold policy choices. Perhaps, just perhaps, it's not a lack of 'courage' but a surplus of self-serving policies and an inability to truly address [The Illusion of Safety: Engineering the Death of Real Market Risk] for the common mwananchi, allowing the same old mistakes to fester. He claims Kenya has the same potential if we stick to his long-term reforms, which usually means long-term pain for everyone else.

And who could forget the Affordable Housing Programme, which Ruto proudly defended? He might dismiss the "Zakayo" moniker, the court battles, and the protests as mere 'noise,' but those were real cries of citizens against a program many see as a burden, not a blessing. "Nairobi inabadilika," he says, with houses magically appearing in Kibera and Mathare. But for whose benefit, exactly? The elite, or the hustlers genuinely struggling to put food on the table, let alone afford a new house in what promises to be a very different Nairobi?

He also trotted out the usual suspects - 600 new markets, 180,000 student hostels, and the grand promise of "Afya leo tumesema iwe ni haki ya kila Mkenya" through the Social Health Authority. Sounds wonderful on paper, doesn't it? But we've heard these tunes before. "Innovative financing models" for infrastructure projects like the Rironi-Mau Summit highway often translate to opaque deals and more national debt that *we* eventually pay, not some mythical National Infrastructure Fund.

Finally, he invoked China's rise and Norway's wealth fund, declaring, "Kwa mapenzi ya Mungu, tutabadilisha Kenya." And then, the predictable call to register as voters - presumably to rubber-stamp more "courageous" decisions that benefit those at the top. Because nothing says "First World" like being told your democratic participation is just to ensure *his* agenda continues, regardless of the real-world impact on *your* pockets and *your* lives. Same old story, different chapter.
