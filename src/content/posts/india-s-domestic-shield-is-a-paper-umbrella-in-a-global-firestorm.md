While the Reserve Bank and the central government congratulate themselves on stable financial conditions, the global supply chain is fraying at the seams. Trade disruptions are not temporary glitches but the new normal in a multipolar world that has lost its center of gravity. Talk of structural reforms is often little more than a bureaucratic sedative, a desperate attempt to lure foreign capital that is already eyeing the exits as volatility becomes the only certain asset class.

The narrative of being better prepared than in past crises is a dangerous form of hubris. Resilience is a convenient word for a population that has grown accustomed to absorbing the shocks of external incompetence. If the conflict in West Asia escalates, the thin veneer of domestic stability will peel away, revealing an economy still tethered to the whims of maritime chokepoints and the scorched-earth politics of the Levant.

Related to the ongoing erosion of traditional power structures, we see a similar pattern in The Greenland Gambit and the Slow Death of the Dollar, where the old certainties of global finance are being dismantled by geopolitical friction. India’s current struggle is just one chapter in a larger story of a world order that is breaking down faster than its participants can adapt. The transition from a globalized market to a fractured one will not be kind to those who mistake temporary survival for permanent immunity.
