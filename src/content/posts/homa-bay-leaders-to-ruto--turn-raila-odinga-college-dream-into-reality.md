Homa Bay Leaders Beg Ruto: Make Raila's Empty College A Reality

A stalled college project in Homa Bay, initially championed by Raila Odinga, now sees local leaders groveling at President Ruto's feet for funds. Promises of agricultural advancement have withered on the vine, just like many other grand pronouncements in this country.

The dream of the Raila Odinga University of Agriculture and Technology, a supposed beacon of farming knowledge in Kwoyo Village, has been gathering dust since 2022. Back then, it was all pomp and ceremony, a foundation stone laid with much fanfare, but four years later, the only thing growing is disappointment. The land bought with Sh15 million of CDF money is still just land, while the promise of transforming subsistence farmers into commercial powerhouses remains a distant, laughable pipedream.

Tom Mboya University, the supposed parent institution, claims plans are "underway." Underway where? To the nearest bureaucratic black hole, probably. The whole idea, hatched by MP Martin Owino to boost agricultural skills and food security, has clearly hit the same predictable roadblocks as so many other Kenyan development initiatives. It's a classic case of talk is cheap, especially when it's about improving the lives of the common mwananchi. You see this same pattern of grand promises followed by abject failure in so many areas, from [Nairobi’s Annual Drowning: 23 Dead in the City of Failed Concrete](/posts/nairobi-s-annual-drowning--23-dead-in-the-city-of-failed-concrete) to every other failed infrastructure project.

Now, with President Ruto visiting the region, the Homa Bay leaders, particularly MCA Nicholas Owaka, are opportunistically shifting their pleas. Forget about the previous administration; it's Ruto's turn to cough up. They're begging him to allocate funds and issue directives, hoping he'll magically transform their idle land into a thriving academic hub. It’s a desperate attempt to leverage a presidential visit for development, as if a few photo ops will suddenly unlock the national treasury.

The irony is thicker than a week-old ugali. The project was supposedly conceived during Kibaki's era, then pushed by Odinga, and now the same leaders are turning to Ruto, the man they probably spent years badmouthing, to bail them out. It’s the Kenyan political dance as usual: blame the opponent when things go wrong, beg for favours when an opponent is in power. The hope that this college will create jobs and boost the local economy feels more like wishful thinking than a concrete plan.

With the Gor Mahia ring road project also mentioned, it's clear the leaders are throwing everything at the wall to see what sticks. They’re even calling for more sub-counties, as if more administrative units will magically solve the underlying issues of resource allocation and effective governance. The land sits idle, the promises remain unfulfilled, and Homa Bay's leaders are left hoping for a miracle from the current powers-that-be.
