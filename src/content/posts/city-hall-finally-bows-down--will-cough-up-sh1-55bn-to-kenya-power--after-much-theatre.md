So, the big news is that Nairobi County, after dragging its feet and engaging in all sorts of petty squabbles, has finally decided to throw a measly Sh1.55 billion at Kenya Power. This is after they owed a whopping Sh3.01 billion at the end of last year, making them the undisputed champions of not paying their dues. Honestly, you'd think with all the talk of development and progress, the county would at least keep the lights on.

This whole saga has been more entertaining than a Tusker Premier League match. Remember when Kenya Power dared to disconnect their power? City Hall’s response? Clamping their vehicles and dumping rubbish at their premises. Classic Nairobi drama. It’s like watching toddlers fight over a toy, except the toy is essential public services and the toddlers are elected officials. One wonders if this is part of a larger pattern, much like the Middle East Mayhem Means Kenya's Economy is Toast, where global events feel distant but local mismanagement hits hard.

Now, they're mumbling about verifying meters before they actually cough up the cash. Typical. It’s always "subject to verification" or "pending further review" when it comes to actually parting with money. They even tried to pull a fast one, demanding Sh5.64 billion in "wayleave charges" based on some obscure Finance Act from last year. It’s all a grand performance to distract from the fact they’ve been using electricity without paying for it for ages.

The Sakaja administration, which was apparently all about "crossing fingers and hoping for the best" with this bill, has suddenly remembered they have a budget. Or perhaps Parliament breathing down their necks finally got their attention. It's a shame that such basic obligations require this much public spectacle. You'd think running a city would be more about efficient service delivery than a constant tug-of-war over unpaid bills.

It’s still unclear if they'll ever pursue that Sh5.64 billion claim, or if it was just another smokescreen. What is clear is that Kenya Power has been struggling to collect what's owed, and Nairobi County has been its biggest headache. One can only hope this Sh1.55 billion is the start of them actually acting like responsible adults, but I wouldn't hold my breath. The Bullion Mirage: Gold Withers Under Real-World Fire might be about precious metals, but the principle of illusion versus reality applies just as well to city finances.

This whole dance highlights the perpetual struggle between service providers and defaulting entities, a story as old as time in this city. Let's just hope the lights don't flicker out again while they're "verifying" things.
