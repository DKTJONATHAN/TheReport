In this country, the line between a civil servant and a professional heckler is thinner than a politician’s promise. Take Richard Omondi, better known as Makamu wa Makamu. Until James Orengo’s administration suspended him, most people thought he was just another guy with a loud voice and a client list. It turns out he was actually a County Information Management Officer. That is a very fancy title for someone whose primary job description seems to involve moving crowds and swinging votes for his "clients."

It is the classic Kenyan script. We pay taxes so the government can hire people to shout at us. The only reason we even found out Omondi was on the payroll was because he finally said something so offensive it forced the county’s hand. It is much like [The Oratory Trap: Why Sifuna’s Stories Won-t Fill Your Plate](/posts/the-oratory-trap--why-sifuna-s-stories-won-t-fill-your-plate) because it proves that in the village of Kenyan politics, noise is the only currency that never devalues, even when the stomachs of the voters are empty.

These political gangs do not just appear from thin air. They are nurtured, funded, and protected by the very people who stand on podiums preaching about peace and development. These "crowd movers" are the ground troops for election cycles, and when the voting stops, they get tucked away into county offices with nice titles and predictable salaries. The hostility in Siaya did not die with Were-it just took a short nap before getting back to work for the next highest bidder.

Civil society is currently doing its usual dance, issuing statements about "dignity in politics" and "stricter regulation." It is cute, really. They act as if sexist speech and political thuggery are bugs in the system rather than the main features. Law enforcement has failed to punish these offenders for decades, creating a culture of total impunity where being a loudmouth is a legitimate career path with health benefits.

Do not expect anything to change just because one guy got suspended for a few weeks. For every Omondi who gets caught, there are ten others waiting in the wings, ready to trade their dignity for a government paycheck. This is not about information management-it is about keeping the circus running while the audience pays for the privilege of being insulted by the performers. As long as these gangs are enabled by the elite, the cycle of hostility will remain the only thing that actually works in the county.
