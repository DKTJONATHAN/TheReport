Meta's swift disposal of its contract with Sama, the Kenyan outfit that provided over a thousand workers to sift through the intimate detritus captured by Meta's AI Glasses, is less a sign of corporate awakening and more a testament to practiced damage control. To be outraged at Meta for firing these individuals is to miss the point entirely. The moment that investigative report hit the airwaves, Sama's operation in Kenya was doomed.

Meta operates as a criminal enterprise, though not always in the eyes of the law. Its offenses are more often against public perception and basic human decency. Consider the Onavo VPN debacle. Was it illegal? Who knows. Was it appalling? Absolutely.

Let's assume, for the sake of argument, that Meta isn't technically breaking any laws by routing video footage from its AI Glasses to contractors in Kenya for review. They're likely still doing it, just in a different, less visible sweatshop. The critical element isn't legality; it's the visceral revulsion the *knowledge* of their practices engenders. This repulsion is, of course, contingent on public awareness, something Meta actively works to suppress.

If Meta's AI Glasses packaging explicitly stated that user footage might be reviewed by third-party contractors without explicit, informed consent-beyond the boilerplate legalese buried in impenetrable terms of service that no one reads-sales would plummet. The social stigma attached to wearing such devices would also skyrocket.

This clandestine operation, conducted on the other side of the planet, is precisely the strategy. Lower wages are a bonus, not the primary driver. The true advantage lies in the sheer distance, making the operation harder to expose. It's a minor miracle that Swedish reporters even found these contractors.

Many illegal acts are inherently scandalous, but the inverse is also true: many scandalous acts are perfectly legal. All of them, however, require a cover-up. This operation must remain quiet, shielded from public view, because its very existence is an affront. The notion that "AI" is sometimes just a euphemism for a global network of human labelers is damaging enough. More damning still is the fact that users confide in and expose themselves to these "AI" products, believing their interactions are private, when in reality, they might be scrutinized by paid individuals tasked with refining the algorithms. A significant portion of the user base is likely only engaging with these technologies due to this profound ignorance.

As Benjamin Franklin aptly noted, "Three may keep a secret, if two of them are dead." Meta's model thrives on keeping secrets, and its reliance on anonymous, distant labor is central to that strategy. So go ahead, enjoy your Meta-powered WhatsApp and Instagram. Just remember to keep that Swedish report handy, especially if you ever encounter someone sporting those AI Glasses.

The lack of traction for stories originating from non-English speaking countries, even with the internet and translation tools, is a recurring flaw in our media landscape. This joint investigation by Svenska Dagbladet and Göteborgs-Posten, though potentially explosive, faded quickly. It’s easy to imagine a different outcome had it been published by a major Western outlet. The absence of leaked images or video further diluted its impact. While the testimony of contractors witnessing users engaging in private acts is disturbing, the lack of visual evidence, however illogical, significantly dampens public outrage. Human nature, it seems, is more swayed by what it sees than what it hears, especially when the report comes from afar.
