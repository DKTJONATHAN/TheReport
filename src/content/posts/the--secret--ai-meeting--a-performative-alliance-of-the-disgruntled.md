So, a gaggle of 90 assorted cranks and has beens, from your garden variety church leader to your fringe political agitators, decided to huddle in a New Orleans hotel ballroom. The premise? A "secret" meeting on AI, where individuals whose primary shared trait seems to be a profound misunderstanding of advanced technology found themselves in the same room, presumably to marvel at their own collective anxieties. The Future of Life Institute, a group whose primary function appears to be generating more acronyms than actual solutions, is peddling the notion that this gathering birthed some sort of "Pro Human AI Declaration." It’s a document, of course, filled with the usual platitudes about "humanity first," "avoiding concentration of power," and "preserving agency." In other words, a document designed to sound important while saying absolutely nothing concrete.

The signatory list is a who's who of the perpetually aggrieved and the perpetually irrelevant. We have labor unions, religious organizations that likely still believe the internet is a fad, and the same progressive ideologues who brought us Bernie Sanders' perpetual campaigns for nothing. Toss in some conservative talking heads, the perennial Ralph Nader, and the ever-present Steve Bannon, and you have a recipe for a truly incoherent coalition. The goal, apparently, is to corral these disparate, often opposing, forces into a unified front against the terrifying specter of technological progress, or more accurately, against the entities that are actually *doing* things with it.

The organizers, bless their naive hearts, seem genuinely surprised that these disparate groups, brought together by a shared fear of being left behind, could find common ground. The idea that autonomous lethal weapons shouldn't be entirely AI powered, or that companies shouldn't exploit children's emotions, is hardly a revelation. It's the bare minimum of common sense, the kind of stuff that passes for profound insight in rooms where actual innovation is being avoided. The commentary about "woke" vs. "based" AI is particularly telling, a desperate attempt to inject partisan theater into a discussion that requires a level of technical understanding far beyond their capabilities.

This latest pronouncement is being positioned as a successor to the 2017 Asilomar Principles, a document that, crucially, included actual people involved in the AI industry. This time, the tech titans were conspicuously absent. Why? Because, according to the organizers, corporate interests would "dominate." Translation: these civil society groups are tired of being ignored by the people actually building the future. They want a seat at the table they didn't earn, a way to leverage public fear to gain regulatory power they couldn't achieve through merit.

The narrative being spun is that the US government, once a potential bulwark against unchecked technological development, has now become a cheerleader for corporate interests. This, of course, conveniently ignores the reality that these same civil society groups have spent decades lobbying for or against government action depending on their immediate, often self serving, agendas. The call to "force the government to do it" if it won't act on its own is a classic power play, a demand for leverage disguised as democratic imperative.

The "Protect What's Human" ad campaign and the subsequent poll are just more evidence of this performative desperation. They’re polling to confirm what they already believe: that people are scared of what they don't understand. The overwhelming support for vague principles like "preventing harm to children" is hardly a testament to the brilliance of their declaration. It's a testament to the fact that most people, when asked if they want bad things to happen, will say no. The real challenge, which they conveniently sidestep, is articulating *how* to achieve these goals without stifling innovation or resorting to the same monopolistic practices they claim to oppose.

The pronouncements from figures like Alan Minsky, lamenting the "contempt for the idea that basic protection must come before profits," are rich given the history of political maneuvering by these very groups. The idea that the public will rally to their banner simply because they've discovered that tech billionaires prioritize profits is hardly a groundbreaking insight. It's the oldest story in the book, and these players are simply trying to hitch their wagon to it, hoping to carve out their own piece of the pie before it's all gone. The "Pro Human AI Declaration" is less a genuine movement and more a desperate attempt by those on the periphery to reassert their relevance in a world rapidly outgrowing them.
