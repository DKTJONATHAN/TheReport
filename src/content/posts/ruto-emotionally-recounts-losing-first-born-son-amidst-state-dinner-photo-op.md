President Ruto's Emotional Recall of Son's Death: A Performance for the Public?

Ruto’s teary-eyed recollection of his first-born son’s death during the National Prayer Breakfast in Nairobi, while holding hands with his wife Rachel, certainly tugged at some heartstrings. He framed it as a deeply personal tragedy driving his push for Universal Health Coverage. But let's be real, in Nairobi, every tear shed by a politician is dissected. Was this genuine grief on full display, or a well-rehearsed act to garner sympathy and push his agenda, especially after that embarrassing encounter in New York where he was likened to Afghanistan regarding maternal care?

The President painted a grim picture, comparing Kenya's maternal and newborn mortality rates to that of a war-torn nation. He recounted a woman’s sharp words in New York, a moment he found “embarrassing” and “tragic.” It’s easy to trot out personal tragedy when confronted with harsh realities, especially when your administration's healthcare policies are under scrutiny. The UHC push, while sounding noble, often translates to more taxes and questionable service delivery in this city. We've seen these promises before, haven't we? It reminds me of how sometimes, even with genuine intent, the system just grinds you down, like the struggles faced by artists trying to make ends meet, as seen in [Pascal Tokodi Denies Ex-Wife Left Him Broke, Claims Manager Is His Financial Savior](/posts/pascal-tokodi-denies-ex-wife-left-him-broke--claims-manager-is-his-financial-savior).

He stressed that health is a constitutional right, not a privilege, and that he's willing to do "anything" for UHC to succeed. Sounds good on paper, but “anything” can mean anything, especially when it comes to public funds. We’ve seen plenty of ambitious projects launched with great fanfare in Kenya, only to sputter and die due to corruption or sheer incompetence. The constant talk about reforms in the healthcare sector feels like a broken record, with the same issues persisting while politicians engage in photo ops.

The image of President Ruto and First Lady Rachel Ruto holding hands at the White House, a picture of unity and shared experience, now gets a different shade when juxtaposed with his tearful confession back home. It’s a calculated move, a public display of a private pain, used to bolster his image and his policies. While his personal loss is undoubtedly profound, it’s hard to shake the feeling that in the cutthroat world of politics, even grief can become a political tool.
