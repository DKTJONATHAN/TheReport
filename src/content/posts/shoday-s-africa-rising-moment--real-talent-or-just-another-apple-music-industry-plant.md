Apple Music has a new favorite child, and his name is Shoday. He is the latest cover star for their Africa Rising program, which is basically corporate-speak for "we need to fill this playlist with someone new before the subscribers get bored." Shoday, hailing from Ogun State and Lagos, is being touted as the next big thing after a few singles like Caution and Dey 4 You caught some steam. It is the usual industry cycle: find a kid with a decent haircut and a melodic hook, slap him on a billboard, and hope the global audience swallows the bait without asking too many questions.

Let us talk about the math for a second, because the timeline is giving absolute science fiction. The official announcement mentions a January 2026 single called Paparazzi. Is Shoday a time traveler, or is his PR team just that bad at proofreading? Either way, he is being pushed onto the Lagos charts and even snagged a spot on the Best Songs of 2024 list. It feels like the industry is trying so hard to manufacture a superstar that they are accidentally announcing hits from the future. It is almost as messy as the local authorities trying to look busy while the [DCI finally bags a small fish with big boots](/posts/boyi-the-boogeyman--dci-finally-bags-a-small-fish-with-big-boots) instead of going after the real sharks.

Shoday just dropped his debut album, Hybrid, which claims to mix street-influenced Afrobeats with "introspective elements." That is usually code for "I am singing about my feelings because my producer told me it makes me look deep for the critics." He is currently doing the rounds on Africa Now Radio with Nandi Madida, repeating the same "I feel blessed" script that every artist reads when they are finally getting that sweet Apple paycheck. It is predictable, it is polished, and it is about as authentic as a designer bag from a back-alley market in Lagos.

He even apparently did a debut tour in the UK in 2025 - again with these futuristic dates - hitting London, Manchester, and Birmingham. It is the standard Afrobeats starter pack: get some UK numbers, act like you are a global icon, and hope the Grammys are paying attention. Shoday seems like a talented enough guy, but the Africa Rising tag is becoming a revolving door for anyone with a decent marketing budget and a pulse. We will see if he is still on the cover when the next developing talent with a better publicist comes knocking next month.
