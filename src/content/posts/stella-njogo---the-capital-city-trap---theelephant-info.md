STELLA NJOGO - THE CAPITAL CITY TRAP: NAIROBI'S FLOODS SHOW AFRICA CAN REDESIGN ITS CITIES, OR DROWN IN THEM

WARNING: Nairobi's floods are not just about climate change. They expose the raw inequality and systemic failures that have built our cities for survival, not dignity. This is the capital city trap, and it's suffocating us.

CAPTION: Another day, another disaster. Nairobi's flooded streets are a stark reminder of who gets left behind.





So, Stella Njogo writes about Nairobi's floods. Big shocker. Images of people wading through sewage, cars floating away, shops drowning - it's the same old story. Everyone's shouting "climate change," like that's some magic excuse. Sure, the weather's going wild, but let's be real, this isn't just Mother Nature flexing her muscles. This is about how this city, and many like it across Africa, were built by people who clearly never had to worry about their stock washing away.

The article spells it out: our cities were designed for survival, not dignity. The fancy neighbourhoods, up on the hills, with their drainage and trees, they're fine. They have associations to keep things "proper." Meanwhile, the rest of us, the ordinary Kenyans trying to make a living, are stuck in floodplains, on riverbanks, in places that were never meant to be built on. When the water rises, it doesn't care about our aspirations. It just follows the path of least resistance, which usually means straight through our homes and businesses. It’s a clear map of the inequality baked into this city’s very foundations.

Nairobi. The "concrete jungle where dreams are made," apparently. Generations have flocked here, chasing opportunity because that's where all the money, the jobs, the education is concentrated. Nearly 6 million people cram into this city daily, pushing infrastructure to its breaking point. This is the capital city trap: where an entire country's ambitions are suffocated in one overcrowded, overwhelmed centre. We've seen this before; places like Nairobi, where the only thing that trickles down is blood, are built on this flawed premise.

But here's the sliver of hope, if you can call it that. The digital age means we don't have to be glued to the capital for a decent shot at life. Remote work, digital entrepreneurship - these things are changing the game. Instead of cramming everyone into Nairobi, we could be building up regional hubs. Imagine towns within a few hours of the city, with more space, lower costs, and still plugged into the global market. Kenya's already shown it can leapfrog old tech with mobile money; maybe it can leapfrog old urban planning too.

Crises have a nasty habit of forcing change. COVID proved that people can work from anywhere. And these floods, as miserable as they are, could be the push we need to rethink our infrastructure and urban design. Look at Dubai; they got hit, and they're already talking compensation and upgrades. It’s about leadership, and making sure people's dignity and security are actually a priority, not an afterthought. Africa has the talent, the young population, the digital savvy. What it needs is distributed opportunity, not just this mad rush to the capital.

The question is, will we learn? Will we rebuild the same broken systems that led us here, or will we finally design cities and economies where prosperity actually flows across the country, not just pools in one choked-up core? The floods are loud, but this question is even louder.
