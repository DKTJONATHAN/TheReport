So, the boys in blue managed to stop a car on the highway, apparently full of the green stuff. From Busia, heading to Nairobi, where it was supposed to be "broken down for wider distribution." Break it down for who? For the same people who always get their hands on it, while these small-time mules get thrown to the wolves. It’s a classic Nairobi hustle, just with a bit more paperwork this time.

Then, just to keep things interesting, they nabbed some Ugandan fella at the border with a suspiciously heavy briefcase. Seventeen bales. Meticulously wrapped. Sounds almost artistic, doesn't it? He’s another one who thought he was clever, and now he’s just another statistic to pad the DCI’s quarterly report. Let’s be honest, these are just the ones they catch. The real players are probably laughing all the way to the bank, or perhaps attending some fancy maritime conclave, like that Kenya Navy Commander Maj Gen Paul Otieno.

The DCI types are crowing about jurisdiction, about domestic and international law. Sounds impressive, doesn't it? Meanwhile, back on the ground, it’s the same old story. Seizures in Machakos, more rolls and sachets found. A man in Bomet gets 50 years and a Sh50 million fine for 278kg. Fifty years. That's a lot of prison time for something that’ll be back on the streets before you can say "corruption." It makes you wonder if the compensation team warning about fraudulent claims regarding police brutality hasn't got a point about how these things are handled. [Police brutality: Compensation team warns of fraudulent claims](/posts/police-brutality--compensation-team-warns-of-fraudulent-claims)

They even managed to scoop up some firearms and weapons along with the narcotics in the Coast region. Because why not? It all adds to the drama, doesn't it? And in Kilifi, more rolls and more arrests. It's a constant cycle, a revolving door of petty criminals and confiscated goods. It keeps the lights on at DCI headquarters and ensures there’s always something to report.

So, the "plot" is foiled. A few people will be processed, maybe a judge will have a good day, and the street price of bhang will fluctuate for a week. And then, in a month or two, we’ll read about another successful raid, another consignment intercepted. It’s the Nairobi way. Always something to distract you from the real issues.
