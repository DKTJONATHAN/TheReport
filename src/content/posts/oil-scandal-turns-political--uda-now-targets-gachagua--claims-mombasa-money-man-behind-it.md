So the multi-billion shilling oil scandal is officially a political football. Forget about accountability and recovering stolen cash; it’s all about settling scores now. The United Democratic Alliance (UDA) is the latest to jump into the mudslinging arena, and their target is none other than former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua. They're not even trying to hide it anymore.

According to UDA Secretary General Hassan Omar, Gachagua is allegedly being propped up by some shadowy "Mombasa-based benefactor" who's supposedly tangled up in this whole oil mess. Omar, in his press conference, didn't mince words, calling Gachagua out for being eloquently prepped to "divert investigations and to benefit those perpetrating this massive economic crime." It’s the same old script, just a different cast of characters.

To make matters worse, UDA also decided to throw Kiharu MP Ndindi Nyoro under the bus, labeling him a "pseudo-expert." Apparently, both Nyoro and Gachagua are accused of misleading the public about the scandal. It’s a classic case of political theatre, where the real issues get buried under a mountain of accusations and counter-accusations. We’ve seen this play out before, especially when it comes to shady financial dealings. Remember how Zakayo's supposed "First-World Fever Dream" was funded by questionable alliances? This oil scandal feels cut from the same cloth.

Honestly, it’s exhausting. The focus should be on finding out who plundered billions, not on who is whispering in whose ear or who has an alleged financier in Mombasa. But this is Kenya, and scandals are just opportunities for politicians to boost their own relevance and attack their rivals. It’s a cycle that never ends, and we, the common mwananchi, are left holding the bag.

The entire spectacle is a stark reminder of the rampant corruption and the lengths to which our leaders will go to protect themselves and their cronies. While they are busy playing their games, the country bleeds. The promises of fixing the nation, like those discussed on shows like [Fixing the Nation](NTV), often seem like distant dreams when the foundation is built on such shaky, corrupt ground.

This oil scandal is just another chapter in the long, sad story of Kenyan politics. Expect more mudslinging, more accusations, and very little in the way of actual justice or recovery of lost funds. It's a cynical game, and we're all just spectators being fleeced.
