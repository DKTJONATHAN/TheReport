While the gathering served as a stage for India to showcase its burgeoning startup ecosystem, the primary objective was strategic. By hosting the summit, New Delhi sought to position itself as the preeminent voice for the Global South. Prime Minister Narendra Modi emphasized a shift toward human-centric AI, arguing that the technology must be sensitive and responsive to the needs of developing nations rather than merely serving as a machine-centric tool for advanced economies.

This summit represents the latest evolution in a series of global AI gatherings. Following the safety-focused summits in Bletchley Park and South Korea, and the governance-oriented Action Summit in Paris, India's rendition focused on inclusive growth and sustainable development. Analysts note that this shift from safety to impact highlights a growing divide between Western concerns over existential risks and the Global South's focus on economic deployment and affordable access.

However, the summit's rhetoric faces a stark reality on the ground. India's domestic legal framework remains rooted in the Information Technology Act of 2000, a piece of legislation that predates the modern AI era. Legal experts argue that the lack of AI-specific laws creates significant uncertainty regarding liability, accountability, and data security. While the European Union has moved forward with the EU AI Act, India is still navigating the complexities of creating a comprehensive framework that balances innovation with regulation.

The tension between global ambition and domestic enforcement became evident just days before the summit. The Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology issued new directives requiring social media platforms to remove problematic AI-generated content within a three-hour window. Additionally, platforms must now implement permanent labels on synthetically generated information. Critics argue these timelines are practically impossible to meet and may lead to over-compliance, resulting in the unnecessary removal of legitimate content.

Furthermore, the summit has faced criticism for the prominent role given to major technology corporations. Some civil society advocates argue that seating tech giants as equal stakeholders with national governments allows these firms to directly shape the rules intended to govern them. This dynamic, critics suggest, risks turning such summits into political theater rather than forums for genuine democratic reform.

As India seeks to become a global AI powerhouse, the path forward requires more than diplomatic optics. The success of its impact agenda will likely depend on whether it can modernize its domestic legal architecture while protecting the digital rights of its citizens.
