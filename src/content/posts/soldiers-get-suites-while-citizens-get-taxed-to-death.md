So, the big man finally made it to Roysambu. Not for a quick Airbnb plot or to sample the local nightlife, but to hand over keys to 500 units at the military camp. They call it a milestone. I call it another Tuesday in Kenya where the people with the boots get the best beds while the rest of us are still trying to figure out where our housing levy is actually going. This Public-Private Partnership thing sounds fancy, but in Nairobi speak, it just means some well-connected contractors are making a killing while we foot the bill for "dignified accommodation."

Hon. Soipan Tuya says this is a moral obligation. It is funny how morality only seems to apply when we are talking about the disciplined forces. What about the moral obligation to fix the [urban housing challenges](/posts/key-economic-data--retail-power-shift--and-urban-housing-challenges-in-focus) that make living in this city a daily extreme sport for civilians? We are told these 500 units are just Phase One, part of a bigger plan for 3,069 units. If you have lived in Nairobi long enough, you know that "Phase One" is often the only phase that actually looks like the glossy brochure.

The President spent his Thursday talking about correcting years of neglect. It is the same script every time. They paint a few blocks, bring in a brass band, and pretend they have solved the housing crisis. Meanwhile, the average Nairobian is one rent hike away from moving back to the village. The Government is deliberately integrating the disciplined forces into the national housing agenda because they know who holds the power. It is much easier to keep the peace when the soldiers are comfortable in Roysambu and Gilgil while the rest of us struggle.

Then there is the promise of Phase Two. Another 10,095 units across various installations. I will believe it when I see it, and even then, I will be looking for the catch. General Kahariri says this strengthens morale and focus. Of course it does. Anyone would be focused if they did not have to worry about a landlord knocking on their door on the fifth of the month. But for the Kenyan taxpayer, the only thing being "strengthened" is the grip the taxman has on our shrinking pockets.

The guest list at the event was a who-is-who of the military elite. From Lieutenant Generals to Principal Secretaries, everyone was there to clap for a project that should have been finished decades ago if the money had not vanished into "other" priorities. They talk about strategic imperatives while the city chokes on its own lack of planning. At the end of the day, 500 houses in a camp do not change the fact that for most of us, affordable housing is just a expensive myth we are forced to fund.
