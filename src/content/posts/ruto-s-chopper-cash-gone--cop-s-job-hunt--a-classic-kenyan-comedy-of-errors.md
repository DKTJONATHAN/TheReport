Samuel Wanyoike, fresh out of the academy and probably still dreaming of a steady paycheck, finds himself in Garbatulla in 2007. His first big assignment? Guarding an ODM rally. You know, the kind of place where promises fly thicker than mosquitoes and the only thing certain is that things will go sideways.

And go sideways they did. Apparently, some bigwig politician, Day Jirongo no less, was bragging about some past political maneuvering involving Jaramogi and Moi. The details are as hazy as a Nairobi smog, but what's clear is that a chopper, likely full of campaign funds, took a hit. Some say cash vanished. Others say it was just bad accounting, a common ailment in Kenyan politics.

This whole mess seems to have landed Wanyoike in hot water. He’s just trying to get his job back, a Herculean task in a system where loyalty is fleeting and your career can be over before it truly begins. It’s a story as old as time in this country: the little guy suffers while the big players whisper their excuses. Reminds me of how Matiba had to build his empire from scratch, no handouts, just pure grit. Unlike some others who seem to inherit power and then fumble it like a hot potato.

President Ruto, the man who supposedly campaigned against dynasties, is now busy charming them. And his strategy? Gathering "alternative campaign foot soldiers." Translation: he's trying to buy loyalty and build his army of bootlickers for the next election. Meanwhile, institutions like Parliament, the Judiciary, and the National Police Service are apparently "dens of corruption." Shocking, I know. It’s all part of this grand re-election strategy, a never-ending cycle of promises and power grabs. It’s like we’re stuck in Welcome to the 2027 Rehearsal, where the game is rigged and the only thing you can count on is disappointment.
