Oh, look at us. Everyone is popping expensive champagne because the IFPI says Sub-Saharan Africa finally hit the $110 million mark in music revenue. We are the fastest-growing region in the world, apparently. But before you start tweeting about "Africa to the world," let me pour some cold water on that burning ego. Most of that money is doing a disappearing act faster than a deadbeat dad on payday. While your favorite stars are busy posing with rented Lamborghinis, a 2025 Harvard report confirms that we are only keeping a "negligible percentage" of that wealth. It turns out, being a global sensation does not actually mean you have a comma in your bank account.

Welcome to the "Black Box Problem," the industry's favorite way of saying your money is gone and you are too clueless to find it. While our artists are obsessed with getting "likes" and dance challenges on TikTok, they are completely ignoring the actual business. They distribute their songs, pray for a viral moment, and then leave the publishing royalties to rot in international pools. If a song plays in a New York bar or a London club, the money is generated, but if the artist did not register their lyrics and melody correctly, that cash stays in the "Black Box" forever. It is a systemic heist, and honestly, the hypocrisy of crying about "extractive systems" while refusing to hire a proper lawyer is getting old.

Even the local attempts to fix this feel like a chaotic episode of a reality show. In Rwanda, the now-defunct RSAU managed to distribute a pathetic $65,000 over several years. That is barely enough to buy a round of drinks for the entourage. Meanwhile, across the border, Uganda is trying a legislative approach. President Museveni wants to put tracking devices in businesses to make sure every song play is recorded. It sounds like a tech dream, but we all know how government-led "transparency" usually ends up. In this region, [Nairobi Is No Sanctuary For The Brave](/posts/nairobi-is-no-sanctuary-for-the-brave), and neither is the music business for the unorganized.

South Africa is the only one actually showing up to the party with their homework done. SAMRO distributed over R429 million last year, which is what happens when you actually have reciprocal agreements and a system that does not collapse at the sight of a spreadsheet. They are treating music like the business it is, while the rest of the continent is still treating it like a hobby that happens to pay for flashy jewelry. If you are not registered with a Performance Rights Organisation, you are not an artist; you are just providing free labor for global streaming giants who are laughing all the way to the bank.

Thankfully, some people in Kigali are finally waking up to the smell of burning cash. Entrepreneurs like Prince Nshogoza at Dajah Group are using tech like Dajahtunes to hunt down this "hidden money." They are focusing on things like mechanical and micro-sync royalties, which sounds boring until you realize it is the difference between owning a mansion and living in a studio apartment. They are trying to bridge the gap for producers like Muriroo and Producer X, who have been getting the short end of the stick for years. It is about time someone realized that "exposure" does not pay the electricity bill.

If we do not get our act together, this $110 million milestone is not a floor; it is a ceiling. The "Black Box" is not some mysterious void; it is a mirror reflecting our own administrative laziness. We can keep fueling global charts with our creativity, or we can actually start acting like the owners of the "bankable business assets" we claim to have. Until then, keep enjoying those viral dance videos, because for most artists, that is the only payment they are ever going to see.
