Ruto: Kenya's Fuel Prices Show It's "Middle-Income" While Neighbors Are "Least Developed"

President Ruto defends soaring fuel prices by claiming Kenya's "middle-income" status justifies costs higher than its "least developed" neighbors, while opponents threaten protests.

Nairobi motorists are paying the highest fuel prices in the region.

Politics

Fuel Prices, Ruto, Kenya

Gas Station

President William Ruto, addressing a church service in Karen, has decided to lecture us all on economics, telling Kenyans that our eye watering fuel prices are simply a reflection of our "middle-income" status. Apparently, comparing ourselves to Uganda, Rwanda, or Tanzania is like comparing apples and… well, tarmac. Kenya, he says, is a middle-income country, and its neighbors are "least developed." So, naturally, we should expect to pay more. Forget the fact that landlocked nations are somehow managing to ferry fuel through our port and sell it cheaper. That's just good old Kenyan efficiency, I suppose.

Ruto’s defense hinges on Kenya's supposed superior infrastructure. He bragged about 20,000 kilometers of tarmac to maintain and another 6,000 under construction, a figure he claims dwarfs the combined road networks of seven neighboring countries. So, basically, we’re paying premium prices for potholes and unfinished projects, while our neighbors get a better deal. It’s a real testament to how well this government is managing the economy, isn't it? This reminds me of when they were pushing this whole "sell Kenya, new Sh5trn ‘Singapore’ plan abroad" nonsense; more hot air to distract from the reality on the ground.

Meanwhile, the opposition and "young people" are getting restless, with threats of nationwide protests over the cost of living. Kikuyu MP Kimani Ichung’wah, however, dismisses these threats, claiming chaos won't bring fuel prices down. Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen is also in full swing, accusing politicians of using youths as "goons" to cause unrest. It's a classic deflection tactic: blame the disgruntled for being disgruntled, rather than address the root cause. They’ll be arresting anyone who complains about the price of bread next, I imagine.

The stark reality is that Kenyans are paying a hefty Sh206.97 per litre for petrol and Sh206.84 for diesel, making us the most expensive fuel market in the region. Rwanda, Tanzania, and Uganda are all selling fuel at considerably lower prices, even with their supposedly less developed economies and reliance on our port. It seems Ruto's "middle-income" status comes with a very high price tag for the ordinary Kenyan, while his administration continues to spin tales of progress and development.
