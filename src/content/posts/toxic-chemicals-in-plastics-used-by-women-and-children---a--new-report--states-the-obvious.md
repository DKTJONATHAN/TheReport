Another day, another 'groundbreaking' study telling us what we already suspected: your cheap plastic kitchenware and your child's favourite toy are slowly poisoning them. The Centre for Environment Justice and Development (CEJAD) just dropped a report, hot off the press, confirming that everyday plastic products across Nairobi are packed with toxic chemicals. Consumers, especially our women and children, are getting a dose of poison with their daily routine. It's not a secret; it's just now 'official.'

They sampled everything from baby bottles to microwave lids, collected from Westlands to Utawala. The culprits? Brominated Flame Retardants (BFRs) to make plastics less flammable (how thoughtful) and Bisphenols (BPAs) to make them hard and clear. You know, the stuff that's been linked to all sorts of nasty health issues. And here's the real kicker: nine baby feeding bottles tested positive for BPAs, even though *four* of them were brazenly marketed as 'BPA-free.' Europe banned BPAs in baby bottles back in 2011. Us? We're still here, trusting labels.

So, what's the big deal? Well, if you're a child, your underdeveloped systems make you a walking sponge for these toxins - leading to intellectual disabilities, brain disorders, and cancer. If you're a woman, congratulations! You get a bonus dose. These chemicals mimic estrogen, and since women have higher levels, they're more vulnerable to conditions like PCOS (now PMOS) and infertility. Exposure is heightened because we're the ones constantly handling these items in the kitchen. It's a classic case of ignoring glaring issues while the public suffers, much like how leaders can get so focused on [Wavinya’s Road To Riches: How To Triple Debt and Ignore Potholes](/posts/wavinya-s-road-to-riches--how-to-triple-debt-and-ignore-potholes) instead of fixing the fundamental cracks.

And the response from our esteemed authorities? Nema's head of Chemicals Division, Robert Mogoi, conceded that Kenya can't even *verify* these findings locally because we lack the accredited capacity. Talk about a national embarrassment. He also kindly informed us that a vital regulation on Extended Producer Responsibilities - which would actually hold manufacturers accountable - was put on hold by the court last year. So, the people making and importing this junk? Zero accountability. But don't worry, Nema has about eight chemicals 'under surveillance.' That sounds reassuring, doesn't it?

The Kenya Bureau of Standards chimed in, reminding us that 'standards are important.' Oh, really? Who knew? They're now 'informing the development of Kenyan standards.' Because nothing says urgent action like 'informing the development.' While we're pushing for a 'circular economy' through recycling, Florence Kitum from KEBS wisely pointed out that 'safe recycling depends upon safe chemistry.' Well, Belinda says, when the chemistry starts with poison and the oversight is non-existent, 'safe' is just another word for 'hope and pray.' Don't hold your breath for real change, Nairobi.
