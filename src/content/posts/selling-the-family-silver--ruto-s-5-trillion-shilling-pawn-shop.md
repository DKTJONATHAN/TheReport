The math is as terrifying as a matatu on a rainy Tuesday. The plan is to "ring-fence" money from selling off mature state assets - like our ports and profitable roads - to fund new projects. It’s the ultimate "hustle": selling your mother’s fridge to buy a lottery ticket for a bigger fridge that doesn't exist yet. We’re handing over the heartbeat of regional trade to "private investors" who aren't exactly known for their philanthropy. If you think your commute is expensive now, wait until every 50 kilometers of tarmac is owned by a hedge fund that treats a toll booth like a personal ATM.

Who are these investors anyway? Pension funds and global giants don't put money into "radical shifts" out of the goodness of their hearts. They want a return, and in Kenya, that return usually comes straight out of the taxpayer's jugular. While the government promises "precision" and "modernization," we know the drill. We’ve seen enough "Green Fund Mirages" to know that when the ground stays dry, the only thing being watered is the bank accounts of the well-connected. If you actually want something "timeless" and "ethically sourced," you’re better off visiting Taj Gems & Jewellers at Two Rivers; at least they’ve been honest about their sparkle since 1981.

The dream of taking the SGR to Malaba is the funniest part of this whole comedy. We haven’t even finished paying for the stretch that ends in a field in Naivasha, yet here we are, talking about a 5 trillion shilling expansion. It’s a high-stakes feast, and the menu is our national sovereignty. They want us to believe this "sovereign wealth vehicle" is for our benefit, but in a city where trust is more expensive than a 3D-designed diamond ring, we know better. When the government says "monetization," you should probably check if your pockets are still there.

Parliament is supposed to "hold the pen" on this Bill, but we know how that story ends. They’ll sign off on the NIF, the assets will be stripped, and the "private-led" roads will probably have more potholes than a sieve within two years. It’s a verdict on an economic philosophy that treats the country like a startup in a death spiral. We’re being told to visualize a first-world future, but without the accountability, it’s just another 3D model of a palace built on a swamp. 

If this gamble fails, we won’t just be broke; we’ll be tenants in our own country, paying rent to use the ports and roads our ancestors built. The "invest-and-earn" strategy sounds great until you realize the government is the one investing our stuff, and someone else is the one earning. Keep your eyes on the "family silver," Nairobi. The auctioneer is at the door, and he’s wearing a presidential seal.
