Oh look, another awards ceremony. Because if there is one thing Kenyan influencers need more than a ring light and a Shein haul, it is a plastic trophy to tell them they are "shaping the future." The Click Awards 2026 has officially opened its nominations, and the theme is "Building Scalable Creator Enterprises." Honestly, the only thing scaling right now is the audacity of people calling a thirty-second TikTok dance a "sustainable business model." Director Martin Muli says this is about "professional credibility," but we all know it is really about which creator can beg their followers for the most votes before the gala dinner.

The numbers being thrown around are supposed to impress us. Apparently, there are 18.4 million social media users in Kenya, and brands splashed 645 million shillings on influencer marketing last year. That is a lot of money spent on people who forget to use the "paid partnership" tag until they get called out by the taxman. While these "entrepreneurs" claim to be redefining culture, most of them are just redefining how much filter one human face can take before it looks like a waxwork from a budget museum.

But here is the real tea: the government is planning to drop 100 million shillings a year to contract influencers to "amplify official messaging." Translation? They are buying your favorite "relatable" creator to tell you that everything is fine while the country struggles. It is the same old story of using a polished image to mask a messy truth, much like [A South African Win and a President’s Photo-Op: The Green Grass of Karen Hides the Brown Reality](/posts/a-south-african-win-and-a-president-s-photo-op--the-green-grass-of-karen-hides-the-brown-reality) where the optics always matter more than the actual outcome. If you thought your timeline was annoying now, wait until every second post is a government-funded "awareness" campaign disguised as a lifestyle vlog.

The awards are split into "Sector Leadership" and "Ecosystem Builders," which is just corporate-speak for "people who make content" and "the agencies that take a 40 percent cut of their deals." Muli claims this is about "visibility and legitimacy," but in a regulated digital economy, it feels more like a way to keep everyone in line. Why rebel against the system when you can get a nomination for "Best Digital Brand Partner" and a free goody bag? It is all very cozy, and very, very calculated.

Nominations stay open until March 15, so expect your favorite influencers to start acting humble and "blessed" any minute now. They will be talking about "growth" and "sustainability" while checking their engagement rates like a hawk. It is a cycle of validation that never ends, and while the awards might give them "structure," it certainly won't give them a personality. Get ready for a month of desperate "link in bio" posts and fake smiles from people who wouldn't know a "scalable enterprise" if it hit them in the face.
