So, Uganda's Parliament has decided to get all serious about copyright, slapping on some fancy new amendments with "harsher penalties" and "broader protection." The Attorney General, bless his heart, is talking about rights and registration like it's some sort of magic wand. He even had the audacity to say it'll be "difficult to tell someone that they are infringing your rights when they did not know they were your works." Newsflash, Mr. AG, that's precisely what happens in this part of the world. People do what they want, and then we have these elaborate legal dances.

Now they're talking about royalty payments through some "national payment system framework" and registrar monitoring. Collecting societies getting "expanded powers"? Sounds like more bureaucracy, which, in my experience, usually just means more opportunities for someone to skim a little off the top. And the penalties - up to 10 years in jail or hefty fines for playing someone's music or showing their movie without permission. It’s a nice thought, but let’s be real, how many of these "infringers" will actually see the inside of a jail cell? Unless you're a big fish, you're likely to just get a strongly worded letter.

They're even trying to sort out "orphan works," those forgotten pieces of art whose creators vanished. The government can license them, but they have to pay up annually. If the artist suddenly reappears, the rights revert. It's like a bureaucratic scavenger hunt for intellectual property. All this to align with international treaties like the Berne Convention. It’s a nice nod to the West, but out here, it’s usually a case of copy-pasting the law and hoping for the best. We've seen similar grand pronouncements before, like all the talk around youth empowerment, but it often feels like another round of tea and tired buzzwords for Kenya's disenfranchised youth.

The real kicker, though? The parliamentary committee itself admits they haven't even touched on AI. So while they're busy making rules for old-school piracy, the future is already here, and it’s probably creating content faster than any of these laws can keep up. Meanwhile, over in Kenya, KECOBO is doing its own song and dance, inviting "public comments" on a new copyright bill. It's all about the digital economy, online content, and tighter enforcement. They're even asking how to regulate those collective management organisations. It’s a whole lot of process, and you can bet the outcome will be just as messy and complicated as everything else.

The creative industry groups have been lobbying for years, arguing that weak enforcement and poor royalty collection have kept artists from earning a living. It's a noble cause, but it feels like a fight against a tidal wave. These laws are supposed to strengthen legal protection and reduce ownership disputes, but the real test isn't the paper it's written on; it's how it's actually implemented on the ground. And if history is any indicator, the ground in this region is a lot rougher than any law book can account for.

So, Uganda’s Parliament has "approved" this new copyright law with "harsher penalties." Good for them. It’s a start, I suppose. But until the actual enforcement matches the grand pronouncements, and until they figure out how to deal with the digital future, it’s just more ink on paper. And in Nairobi, we’ve learned to treat ink on paper with a healthy dose of skepticism.
