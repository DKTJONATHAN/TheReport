The vaunted sanctity of celebrity art collections has been further tarnished as Ant and Dec, presenters known for their lighthearted on screen personas, are now embroiled in a legal battle over alleged financial impropriety in their acquisition and disposition of Banksy artworks. A High Court judge has, with a degree of judicial caution, deemed there to be a "good arguable case" that the duo has been wronged in their dealings, sanctioning a court order to compel an art dealer to reveal information about transactions involving an unnamed intermediary, referred to as 'X' in legal proceedings.

This legal action stems from claims that 'X', who was tasked with managing their "personal and joint art collection," has extracted "secret and unauthorised profits" from various deals. The presenters seek to unravel what they perceive as a calculated deception, aiming to trace funds they believe have gone missing. Judge Iain Pester’s ruling, while stopping short of finding 'X' liable, validates the presenters' contention that a "legally recognised wrong has been committed."

The scale of the alleged misappropriation is significant. The court heard of a £550,000 transaction involving a set of six Banksy prints depicting Kate Moss in the style of Marilyn Monroe. Ant and Dec contend that the original seller only received £300,000, leaving a substantial £250,000 unaccounted for - a sum they assert was pocketed by the intermediary. Similar accusations have been leveled regarding the sale of 22 artworks, including a version of Banksy's iconic 'Napalm'. In this instance, an artwork reportedly sold for £13,000, yet the presenters were allegedly informed they received only £11,000, a discrepancy of £2,000.

The disclosure order now compels art dealer Andrew Lilley and his firm, Lilley Fine Art Ltd, to provide details of their dealings with 'X'. While Lilley and his company are not accused of direct wrongdoing, their involvement in the "flow of money" makes them a crucial conduit for the information Ant and Dec are pursuing. Lilley, for his part, claims to have been an unwitting participant, stating he was "caught up in this mess" and was merely purchasing art at what he believed to be fair market value, ignorant of any alleged machinations behind the scenes. His compliance with the court order is anticipated, despite previous refusals based on confidentiality. This case serves as a stark reminder that even within the seemingly glamorous world of art collecting, financial integrity is far from guaranteed, and the pursuit of profit can lead to shadowy dealings.
