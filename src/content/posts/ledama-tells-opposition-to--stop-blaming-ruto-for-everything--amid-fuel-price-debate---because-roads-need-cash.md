Ledama Tells Opposition to 'Stop Blaming Ruto for Everything' Amid Fuel Price Debate - Because Reality Bites

A slap on the wrist for the opposition, then. Narok Senator Ledama Ole Kina has finally decided to grace us with some brutal honesty about fuel prices, telling the opposition to stop their childish games of blaming President Ruto for every single cent that goes up in smoke. Apparently, roads don't build themselves on good vibes and opposition rhetoric.

Ole Kina, bless his cynical heart, points out the blindingly obvious: fuel levies are what actually fund our sprawling road network. He throws out numbers comparing Kenya to Uganda and Tanzania, suggesting we're not exactly slacking when it comes to paved surfaces. So, yes, when fuel prices climb, it’s not just Ruto being greedy; it’s also less money for the tarmac you so desperately complain about not having. It’s a trade-off, folks. You want cheaper petrol, you get fewer potholes. Simple as that. This isn't exactly a new revelation, but somehow it needs to be explained to grown adults.

President Ruto, chiming in from his Sunday sermon, echoes the sentiment. He’s out here calling Kenya a "middle-income country," which apparently means we’re expected to fund our own grand infrastructure projects, unlike our poorer neighbours. He proudly boasts about Kenya’s tarmac mileage, claiming it’s more than the combined effort of several East African nations. Transformation, he calls it. Or maybe just a very expensive way to keep us all stuck in traffic, but hey, at least it’s *our* traffic.

The government’s temporary VAT cut on fuel, a move that’s supposed to be a lifeline, is just another temporary band-aid on a gaping wound. It’s a short-term fix while we all continue to grapple with the long-term reality that infrastructure costs money. We've seen this movie before, haven't we? It's like that old story about [Blood, Billions, and the Big Lie: Kenya’s Road Carnage Industry](/posts/blood--billions--and-the-big-lie--kenya-s-road-carnage-industry) - a lot of talk, a lot of money, and a lot of us still stuck paying the price.

So, while the opposition continues to point fingers and the government defends its fiscal gymnastics, the reality for the average Nairobi motorist remains the same: fuel prices are a bitter pill to swallow, and the promise of better roads is a perpetual, expensive negotiation. Don’t expect any miracles; this is Kenya, after all.
