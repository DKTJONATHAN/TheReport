So, Eric Omondi, the man who thinks he's revolutionizing Kenya with a pen and a Twitter account, is back. This time, he's peddling a "referendum" to reduce our 47 counties to a quaint eight. He claims he's got 430,000 signatures from Gen Z, apparently eager to hand over their future to one man's grand idea. Another 500,000 and *poof*, Kenya becomes a streamlined utopia. Or, more likely, another Omondi circus.

According to his pronouncements, this grand plan involves ditching women reps, senators, nominated members, and even Cabinet secretaries. Because clearly, the problem isn't corruption or mismanagement, but the sheer *number* of people supposedly doing those things. It’s a classic "cut off your nose to spite your face" kind of logic, promising a "new Kenya" by simply making less room for anyone to do anything.

Omondi's diagnosis of devolution as Kenya's "biggest cancer" is almost laughable, were it not for the fact that many of us actually see the potential good in it, even if it’s currently riddled with the same old problems. He’s right about Raila Amollo Odinga’s initial idea for development, but conveniently forgets that corruption, nepotism, and tribalism are not unique to devolution; they're the stale bread of Kenyan politics. This whole over-representation argument, that one Kenyan is served by 18 officials, is just a numbers game to distract from the real issue: accountability, or the severe lack thereof. It reminds me of when we discussed how corporate greed can have ripple effects, and how this often mirrors political failings [The Hardwood Hegemony: Exporting American Exceptionalism through Corporate Gladiators](/posts/the-hardwood-hegemony--exporting-american-exceptionalism-through-corporate-gladiators).

This whole "one Kenyan represented by 18 people" is just a caricature. Does anyone truly believe that a village elder is on the same level as a Deputy President? Omondi is cherry picking positions to paint a picture of absurdity, hoping that the sheer volume of titles will blind people to the fact that this isn't about efficient governance, but about a comedian seeking attention. He launched this same charade in August 2024, and here we are, still talking about signatures.

Let's be real. This isn't about improving Kenya; it's about Eric Omondi getting airtime. While the idea of streamlining governance might sound appealing on paper, Omondi's approach is less about solutions and more about creating a spectacle. He’s like a child who breaks a toy and then demands a completely new one, ignoring the fact that he might have been the one wielding the hammer.

So, while Omondi counts his digital signatures, the rest of us will be here, dealing with the actual realities of governance, or lack thereof. Maybe Gen Z will sign, but don't expect any actual change. Just another Tuesday in Kenya.
