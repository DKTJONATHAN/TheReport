Only in Kenya do you see a company crying about a 10 percent drop in revenue while still handing out a Sh70 per share dividend to their fat-cat shareholders. BAT Kenya is out here posting a gross profit of Sh7.7 billion like they are doing us a favor. It does not matter that the rest of us are struggling to buy unga; as long as the nicotine addicts keep puffing away, the board members in their air-conditioned offices are smiling. They call it productivity initiatives, but we all know it is just a polite way of saying they squeezed every last cent out of their operations while the common man suffered.

Managing Director Crispin Achola is blaming "illicit cigarettes" for their revenue woes, claiming that 45 percent of the market is now illegal. It is the classic Nairobi corporate move: blame the shadowy underground for your problems while still reporting billions in profit. If nearly half the market is fake, then clearly the government is sleeping on the job, losing Sh12 billion in taxes. But do not worry, they will just recover that money by taxing your side-hustle or your basic bread while the cigarette manufacturers keep their margins high.

The company claims operating costs fell by 15 percent thanks to cost control measures. In Nairobi parlance, that usually means somebody lost their job or some poor intern is doing the work of three people for the same pay. While the UK is reporting rising unemployment and India is watching its wholesale prices climb, BAT is patting itself on the back for being lean. It is the same kind of performative nonsense we see when people try to sell you a miracle; it is almost as ridiculous as [The Tanzanian Salon Trading Tea for Tabernacles](/posts/hallelujah-hair-or-just-hype--the-tanzanian-salon-trading-tea-for-tabernacles) where faith is just another product on the shelf.

They are also celebrating a Sh200 million finance income because the Kenyan shilling decided to behave for five minutes against the US dollar. Do not get too comfortable, because geopolitical risks are rising faster than a matatu fare on a rainy Friday. With the US capturing Venezuelan presidents and global inflation pushing up prices from London to New Delhi, this stability is as fragile as a politician's campaign promise. We are one bad news cycle away from the shilling doing a somersault again and the cost of living hitting a new ceiling.

At the end of the day, the Sh70 dividend is the only thing that matters to these people. They will keep talking about challenging environments while their bank accounts grow. Meanwhile, Justice Lawrence Mugambi is out here reminding governments about salary increase laws that they ignored, proving that whether it is the private sector or the state, the rules only apply to the people at the bottom. Keep smoking, Nairobi; you are literally paying for their next holiday in the Maldives while you cough your way through the January bills.
