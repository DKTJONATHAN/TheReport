Oh, darling, pull up a chair because the self-congratulation in Los Angeles reached toxic levels on Sunday night. The Screen Actors Guild decided to rebrand their little shindig as "The Actor Awards," because apparently, "SAG" was just too many syllables for the TikTok generation. Kristen Bell hosted the whole Netflix-streamed affair at the Shrine Auditorium, even performing a musical number about the name change. It was giving high school theater department with a billion-dollar budget, and frankly, we all deserved a refund for the minutes of our lives we won't get back.

In the film categories, "Sinners" took home the top ensemble prize, proving once again that if you put Michael B. Jordan and Ryan Coogler in a room together, the industry will collectively faint with joy. Michael B. Jordan walked away with Best Leading Actor, leaving the internet's boyfriends, Timothee Chalamet and Jacob Elordi, to rot in the audience with nothing but their expensive suits and disappointment. It is the same old story: Hollywood loves a safe, bankable duo while pretending they are taking "risks" with new cinema.

The TV side of things was even more of a predictable slog. Seth Rogen's "The Studio" won for comedy ensemble, which basically means Seth Rogen spent the night high-fiving himself. He did give a "touching" tribute to the late Catherine O-Hara, who won posthumously for her role. While it was a sweet moment, the industry's sudden reverence for talent after they have passed is always a bit rich. It reeks of the same performative empathy we see in [the PR fireshield William used](/posts/the-pr-fireshield--william-s-strategic--concern--over-the-epstein-stench) to distract from his own messy associations. These people only care about legacy when it helps the ratings.

Noah Wyle and "The Pitt" took home the drama awards, which feels like a glitch in the Matrix. Is it 1996? Did someone forget to tell the voters that "The White Lotus" and "Severance" actually exist? Watching the industry ignore boundary-pushing television in favor of a comfortable medical drama ensemble is the height of laziness. Keri Russell won for "The Diplomat," which is essentially the only reason anyone is still watching that show. She is doing the heavy lifting while the rest of the cast is seemingly there for the craft services.

The veterans really showed up to claim their territory, with Sean Penn winning for "One Battle After Another" and Amy Madigan taking home a trophy for "Weapons." It is as if the voters looked at the ballot and just picked the names they recognized from the nineties. Even Harrison Ford showed up to collect a Life Achievement Award from Woody Harrelson, looking like he would rather be anywhere else - preferably in a cockpit or a quiet forest. 

By the time the stunt ensemble winners were announced during the pre-show, most of us were already checked out. "Mission: Impossible - The Final Reckoning" and "The Last of Us" won, because of course they did. Another year, another room full of millionaires patting each other on the back while the rest of us pay for the privilege of watching them stroke their own egos. At least the red carpet looks were decent, though most of these stars looked like they were dressed by a committee of people who hate joy. Until next time, stay cynical.
