To understand India’s current position, analysts point to the five-layer AI stack popularized by Nvidia. At the foundation lies energy and compute infrastructure. While India offers competitive power costs, it continues to struggle with grid reliability and a total dependence on imported semiconductors and GPUs. This creates a strategic vulnerability, particularly as the United States and China aggressively subsidize their domestic hardware capabilities. Without a stable foundation of hardware and energy, India remains reliant on external providers for the very tools required to process its own data.

The middle layers of the stack - foundation models and platforms - represent the current frontier of the AI arms race. While the US and China have established dominant models such as GPT-4 and DeepSeek, India’s strength is currently limited to the application layer. Indian firms excel at creating cost-efficient solutions for local sectors like agriculture and payments, yet they lack the globally competitive foundation models that define industry standards. This gap has turned India into what some critics describe as an open data pond for foreign entities.

The interest from US technology firms is driven by two primary factors: data and market scale. With China’s data ecosystem largely closed to foreign players, India represents the world’s largest source of open, multilingual, real-world data. Global firms are investing heavily to secure a continued supply of this information to train models designed for a global audience. Furthermore, with nearly 100 million weekly ChatGPT users, India is the second-largest market for the platform, offering a scale of distribution that is unmatched in the developing world.

However, this relationship is increasingly viewed through the lens of economic sovereignty. Policymakers are beginning to argue that if data flows abroad without restriction, the incentive to build local AI systems diminishes. There is a growing consensus that India must treat data as a strategic asset, similar to how it used import tariffs to build its domestic automobile industry. The concern is that without decisive regulation, India will merely supply the raw material - data - only to buy back the finished AI products at a premium, with little accountability for the social or economic harms those tools might cause.

The challenge of building domestic models is further complicated by the structure of the Indian corporate sector. While the IndiaAI Mission aims to support startups, these smaller entities often lack the capital and compute resources to compete with Silicon Valley. Meanwhile, large Indian IT conglomerates, which possess the necessary capital, face a conflict of interest. Their existing business models are often built on providing services to the very US tech giants they would need to compete against. This dynamic risks leaving the domestic market open to early acquisitions of promising startups by foreign firms.

As the industry shifts, the impact on the workforce will be profound. AI is expected to automate routine coding and back-office tasks that have long been the bedrock of the Indian outsourcing industry. While this poses a threat to traditional job roles, it also creates an opening for "last-mile" integration. The demand for professionals who can customize AI tools for local regulatory compliance, cybersecurity, and industrial application is expected to surge. Preparing the workforce for this transition is as critical as building the hardware itself.

The current situation mirrors shifts in other sectors where legacy models are being disrupted by new digital realities. Just as the music industry has found that nostalgia is the only currency left for our aging music stars (/posts/nostalgia-is-the-only-currency-left-for-our-aging-music-stars), the Indian technology sector must avoid clinging to outdated service-based models. The choices made by New Delhi in the coming months regarding data localization and compute investment will determine whether India emerges as a rule-maker in the AI era or remains its largest marketplace.
