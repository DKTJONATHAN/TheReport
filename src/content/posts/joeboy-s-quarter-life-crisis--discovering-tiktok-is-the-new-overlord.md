Joeboy is finally waking up and smelling the digital coffee, and he is clearly not liking the roast. In a recent chat with Jay On Air, our resident "Baby Boy" decided to play the role of the wizened elder, lamenting how hard it is for the "new kids" to break out. Apparently, having a decent voice and a label that doesn't steal your royalties isn't enough anymore. Now, you have to be a full-time content creator, a part-time comedian, and a professional attention-seeker on TikTok. Welcome to the real world, Joeboy, where the music is often just a background track for a 15-second dance challenge performed by people who couldn't name your last three singles if their lives depended on it.

It is hilarious to hear this coming from someone who rose to fame when the barrier to entry was basically "be cute and have one catchy hook." Joeboy claims that "back then," a great brand and marketing were the keys to success. What he really means is that life was easier when you didn't have to compete with every Gen-Z influencer with a ring light and a dream. The industry has always been a meat grinder; it just used to happen behind closed doors. Now, the mess is public, much like the [tea and tired buzzwords for Kenya's disenfranchised youth](/posts/another-round-of-tea-and-tired-buzzwords-for-kenya-s-disenfranchised-youth) that we see served daily by people who pretend to care about "the culture" while chasing a viral moment.

The singer feels for the new artists because they have to be "on everybody's faces." Oh, the horror! Imagine having to actually engage with the fans who pay for your designer sneakers. Joeboy is acting like personality is a new requirement in show business. Newsflash: if you don't have a personality, you're just a glorified karaoke machine. The hypocrisy is thick enough to choke on. These stars love the "visibility" when it means six-figure brand deals and private jets, but the moment it requires a bit of digital labor, it becomes a "demanding challenge" that deserves a pity party.

Let's be honest, the industry's "evolving dynamics" are just a fancy way of saying the gatekeepers are losing their grip. In the past, labels decided who was "mainstream" based on who played ball. Now, a teenager in a bedroom can out-stream a studio-backed artist just by being more interesting. Joeboy’s nostalgia for the "good old days" is really just a fear of becoming irrelevant in an age where your Spotify monthly listeners depend on how well you can trend. If the music is actually great, it will find a way, but maybe these artists are just scared that their talent isn't quite loud enough to drown out the noise of the internet.
