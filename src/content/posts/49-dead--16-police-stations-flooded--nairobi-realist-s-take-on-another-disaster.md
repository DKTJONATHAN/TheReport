So, the "devastating rains" have officially claimed 49 lives. Forty. Nine. And the National Police Service, in their infinite wisdom, has issued a statement extending their "deepest condolences." How very comforting. Meanwhile, thousands of families are out on the streets, their homes washed away like yesterday's news. It’s a good thing the weather report is so reliable, right?

And get this - even the police stations are not safe. Sixteen of them are apparently underwater, which I'm sure is a real hindrance to them doing... well, whatever it is they do that the floods don't interrupt. They say they're working to ensure "uninterrupted policing services." I’d love to see that logistical nightmare. Maybe they can dispatch officers via canoe. It's not like the police are the only ones struggling; the Red Cross is reporting even more displaced people than the police, because numbers are always so clear in this country.

The kicker? Over 20,000 acres of farmland are gone. Gone. That’s our food security, folks. But hey, at least the weatherman is warning us about more rain. It’s almost like they’re rubbing it in. They say the intensity is reducing, giving "hope." Hope. We've heard that one before. It's always something with this country, isn't it?

It reminds me of when [Mbadi's Statistical Fairytales Cannot Fill Empty Stomachs](/posts/mbadi-s-statistical-fairytales-cannot-fill-empty-stomachs). Promises and pronouncements, while the reality on the ground remains bleak. This isn't a surprise; it's just the usual script. The water rises, lives are lost, property is destroyed, and we get official statements and warnings. And then, a few months from now, we'll be talking about something else, until the next disaster strikes. Rinse and repeat.
