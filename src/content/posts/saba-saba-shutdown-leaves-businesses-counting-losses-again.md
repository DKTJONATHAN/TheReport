Saba Saba Shutdown: Another Day, Another Shilling Vanishes











So, Tuesday was another day in the life of Nairobi, where "security" means turning the city into a ghost town. Police were out in full force, sealing off roads and turning perfectly good matatus and buses into expensive paperweights. Thousands of us were left to walk, because apparently, the right to protest is secondary to the right to inconvenience everyone else. Businesses, of course, took the hit. They’ve learned their lesson: when the government decides to "secure" a protest, it’s better to just shut down and save yourself the headache, and potentially, the property damage. It's a shame, really, that the only way to ensure security is to cripple the economy.

The economic cost of these "security operations" is becoming as predictable as the sunrise. Transport, retail, banking, the informal sector - everyone loses, and who do you think pays for it in the end? Us, the common mwananchi, with higher prices for everything. Carole Kariuki from Kepsa is flapping her gums about bribery and learning from other countries. Bless her heart. As if our dear leaders listen to anything that doesn't involve lining their pockets. This whole charade just smells of inefficiency and a convenient way to control movement, not necessarily to protect businesses, but to control the narrative.

Meanwhile, the Nairobi Securities Exchange, bless its heart, decided to have a party. While the rest of us were trekking miles or watching our businesses bleed, the market cap shot up. Go figure. It’s almost as if the real economy, the one that matters to the guys in suits, is completely detached from the reality of the streets. This is the kind of disconnect that makes you wonder if we’re all living in the same country. It’s a classic example of how the rich get richer even when the rest of us are stuck in the mud, much like how some powerful figures seem to feast while others starve, a concept I’ve touched on before in [The Broad-Based Buffet: When Enemies Share a Plate While You Starve](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-enemies-share-a-plate-while-you-starve).

Outside the capital, it was more of the same, just on a smaller scale. Mombasa saw a few brave souls under heavy surveillance, while Kisii and Eldoret tried to pretend business as usual, with police lurking like unwelcome wedding guests. This pre-emptive road closure strategy is becoming the government’s go-to move. Why bother with the mess of dispersing a crowd when you can just prevent them from gathering in the first place? It’s a strategy that hits transport operators and the informal sector the hardest, people who live day-to-day. Their livelihoods are as fragile as a politician’s promise.

And so, the cycle continues. Kenya’s economy, already struggling with high living costs and borrowing expenses, gets another kick in the teeth. Business confidence is about as stable as a Jenga tower in an earthquake. It’s no wonder companies are scrambling to set up remote work and digital services. They’re not just preparing for protests anymore; they're preparing for the inevitable chaos that comes with living in a country that seems to specialize in creating its own problems.
