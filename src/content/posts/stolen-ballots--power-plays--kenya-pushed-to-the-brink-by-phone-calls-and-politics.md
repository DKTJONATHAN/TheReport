Stolen Votes, Shouted Matches: How Phone Calls and Power Pushed Kenya to the Brink in 2007

This is the story of how Kenya nearly imploded, again, over a stolen election. Forget the fancy reforms, the system's still rigged, and our politicians? They're still playing their dirty games.

December 30, 2007, Bomas of Kenya. The "orderly hall" was a circus. Politicians bellowing, some ODM types actually getting close enough to the commissioners to maybe pass on a flu. Samuel Kivuitu, bless his struggling soul, trying to read results while the whole place was on fire, metaphorically speaking. The UN mediators, I hear, were tearing their hair out with the PNU lot's "insouciant attitude." Translation: they didn't give a damn while the country was about to go up in smoke.

It was touch and go. For a hot minute, it looked like Kibaki wouldn't pull it off against Odinga. A brief glimmer of hope, quickly extinguished by the usual Kenyan brand of "democracy." Each mess, every single one, just exposed how broken the whole damn system is, forcing a few token reforms that mean squat when the real game is played behind closed doors. We've seen this movie before, haven't we?

And don't even get me started on the run-up to the 2013 elections. The former IEBC boss spilling the beans on all the "dirty games" the politicians pulled. It’s a recurring theme, isn't it? Whether it's election rigging or the mess that is our local football governance, like the FKF turning the league into a [village kiosk](https://example.com/posts/governance-by-vibes--how-fkf-turned-the-league-into-a-village-kiosk), the same old tricks are at play. This isn't about voters; it's about power.

These so-called elections are just elaborate theatre. The real decisions are made in backrooms, with phone calls and backroom deals. The reforms? Just window dressing to make us feel like we have a say. The truth is, the power brokers still pull the strings, and we're left to pick up the pieces.







By the afternoon of December 30, 2007, the usually orderly hall inside the Bomas of Kenya was a spectacle of pure chaos. Politicians were shouting across the stage, with some ODM stalwarts even making a move towards the very table where the commissioners were seated. Security officers had to form a ring around the commission as Samuel Kivuitu, the Electoral Commission of Kenya chairman, visibly struggled to read out results amidst the deafening uproar.

UN archives also reveal the mediators' deep frustration with the PNU side for their casual, almost dismissive attitude, despite the dire situation the country was teetering on. At one point, as more results trickled in, it became starkly apparent that President Kibaki might actually lose to Odinga. A brief moment where sanity seemed possible, before the usual Kenyan brand of "democracy" asserted itself.

Each election cycle, in its own unique way, has exposed the deeply ingrained systemic weaknesses within Kenya’s electoral process. This constant exposure has forced a string of judicial, legislative, and technological reforms. However, the effectiveness of these reforms remains a constant question mark.

We've heard it all before. Former IEBC bosses have revealed the dirty games politicians played in the run-up to the announcement of the 2013 presidential elections results. It’s a familiar story of manipulation and power plays. Whether it’s the electoral commission or the mismanagement of our national football team, as seen in the [FKF turning the league into a village kiosk](https://example.com/posts/governance-by-vibes--how-fkf-turned-the-league-into-a-village-kiosk), the underlying issues of cronyism and self-interest persist.

The truth is, these "elections" are often just elaborate performances. The real power plays, the actual decision-making, happens in hushed phone calls and closed-door meetings. The reforms are mere window dressing, designed to placate the public while the established power structures remain largely untouched.

The system is rigged, and our politicians are masters of the game. They exploit every loophole, every opportunity, to maintain their grip on power, leaving the rest of us to navigate the fallout of their ambition. This isn't about the people's will; it's about the will of the few who control the levers of power.
