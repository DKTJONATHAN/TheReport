Just when you thought the soft-life influencers had a monopoly on faking it until they make it, the OGs of the Kenyan scamming scene have entered the chat. While we are out here losing sleep over which socialite is dating which "investor" in Kilimani, a literal ghost from the 1990s is trying to snatch Sh3.5 billion from our empty pockets. Westmont Holdings, a Malaysian firm that was officially wound up and "dead" for over a decade, has somehow clawed its way out of the corporate grave to demand a massive payout for the Grand Regency Hotel. It is giving very much "zombie apocalypse," but make it high-finance fraud.

Let us talk about the audacity of these people. This whole mess smells like the Goldenberg scandal, which is basically the grandmother of all Kenyan tea. We are talking about Kamlesh Pattni and his band of merry men who turned the 1990s into a free-for-all heist. Now, the Court of Appeal is entertaining this dead company like it is a legitimate comeback tour. Honestly, if these guys put half as much effort into honest work as they do into legal gymnastics, Kenya would be the new Dubai. Instead, we are stuck paying for a hotel dispute that is older than most of the people dancing on TikTok.

The irony is truly delicious. While the DCI is busy raiding suburban homes and we see headlines like [From Flexing on IG to Filtering Jail Bars: The 27 'Entrepreneurs' Interpol Just Dragged](/posts/from-flexing-on-ig-to-filtering-jail-bars--the-27--entrepreneurs--interpol-just-dragged), the real villains are wearing expensive suits and filing motions in court. These modern-day scammers could never compete with the sheer scale of the Sh318 million claims being tossed around like pocket change. It makes you realize that the Instagram flex is just child's play compared to the billion-shilling ghosts haunting the Central Bank of Kenya.

Can we please discuss how taxpayers are always the ones left holding the bill after the party ends? This Sh3.5 billion could fund a lot of things, like fixing our crumbling infrastructure or supporting actual talent, but instead, it might go to settling a grudge match between corporate phantoms and the government. It is the ultimate toxic relationship that just refuses to end. While the digital elite are trading their authenticity for likes, these old-school players are trading our national future for a payday they do not even deserve.

So, the next time you see a celebrity crying about a "fake" lifestyle on social media, remember that the biggest frauds are happening in wood-paneled courtrooms. The ghosts of Goldenberg are not just coming back; they are coming for your tax money with a smile and a legal brief. Stay cynical, Nairobi, because in this city, even the dead firms find a way to make a profit while you are busy scrolling through your feed. The hustle is real, but the scams are even more permanent.
