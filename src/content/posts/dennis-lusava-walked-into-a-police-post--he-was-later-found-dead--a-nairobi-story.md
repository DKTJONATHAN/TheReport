So, Dennis Lusava walks into a police post looking for help, probably thinking he'd get some protection. What happens next? He's later found dead. Surprise, surprise. In this city, walking into a station seeking justice often feels like signing your own death warrant. It’s a recurring nightmare, where the very people sworn to protect us are either complicit or utterly useless.

This isn't some isolated incident, mind you. We've seen this script play out before, a grim tableau of "delayed justice and failure by police to act decisively." Remember the judgment detailing domestic violence where police just stood by? This isn't just about one man's tragic end; it’s about a system that actively lets people down. It's a familiar script in this country, another body unearthed, another life gone, much like the horrifying discoveries in [Kericho Mass Grave Shocker](/posts/kericho-mass-grave-shocker--more-bodies-than-expected--mostly-little-ones--dug-up-in-kenya).

And when the heat is on, what do we get? Official denials, of course. Matiang'i, for instance, dismissing serious encounters as "coincidental." It's the standard political playbook: deny, deflect, then hope we all forget. Meanwhile, the truth, if it ever surfaces, is usually buried under layers of bureaucratic incompetence and outright malice.

They want us to renew our subscriptions, don't they? "Don't miss out on the great content on Nation.Africa," they say. And what is this "great content" exactly? More stories like Lusava's, more tales of despair, more evidence that the promises of a better Kenya are just that- promises. You pay them to read about the mess that never gets cleaned up, the justice that never arrives. It's a cruel joke, really.

While varsity management rattles on about "rationalisation of staff" and "auditing academic staff certificates"-distractions, if you ask me-the real rot continues festering in the institutions that matter most: our law enforcement, our judiciary, the very fabric of our society. Dennis Lusava's death is just another painful reminder of what Nairobi truly is for too many: a place where seeking help can be the most dangerous thing you do.
