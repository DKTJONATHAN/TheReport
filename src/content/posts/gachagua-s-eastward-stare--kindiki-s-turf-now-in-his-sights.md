So, Rigathi Gachagua, fresh off his little triumph in Nyandarua where his guy trounced Ruto's UDA candidate by a landslide - 85% to 13%, talk about a kicking - is now eyeing the eastern side of Mt Kenya. The analysts, bless their hearts, are calling it a "carefully calculated strategy." What else would it be? This is Kenyan politics, not a charity drive. He's trying to pry the whole region away from Ruto, and frankly, after that Nyandarua drubbing, who can blame him for smelling blood?

This whole drama with Gachagua and DP Kindiki being on opposite sides for two years? It’s just another Tuesday in this country. Now Gachagua’s apparently telling his own successor to get off his back while he goes after Ruto and Murkomen. And Kindiki? He's making noises about not letting the region be divided. It’s a classic Nairobi soap opera, just with more political jargon and less actual progress for the mwananchi.

The win in Ol Kalou is being painted as a rejection of Ruto's second-term bid by Mt Kenya. Maybe it is, maybe it isn't. What it certainly is, is a sharp jab to Ruto’s ego and a clear message that Gachagua isn’t just going to fade away quietly. He's got his own party now, the Democracy for the Citizens Party, and he's not afraid to use it to remind everyone who still holds sway in certain corners.

This is all happening while the country struggles with things like fuel prices that are draining our pockets faster than you can say "economic stimulus," as we saw with the [fuel price spike costs Kenya 20B in two months](/posts/fuel-price-spike-costs-kenya-20b-in-two-months-as-renewable-push-grows). But hey, who cares about that when there's a turf war brewing in Mt Kenya? The politicians will always find something more important to squabble over, usually at our expense.

Gachagua’s move eastwards is a clear play to consolidate his influence, aiming to control the entire Mt Kenya voting bloc. It’s a power play, pure and simple. He’s not interested in national unity; he's interested in his own political survival and relevance. And if that means throwing shade at Ruto and challenging Kindiki on his home turf, then so be it. This isn’t about serving the people; it’s about serving oneself.

The analysts are going to keep analyzing, and the politicians will keep politicking. Meanwhile, we’re left watching the same old song and dance, wondering if any of this actually benefits anyone besides the ones making the noise. It’s a familiar script, and the ending is rarely a happy one for the rest of us.
