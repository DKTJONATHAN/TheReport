Martha Karua Tells Wandayi to Resign, Fuel Crisis Blame Game Continues






So, Martha Karua has decided it's Opiyo Wandayi's head on the chopping block this time. Her X account, the modern-day town crier, is buzzing with demands for the Energy CS to pack his bags over this endless fuel crisis. She’s talking about global tensions, sure, but let’s be real, the real drama is happening right here, with our very own brand of mismanagement and, surprise surprise, corruption. It's not exactly rocket science when the whole country is dry.

Karua’s not mincing words, calling the situation "chaos" and pointing out that fuel shortages are popping up from Nyeri to Nyamira. Meanwhile, transporters are already sharpening their knives, ready to hike prices and stick it to the already squeezed Mwananchi. It’s the same old story, really. We've seen how these promises of stability crumble faster than a badly made chapati, and it's always the ordinary Kenyan who ends up paying for it. This reminds me of how sometimes, even the big players get caught in these tangled webs, like we saw with [The Semaglutide Shakedown: Why Corporate Monopolies Always Win](/posts/the-semaglutide-shakedown--why-corporate-monopolies-always-win).

Apparently, Wandayi couldn't even be bothered to show up for a parliamentary probe. Karua, pulling out her "I've been here before" card from her Justice Minister days, is adamant that a CS is responsible, no matter what. She’s right. The buck stops somewhere, and it usually lands squarely on the minister’s desk. If he truly doesn't know what's happening with fuel reserves and suitcases of cash disappearing, then he's either incompetent or complicit. Neither is a good look for someone in charge.

She’s practically yelling at him to "take political responsibility" and "throw in the towel." It’s a bit theatrical, but at least someone’s demanding accountability. It’s not enough to just say "I didn't know." That’s a confession of failure, plain and simple. While Wandayi's juniors are being rounded up, Karua insists he should be heading for the exit too.

The funny thing is, the committee chair, David Gikaria, is giving Wandayi the benefit of the doubt, saying he's always shown up before. Maybe he just had a really good excuse this time, like his car ran out of fuel or something. Meanwhile, we've got Petroleum Principal Secretary Mohamed Liban and Kenya Pipeline MD Joe Sang already out the door, along with the Energy Regulatory Authority boss. It seems the government is finally admitting there’s a scandal, with talk of substandard fuel and inflated prices under this whole G2G deal.

So, as the government tries to put out fires with inquiries and disciplinary actions, Karua is just adding more fuel to the political blaze, demanding Wandayi's head. It’s a classic Kenyan drama: fingers pointed, resignations demanded, and the public left wondering when, if ever, things will actually run smoothly.
