So, Jimi Wanjigi, the man who probably sleeps on a pile of uncashed cheques, is suddenly playing the voice of reason, or at least the voice of your empty pockets. He’s out here claiming Kenya shouldn't be paying for loans that never even bothered to grace Parliament with their presence or make it into the national budget. According to him, if it wasn't approved by the big wigs in suits, it's not our problem. Our sovereignty, apparently, is tied up in constitutional paperwork, not some backroom deals.

He's essentially telling us we're in a "bankrupt state" and need a serious, transparent debt audit, which he reckons can be done in six months. Six months! While we're all scrambling to pay our electricity bills, this man wants to hit the pause button on debt payments, especially the "odious" ones - you know, the ones that probably lined some already fat pockets. It's a bold move, questioning the sacred cow of debt repayment, and you can bet this will be the flavour of every political debate leading up to 2027.

This isn't just about numbers, folks. This is about KSh12.856 trillion in public debt, with KSh7.186 trillion sitting here domestically and KSh5.671 trillion out there with foreign lenders. We’re coughing up KSh633.08 billion for external debt service and another KSh709.30 billion just in domestic interest payments by April 2026. This isn't just a technical problem anymore; it's a moral crisis, an economic headache, and a political grenade. Wanjigi wants us to ask: who borrowed, who approved, where did the money vanish to, did Parliament bless it, and did we, the common mwananchi, actually get anything tangible out of it?

And then there's the jab at President Ruto, who Wanjigi claims has lost credibility. He also threw shade at the opposition, saying their sole focus on a single person is as uninspiring as a government press release. He's arguing that simply removing Ruto isn't enough; we need a concrete alternative, a plan that includes managing debt, protecting citizens, and, yes, finally pumping more cash into our struggling counties. He even promised to do just that if elected president, which, let's be honest, sounds like a breath of fresh air after years of being told there's no money for anything important. It makes you wonder what else has been cooked up behind closed doors, perhaps similar to the issues exposed in the [Kenya Energy Sector's Fuel Data Scheme](/posts/kenya-energy-sector-s-fuel-data-scheme-exposed--more-corrupt-officials-playing-games).
