Welcome to Kenya, where a "global athletics hub" takes over ten years to reach 33 percent completion. Sports Kenya Director General Timothy Kilimo is walking around with a straight face telling us the Kipchoge Keino Stadium will be ready by 2026. If you do the math, they have been moving at a rate of roughly three percent per year. At this pace, my unborn grandchildren might be the ones to finally see a hurdle cleared on that "World Athletics-approved" track.

We are being told this project will cost over Sh3.5 billion. It is the same old script: stall the construction, let the site rot for a few years, and then bring in a foreign contractor like Synohydro Corporation to "restart" the work with a fresh, inflated budget. While the elites in Nairobi and Uasin Gishu play musical chairs with public funds, our world-class athletes are still training in the dust, winning medals in spite of this government, not because of it.

This level of administrative failure is not unique to the sports ministry. It smells exactly like the [Meru County Crisis](/posts/meru-county-crisis-victimhood-politics-kawira-mwangaza) where personality cults and political theater are used to mask the absolute lack of real development. In Eldoret, they call it the "City of Champions," but the only thing they are champions of right now is the art of the long-term con. They promise FIFA specifications and international standards while the grass on the pitch is probably being eaten by local goats.

Governor Jonathan Bii says the region has "little infrastructure to show" for its thousands of medals. That is the understatement of the century. It is embarrassing that the legendary Kipchoge Keino has his name attached to a construction site that has been dormant since the Rio Olympics trials in 2016. Instead of a modern venue, we have a graveyard of taxpayer money and a series of "inspection tours" that serve as nothing more than photo opportunities for politicians in expensive suits.

Now they are already dangling the next carrot, claiming they will build the 64 Stadium once this one is done. It is a classic distraction technique. Why finish one project when you can promise two and deliver none? By the time 2026 rolls around, there will be a new "revised timeline," a new contractor, and another Sh3 billion "needed" to fix what was already supposedly paid for. Don't hold your breath; the only thing being cemented in Eldoret is the status of the stadium as a permanent monument to government incompetence.
