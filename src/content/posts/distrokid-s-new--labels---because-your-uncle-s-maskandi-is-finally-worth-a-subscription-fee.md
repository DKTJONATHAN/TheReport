Oh, look at that. DistroKid has finally decided that Maskandi and Xigaza aren't just "World Music" leftovers. In a move that is being hailed as a "cultural milestone," the New York based distributor is finally letting South African artists label their music correctly. It only took them about a decade too long to realize that a Zulu guitar isn't the same as a generic folk track from Nebraska. While the suits are busy patting themselves on the back for being so "inclusive," the rest of us are wondering how much of this is about heritage and how much is about capturing those sweet, sweet subscription fees from the continent.

Market growth consultant Unathi Memela is out here giving us the PR sermon of the year, claiming this move is about "honouring South Africa’s diverse music culture." Please. We have heard this gospel before. It sounds a lot like the same old tune we see in politics where [Riggy G's New Gospel: Same Wolves, Different Sheepskin](/posts/riggy-g-s-new-gospel--same-wolves--different-sheepskin) promises heaven while the congregation stays broke. Calling it a "bridge between tradition and innovation" is just fancy talk for making sure the algorithm can squeeze more data out of rural KZN and Limpopo.

Let’s talk about the real reason everyone is suddenly so "supportive" of traditional artists: the money. By giving these genres their own tags, the industry claims it will enhance royalty tracking for groups like SAMRO and CAPASSO. If you believe that South African artists are actually going to see a massive spike in their bank accounts because of a dropdown menu, I have a bridge to sell you. We know how these collection societies work; they are more likely to lose your paperwork than find your royalties. This isn't a revolution; it is just a way to make the paperwork look prettier while the artists keep busking for peanuts.

The PR spin also claims this will help younger generations engage with their heritage in a "modern context." Translate that for me, darling. Does it mean we’re about to get a flooded market of low-effort Xigaza remixes on social media? We have seen what happens when "tradition" meets the digital thirst for relevance. Just look at the mess surrounding [Vera Hill and the Thirsty Masses: Why the 'ChiChi Call' is Just Another Basic Trap](/posts/vera-hill-and-the-thirsty-masses--why-the--chichi-call--is-just-another-basic-trap) to see how quickly "authentic" sounds get chewed up and spat out by the hype machine.

At the end of the day, DistroKid is a business, not a museum. They aren't "preserving heritage" out of the goodness of their hearts. They’re positioning themselves as the gatekeepers of the "global digital era." It is cute that Maskandi and Xigaza are finally being "allowed" into the club, but let’s not pretend the doorman isn't still checking for a cover charge. If visibility was the goal, they would have done this years ago. Now, it is just another day in the music industry’s long history of colonizing culture under the guise of progress.
