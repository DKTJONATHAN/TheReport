NCBA Hands Over Chery Vehicles to Rubis Energy: Another Day, Another Deal for the Big Players

This whole song and dance about NCBA handing over 24 Chery Tiggos to Rubis Energy is just another performance for the usual suspects. They've been "partnering" since 2004, which in Nairobi terms means they've been finding ways to grease each other's palms for almost two decades. It’s all about "trust," "innovation," and "shared growth" - code for "we're making money together, and you, the little guy, are footing the bill."

Apparently, these fancy Tiggos are just the tip of the iceberg. NCBA is "leasing and managing" a whole fleet of 72 vehicles for Rubis. They spin it as "optimising capital allocation" and "maintaining high fleet availability." What that really means is Rubis gets to look like they have the latest wheels without actually spending the upfront cash, and NCBA gets to collect interest and management fees for years to come. It’s a classic win-win for them, a slow drain for everyone else.

The NCBA bigwig, James Gossip, is babbling about being a "solutions partner" and "trusted advisor." Please. They're not in the business of charity. They've moved "from a banking provider into a strategic partner" because it's more profitable. They "structure solutions around each customer’s operating model" - meaning they tailor their loans and leases to squeeze the most out of businesses, especially with the economy doing its usual Kenyan tightrope walk. With rising costs and currency pressures, businesses are desperate for "asset-light" models, and banks like NCBA are more than happy to oblige, as long as they get their cut. This whole affair reminds me of the convoluted deals we've seen before, like when [Billy Mwangi's story](posts/Billy-Mwangi) highlighted the opaqueness of certain high-level arrangements.

Rubis's MD, Frederic Maupetit, chimes in with his mission to "serve our customers well." Bless his heart. He’s talking about serving his "thousands of commercial and industrial customers" with these new cars. Translation: more shiny vehicles to ferry their sales reps and technicians around, ensuring they can keep pushing Rubis fuel and services. It's all about optics and a slightly more comfortable way to extract money from the public. "Better tools mean better service," he says. Yes, better tools for them to make more money.

And let's not forget the "integrated corporate banking platform" they're so proud of. It's a "one-stop-shop" for asset finance, trade finance, cash management - basically, every way they can get their hands on your money. The energy sector is a "key focus," of course, because that's where the big money is. Fuel consumption is up, electricity is up, and NCBA is right there, ready to "support players across the energy value chain." They’re not supporting them; they’re enabling them to perpetuate the cycle.

The whole thing is a masterclass in financial engineering, making it sound like a noble endeavour when it's just a sophisticated way to keep the wheels of commerce turning for the benefit of the few. While they talk about "shared growth," it’s always the same story: the banks and the big corporations get richer, and the rest of us are left to navigate the rising costs and fuel our journeys with the hope that someday, perhaps, we might see a sliver of this so-called "shared growth."
