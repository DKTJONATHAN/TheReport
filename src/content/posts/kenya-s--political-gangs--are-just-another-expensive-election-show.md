So, we’re heading into another election cycle in Kenya, and what’s the big new development? The same tired old story of hired goons causing mayhem. Brian Ongore, a Nairobi resident, sums it up perfectly: seeing a group of young men now sparks panic, not conversation. It’s the "living in the jungle" feeling, apparently. This isn't just in Nairobi; Kisumu and Nyahururu have seen their share of "opposition-linked gatherings" conveniently disrupted by these paid troublemakers. It’s a classic Kenyan election strategy: why bother with actual policies when you can just hire a few hundred boys for a few dollars a day to do your dirty work? And the police? Well, they're often too busy looking the other way.

The French news agency AFP is reporting that these "goons" are being snagged for as little as $4 a day. That’s less than a decent street meal in Nairobi. Poverty and ruthless competition, they say. Predictable. We saw it happen at a civil society forum at All Saints Cathedral, a place meant for serious discussion, disrupted by thugs who, according to Amnesty International Kenya, "confessed to having been sent by some powerful government officials." Powerful officials, huh? Shocking. It’s the same old song, really, where the politicians play their power games, and the unemployed youth become the disposable pawns. It makes you wonder if any of this is about actual governance or just a brutal competition for power, much like how we've seen some leaders gamble with borders for political mileage.

Even President Ruto, bless his heart, has finally noticed this "growing presence of political gangs." He calls them "innocent children being used by bankrupt politicians." True, but conveniently forgetting who's bankrolling them. He vows they'll be "tracked down," but we've heard these promises before. Security analysts like Byron Adera point out it's not new, just "more organized." Political enterprise, he calls it, propped up by "goonism." And guess who’s most vulnerable? The unemployed youth. Adera’s right to worry about partisan policing and whether the outcome will truly reflect the will of the people or the will of the politicians pulling the strings.

Governance analyst Sheila Owigo Olang hits the nail on the head: "impunity." These faces are familiar, she says, backed by "very strong sponsors" because there are "no consequences." It’s not just rallies anymore; churches are targeted. And women, the biggest voting demographic, are particularly fearful. Olang herself has experienced intimidation. When women are scared to participate, the outcomes are "illegitimate." It’s a vicious cycle, echoing the dark days of 2007 when violence overshadowed an election. The Kofi Annan Foundation is already sounding the alarm, citing political tensions, economic hardship, and public mistrust. So, will Kenya's 2027 election be about voters, or about who can hire the most muscle? Based on past performance, don't hold your breath.
