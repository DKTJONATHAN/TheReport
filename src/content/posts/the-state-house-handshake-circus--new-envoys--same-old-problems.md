Another day in the glass house on the hill, and another set of foreigners has arrived to tell us how much they love our "potential." President William Ruto spent Thursday, March 5, 2026, playing the gracious host to newly appointed ambassadors from Jordan, Saudi Arabia, and Mozambique. It is the same old script: the red carpet is rolled out, the suits are pressed, and the handshakes are firm, all while the average Kenyan wonders if these "bilateral relations" will ever actually put unga on the table.

The ceremony at State House was filled with the usual flowery language about "fostering bonds of friendship." We have seen this performance before, most notably during [the state house tea party](/posts/the-state-house-tea-party--ruto-s-sh2-billion-pity-payment-for-kemu) where billions are shuffled around like pocket change while the rest of the country begs for crumbs. Ruto welcomed Rulan Samara of Jordan, Saad Abdullah Al Nofe of Saudi Arabia, and Jacinto Maguni of Mozambique with promises of "working together," which is usually diplomatic code for finding new ways to manage our mounting national crises.

Ruto’s speech was a masterpiece of empty optimism. He spoke about "prosperity" and "aspirations," words that sound great in a high-ceilinged room but lose their flavor by the time they reach the dusty streets of Nairobi. He authorized these envoys to pursue their governments' interests within our territory, a phrasing that should make any realist nervous. Historically, when foreign governments pursue their "interests" here, it is the Kenyan taxpayer who ends up footing the bill for the privilege of being exploited.

This "tradition" of presenting letters of credence is essentially a formal green light for these diplomats to begin their "economic activities." In a country where the cost of living is a constant chokehold, these ceremonies feel like watching a landlord welcome new tenants while the roof is still leaking. We are told this is a "crucial step" for the nation, but for those of us living in the real Kenya, it is just another photo op in a long line of expensive distractions.

As the ambassadors expressed their gratitude and headed off to their air-conditioned offices, the reality on the ground remained unchanged. The "warm welcome" at State House does not translate to lower fuel prices or better healthcare. It just means the guest list for the next national debt meeting has been updated. Ruto looks forward to working with them, but the rest of us are just waiting to see how much this latest round of "friendship" is going to cost us in the long run.
