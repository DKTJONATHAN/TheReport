So, the Kenya Defence Forces, our very own protectors of national security, have decided their new battlefield is Mihang’o Primary School. They’re planting trees, over 5000 of them. Apparently, this is part of some grand commitment to environmental conservation and community engagement. The headteacher, Mrs. Mary Ondieki Chemutai, is all smiles and appreciation. Sounds lovely, doesn't it? Like a scene straight out of a feel-good propaganda film.

The KDF commanders are trotting out their "Environmental Soldier Programme," a fancy name for telling soldiers to get their hands dirty with soil instead of whatever else they might get their hands dirty with. They talk about environmental stewardship and sustainable development, as if planting a few saplings will magically fix the mess this country is in. It’s all part of the government's grand plan to plant 15 billion trees by 2032. Ambitious, yes. Realistic? We’ll see.

Let’s not be fooled by the greenwash. This is likely just another photo opportunity, a way for the military to look good in the eyes of the public. While they’re busy playing gardener, have they considered the real environmental degradation happening elsewhere? The kind that’s harder to plant away? It reminds me of how we often focus on the superficial, like the [welcome to JKIA: the world’s most expensive parking lot](/posts/welcome-to-jkia--the-world-s-most-expensive-parking-lot), while ignoring the deeper rot.

Environmental conservation is a shared responsibility, they say. Funny, because when it comes to resource management and actual sustainable development, the people with the real power seem to be the ones most disconnected from the earth. These tree-planting exercises are a nice gesture, a temporary distraction, but they don't address the systemic issues that lead to environmental destruction in the first place. It’s like putting a band-aid on a bullet wound.

The list of KDF-affiliated entities reads like a who's who of government-linked businesses, from Kenya Meat Commission to Kenya Shipyards Limited. One wonders if their real "commitment" lies more in these ventures than in nurturing a few trees. While they are busy with their PR exercise, perhaps they could also focus on ensuring that actual environmental laws are enforced, rather than just planting symbols of hope that may never take root.

Ultimately, it's a nice story for the headlines. KDF, heroes of the soil. But behind the smiles and the shovels, the cynicism remains. It’s the same old song, just with a greener tune. We’ll be watching to see if these seeds of service actually grow into something substantial, or if they’re just another fleeting trend in the grand theatre of Kenyan governance.
