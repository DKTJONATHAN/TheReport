Murkomen Orders Paramilitary Training for Sergeants-at-Arms - More Money Thrown at Old Problems

Nairobi’s finest, the county assembly Sergeants-at-Arms, are about to get a taste of the military. Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen, in his infinite wisdom and a year after some Nairobi hooligans decided Parliament was a playground, has decreed that these guys need to be kitted out like commandos. Apparently, a few "emerging security threats," which I suspect means more politicians throwing tantrums, require more than just stern glares and a clipboard. So, expect them to be doing push-ups instead of guarding empty chairs.

The CS blathered on about budgetary constraints, a familiar tune in this country. Yet, he’s still finding funds for this paramilitary jamboree, training not just county folk but also the supposedly more competent Senate and National Assembly sergeants. It’s all about a "uniform security framework," which, knowing how things run here, will likely mean more meetings, more committees, and a whole lot of paperwork nobody will read. The whole thing feels like a knee-jerk reaction, a shiny new plan born out of the embarrassing June 2024 Parliament invasion.

Murkomen even had the audacity to suggest these Sergeants-at-Arms should be part of county security meetings. As if the existing security apparatus in this city isn't already a masterclass in inefficiency. He’s talking about threats beyond "physical confrontations," mentioning misinformation and cyber risks. This is the same country where online scams run rampant, a topic we’ve touched on before, like with the illusion of wealth peddled by forex influencers. Now we’re supposed to believe these new recruits will magically fend off digital dragons?

He’s also calling for legislative amendments to protect these officers from political meddling. Bless his heart. He warns them against "unlawful processes" like switching off microphones. You know, the kind of petty drama that makes watching county assemblies more entertaining than a poorly produced reality show, a bit like the gimmicks politicians try to get youth attention. The law will protect them, he assures, as if the law in Kenya has ever been a reliable bodyguard for anyone not already drowning in cash.

This whole initiative is being sold as a comprehensive strategy. Professionalising security, better coordination, legal safeguards. It’s the same old song and dance, just with a slightly more aggressive beat. Institutions outlast individuals, Murkomen wisely stated, as if anyone cares about the institution of democracy when there’s a chance for a fat training allowance or a new uniform. Let’s see how long this "paramilitary" sparkle lasts before it’s just another underfunded, overhyped government project.







Interior and National Administration Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen has declared that County Assembly Sergeants-at-Arms will undergo paramilitary training to bolster security for Members of County Assemblies (MCAs) and legislative premises. Speaking at the 3rd National Conference of Sergeants-at-Arms in Machakos on March 27, 2026, Murkomen stressed the vital role these officers play in protecting legislative chambers nationwide.

Murkomen acknowledged the familiar hurdle of budgetary constraints but proposed collaborative efforts to facilitate this paramilitary training for county assembly Sergeants-at-Arms. He highlighted the necessity of equipping these officers with skills to counter evolving security threats, even amidst financial limitations. The training initiative will also extend to Sergeants-at-Arms in the Senate and National Assembly who have not yet received specialized preparation, aiming for a unified security framework across all Kenyan legislative institutions.

In a move to enhance collaboration, CS Murkomen directed county security teams to incorporate County Assembly Sergeants-at-Arms into their regular security meetings. He emphasized the need for closer coordination between Parliament, county assemblies, and national security agencies to ensure robust protection. This announcement follows the June 25, 2024, invasion of Parliament, which Murkomen described as a significant low point, exposing vulnerabilities in the country's democratic institutions and serving as a wake-up call.

The CS pointed out that threats to legislative institutions now extend beyond physical confrontations to encompass misinformation, cyber risks, and procedural manipulation. He called for legislative amendments to insulate the Office of the Sergeant-at-Arms from political interference and to make it punitive for Members to resort to force to achieve their objectives. He cautioned officers against participating in irregular acts such as tampering with microphones or CCTV to manipulate proceedings, urging them to stand firm, as the law should protect them.

Murkomen urged stakeholders to implement concrete reforms, stating that "Institutions outlast individuals. The systems we build today will protect future generations and uphold democracy." This initiative is presented as part of a broader strategy to professionalize security within Kenya’s legislative bodies, combining paramilitary training, improved inter-agency coordination, and legal safeguards. The goal is to prevent a recurrence of the 2024 parliamentary invasion and to effectively respond to the dynamic landscape of security challenges, a landscape that often feels like a crime scene in itself, as we've seen with the chaotic online spaces.
