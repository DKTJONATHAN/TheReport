Colton Underwood is back on his favorite hobby-horse: playing the misunderstood victim. In a recent chat with fellow reality-show-villain Harry Jowsey, the former Bachelor attempted to "set the record straight" about rumors that he stormed off the set of The Traitors reunion. According to Colton, he did not flee in a huff; he simply used a ten-minute break to talk to his "team" and hydrate. It is the classic PR pivot: turning a moment of alleged instability into a disciplined professional choice. Honestly, hearing him moan about the "mental energy" required to fight internet rumors is almost impressive when you consider his track record.

This brand of [narcissism on ice: laegreid hijacks olympic glory for personal damage control](/posts/narcissism-on-ice--laegreid-hijacks-olympic-glory-for-personal-damage-control) is a recurring theme in the reality television circuit, where stars try to rewrite their own scripts once the cameras stop rolling. Colton claims he does not have the time to combat "every negative lie," yet he seems to have plenty of time to sit on podcasts and talk about how much he does not have the time. The hypocrisy is thick enough to choke on. If he really wanted peace, he would stop signing up for competitive reality shows where drama is the only currency.

Let us not forget why the public is so quick to believe the worst about this man. We are talking about the guy who was hit with a restraining order after his ex-girlfriend, Cassie Randolph, accused him of stalking and harassment. There were reports of a tracking device under her car-a detail that no amount of "coming out" PR tours can fully scrub from the collective memory. For him to now complain about "negative lies" online is a bit rich, considering his past legal woes were not exactly fictional campfire stories.

Even his castmates on The Traitors were not buying the soft-launch of his new persona. Lisa Rinna, the queen of stirring the pot, famously called him a "stalker" on social media. It seems the "reformed" Colton is finding it hard to convince anyone that he is anything other than a man who struggles with boundaries. Casting him on a show called The Traitors was almost too on-the-nose, but seeing him try to play the "mental health" card to dodge accountability is the real betrayal here.

At the end of the day, Colton Underwood is just another celebrity trying to manage a brand that has been permanently stained. He can talk about pre-planned breaks and drinking water until he is blue in the face, but the public sees the pattern. You cannot stalk your way into our hearts and then demand we respect your "mental energy" when things get a little uncomfortable on a reunion stage. Pick a struggle, Colton.
