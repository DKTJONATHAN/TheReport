So the DCI finally caught Evans Simiyu Wanyonyi, or "Boyi" for the fans. They are calling it a "prolonged cat-and-mouse pursuit," which is a fancy way of saying they kept tripping over their own feet while this guy lived his best life in Waitaluk. It is the usual Nairobi script: paint a petty thief as a mastermind to make a dawn raid look like a scene from a Hollywood blockbuster instead of just a bunch of guys waking up a village at 5:00 AM to arrest a man in his pajamas.

When you look at the "exhibits" recovered, the comedy really starts. We are talking about police boots, a mess tin, and hospital equipment. It is not exactly high-stakes heist material. If a fugitive is running around in official police gear and stealing from clinics, it says more about the porous state of our government "official stores" than it does about his criminal genius. Our institutions are basically open-air markets for anyone with a heavy pair of pliers and a little bit of audacity.

This whole charade reminds me of how the elite operate in this city, just on a much smaller, sadder scale. Whether it is a village thief with a stolen solar panel or the political class described in [The Broad-Based Buffet: When the Vultures Start Biting Each Other](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-the-vultures-start-biting-each-other), the appetite for what does not belong to you remains the same. The only real difference is that "Boyi" gets a "dramatic raid" and a cold cell, while the big fish get a seat at the committee table and a "consultation" fee.

The DCI is busy touting this as a "significant breakthrough" in dismantling criminal networks. Let us be real for a second. If "Boyi" is the top of the food chain in Trans Nzoia, then the bar for notoriety has officially hit the floor. While the detectives are busy documenting solar batteries and connecting cables like they found a drug lord’s hidden stash, actual tragedies like the fire at Yare Plaza in Eastleigh are gutting the livelihoods of people who actually worked for what they have.

Now we wait for the "arraignment," another piece of state-funded theatre. The suspect will sit in a dock, the public will get a few photos of "crude weapons," and the DCI will issue another statement about their "commitment to sustained crackdowns." Meanwhile, the recovered items will sit in a police store until they probably get "misplaced" again. It is a cycle as old as the potholes on Mombasa Road: lots of noise, very little progress, and the taxpayer always picks up the bill for the fuel.
