Policy Disruptions Intensify as Ruto's Government Tries to Pacify Kenyans

The Ruto administration is desperately throwing crumbs at a hungry populace, a classic pre-election playbook. With inflation gnawing at pockets and global rumblings adding to the pressure, expect more promises than substance.

Belinda Achieng: Nairobi's front-row seat to political theatre.

Nairobi

Politics, Economics, Inflation

Politicians

As the election clock ticks louder, President Ruto’s government is busy performing a circus act with Kenya’s economic policies. Fuel tax cuts, suspended power tariff hikes, and a sudden embrace of universal higher education funding - it’s all just smoke and mirrors to distract from the economic reality. This isn’t about fixing the country; it’s about fixing the polls. Remember when your payslip was already a crime scene? This is just the government adding a few more band-aids, but the wound remains deep.

Analysts, bless their hearts, call it "policy disruption." I call it the same old song and dance we see every five years. Karaya Mokaya from ICPAK spells it out plainly: the economic policy is "hijacked" to favour the ruling party. They’re peddling attractive policies that aren't anchored in sound economics, all to woo us for votes. The inevitable consequence? More borrowing, more debt, and a future where we’re all paying for this electoral gamble.

The flip-flop on higher education funding is a prime example. After introducing a model that choked universities and burdened poor households, they’re now talking about full government funding. President Ruto claims the previous "Differentiated Unit Cost" model failed, leading universities to near closure. Now, the plan is to go "universal," ignoring a child's background and focusing on merit. Let’s just hope this isn't another grand gesture that crumbles under its own weight, leaving students stranded once again.

Meanwhile, the fuel tax reduction, extended for another three months, and the suspension of electricity tariff increases are temporary reprieves, not solutions. These concessions, born out of necessity and global price surges, come with a hefty price tag, likely leading to more borrowing. As Mokaya points out, these aren't financially sustainable long-term. Expect a supplementary budget soon, another layer to this elaborate charade.

Kenya’s inflation might have seen a slight dip, but don't break out the champagne. At 6.4 percent, it’s still stubbornly high, a constant reminder of the economic pressures we face. While transport and food costs offered a brief respite, the lingering impact of global energy price hikes is a stark indicator that our economic stability remains fragile, precariously balanced on political expediency.
