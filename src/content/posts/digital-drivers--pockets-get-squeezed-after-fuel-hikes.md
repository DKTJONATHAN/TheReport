Digital Drivers' Pockets Get Squeezed After Fuel Hikes

Nairobi drivers are struggling as fuel costs soar and ride-hailing platforms drag their feet on fare adjustments, leaving them with less cash.

Digital drivers are facing a double whammy: rising fuel prices and platforms that are slow to update fares, squeezing their already thin earnings.

Fuel

Fuel price hikes are hitting Nairobi's ride-hailing drivers hard, with many reporting significantly reduced earnings as they're forced to absorb the extra costs. The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) has jacked up prices, and while the government chipped in with a slight VAT cut, it's barely a drop in the ocean for these drivers. They're stuck between the rock of expensive fuel and the hard place of unadjusted app fares.

Patrick Waigwa, a Bolt driver, isn't mincing words. He says Bolt hasn't budged on its pricing formula despite drivers raising hell. "For me, it is a loss of about three hundred shillings in what I take home," he laments, adding that people are already cutting back on non-essential travel, further reducing potential earnings. It’s a vicious cycle where the drivers end up carrying the can for everything.

Faras driver Joseph Kimuhu echoes the same frustration. Clients just point to the app's fare, oblivious to the fact that the apps haven't caught up with reality. This means drivers are literally losing money on every trip. It’s not just Bolt; Uber drivers like Nduruchi Chesoli are also feeling the heat, creating awkward tension with passengers who are themselves feeling the economic pinch. It’s a messy situation where everyone is straining.

This isn't just a digital problem; traditional transport is also feeling the pinch. Long-distance buses have already hiked their fares. Industry folks are warning that these delays in fare adjustments will only deepen losses for drivers and push up inflation for everyone else. It’s the same old story in Nairobi: the little guy gets squeezed, and the big platforms count their profits.
