If you think these politicians are knocking on your door because they suddenly care about your sukuma wiki budget, you probably still believe the government when they say the economy is recovering. This shift to door-to-door campaigning is just a polite way of saying they are too broke or too scared to face a real crowd. Using Ramadan as a cover for their tactical pivot is the kind of hypocrisy we have come to expect from this lot. Whether it is Isiolo, Embu, or Kakamega, the goal is the same: find a way to stay at the trough while pretending to be humble servants of the people.

The former Deputy President is out here trying to reclaim kingpin status with his new DCP outfit, as if we have forgotten his previous roles in this long-running tragicomedy. It is a desperate fight for relevance in West Kabras and the Mt Kenya region. Meanwhile, UDA is busy trying to cement its authority, treating these by-elections like a game of Monopoly where the citizens are just the cardboard pieces being moved around. They call it a litmus test, but for the average Kenyan, it is just another day of watching the elites fight over who gets to hold the whip.

Even James Orengo sounds tired, crying about MoUs and the death of Raila Odinga's party while his colleagues are busy [opening the gates to God-knows-what](/posts/opening-the-gates-to-god-knows-what) by negotiating mega deals with the ruling party. The so-called united opposition is folding faster than a cheap tent in a Nairobi storm. They talk about active negotiations like they are doing us a favor, but we all know a mega deal usually means a mega payday for those at the top and a mega headache for everyone else.

While the big fish are busy shaking hands and splitting the loot, the MCAs are left scratching their heads, complaining that they have not been briefed on their place in the new structure. It is almost funny if it was not so pathetic. These are the people supposed to represent us at the grassroots, yet they are as clueless as the rest of us about the secret handshakes happening in high-end hotels. They are just waiting for instructions on how to sell the next big lie to their constituents.

In the middle of this political circus, a 25-year-old who went abroad to seek a better life goes missing after a single WhatsApp text. It is a grim reminder that while the political class argues over kingpin status, the youth are either disappearing into the void or being used as pawns, much like the journey of [Billy Mwangi: From State Sponsored Abduction To Senate Bid](/posts/Billy-Mwangi). Our priorities are so skewed that a ward mini-poll gets more airtime than a family losing their child to the unknown. That is the Nairobi reality: the loud stay rich, and the quiet simply disappear.
