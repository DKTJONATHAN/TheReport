East Africa's Jobs Are Mostly Jua Kali, And The Government Is Loving It (For Now)

This report from Moody's is basically saying what we already know: Kenya and its neighbours are running on the hustle. But while it keeps the youth from getting into too much trouble, it's emptying the government's pockets and strangling any real economic growth.

Picture this: 88 percent of Sub-Saharan Africa is out here hustling in the informal sector. That's nearly everyone you see on the street - the mama mbogas, the boda-boda guys, the guys building things for a daily wage. They're keeping busy, sure, and that's a win for the politicians who don't want a restless crowd on their hands. It's like putting a lid on a boiling pot, but the pot is still about to explode.

The real kicker? Our governments are losing out on billions. Because these businesses aren't registered, they’re not paying taxes. So instead of getting a steady stream of income, they're scraping by on what they can get from indirect taxes and import duties. Moody's says we're collecting way less revenue than the rest of the world, and that potential VAT revenue is just vanishing into thin air. It's like trying to run a household on pocket change while the bills keep piling up. Remember when we heard about [Top Petroleum Officials Arrested: Kenya's Fuel Security In Question](/posts/top-petroleum-officials-arrested--kenya-s-fuel-security-in-question)? That's the kind of mess you get when the system is leaky.

And it’s not just about the money. This widespread informality means lower productivity, smaller paychecks, and less investment. It’s a vicious cycle. Young people are flooding the job market, but there aren't enough real jobs. So they do what they have to, which is join the hustle. But without access to loans and proper support, these small businesses are stuck. It’s like expecting a seedling to grow into a mighty tree without water or sunlight.

The UN is screaming about this, saying if we don't sort it out, we'll be in deeper trouble with debt and dwindling aid. They're talking about financial inclusion and entrepreneurship, which sounds nice, but it's a pipe dream when the system is designed to keep everyone in the shadows. Unless governments actually start making it easier and more beneficial to be formal, this jua kali economy is just going to keep growing, and our economies will keep sputtering along.

The only way out, according to Moody's, is to drag these businesses and workers into the formal economy. But that's a massive undertaking, and let's be real, it's a lot easier said than done. For now, we're stuck with a generation of youth keeping themselves busy in the informal sector, and the government is happy to look the other way. It's a temporary fix, and the long-term cost is going to be brutal.
