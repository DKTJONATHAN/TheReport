So, a bunch of bankers from Egypt and Morocco landed in Nairobi to see how we do things. They probably expected a masterclass in resilience, but instead, they found a tragic comedy. We love to brag that agriculture is the backbone of our economy, accounting for 22 percent of our GDP and employing nearly half the workforce. But here is the punchline: our farmers make about 900 dollars a year, while their counterparts in Egypt and Morocco make three times that. We are essentially the most hardworking poor people on the continent, sweating for pennies while the rest of the world uses actual machines and government-backed capital.

The difference is simple and embarrassing. In North Africa and Turkey, they treat agriculture like a strategic asset, not a side hustle. They have massive state-owned banks that actually function. These institutions provide subsidized loans, deep rural networks, and a safety net that is more than just a political slogan. Meanwhile, our Agricultural Finance Corporation is a toothless lion with no money and no deposit-taking power. We leave our farmers to the mercy of private banks who treat a small-scale maize grower like a high-risk gambler. It is the same old story of [The Cannes Mirage: Selling Dreams in France While the Local Creative Scene Rots](/posts/the-cannes-mirage--selling-dreams-in-france-while-the-local-creative-scene-rots) where we sell a shiny image of a "vibrant private market" while the reality on the ground is just decay and desperation.

Our system is designed to fail. We ask the most vulnerable people in the country to carry the heaviest risks alone. While a Moroccan farmer gets a buffer against climate change through subsidized rates, the Kenyan farmer is out there praying for rain and hoping the bank does not auction his only cow. Our private lenders have commercial return expectations that a seasonal crop simply cannot meet. We are essentially running a marathon with both legs tied together and wondering why we are losing to countries with half our agricultural workforce.

Now we have the Kampala Declaration, another fancy piece of paper signed in early 2025. It promises to boost productivity by 45 percent and demands that governments spend 10 percent of their budget on agriculture. Kenya is currently sitting at a pathetic three percent. To think our government will suddenly find the "collective will" to triple that funding is a level of optimism I simply cannot afford. We love to talk about "green finance" and "structural reforms" in Nairobi hotels, but until we build an actual engine to de-risk the sector, it is all just hot air.

The reality is that we are not going to "reimagine" our way out of this. If we keep pouring money into the same leaky pipes, the only thing that will grow is the frustration of the 56 million people who are tired of hearing that they are the "backbone" while their ribs are showing. We need a state-led mechanism that actually works, or we should just stop lying to ourselves that we are an agricultural powerhouse. We are just a country that likes to play farm-house while the adults in North Africa run a real business.
