Naturally, Grammarly, a company built on the illusion of effortless perfection, has stumbled. Their much-touted "Expert Review" AI feature, which brazenly paraded editing suggestions as if they originated from esteemed figures like Stephen King and Neil deGrasse Tyson, is now facing a class action lawsuit. This isn't innovation; it's outright intellectual larceny dressed up in algorithmic jargon.

The suit, spearheaded by investigative journalist Julia Angwin, highlights a predictable outcome of unchecked ambition in the AI space. Grammarly, or its parent company Superhuman, decided it was perfectly acceptable to profit from the reputations and life's work of hundreds of writers, journalists, and academics without so much as a by-your-leave. The AI agent, a sophisticated digital charlatan, was designed to mimic their authority, to lend their names to the mundane task of proofreading, all while offering no actual value or consent from the purported experts.

The legal argument is, as Angwin's attorney points out, rather straightforward. Laws exist in New York and California, where Superhuman is based, that prohibit the commercial exploitation of a person's name and likeness without permission. This isn't a novel concept; it's basic common sense and legal precedent. Yet, in the relentless pursuit of market share and profit, tech companies like Grammarly seem to believe they operate in a legal and ethical vacuum.

Superhuman's statement, predictably full of apologetic platitudes and promises of future reform, is a transparent attempt to mitigate damage. "We clearly missed the mark," claims Ailian Gan, director of product management. Of course, they missed the mark. They aimed to exploit intellectual property for financial gain, and when caught, they feign surprise. The CEO, Shishir Mehrotra, echoes this sentiment with a defiant denial of the claims' merit while simultaneously admitting they need a "better approach." It's the standard corporate playbook: deny, deflect, and promise to do better once exposed.

This episode is a stark reminder of the pervasive cynicism embedded in the tech industry. When the promise of AI is invoked, the ethical guardrails often evaporate, replaced by a ruthless drive for monetization. Grammarly's "Expert Review" was not an attempt to democratize expertise; it was a calculated gambit to leverage the authority of others for commercial advantage. The lawsuit is not just about financial damages; it's a necessary pushback against the wholesale appropriation of intellectual capital that defines so much of our current technological landscape. The legal system, it seems, is still the last bastion against the unfettered greed of these digital impresarios.
