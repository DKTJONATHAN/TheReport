Look, it’s not exactly a shocker, is it? The Kioko saga is just the latest drama in the never-ending Kenyan theatre of corruption and incompetence. Our county governments, bless their hearts, are busy signing contracts like they’re handing out free mandazi, all while having absolutely no clue where the money will magically appear from. It's Sh183 billion, people. That’s not loose change; that’s enough to fix a few potholes and maybe, just maybe, pay the people who actually did the work.

Nairobi County is leading the pack in this spectacular display of fiscal irresponsibility, boasting a cool Sh86.8 billion in outstanding dues. And it's not just us; Kilifi, Machakos, Kiambu - they're all in the same sinking boat. These aren't just numbers on a spreadsheet; these are businesses, small enterprises, and families who are being squeezed dry because some politician decided to play big spender without a proper budget. It’s the same old song and dance we've been hearing since devolution kicked off in 2013.

The excuses are always the same: political pressure, "demonstrating development." It’s all about the optics, not about actually having the cash. They issue tenders, launch projects, and make promises before they've even seen a cent of approved budget. It’s like going to Westlands for a fancy dinner and ordering the most expensive steak without checking your wallet. Then you wonder why you’re being chased out by bouncers. This isn't just poor planning; it's a deliberate act of economic sabotage. Remember that Sh12bn bad fuel scandal? This is just a different flavor of the same rot.

Now, the Senate is finally waking up, talking about freezing projects. About time, I suppose. But talk is cheap, and we’ve heard it all before. What we need are actual teeth. Accounting officers who sign unfunded deals should face personal consequences, not just a slap on the wrist. We need transparent systems where we can actually track where this money is supposed to be going and why it’s not getting there.

Ultimately, it all boils down to the same fundamental rule that these geniuses in county halls seem to conveniently forget: No budget, no contract. It’s not rocket science. It’s basic financial sanity, something that seems to be in very short supply when public funds are involved. Unless we enforce this, devolution will continue to be a gravy train for the corrupt and a source of perpetual misery for honest business owners.
