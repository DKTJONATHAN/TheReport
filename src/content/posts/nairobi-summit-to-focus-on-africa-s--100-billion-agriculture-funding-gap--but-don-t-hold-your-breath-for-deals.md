So, Nairobi's getting another summit. This time it's called the Financing Agri-Food Systems Sustainably (FINAS) 2026, and they're promising to tackle Africa's colossal $100 billion agricultural financing gap. Over a thousand people - policymakers, financiers, the whole lot - are expected to flock here, no doubt eager to "explore ways of unlocking capital" for our food systems. Because that's exactly what we need, another conference to talk about problems we've been talking about for decades.

Apparently, agriculture is the backbone of Africa, contributing up to 30 percent of GDP and employing most of us. Yet, commercial banks, bless their risk-averse hearts, barely throw a measly 5 percent of their lending at the sector. This summit brings in the usual suspects: Tanzania, Nigeria, South Africa, Egypt, and a parade of foreign development agencies like the World Bank, FAO, JICA, and GIZ. They'll all be here to assess if investments are "delivering intended results" and "identify new opportunities." Translation: more reports, more presentations, and more opportunities for people to look important.

Kenya's Principal Secretary for Agriculture, Dr. Paul Ronoh, bless his optimism, says this is a "critical time for Africa’s agricultural transformation agenda." He’s hoping for "results-oriented financing" with "measurable outcomes." And GIZ's Dr. Sophia Baumert is thrilled that FINAS has "evolved into a pan-African forum that promotes accountability and stronger partnerships." It’s wonderful they’re fostering collaboration, especially when we’ve seen so many initiatives crumble, much like the promises made to victims of financial scams, as exemplified by those who've been [scammed out of significant sums]( /posts/eighty-year-old-scammed-out-of--285k--blames-schwab-for-negligence).

Before the main event, there's a series of high-level dialogues planned, focusing on an Agricultural Development Fund, the Kampala Declaration, green finance, and a private sector working group. The three-day program will be packed with ministerial roundtables, keynote addresses, and investment sessions on policy, finance, climate resilience, and trade. They'll even be shuttling attendees to shiny innovation hubs like Konza Technopolis and Tatu City, because nothing says "solving agricultural poverty" like a tour of fancy real estate.

AGRA’s Prof. Hamadi Boga is keen to move "beyond commitments to coordinated action," aiming to transform "policy ambitions into bankable investments." And FSD Kenya's Mr. Jared Ochieng' is calling for "stronger collaboration" to develop "innovative financing solutions" because Africa's food systems face "limited financing, market constraints, conflicts and climate change." Yes, Mr. Ochieng', we're well aware of the problems. The question is, after all this talking, will any actual money find its way to the farmers, or will it just be another expensive exercise in self-congratulation?

The organizers are predicting "new partnerships and investment opportunities." Let's hope they're right, but given Africa's track record with these grand pronouncements, it's more likely we'll end up with a few more glossy brochures and a lingering question: when will talk actually translate into tangible progress for the people who feed this continent?
