So, the big boys in the Middle East are having a spat, and guess who gets kicked in the teeth? Us, East Africa. Ships are doing U-turns around the Strait of Hormuz, and suddenly our ports in Mombasa and Dar es Salaam are buzzing with more vessels. Great, right? Except they're not carrying a drop of the petrol we need to keep our matatus running and our businesses churning. Kenya and Tanzania are holding their breath with barely a few weeks' worth of fuel in stock, while our landlocked cousins - Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, DRC, and South Sudan - are about to get a brutal lesson in how interconnected our pathetic economies are.

Kenya, as usual, is leading the pack in the race to the bottom. Our Kipevu Oil Terminal is as empty as a politician's promise, with not a single tanker carrying actual fuel scheduled. Tanzania at least has a couple of chemical/oil product tankers on the way, but that's hardly going to keep the lights on, is it? Meanwhile, the Kenyan authorities are out here putting on a brave face, talking about "operational challenges" and "hoarding" - the usual song and dance. But the independent retailers, the ones who actually serve us normal folk, are already screaming blue murder about a full-blown crisis in the next two weeks. It’s like they’ve never heard of supply and demand, or perhaps they're just masters of denial.

Our government officials, with their usual flair for downplaying disaster, insist everything is "stable." Daniel Kiptoo from Epra is warning wholesalers against hoarding, like that’s ever worked. They even released 100 million litres of Super petrol, a drop in the ocean that might last us ten days if we’re lucky. But the real kicker? Even with all this drama, Kenya’s Petroleum Principal Secretary Mohamed Liban is blaming "panic buying driven by speculation." So, we're supposed to be calm while our wallets get lighter and our fuel tanks get emptier? This smells like another one of those situations where the rich get richer, and we’re left scrambling. It’s reminiscent of the issues we've seen before, like those whispered about in [BRIBES, BETS, AND THE NEW BOARDROOM BOSS: CAN GEORGE KITHI FIX THIS MESS?](/posts/bribes--bets--and-the-new-boardroom-boss--can-george-kithi-fix-this-mess).

And don't even get me started on Uganda. Their pump prices are already doing a victory lap towards 2022 levels, even though the fuel they're using was shipped *before* this Middle Eastern tantrum. They're blaming everything from global prices to oil marketing companies trying to balance premium and ordinary customers, which basically translates to them jacking up prices because they can. Then there's the proposed excise duty hike, which the Finance Ministry claims will have "little impact." Right. Because adding more pain to an already burning situation is always a good idea. It’s a classic case of the government digging into our pockets while pretending to solve a problem they’re arguably contributing to.

The whole situation is a mess, a predictable mess. While some countries like Rwanda and Burundi are bracing for longer-term pain, and South Sudan is just expecting shortages, Uganda is being told not to panic because their National Oil Company and Vitol have supposedly hedged the market. They’re even talking about ships coming from "different parts of the world" to cover 60 days of supply. Sounds nice on paper, but let's be honest, when have our official reassurances ever translated into consistent, affordable fuel at the pump? We’ll believe it when we see it, and until then, expect more queues, higher prices, and the usual complaints.

Ultimately, this whole kerfuffle is a stark reminder that our reliance on imported fuel makes us vulnerable to every geopolitical sneeze from halfway across the world. The "stable supplies" and "sufficient stock" narratives are just that - stories to keep us from rioting. The reality is, when the global markets get shaky, we’re the ones who end up paying the price, literally. It’s a cycle that never seems to end, and frankly, it’s exhausting.
