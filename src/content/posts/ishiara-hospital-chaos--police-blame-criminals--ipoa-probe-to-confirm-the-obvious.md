So, the National Police Service (NPS) wants us to believe that the whole mess at Ishiara Level 4 Hospital was just a few rogue elements hijacking a "peaceful demonstration." Two dead, several injured, businesses vandalized - all thanks to these phantom criminals who apparently materialized out of nowhere, unlike the actual protestors complaining about rotten healthcare. The Evurore Ward community had the audacity to officially notify the authorities about their little walk. How quaint.

Of course, the police statement, oozing with the usual bureaucratic jargon, confirms the deaths and injuries. They claim the protests "began well," but then, *shockingly*, devolved into criminal acts. It’s like they expect us to forget that sometimes, people get angry when things are consistently bad, and maybe, just maybe, the police response isn't always the gentle hand of justice. We've seen this song and dance before, haven't we? It reminds me a bit of how [Kenya Police FC steal three points off KCB FC, because of course they did](/posts/kenya-police-fc-steal-three-points-off-kcb-fc--because-of-course-they-did) - predictable outcomes dressed up as something more.

The police, in their infinite wisdom, say they "swiftly intervened to restore law and order." What they conveniently omit is that this intervention was met with "more violence from criminal elements." Ah yes, the classic "they attacked us first" defense. Apparently, these "criminals" were armed with stones and other projectiles. It’s almost as if people throwing things at heavily armed police is a sign of desperation, not just random criminality. But who are we to question the official narrative?

Now, the Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) is being notified, and the police have pledged "maximum cooperation." This means more statements, more meetings, and eventually, a report that will likely confirm what we already suspect: things went south, lives were lost, and accountability remains a distant dream. The public is being asked to share any information, as if anyone trusts the system enough to come forward. It's all a show of transparency, a pledge of commitment to the law. We’ll see.
