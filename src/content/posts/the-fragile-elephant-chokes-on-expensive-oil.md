Brent crude is currently the noose around the neck of the global south. When Trump opened his mouth, prices dipped momentarily, but the relief was a mirage. Brent roared back to 104 dollars, leaving India’s specific crude basket hovering near a disastrous 120 dollars. This is the price of dependency. While West Asia continues its cycle of mutual destruction, the nations that built their growth stories on cheap energy are finding that their foundations were made of sand.

The Rupee is circling the drain, flirting with the 94-per-dollar mark. It is a record low that signals a total loss of confidence from the international community. Foreign exchange markets are rarely fooled by patriotic rhetoric or the desperate interventions of central banks. They see a Balance of Payments deficit of 85 billion dollars looming like a guillotine. It is a repeat of a tired historical pattern where global capital feasts on the vulnerable, much like the systematic stripping of the Rust Belt in decades past.

Even the economists at Emkay Global are beginning to use the language of retreat. Trimming GDP forecasts to 6.6 percent is a polite way of describing stagnation for a nation with a billion mouths to feed. When inflation rises and the terms-of-trade shock becomes permanent, the "emerging market" label starts to look like a cruel joke. The global realist knows that "realistic yet manageable" is simply economist-speak for bracing for a long, cold impact.

The international impact of this collapse will be felt far beyond the subcontinent. As India’s purchasing power is liquidated to pay for expensive barrels of oil, the global consumer base shrinks. We are witnessing the dismantling of the globalist dream in real-time. The war in West Asia is not just a regional skirmish; it is a catalyst for a massive redistribution of poverty, ensuring that the last breath of industrial growth is sucked dry by the very energy that once powered it.
