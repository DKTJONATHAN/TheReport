NAIROBI'S OFFICE GLUT: KENYA PROPERTY'S "SAFEST BET" NOW A PROPERTY NIGHTMARE
Nairobi's once-shining office towers are now a testament to broken dreams and overblown ambition, with "To Let" signs plastering the skyline. What was touted as Kenya's most secure investment is now a sinking ship, leaving landlords scrambling and developers rethinking their life choices.

Just look at Upper Hill, Westlands, and even parts of Kilimani - the shiny new towers that were supposed to scream "success" are now gathering dust. Floors sit empty for months, a stark reminder that "booming economy" was always more of a marketing slogan than reality. Landlords are practically begging for tenants, throwing discounts and free rent at anyone who will sign. Some developers, bless their naive hearts, have even put the brakes on new projects. Apparently, building monuments to speculation wasn't as profitable as they thought.

This whole mess is a big, fat kick in the teeth for anyone who believed the hype. The younger generation, surprise, surprise, isn't just looking for a fancy desk to call their own. They want more than just a roof over their heads, and frankly, who can blame them? They're smarter than the suits who keep pumping money into these empty boxes.

And the irony? Many Kenyans own homes but still rent near their workplaces. This whole "office glut" means those expensive leases are now even more of a joke. Meanwhile, companies like Anand Shah's are trying to make sense of it all, offering shared workspaces. It’s a smart move in a market that’s clearly gotten ahead of itself. It's a far cry from the days when [Invest Kenya Advances Strategic Investment Partnerships at Italy-Kenya Economic Forum in Rome](/posts/invest-kenya-advances-strategic-investment-partnerships-at-italy-kenya-economic-forum-in-rome), promising a future of endless growth. Looks like that growth decided to take a detour.







The gleaming office towers that dominate Nairobi's skyline were once symbols of a booming economy and some of the most sought-after investments in Kenya's property market. Today, many tell a different story.

In Upper Hill, Westlands and parts of Kilimani, "To Let" signs have become a common sight. Entire office floors remain vacant months after completion. Landlords are offering rent discounts, rent-free periods and flexible lease terms to attract tenants, while some developers have quietly suspended new projects altogether. What was once considered one of the safest bets in real estate has become one of the sector's biggest challenges.

Trends show that the younger generation wants more than a roof over their heads. Many Kenyans own homes but still rent houses near their workplaces.

This situation highlights a fundamental disconnect. While some are trying to navigate these choppy waters, like those at Move who aim to help borrowers save interest payments running into millions of shillings, the core issue remains: the commercial property market, once Kenya's golden goose, is now laying rotten eggs. Anand Shah’s business model seeking to help entrepreneurs and SMEs alleviate the costs of running an office by creating shared work spaces at a daily, weekly and monthly rate is a pragmatic response to this disaster, a small island of sense in a sea of overconfidence.
