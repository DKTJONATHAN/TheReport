Welcome to Nairobi, the city of grand ironies and even grander walls. While we are busy shouting about the rule of law from the rooftops, the "Temple of Justice" at Milimani is physically preventing people from even entering the building. A certified urgent petition has finally exposed what every Kenyan with a disability already knew: the Judiciary is a members-only club for the able-bodied. It’s a bitter joke that the very institution meant to interpret Article 54 of the Constitution is currently in open defiance of it.

If you’re a lawyer in a wheelchair, your professional dignity depends on being carried up flights of stairs by strangers like a sack of potatoes. If you’re a visually impaired litigant, the corridors are a survival course of obstacles. This isn't just a "technical hitch"; it’s systemic exclusion. The Judicial Service Commission (JSC) has had years to fix those lifts and install tactile rails, but apparently, they’ve been too busy with "higher matters" to worry about the people who actually pay their salaries. 

But don’t worry, if the state of public infrastructure makes you sick, there’s always a way to buy your way out of the mess. Enter "37 by INEZA," a low-density luxury development that promises a "profound connection to nature" and a heated swimming pool. While the High Court struggles to provide a basic ramp, Tofauti Lifestyle Limited is offering padel courts and "intentional living" on five acres of verdant land. It’s the classic Kenyan pivot: why fix the courtrooms when you can just build a five-bedroom townhouse with a DSQ and pretend the rest of the country doesn't exist?

The contrast is enough to give you whiplash. We have developers meticulously designing "seamless indoor-outdoor living" behind Kiambu Road, while a magistrate with a disability can’t even access their own chambers at Milimani. We are becoming a society of islands - pristine, gated communities for those who can afford "enduring value," and crumbling, inaccessible ruins for those seeking justice. The message is clear: if you can’t climb the stairs, you don’t get a seat at the table.

Justice is supposed to be blind, but in Nairobi, it’s also apparently immobile. This lawsuit by Sheria Mtaani is a wake-up call that will likely be snoozed by the powers that be. We’ll probably get another round of "renovations" that result in zero working elevators but a very nice new carpet for the VIP lounge. It’s the same old story of disruptive forces meeting a stagnant system.

Maybe if they’d stayed in their luxury townhouses, they wouldn't have had to deal with the rest of us. Stay cynical, Nairobi.
