Kenya Cuts 2026 Growth Forecast as Middle East Crisis Weighs Down Economy, Shocker

The National Treasury, in its infinite wisdom, has decided our economy will grow by a measly 5.0 percent in 2026, down from the optimistic 5.3 percent they’d conjured up before. Apparently, the Middle East is messing with our supply chains, scaring away tourists, making remittances dry up like a forgotten mandazi, and generally making everything more expensive. So, our esteemed Kiptoo tells us, we've revised things down. What a shocker.

Despite this “downgrade,” Kiptoo insists Kenya is still a shining star in Africa, with a diversified economy that can supposedly withstand external shocks. Right. They brag about services making up 55 percent of GDP and agriculture adding another 23 percent. It’s all well and good on paper, but ask any mama mboga or boda boda rider if they feel this diversification when the price of fuel goes up or when the rains decide to skip town, leaving us to deal with droughts and floods, not to mention the billions siphoned and energy bosses nabbed in Nairobi's oil smokescreen.

The Treasury also claims inflation is “within target” - a target that seems to conveniently shift. It crept up from 4.4 percent to 6.7 percent and then a slightly less alarming 6.4 percent, all thanks to some "government interventions" like fuel subsidies and a temporary VAT reduction on petrol. Of course, this relief has been extended for another three months. Because what better way to boost an economy than to keep pumping money into fuel, right?

Never mind all that, Kiptoo wants us to believe things are rosy. He’s gushing about easing monetary policy, lower interest rates, and private sector credit growth that’s suddenly quadrupled. Our exports of tea and flowers are apparently doing great, but don't look too closely at the gaping hole left by higher imports of fuel, food, and capital goods. And our foreign exchange reserves? At their highest level ever, Kiptoo claims, saving the shilling from "global volatility." If only that buffer helped the common mwananchi pay for their daily bread.

The Nairobi Securities Exchange is supposedly among the world’s best performers, thanks to “improved macroeconomic stability.” Don’t forget the Kenya Pipeline Company IPO and the Safaricom stake sale, which have supposedly boosted liquidity and will fund some new infrastructure fund. Meanwhile, revenue collections missed their target by Sh90 billion, but hey, expenditure was also lower because development funds weren't absorbed. Classic. The government still faces pressure from rising costs, wage demands, and… wait for it… emergency spending linked to droughts and floods. Fiscal consolidation remains a priority, they say, while declaring an end to new pending bills as they plan the 2027 budget. It’s all just another day in paradise.
