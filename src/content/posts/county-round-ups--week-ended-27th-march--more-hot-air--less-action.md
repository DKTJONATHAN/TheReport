So, the Cabinet Secretary for Mining, Blue Economy and Maritime Affairs is out with more invitations. Copper in Tharaka Nithi, manganese in Tana River, coltan in Embu, niobium and rare earths in Kwale, chromite in Samburu. All "strategic minerals" for "commercial development". Translation: Get ready for some well-connected individuals to get their hands on more of our land, while the rest of us get the environmental fallout and pocket change, if anything. It’s the same old song, just a different mineral tune.

Meanwhile, down in Garissa, Kilifi, and Narok, governors are busy appointing people. Mohamed Mohamud Gurhan to Dadaab Water, Marjisanaa Yahya for Transport in Kilifi, Kool Kuntai Isaack for Public Works in Narok. Three-year terms, approvals, statutory provisions - sounds fancy. Let’s just hope these appointments actually lead to something more than just more meetings and more salaries. We’ve seen how well that works out when focusing on the *illusion of protection* provided by officials.

And then there’s NEMA, the environmental watchdog that seems more like a paper-shuffling machine. They’re inviting public comments on EIAs for housing developments in Nairobi and Naivasha. "Anticipated environmental impacts and mitigation measures." Right. Because last time we checked, slapping up thousands of concrete boxes in densely populated areas never has any real impact, does it? Especially when we're talking about "supporting commercial exploration and development" of land, which usually means pushing out the original inhabitants.

The County Government of Mombasa is waving penalties and interest on property rates, a little sweetener to ease the pain. The Meru County Assembly has its calendar sorted for 2026. And somewhere in Eldama Ravine and Westlands, more luxury apartments are being proposed, complete with their own environmental impact reports. It’s all about progress, you see. Progress for whom, though? That’s the question nobody seems eager to answer. The only certainty is that the wheels of bureaucracy keep grinding, and the public is expected to just nod along.
