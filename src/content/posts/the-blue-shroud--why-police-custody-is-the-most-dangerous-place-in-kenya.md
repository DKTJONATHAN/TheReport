Welcome to the 254, where a police station is less of a sanctuary and more of a departure lounge for the afterlife. Francis Mukura was only 25 when he was last seen alive in the hands of the Murang'a police on February 17, 2026, at Kagundu-ini Market. While the official reports will probably dance around the truth for the next decade, the reality is as cold as a morgue slab. It is just another day in a country where the people paid to protect you are often the ones you should be running from.

His sister, Grace Wanjiru, described the ordeal as a cruel mockery. The "abductors" in uniform allegedly played a sick game of pretend, acting like the boy was still breathing while he was likely already gone. This level of deception is not just standard procedure; it is a way of life for those who have traded their souls for a bribe. It reminds me of the same twisted narrative found in [The Fall of the Duke of Disgrace](/posts/birthday-cake-and-handcuffs--the-fall-of-the-duke-of-disgrace), where the masks of authority eventually slip to reveal the rot underneath.

We are told that things are changing because a few officers from the June 2024 anti-Finance Bill protests finally swapped their fatigues for prison stripes. One policeman even managed to land a lengthy jail term for a brutal assault that led to a civilian's death. But let's be real for a minute. For every officer who ends up behind bars, there are hundreds more at roadblocks and precincts wondering if your life is worth the price of a cheap lunch. Justice in this town is a lottery, and the house always wins.

Then there is Nakuru, the city that forgot it was a city. Four years after attaining its fancy status, it still goes to sleep as soon as the sun disappears. Why? Because darkness is when the real "mysteries" happen. When the people meant to patrol the streets are the ones you fear most, you do not stay out late to admire the scenery. You go home, lock your doors, and hope your name does not end up on a missing persons poster.

An innocent soul was lost because of greed, and the family is left with a wound that will never heal. The lack of remorse from the authorities is the loudest part of this whole tragedy. We can celebrate the occasional conviction all we want, but the system is built to protect the predator, not the prey. Mukura is just a statistic now, another name added to the long list of those who learned the hard way that in this country, "custody" is often just a polite word for a death sentence.
