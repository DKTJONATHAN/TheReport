So, here we are again. Tomorrow, the good people of Kenya, pushed to the brink by yet another fuel price hike, are expected to hit the streets. Amnesty International Kenya, bless their idealistic hearts, has decided to grace the National Police Service with an "advisory" - a polite way of saying a lecture. They're reminding the boys in blue that Article 37 of our glorious Constitution supposedly guarantees the right to assemble, demonstrate, and all that jazz. As if the cops don't know that.

Amnesty's also been digging through their archives, no doubt. They're trotting out the same old stories of excessive force, arbitrary arrests, and journalists getting roughed up. Apparently, individual officers and their commanders might actually face consequences for this nonsense. Good luck with that. We've seen court decisions, we've seen policy directives, and yet, here we are, talking about the same tired script. This isn't the first time they've emphasized that treating protests like battlegrounds is a "grave failure of policing." Just like how a scholarship won't magically fix the potholes, these pronouncements rarely translate into actual change on the ground. [Scholarship Won't Fix the Potholes: The Great Public Finance Myth](/posts/scholarship-won-t-fix-the-potholes--the-great-public-finance-myth).

The High Court in Kisumu apparently got all emotional about civilians being shot in their homes or while fleeing state violence. They even called it "unlawful and unconstitutional." It's almost as if they're surprised that police might actually use live ammunition against unarmed people. The court’s words about firearms only being used "where strictly unavoidable to protect life" sound lovely, but let's be real, when have the police ever been that precise? It’s Kenya, not a Swiss watch convention.

So, Amnesty's "advisory" boils down to "no more loss of life, no excessive force, and no silencing journalists." Groundbreaking stuff. It's a reminder that public order and human rights aren't supposed to be enemies, but when has that ever been the reality for the common Kenyan trying to voice their discontent? We can only hope Mathias T. Kinyoda’s phone doesn't ring off the hook with good news. More likely, it’s just another Tuesday in Nairobi, where hope is as scarce as a fuel discount.
