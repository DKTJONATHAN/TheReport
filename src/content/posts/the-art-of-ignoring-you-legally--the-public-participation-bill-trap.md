So, the honorable members have finally figured it out. After the courts kept embarrassing them by tossing out half-baked laws like the Finance Bill, Samuel Chepkonga and Otiende Amollo have decided to give us the Public Participation Bill, 2025. Do not get it twisted; this is not about giving you a seat at the table. It is about building a legal wall so high that no judge can say they did not follow the rules while they were busy ignoring your screams. This bill is less of a bridge and more of a shield for a government tired of being told "no" by the judiciary.

They talk about "standardizing" the process across ministries and counties. In a city where we have seen how "standard" usually means "the bare minimum to avoid a lawsuit," this is just a checklist for bureaucrats. They want to decide the time, the venue, and the mode. If you cannot make it to a town hall because you are stuck in traffic or working three jobs to pay for their lifestyle, that is your problem. They will have their radio ad and their notice board, and then they will proceed to do exactly what they planned from the start. It is a performance, not a partnership.

The bill claims it will demand "real substance" by making authorities analyze our views. We have heard this script before. It is the same kind of political theatre we see when leaders try to rebrand themselves to look like the people's choice, much like the [manufacturing of George Natembeya](/posts/new--supremo---same-old-hunger--the-manufacturing-of-george-natembeya) where the packaging changes but the underlying hunger for control stays the same. They will "consider" your submission, write a report that says they considered it, and then vote for the next tax hike anyway. 

One of the most telling parts of this proposal is the requirement for participants to remain "respectful and civil." After the 2024 protests shook the foundations of Parliament, they are clearly terrified of a public that has lost its politeness. They want a sanitized version of democracy where you sit quietly, wait for your two minutes of fame, and say "thank you" while they prepare the next levy. If you get too loud or too honest about your frustration, they will just call it "disorder" and shut the forum down. They do not want your feedback; they want your compliance.

This bill is not for the person in the street; it is for the lawyer in the courtroom. It is a manual on how to legally ignore the Kenyan people without getting caught on a technicality. By the time they are done "facilitating" our involvement, we will be so buried in "frameworks" and "responsible authorities" that we will forget we were supposed to be the ones in charge. It is just another day in Nairobi, where the more things change, the more they stay the same behind a polished legislative seal.
