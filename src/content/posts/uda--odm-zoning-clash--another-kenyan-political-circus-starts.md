ODM, the party that once roared, is now doing a pathetic jig, begging for safe seats. Gladys Wanga and her crew are trotting out the same old song and dance: "Nyanza, Western, Coast are ours, Ruto, keep your grubby UDA hands off!" This whole "electoral zoning" nonsense is just a fancy way of saying they want to avoid a fight they know they'll lose. They're desperate to cling to power in regions where they think they still have a grip, especially after losing their biggest star.

The audacity! They want equal power-sharing, even the deputy president post. It's clear they're not thinking about governing, but about grabbing as much as they can before they vanish. Remember those by-elections? A little quid pro quo, a favour for a favour. Now they want to formalize this cosy arrangement, locking out any real competition. Hassan Omar in Mombasa is right to call them out. "The Coast belongs to the people," he says. Exactly! It's not some ancient ODM kingdom to be inherited.

The real joke is the belief that ODM is still the powerhouse it once was. Since Raila Odinga, bless his departed soul, kicked the bucket, the party's been a mess. Factional fights, squabbling over who gets to be the next big shot, all while their former leader's legacy crumbles. They're hoping the ghost of Odinga will scare UDA into submission, but the younger lot in UDA aren't buying it. They see the cracks, the desperation. It’s like watching a sinking ship try to negotiate passage with a passing freighter.

They conveniently forget how much of ODM's past success was tied to one man's charisma. Now, without him, they're like a band without its lead singer, playing off-key. Their claims of popularity in certain regions are looking increasingly hollow, especially with the recent political shifts. It’s a tough gig, trying to maintain relevance when your main selling point is gone. You can read more about the fickle nature of Kenyan politics in my piece on [FKF Awards 'Ghost' Walkovers Amidst WPL Boycott Crisis](/posts/fkf-awards--ghost--walkovers-amidst-wpl-boycott-crisis---nairobi-realist).

So, as these "negotiations" unfold, remember that it's all about power, not progress. ODM is trying to salvage what they can from a collapsing structure, and UDA is probably enjoying the spectacle. Don't expect any grand pronouncements of national unity. Expect backroom deals, compromises that benefit the few, and the same old political theatre that keeps us all guessing.

This whole zoning thing? It's a desperate gamble. They're afraid. Afraid of losing their grip, afraid of being irrelevant. They're trying to preserve what little they have left, using the UDA's need for a broader coalition as leverage. It's a familiar story in this country, where promises are made and broken faster than a politician's loyalty.
