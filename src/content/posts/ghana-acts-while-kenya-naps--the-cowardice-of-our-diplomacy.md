Accra is currently showing us how it is done, while Nairobi is busy being Nairobi. When a supposed Russian national decided to turn Ghanaian women into non-consensual content for the dark corners of the internet, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Ghana did not just tweet a lukewarm prayer. They summoned Sergei Berdnikov, the Russian Ambassador, to explain why their citizen thinks West Africa is a private film set for his depravity. In Ghana, dignity is a diplomatic issue. In Kenya, it is just another Tuesday where the government pretends not to see the smoke until the whole house is on fire.

The Russian Embassy is already doing the classic dance of denial, claiming they cannot confirm the individual's identity or if his name is even real. It is the standard script. But at least Ghana is forcing them to read it out loud. They are talking about international mechanisms and seeking justice, even without a binding extradition treaty. They are making it clear that you cannot just come into their house and violate their women without consequences. It is a level of backbone that seems to have been exported from Kenya long ago.

Back home in the 254, the silence from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs is deafening. We have the Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act, which promises a Sh 200,000 fine or two years in jail. To a foreign national with a bit of hard currency, that fine is basically the cost of a weekend in Diani. It is a rounding error. While Gender CS Hanna Cheptumo is busy condemning the actions like a concerned auntie at a funeral, the people with the actual power to make a diplomatic stink are nowhere to be found.

This systemic apathy is exactly what happens when a country decides that its people are secondary to its optics. It feels like we are living through [The Ghost in the Ledger: How Private Equity is Cannibalizing the African Commons](/posts/the-ghost-in-the-ledger-how-private-equity-is-cannibalizing-the-african-commons) where the common man is just a resource to be exploited by outsiders while the state looks the other way. We are being sold out in boardrooms and in bedrooms, and the only response from our leaders is a lukewarm press statement that barely mentions the perpetrator's origin.

So, here we are. Ghana is treating its citizens like human beings with rights that cross borders, and Kenya is treating its women like a domestic legal headache that might go away if we ignore it long enough. If you are looking for protection from the state, you are better off looking for a needle in a haystack on River Road. In Nairobi, if you are not the one holding the camera or the one being paid to look away, you are simply the victim waiting for your turn in the headlines.
