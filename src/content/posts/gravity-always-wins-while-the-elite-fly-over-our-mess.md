Another day, another metal bird falling from the sky because the people running this country would rather fly over the mess they made than fix a single pothole. This time, Nandi County played host to a tragedy that claimed six lives, including an MP who probably thought he was untouchable at ten thousand feet. It is a recurring theme in the 254: the higher they climb, the harder they hit the red soil of the North Rift region.

Police Commander Samuel Mukusi gave the usual dry report to Citizen TV, confirming that the legislator, the pilot, and four others are gone. The cause is still "undetermined," which is Kenyan government-speak for "we will hold a press conference and then bury the findings under a pile of other scandals." We are used to this rhythm of sudden silence followed by a lot of noise that signifies absolutely nothing.

President Ruto was quick with the "thoughts and prayers" routine, mourning the people of Emurua Dikirr and the victims in Mosop. It is a well-rehearsed script that we have seen before, especially when the political landscape shifts. Much like how [BABA IS GONE AND THE VULTURES ARE HAVING A FIELD DAY](/posts/baba-is-gone-and-the-vultures-are-having-a-field-day), every vacancy in this country is just a fresh opportunity for the political elite to start circling the carcass before the bodies are even cold.

They haven-t released all the names yet, probably waiting to see which families have enough clout to demand answers and which ones can be silenced with a standard funeral donation. In the meantime, the "investigations" will continue at the speed of a snail on a holiday. It is the same old story of systemic failure that keeps this country running on fumes and funeral hymns.

Gravity does not care about your party affiliation or how many millions you have tucked away. When the engines fail in the North Rift, you are just another statistic in a long line of avoidable disasters. We will see the flags at half-mast, hear the flowery eulogies, and then wait for the next chopper to remind us that the sky is just as broken as the ground we walk on.
