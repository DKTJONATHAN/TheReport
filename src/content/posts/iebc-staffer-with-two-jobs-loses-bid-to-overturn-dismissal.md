IEBC Man With Two Jobs Loses Case To Keep Them, Because Apparently The Law Matters Sometimes

So, the Employment and Labour Relations Court decided that one Calisto Oyugi, an ICT officer at the IEBC, can't have his cake and eat it too. Apparently, holding down two public sector jobs simultaneously is a "breach of the law," and the court, in its infinite wisdom, said he can't benefit from his own dodgy dealings. Shocking, I know.

This whole shenanigan brings to mind the usual rot we see in these institutions. It's not just about one guy trying to game the system; it's the ripe smell of [corporate incompetence revealed](/posts/claude-code-leak-exposes-tamagotchi-pet-and-always-on-agent---more-corporate-incompetence-revealed) that hangs heavy in the air. While this Oyugi character is getting his comeuppance, it’s a tiny speck in the ocean of… well, everything else.

And speaking of things not working as they should, the IEBC is now asking voters who registered before 2012 to get themselves back on the list. Because apparently, their previous efforts were just a suggestion. Meanwhile, Professor Francis Aduol is busy explaining himself to the Senate over "mismanagement." It’s almost like a themed episode of Kenya’s favorite drama, "The Rot Continues."

Let's not forget that the IEBC’s seven commissioners and chairperson are just starting their six-year terms. Six years. Plenty of time to perpetuate the same old habits, I suppose. Especially when most directorate heads have been "acting" for over two years. This isn't leadership; it's just a prolonged state of uncertainty, a true testament to the [institutional decay of global hegemony](/posts/the-institutional-decay-of-global-hegemony), right here at home.

And the cherry on top of this delightful sundae of public service? They've uncovered "fraudulent practices." Duplicate payroll names, irregular promotions, and payments to ghost workers. It’s a classic trifecta of how taxpayer money disappears faster than free chai at a political rally. You can't make this stuff up.
