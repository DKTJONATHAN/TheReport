Ruku: Ruto Will Win 2027 Elections Even Without Mt Kenya West Support - So They Say

A stark warning from Cabinet Secretary Geoffrey Ruku, who seems awfully confident that President William Ruto can pull off another win in 2027, even if a significant chunk of Mt Kenya decides to ditch him. According to Ruku, the development projects are "visible for all to see," a claim that, knowing Nairobi, is usually followed by a hefty price tag and some questionable procurement. He's accusing the opposition of "sowing discord," which is code for "they're pointing out the obvious problems."

Ruku’s logic, if you can call it that, relies on the 2013 election, where Uhuru allegedly won without overwhelming Mt Kenya support. This is the kind of historical gymnastics you expect when people are trying to convince themselves - and us - that a political future is secure. He's basically saying that the East of Mt Kenya is still on board, and the West can go pound sand. It's all very dramatic, like a local soap opera, but with real consequences for your tax shillings.

The CS even had the audacity to dismiss former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua's influence, claiming the West has "chosen to walk alone." This sounds less like a strategic political move and more like a childish spat. Ruku's reference to Ol Kalou defying Moi back in the day is just a desperate attempt to draw parallels that don't quite fit. The point is, even if some corners of Mt Kenya feel left out, Ruku is assuring us that Ruto and his running mate, Kindiki, will somehow sweep to victory, probably with the help of more visible, development projects.

It's always the same song and dance, isn't it? Promises of progress, accusations of propaganda, and the perpetual hope that a few shiny new projects will distract from the real issues. This whole narrative feels as flimsy as some of the infrastructure projects I've seen debated, like [The Kangemi Circus: 16 Classrooms and 80 Billion Reasons to Stay Skeptical](/posts/the-kangemi-circus--16-classrooms-and-80-billion-reasons-to-stay-skeptical). Ruku's confidence is either genuine delusion or a masterclass in political theatre, and frankly, I'm leaning towards the latter.
