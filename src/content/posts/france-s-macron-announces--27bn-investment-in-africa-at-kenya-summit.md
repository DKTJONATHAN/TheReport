Macron Dumps Cash in Africa, Hopes for a Handout (of His Own)

Macron's "$27bn Handshake" for Africa: More PR Than Partnership?

Macron in Nairobi: A $27bn "Investment" or Just Another French Facade?

Politics

France, Africa, Investment

Summit

Emmanuel Macron is strutting around Nairobi, throwing around 23 billion euros like confetti. Apparently, this is a "partnership of equals," with common objectives. Right. It's France's first summit in an English-speaking African nation, and suddenly they're all about "mutual investment," with 14 billion from French companies and a paltry 9 billion from African ones. They claim this will create 250,000 jobs. Let's see how many of those actually land here.

Macron’s big talk about African business leaders investing in France, "entirely free of hang-ups," is just more of the same old song and dance. France is clearly feeling the heat as its influence wanes, with their military boys being kicked out of former colonies. Now they’re trying to charm their way back in, positioning themselves as a better trade partner than China or the US. Remember when France used to dictate terms? Those days are long gone, like some of the artworks they "forgot" to return.

And speaking of returns, Macron’s sudden urgency on returning looted art is convenient, isn't it? Just as France is desperate to mend fences. It's a nice gesture, sure, but it doesn't erase decades of exploitation. Meanwhile, Kenya is hosting more foreign soldiers than a war movie, a stark contrast to the Sahel region. It’s funny how some places are welcoming foreign forces while others are showing them the door. This whole "partnership" feels more like a desperate scramble for relevance.

President Ruto, bless his heart, is singing France’s praises, talking about "investment and what Africa has to offer" instead of aid and loans. It’s good optics, but let’s be real. France is looking for opportunities, and Africa, with its vast potential, is a target. The CMA CGM investment in Mombasa port is a concrete example, but is it a win-win, or just another way for France to secure its own interests? We’ve seen plenty of these "deals" before, and the reality is often far less glamorous than the press releases. Just like the political maneuvering Ruto engages in, this is about securing future influence, not genuine equality.
