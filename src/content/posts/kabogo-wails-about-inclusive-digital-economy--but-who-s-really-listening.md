So, our man Kabogo, the Cabinet Secretary for Information, Communications, and the Digital Economy (fancy title, that), is suddenly noticing that a good chunk of Africans might be getting left behind in this whole digital transformation thing. Specifically, the 80 million or so with disabilities. Apparently, our technological leaps and bounds are more like leaps and bounds over the heads of these folks. It’s like we’re building a superhighway, but forgot to pave the side streets.

He’s calling for "inclusive" digital economies, which sounds lovely, doesn't it? Like something you'd find on a motivational poster. He’s worried that innovation is outpacing inclusivity, as if these things are mutually exclusive. Meanwhile, we’re busy with our Digital Superhighways and eCitizen platforms. Kenya's doing its bit, apparently, with the Persons with Disabilities Act and all. Let's hope those reforms actually trickle down, or are we just talking about more paper shuffling, much like the "power seats" of rot we see elsewhere in this city? [Oburu Oginga's New Riverside Drive 'Power Seat': Just Another Seat of Rot](/posts/oburu-oginga-s-new-riverside-drive--power-seat---just-another-seat-of-rot).

The whole three-day shindig in Nairobi, organized by inABLE, is all about "advancing digital accessibility and artificial intelligence solutions." Great. More buzzwords. We’ve got policymakers, advocates, and innovators all gathered to discuss how to make technology work for everyone. It’s a noble cause, I suppose, but I’ve seen these conferences before. Lots of handshakes, earnest pronouncements, and then back to business as usual. Sandra Doreen from Silent Cafe Uganda is talking about communication barriers - you know, the kind that don't get solved by a slick new app.

They're even talking about AI in recruitment. Apparently, it can promote "fair and equitable employment opportunities." Right. Because AI has never, ever shown any bias before. And this whole standardization effort with ARSO and GIZ? It’ll take two years. Two years to make sure accessibility isn’t an afterthought. By then, we’ll probably have robots running the government. The delegates are saying digital accessibility should be a "regulatory requirement," not charity. Groundbreaking stuff. You’d think we'd have figured that out by now, but then again, this is Africa, where “progress” often means taking one step forward and two steps sideways.
