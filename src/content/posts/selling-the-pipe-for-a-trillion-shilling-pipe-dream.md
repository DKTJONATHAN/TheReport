They finally did it. After years of eyeing the Kenya Pipeline Company like a hungry hyena at a butcher shop, the government has offloaded a 65 per cent stake. President Ruto was at the Nairobi Securities Exchange ringing bells and singing songs about ingenuity and prosperity. To those of us living in the real Nairobi, it looks like selling the family cow to buy a flashy suit for a funeral. We are told the Ksh.106 billion raised is the magic key to a trillion-shilling future, but we have heard these fairy tales before.

The logic is that the Ksh.5 billion annual dividends are too modest for the government's appetite. Apparently, waiting twenty years for a return is too slow for a leadership that wants to build mega-projects yesterday. So, they created the National Infrastructure Fund. It is the latest shiny bucket where our money goes to disappear. By privatizing KPC, they are [opening the gates to god-knows-what](/posts/opening-the-gates-to-god-knows-what) under the guise of crowding in private capital. If you believe a 1.2 trillion shilling fund will actually fix your commute without lining the pockets of the well-connected, I have a bridge in Nyali to sell you.

They are even bragging about the democratization of the market. Ruto claims that 61 per cent of the trading is by ordinary Kenyans buying shares worth 1,000 bob. Let us be real. While Mama Mboga is putting her last coins into a volatile stock market through some app, the real players are the ones who already own the game. Giving a starving man a single grain of rice is not participation-it is a PR stunt designed to make the privatization of national assets look like a community project.

The talk of economic stability is the biggest joke of all. The President says the exchange rate is stable and inflation is low by design. Try telling that to anyone buying unga or paying school fees this month. They claim the sugar sector is now paying taxes instead of swallowing them, but we know how these reforms go. First, they say the company is making losses, then they sell it for a song to their strategic partners, and suddenly the taxpayers are left holding the bag for the old debts anyway.

Now they want to securitize the Road Maintenance Levy Fund. That is just a fancy way of saying they are borrowing against the future money we will pay at the petrol pump. It is a cycle of debt and disposal that never seems to end. They call it transformation at State House, but on the streets, it just feels like the country is being auctioned off piece by piece to keep a failing engine running for one more mile. Reality is a lot less polished than a bell-ringing ceremony at the NSE.
