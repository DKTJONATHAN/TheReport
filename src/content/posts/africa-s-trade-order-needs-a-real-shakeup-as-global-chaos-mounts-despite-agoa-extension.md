AGOA EXTENSION: AFRICA NEEDS MORE THAN JUST CHEAP WORDS TO THRIVE AMID GLOBAL TURMOIL

The US extending AGOA until December 2026? Oh, how generous. It’s like throwing a few crumbs to a starving dog while the whole house is on fire. Sure, they signed a law, but let's not pretend this is a golden ticket. The global trade system is a mess, Davos was full of hot air, and tariffs are thrown around like confetti. This isn't a signal of Africa's importance; it’s a desperate attempt to keep some semblance of order in a chaotic world.

The hard truth nobody wants to admit is that AGOA alone is useless. Policy might give you permission to trade, but it doesn't magically build the roads, ports, or factories needed to actually do it. Remember when USAid packed up and left in 2025? That’s the new reality. Predictable grants are a thing of the past. As Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney so eloquently put it, "The old order is not coming back." Nostalgia won't pay for infrastructure, and it certainly won't help small businesses drowning in red tape.

This is a reckoning for African trade infrastructure and finance. We need actual structures, not just more talk. Ports, industrial parks, and especially those SMEs that are the backbone of our economy - they need practical financing, not promises. It’s time for cost-recovery models. Faster borders should mean user fees for maintenance, not endless delays. Trade corridors can be like toll roads, and industrial parks should be run like actual businesses, generating revenue. We can't keep waiting for donor approval; it’s a pipe dream.

Development finance institutions need to get with the program. Blended finance is no longer optional; it’s essential. We need catalytic capital, first-loss equity, and subordinated debt to lure in commercial investors. Without these tools, we’ll remain dependent on dwindling grants, dreaming of a past that’s gone. Banks and investors are starting to see African trade as a viable asset class - predictable cash flows from border fees, rental income from parks, returns from SME finance. These aren't abstract concepts; they are investment opportunities, finally.

For SMEs, the old system was a dead end. Banks wouldn't lend because there was no collateral, not because the business was bad. But even this is changing, slowly. Digital platforms like EASETRADE are using transaction data to assess creditworthiness, bypassing the need for fixed assets. It's an approach that works elsewhere and is now being adapted here. It offers a real chance for SMEs to get into formal trade and finance. It’s a small step, but in this world, where trade rules and aid models are constantly shifting, every little bit of innovation counts, even if it feels like we're just trying to keep up with the global cybercrime syndicates stealing billions.

The theme in Davos was rupture - of trade rules, of aid, of our assumptions about globalization. But ruptures also create opportunities for those willing to adapt. Africa’s trade future won't be built on longing for a grant-funded past. It will be forged through structured partnerships between development and commercial capital, through models that generate returns while serving a purpose. AGOA's renewal is a nod, a whisper, that Africa is still in the global trade conversation. Now, we need to ensure our businesses, infrastructure, and financial systems are ready to actually do something with it. Policy opens the door, but finance and structure are what let us walk through.
