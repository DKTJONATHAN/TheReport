So, Senator Allan Chesang is feeling a bit pinched, is he? Thinks the Standard Group and KTN have crossed a line with their reporting on this Harambee House tender scam. He's gone and fired off a cease-and-desist, giving them a whole 48 hours to retract their story linking him to this ambulance deal. Apparently, the report named some of his "aides" and painted him as a "mastermind" - a word that usually means you're either brilliant or utterly corrupt, and in this town, it's often the latter.

His legal team is spitting fire, claiming the story is "entirely false" and "deliberately designed to mislead." They say the media house didn't even bother to consult him, which, let's be honest, is how these stories usually get written anyway. But according to them, this went beyond fair reporting, stripping him of his "presumption of integrity." Funny how easily that presumption evaporates when a juicy scandal is on the table, isn't it? It’s like chasing after [secret profits in deals](/posts/ant-and-dec-trail--secret-profits--in-banksy-deals-via-court-order), the truth often takes a back seat.

And it gets better. They've also dug up an older allegation from 2021 about a Ksh180 million laptop supply to the deputy president's office. Chesang claims he was implicated, arrested, and arraigned for tender forgery back then, but his lawyers are calling those claims "wholly false" and "published with deliberate malicious intent." It seems like every politician has a dusty closet full of old allegations they’d rather keep locked.

The senator's people are particularly upset about KTN broadcasting this nonsense on Facebook, YouTube, and national TV, reaching "millions." They accuse Standard Group of abandoning "basic journalistic standards." You know, the same standards that often get conveniently ignored when there's a headline to be made. Now, Chesang wants the story pulled from every platform, everywhere.

As usual, Standard Group is playing it cool, no public response yet. So here we are, waiting to see if the senator’s threats amount to anything more than hot air, or if the media house will blink first. In Nairobi, these dramas are as common as traffic jams on Mombasa Road, and usually just as frustrating.
