So, the Teachers Service Commission (TSC) is out here playing whack-a-mole with fake news about April salaries. Apparently, some rumour mill on social media churned out a notice claiming delays due to some drama with KEWOTA and court orders. This whole saga started because, surprise, surprise, there were unauthorised payroll deductions and accusations of nepotism against KEWOTA's CEO. Honestly, the audacity.

The TSC, in their infinite wisdom, has come out to say it’s all hogwash and teachers should only listen to them. As if we haven’t seen this movie before. The fake notice even claimed payroll was closed on April 16th, and payments were supposed to start Friday, but teachers would be left twiddling their thumbs until next week. It’s the classic Kenyan hustle, but for official institutions.

But here’s the kicker. The TSC *did* confirm that the new intern and replacement teachers recruited in January are on the April payroll, and will get their dues, including arrears. So, some people are getting paid. For the rest of us, well, we’ve seen decades of delays and whispers of improved welfare, much like the residents in Uasin Gishu begging Parliament for land titles after years of waiting. [Uasin Gishu's 120,000 Residents Beg Parliament for Land Titles Amidst Decades of Delays](/posts/uasin-gishu-s-120-000-residents-beg-parliament-for-land-titles-amidst-decades-of-delays).

Now, to add a sprinkle of "hope" from the government, President Ruto apparently approved a Ksh24.2 billion allocation for TSC to cover salary shortfalls and health insurance. They even threw in Ksh3 billion for pending medical bills. This is meant to ease pressure, you see. But let's not forget the grand plans for Kenya to become a "Singapore," which often sounds good on paper but rarely trickles down to the common mwananchi, or in this case, the underpaid teacher. [Ruto to ambassadors: Sell Kenya, new Sh5trn ‘Singapore’ plan abroad](/posts/ruto-to-ambassadors--sell-kenya--new-sh5trn--singapore--plan-abroad).

The TSC is basically saying, "Don't believe the hype, only trust us." It’s a classic move to deflect from the real issues. While they dismiss fake news, the reality for many teachers remains a constant scramble, juggling bills and hoping their salary actually shows up when it’s supposed to. This whole circus is a reminder that in Kenya, official communication often comes with a side of confusion and a hefty dose of cynicism.
