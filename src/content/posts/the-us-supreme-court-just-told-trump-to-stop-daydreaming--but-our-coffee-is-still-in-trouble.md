The US Supreme Court finally remembered that the law exists, telling Donald Trump that he cannot just wake up and tax the world because he feels like it. It is a win for Kenya, sure, but let us be real. This man has been treating the global economy like his personal kiosk for the last year. While the 6-3 ruling says he overstepped his powers, it does not fix the mess already made in our textile and coffee sectors. The court basically told him that being President does not make him the King of Commerce, which is a shock to absolutely no one except his own administration.

Chief Justice John Roberts says the President needs clear congressional authorization to play God with tariffs. That is cute, but what about the 130 billion dollars already collected? The court is stopping short of saying where that money goes, which in Nairobi terms is just a polite way of saying the money is gone. Our exporters are celebrating a "win," but they are still standing in a hole that Trump dug with his 10 percent baseline tariff. We are being told to be grateful for a return to the status quo while the US keeps the change.

This whole situation reminds me of how quickly systems can be manipulated by those at the top to protect their own interests. Much like how [private schools quietly sabotaged Kenya's Grade 10 transition](/posts/why-kenyas-private-schools-are-quietly-sabotaging-the-grade-10-transition) to keep their business models afloat, the Trump administration tried to bypass constitutional rules to squeeze every cent out of developing nations. It is always the same script: the big players ignore the rules until someone finally forces their hand, and even then, the small players are left cleaning up the debris.

They say 70,000 jobs are saved for now. But for how long? AGOA got a one-year extension earlier this month, but that is just a small bandage on a very deep gunshot wound. The Trump administration is already signaling that it will look for other legal ways to bring these taxes back. If you think this is the end of the trade war, you probably still believe a politician's campaign promise. We are just pawns in a bigger game between Washington and anyone they decide to bully on a Friday afternoon.

We are supposed to be happy because we get to keep our Ksh95 billion in annual earnings for another few months. But the reality is that our economy is still hanging on the whims of a conservative court and an unpredictable man in a red tie. Celebrate the ruling if you must, but keep your bags packed and your expectations low. The relief is temporary, the global system is still broken, and the real trade agreement we are chasing will probably come with strings that make these tariffs look like a bargain.
