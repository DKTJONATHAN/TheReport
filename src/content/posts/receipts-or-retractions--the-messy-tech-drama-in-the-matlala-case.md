If you thought your favorite pop star's public meltdown was messy, wait until you see the legal circus currently playing out in the South African courts. We have all the ingredients for a prime-time soap opera: high-profile businessmen, missing cellphone data, and a digital forensic expert who is doing the absolute most to protect his brand. Calvin Rafadi, the man behind Bizz Tracer Pty Ltd, found himself in the hot seat this Friday, defending his reputation against claims that he tampered with data belonging to Vusimuzi "Cat" Matlala and his wife, Tsakani. Honestly, the drama is giving major "I don't know her" energy, especially since the "missing" data apparently vanished into a black hole of technical failures.

The prosecution has rolled out an ensemble cast that would make a Hollywood studio jealous, featuring no less than thirteen lawyers. It is the kind of overkill we usually see when a politician tries to rebrand their image, much like the tactics discussed in [The Professor and the Politician: Ruto's New Intellectual Shield](/posts/the-professor-and-the-politician--ruto-s-new-intellectual-shield) where credentials are used as a literal bulletproof vest. Rafadi spent his time on the stand basically reading his own CV, pointing to "appraisal letters" and his close ties with law enforcement as if that automatically makes him a saint. In the world of gossip, we call that "over-compensating," and it rarely ends well once the cross-examination starts.

Witness C, the mysterious figure from the Political Killings Task Team, isn't buying the "oops, technical error" excuse. According to their testimony, the data from the Matlalas' devices was supposedly unretrievable, which is a very convenient story when the stakes are this high. Rafadi, however, is sticking to his script. He told the court that the phones were never even in his possession. It is the classic "not my circus, not my monkeys" defense, but with more expensive suits and a lot more jargon. If he really never had the phones, then someone in the chain of custody has some serious explaining to do before public confidence in the whole system hits zero.

The accused list reads like a "Who’s Who" of people you probably wouldn't want to share a group chat with. You have Dr Riek Machar, who at 73 should really be enjoying a quiet retirement instead of being the headliner in a 67-session marathon trial. Alongside him are seven others, including Puot Kang Chuol and Lt. Gen. Gabriel Duop Lam, all caught up in this digital web. It is a high-stakes game of "He Said, She Said," but instead of leaked DMs, we are dealing with forensic reports that may or may not have been scrubbed cleaner than a celebrity's Twitter feed before an awards show.

Presiding Judge James Alala Deng has hit the pause button for now, adjourning the trial until Monday, March 9, 2026. Yes, you read that right - this trial is moving slower than a washed-up singer’s comeback album. When we return, the cross-examination is expected to get even nastier. Whether Rafadi is a seasoned expert or just another player in a very expensive game of smoke and mirrors remains to be seen. One thing is for sure: in this courtroom, the truth is harder to find than a genuine apology from a social media influencer caught in a 4K lie.
