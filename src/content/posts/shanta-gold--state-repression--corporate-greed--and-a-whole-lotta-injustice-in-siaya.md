So, Shanta Gold finally struck something in Ramula, Siaya. Not prosperity, mind you. More like a jackpot of dead bodies and shattered lives. April 6th, 2026, marked by the unfortunate demise of Henry Otieno and Jack Omenda, all thanks to a little "confrontation" with the boys in blue, apparently protecting Shanta's "mining activities." Seems like this company's been sniffing around for years, promising riches, and delivering division. Since they found their "commercially viable" stash, it's been a masterclass in opacity and mistrust, with the community treated like inconvenient bystanders.

And who’s protecting this shiny new venture? The state, of course. Armed police, sent to subdue unarmed residents demanding a shred of respect and accountability. It’s a clear signal: Shanta Gold operates with impunity, backed by powerful friends who see the law as an optional suggestion. This isn't just a mining dispute; it's a blatant disregard for human rights and the rule of law, all dressed up in the guise of resource extraction. The OCPD Yala, Charles Wafula, even had the gall to spin this tragedy, trying to paint a picture that conveniently ignores the live ammunition fired at citizens exercising their constitutional rights. You see this kind of thing everywhere, from the whispers of the greenback vampire feeding on global chaos [/posts/the-greenback-vampire-feeds-on-global-chaos] to the blatant disregard for justice.

The harassment doesn't stop at the bullets. We’re hearing tales of DCI officers engaging in "distressing and inhumane" arrests, pushing a woman and her sons to the brink, even contributing to a suicide. And then there's the Ministry of Interior playing security chief for what should be a simple regulatory issue. Arbitrary arrests, trumped-up charges forcing families to sell off their livestock just to afford bail - it’s textbook repression disguised as law enforcement. The ODPP, bless their hearts, seems to be nodding along, maintaining charges that reek of questionable arrests and a complete erosion of public trust.

But the real punchline? A court order from Siaya Law Courts, stopping Shanta Gold dead in its tracks on resettlement and compensation, and it's being treated like a mild inconvenience. State agencies are nowhere to be seen enforcing the law, proving that some people truly believe they're above it all. And the environmental assessments? A joke. No resettlement plan, despite displacement being obvious, and licenses still magically appearing. The Ministry of Mining and Blue Economy is clearly not playing by the rules of the Mining Act, 2016, which supposedly demands community involvement and informed consent. Funny how "free, prior, and informed consent" seems to be a foreign concept when there's gold to be dug.

This whole charade is a slap in the face to Kenya's international commitments and basic human decency. The UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights might as well be gathering dust. The silence and inaction from Siaya's elected leaders - the governor, county assembly, senator, area MP, MCA - is deafening. They were voted in to protect these people, but where are they? Some county leaders, who've long been cozy with the company, have suddenly found their voices, but it’s too little, too late. The governor, a senior counsel no less, needs to step up. This isn't a game; it's about lives and livelihoods.

Shanta Gold, your financiers, and your business partners need to understand that this level of disregard for human rights has consequences. We'll be taking this to regional and international bodies, hitting you where it hurts - reputation and revenue. Expect complaints, scrutiny of your gold supply chain, and a serious dent in your business prospects. This isn't just about Ramula; it's about whether any of us are safe when profit trumps people.
