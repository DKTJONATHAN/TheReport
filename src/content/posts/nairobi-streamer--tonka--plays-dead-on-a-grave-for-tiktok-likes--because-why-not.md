So, Tonka, a Nairobi resident with evidently too much time and too little sense, thought it would be a brilliant idea to livestream from Lang'ata Cemetery at 2 AM. While the rest of us are sensibly tucked in bed, this genius decides to wander among tombstones, strike a dramatic pose on someone's final resting place, and solicit cash from viewers. Apparently, the M-Pesa ticker was running so people could chip in for his morbid performance art. Because nothing says "respect for the departed" like a live donation prompt next to a tombstone.

The TikTok went viral, of course. Because what else are Kenyans supposed to do but watch someone treat a sacred space like their personal stage? The comments section apparently exploded with applause for his "mnomaa" antics, as if lying on a grave is some kind of Olympic sport. He even made jokes about the names on the graves and then cheerfully announced he was "back to being alive." Truly groundbreaking content. It's this sort of cheap thrill, this jarring mix of the eerie and the utterly absurd, that keeps these internet personalities afloat in Kenya's burgeoning streaming scene.

But as usual, not everyone is impressed. While some found it funny - because apparently dead people are just props for our amusement now - a good chunk of Kenyans are rightly questioning the legal and, dare I say, spiritual implications of such a stunt. It's a stark reminder that while some are chasing likes and virtual coins, others still hold a modicum of respect for the departed. It makes you wonder if these streamers have any concept of boundaries, or if they'd even stream from a funeral if the M-Pesa ticker was flashing bright enough.

And speaking of people making money off questionable ventures, this whole Tonka saga pales in comparison to the "millionaire" Vindee, who apparently became one overnight. His declaration came with the predictable caveat for anyone he owes money to to step forward. Some fans, bless their naive hearts, are congratulating him, while others are wisely suggesting he unblock his mother. It's a classic Nairobi tale: flash in the pan success, followed by the same old drama. It’s almost as if chasing quick cash, whether through cemetery antics or some other prank, never truly solves anything. You have to wonder how long before we see another story, perhaps about MPs plotting new rules for elections, a topic that feels far more grounded in reality than someone pretending to be dead for TikTok. Meanwhile, in the political arena, Samidoh is busy celebrating a by-election victory in Ol Kalou. The ruling UDA party got a proper spanking, and Samidoh, a staunch DCP supporter, is obviously having a field day. He's posting memes and making pointed jabs, basically gloating about the defeat. It’s the usual political theatre, where supporters revel in the downfall of their rivals, oblivious to the fact that most of us are just trying to survive. It’s all a grand show, isn’t it? One group scrambling for power, the other for likes, and the rest of us just trying to keep our heads above water.
