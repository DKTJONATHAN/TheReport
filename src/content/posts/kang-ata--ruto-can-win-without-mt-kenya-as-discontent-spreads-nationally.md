Kang'ata Says Ruto Wins Without Mt Kenya As Discontent Goes National

Kang’ata admits Ruto can win without the Mt Kenya vote but warns that national dissatisfaction runs deeper than tribal lines, fueled by economic woes and failed policies.

Murang’a Governor Irungu Kang’ata, bless his optimistic heart, is telling us that President William Ruto can still bag re-election even if Mount Kenya decides to take a long nap during voting. Apparently, we’re all supposed to believe that Kenya has suddenly transformed into some enlightened nation where ethnic blocs are a thing of the past. It’s a nice fairy tale, isn’t it?

Kang’ata, in his interview with Kameme TV, spouts this nonsense about how the opposition to Ruto isn't just a Mount Kenya affair. He claims it’s spread far and wide, from Nyanza to wherever else people are apparently having a bad time. The man’s trying to convince us that these policy issues, like the sky-high cost of living and those lovely new taxes, are hitting everyone equally. As if we haven’t seen this script before.

He’s pushing this "issue-based politics" narrative, as if voters are suddenly poring over policy documents instead of looking at which candidate might actually help them put food on the table. This talk about personal impact and livelihoods is just a fancy way of saying people are broke and angry. Anyone who’s been around Nairobi long enough knows that when the wallet gets squeezed, everyone feels it, regardless of where their grandmother hailed from. Remember when we thought the gold rush was real? [The Golden Illusion Shatters Under Geopolitical Pressure](/posts/the-golden-illusion-shatters-under-geopolitical-pressure). Turns out, it was just another mirage.

The governor insists that the country is changing, and voters are looking at things "at a different personal level." This is what happens when your basic needs aren't being met. It’s not some grand political evolution; it’s survival. People are looking at how government decisions affect their pockets, their jobs, and whether they can afford basic commodities. It’s a harsh reality that Kang’ata seems to be discovering, or perhaps just trying to package for public consumption.

So, while Kang’ata is busy painting a picture of a nation united by policy grievances, let’s not forget the usual suspects. The "issue-based politics" argument conveniently sidesteps the fact that political loyalties are still heavily influenced by who you know, where you come from, and, yes, what tribe you belong to. It’s a nice theory, but in Kenya, reality usually bites back harder.
