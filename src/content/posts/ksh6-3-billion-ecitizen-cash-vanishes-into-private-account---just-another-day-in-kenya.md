So, the Auditor General's office has sniffed out another Ksh6.3 billion that’s just… vanished from the eCitizen platform. Apparently, it took a detour into a private account, no Treasury approval, naturally. This isn't some minor misplacement, folks. We're talking about significant public funds, including a tidy Ksh6.2 billion, that were supposedly routed through some mysterious 'Pesaflow' account. The auditors can't even find the bank statements to confirm the total amount. Honestly, it's like they're not even trying to hide it anymore.

The Treasury PS, bless his heart, claims the money's been "seized" and the account shut down. But this whole mess raises a million questions. Were these payment providers even legit between 2014 and 2023? And why was the Auditor General practically begging for access to basic platform data? The Public Accounts Committee is now hauling in everyone remotely connected to eCitizen. It’s a regular circus, and we, the taxpayers, are footing the bill for the show. This whole situation just screams of the kind of systemic rot that makes you wonder if anyone actually cares about fixing things, or if it’s just about who’s in the best position to dip their hands in the cookie jar. It reminds me of how frustrating it can be when you realize that [The Give to Gain Delusion: Why Nairobi’s Glass Ceiling Is Made of Reinforced Concrete].

Meanwhile, our beloved Nairobi Securities Exchange is having a bit of a tantrum, losing Ksh200 billion in valuation. The blame? A little spat between Israel and Iran. Apparently, foreign investors, who seem to have more say in our market than any of us locals, are pulling their money out faster than a rat from a sinking ship. Safaricom, Equity, KCB - the usual suspects - are leading the losses. It's all tied to global inflation and recession fears, with the Middle East drama messing with oil prices. So, when oil goes up, our stocks go down, and our wallets get lighter. Just another Tuesday.

And speaking of oil, the Energy CS is warning fuel companies against hoarding. They're apparently holding back supplies, hoping for a price hike. Because, you know, public interest and all that. The government claims there's enough fuel in the pipeline, but you can bet your last shilling that stations will suddenly be "out of stock" when you need it most. The oil dealers, naturally, are saying the current prices don't reflect the "price changes occasioned by the Israel-Iran conflict." So, while we’re worrying about our savings and the shifty eCitizen dealings, they’re just looking for an excuse to charge us more.

On a slightly less depressing note, President Ruto is promising a new Mombasa-Nairobi highway. Great. More roads, more tenders, more money somehow disappearing before construction even begins. This 2,800km expansion plan is going to cost a fortune, and the US firm involved is already facing hurdles. Then there's NCBA, which somehow managed to increase its dividend and net profit. Good for them, I guess. And Kenyan banks and Saccos are borrowing more from KMRC to give out mortgages. At least some people are trying to get ahead, even if it means stretching repayment terms to nearly 12.3 years. But don't get too excited, because the system is rigged, and it’s always the common mwananchi who suffers the most.
