NAIROBI'S ICONIC SAFARI RALLY ROARS AGAIN, MAKES SAME OLD CASE FOR WRC FUTURE

The dust may have settled, but the same old song and dance around the Safari Rally is back. Another year, another bunch of drivers getting their fancy cars battered across our "brutal" terrain, all for a trophy and some bragging rights. This year, it was some Japanese chap, Takamoto Katsuta, who lucked into a win because all the big names - Solberg, Ogier, Evans - apparently forgot this isn't a Sunday drive, it's the Safari. "Survival-first approach," they call it. I call it knowing when to bail before you become another roadside decoration.

President Ruto was there, of course, flapping his gums about global significance and how this rally "unites East Africa." Bless his heart. Meanwhile, tens of thousands of Kenyans lined the dusty plains, acting like it’s the second coming of Christ, all to watch wealthy foreigners throw mud at each other. This is our "sporting heritage," apparently. Never mind the roads leading to these "spectator zones" are a disaster, but hey, at least we get some shiny cars for a weekend. It’s like watching a more expensive, dirtier version of traffic on Mombasa Road.

This whole "adventure, endurance, and breathtaking beauty" spiel is just PR talk. For the drivers, it’s a mechanical nightmare. "Fesh-fesh" dust that chokes your lungs, rocks that chew up suspension, and mud that swallows cars whole. And the weather? A classic Kenyan lottery - one minute you’re swimming, the next you’re baking. It’s less about skill and more about not getting your multi-million shilling car destroyed by a rogue pothole. Honestly, it makes you wonder if they’re more interested in the horsepower or the potential for breakdowns, much like the endless cycle of reboots in Hollywood.

The corporate sponsors are all over it, of course, setting up fan zones and pretending they’re part of some grand cultural moment. SportPesa's CEO is waxing lyrical about unity. Look, it’s a business, and this rally is a brand-building exercise. People love spectacle, and Kenya’s got the perfect stage for it, even if the infrastructure leading to it is a mess. And now, with the contract up for renewal, everyone’s hoping the "extraordinary turnout" will convince the WRC bigwigs to keep this gravy train rolling.

Behind the WRC circus, our local heroes like Carl Tundo are getting their moment, praised for their "passion." He’s won it five times, apparently. Good for him. He’s probably got the most robust shock absorbers in the country. This rally is a tough gig, no doubt, but let’s not pretend it’s not a massive drain on resources and a headache for everyone not directly profiting from it. Still, it brings people out, it makes noise, and it keeps Kenya on the map for something other than… well, you know.

So, as the engines fall silent and the last of the champagne-soaked drivers head home, the question remains: what’s next? Will the WRC circus pack up its tents and leave, or will they continue to exploit our "iconic" rally for their global championship? Given the politicians’ eagerness to keep it here, and the sponsors’ deep pockets, don't hold your breath for it to disappear. We’ll be back here next year, dealing with the same dust, the same drama, and the same optimistic pronouncements about the future.
