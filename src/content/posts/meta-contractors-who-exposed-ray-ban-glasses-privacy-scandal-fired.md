Meta Contractors Exposed Ray-Ban Glasses Privacy Scandal, Gets Fired

This is not a story about shiny new tech for your convenience. It’s a tale of how your most private moments become fodder for algorithms, and how the people doing the dirty work get the boot when they speak up.

So, these fancy Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses, eh? Supposed to be all hands-free and convenient. Turns out, for some contractors in Kenya working for Sama, they became a front-row seat to people having sex, changing clothes, and, yes, using the toilet. All allegedly without the users having a clue, despite the flashing recording light. Meta, of course, plays innocent, saying they take privacy seriously and user consent is paramount. But when the workers started yapping to Swedish newspapers and a Kenyan journalist, suddenly Meta "terminated its contract with Sama." Convenient, isn't it? Just like *Flying High on Your Empty Pockets: The Pararescue Circus* shows how some operations are never quite what they seem.

Now, Sama insists they were meeting all standards and got no warning. Meta spins it as Sama "not meeting standards." It stinks of retaliation, not performance management. The timing is too neat. These poor souls were apparently expected to just process this intimate footage, no questions asked. And all this so AI can get "smarter" by watching you at your most vulnerable. While regulators in the UK and Kenya start sniffing around, and a class-action lawsuit gets filed in California, Meta and Luxottica, Ray-Ban’s parent company, are likely just counting their legal fees.

This isn’t even Sama’s first rodeo with ethically questionable outsourcing. They were dumped by OpenAI a couple of years back for traumatizing their workers by classifying child sexual abuse material. It’s a pattern, plain and simple. Tech giants find the cheapest labor in the global south to sift through the digital dirt they create, then toss them aside when they become inconvenient.

These glasses might promise to capture memories, but they’re really just feeding a hungry algorithm with the most private, unflattering parts of humanity. The real question isn’t if the tech *works*, but whether you can ever truly trust the corporations peddling it. They’ll sell you convenience, but at what cost to your dignity and the dignity of the people forced to witness your life’s unedited reel?
