So, Eric Omondi, the man who’s now busy protesting the cost of living and eyeing political office in 2027, decided to grace us with details from his childhood. Turns out, his father was a Chief Inspector with a taste for polygamy, boasting five wives and a grand total of fourteen children. Eric, the second born to his mother, the fourth wife, claims it was all "very stable" because the other women lived elsewhere. Right. Because separate houses automatically equate to harmony. It’s Nairobi, not a fairy tale.

His mother’s promise of communication studies in Nairobi hinged on him passing exams, a dream cruelly cut short by her death weeks prior. To his credit, Eric managed to scrape together 127,000 shillings for college fees from a church fundraiser with a turnout that would make most events look like a rave. This level of hustle, raising money from eight people, is classic Kenyan ingenuity, though one wonders what else could have been done with that kind of effort.

But the real punchline, as with most things in life, is loss. The death of his brother Fred, his creative "head," is the blow that still stings. It's the familiar story of siblings trying to make it in this city, a path often paved with broken dreams and premature goodbyes. It reminds you that even in the glitz of entertainment, the harsh realities of life, like the rising cost of living he now protests, are always lurking.

And now, Omondi is pivoting to activism and politics, a move as predictable as Awinja’s next outfit at a launch. He’s joining the ranks of celebrities who think a microphone translates directly to a mandate. Let's see how his polygamous upbringing and brother's death prepare him for the mudslinging of Kenyan politics, or if this is just another grand gesture. One can only hope he’s not blinded by ambition, unlike some of the folks whose shady dealings are exposed even within our police force.
