Why Kenya's So-Called "Realities" Are Just Old Politics Playing Dress-Up

The Kenyan airwaves are choked with the same old propaganda, a predictable script wheeled out whenever the powers-that-be are drowning in their own mismanagement. Mwangi Khimani’s latest musings are just a repackaging of the same tired tactic: blame the Kikuyu, the perpetual "41 against 1" bogeyman, for every economic stumble and Gen Z tantrum. They trot out this divisive nonsense like a ghost from the 2007 violence, hoping we’re too stupid to notice.

But here’s the punchline: the ground has shifted. These old hacks are clinging to an outdated playbook, thinking they can still divide and conquer. They forget that Nairobi isn’t the same village it was two decades ago. Kiambu isn't just a Kikuyu bastion anymore; Luo families are there, thanks to the real estate sharks. Kalenjin folks are running businesses and teaching in Kisumu, Eldoret, Nyeri - everywhere. We're all interconnected now, by rent, school fees, and the shared misery of these leaders’ promises. Trying to start an ethnic fire now is like trying to burn down your own neighbourhood. It’s mutual destruction, plain and simple.

The real disconnect isn't between tribes; it's between these aging politicians and the generation that's actually living through this mess. Gen Z doesn't care about tribal victimhood when their pockets are empty and the fuel prices are climbing. Their struggle is economic, a shared frustration that transcends ethnic lines. When these officials try to deflect from their abysmal fiscal policies with tribal whispers, they don't ignite hatred; they just breed more contempt for their own incompetence. They're pushing an agenda that sounds suspiciously like the same old corruption we've seen before, a theme I’ve explored in [The Anatomy of a Political Assassination](/posts/sex-power-videos-and-baltasar-ebang-engonga).

So, while we should always be watchful, let’s not fall into the trap of fatalistic fear. These politicians are trying to scare us with ghosts because they have zero real solutions for 2026. They want to divide us so we don't notice they're lining their pockets while the rest of us struggle. But the social fabric of Kenya is stronger than their flimsy egos. No community is eager to spill blood for the sake of a politician's crumbling career or wealth. Kenya has grown up, even if its leaders haven't.

The old script of ethnic animosity might still be a useful tool for the desperate, but it no longer defines who we are as Kenyans. Our current reality, with its shared hustles and intertwined lives, utterly demolishes their tired narrative.

The ghost they're trying to summon has no teeth, and frankly, it's getting tired of the same old performance.







The digital airwaves are thick with that familiar, unsettling anxiety, the kind that signals a predictable political maneuver. Social media buzzes with reflections, like Mwangi Khimani’s widely shared piece, warning of a tired strategy: whenever the current regime hits a wall - be it economic mismanagement, crippling fuel prices, or the fiery wrath of Gen Z - they haul out the same old scapegoat. High-ranking officials, critics argue, are attempting a resurrection of the infamous "41 against 1" playbook, pinpointing the Kikuyu community as the architects of national misfortune.

For those who endured the trauma of 2007, these patterns are an immediate red flag. When senior figures echo the same divisive talking points, the fear of history repeating itself is palpable. However, the politics behind this fear are decidedly outdated. The elite may be trying to revive old survival tactics, but they are playing an obsolete game. The Kenya of today is structurally and socially miles apart from the Kenya of 2007. The terrifying endgame of widespread, politician-engineered ethnic clashes is no longer a viable blueprint because the ground beneath the politicians’ feet has fundamentally shifted.

The primary driver is national integration. Two decades ago, many ethnic communities remained largely segregated, confined to regional enclaves. Today, economic necessity, urban expansion, migration, and the decentralization of power through devolution have dramatically redrawn the demographic map. Take Kiambu County, traditionally seen as an ethnic stronghold; it now hosts a significant non-native population, including many Luo families drawn by real estate growth and proximity to Nairobi. Kalenjin citizens, once primarily concentrated in parts of the Rift Valley, now thrive in business, academia, agriculture, and public life across major towns. From Kisumu to Eldoret, and from Nyeri to Mombasa, neighbourhoods are increasingly mixed, businesses are co-owned, and families are intermarrying. When a country reaches this deep level of integration, the logistics of orchestrating localised ethnic conflict begin to collapse.

In 2007, drawing lines of division on a map was relatively straightforward. Today, any attempt to ignite an ethnic fire in a town or estate risks burning a neighbourhood where one’s own kin rent houses, buy groceries, attend school, and run businesses. The cost of conflict is immediate, mutual, and catastrophic for everyone involved. This doesn't mean ethnic hatred has vanished, but the material conditions that once facilitated easy mobilization have dramatically changed. The new Kenya is connected by rent, school fees, the daily hustle, WhatsApp groups, shared debt, shared unemployment, and a shared anger at leaders who consistently overpromise and underdeliver. Even where prejudice persists, it competes with daily interdependence. A boda boda rider, teacher, shopkeeper, student, nurse, landlord, and tenant all understand that chaos destroys livelihoods long before it rescues any politician from accountability. That reality makes ethnic incitement harder to sell and even harder to sustain.

Beyond demographics, there’s a significant cognitive shift occurring within the electorate. The politicians peddling these divisive narratives suffer from a severe generational disconnect. They are addressing an audience that no longer exists in sufficient numbers to execute tribal warfare. The emergence of the Gen Z movement has shattered the old paradigm. This generation does not view national crises through the lens of tribal victimhood. When fuel prices surge, or the cost of living becomes unbearable, a young person in Eldoret suffers precisely as a young person in Kisumu, Nyeri, Mombasa, or Nairobi does. The modern Kenyan struggle is fundamentally economic, not ethnic. The educated Kenyan, and even the uneducated but politically alert youth, can see through the smoke and mirrors. When senior officials use tribal scapegoats to deflect from their abysmal fiscal policies, they do not inspire hatred against their neighbours; they inspire contempt for the politicians themselves. Young people are demanding accountability, transparency, jobs, and dignity-none of which can be delivered by fighting a fellow citizen from another community.

Citizens must remain vigilant. When leaders who once wore the badge of human rights activism or progressive reform begin to indulge in divisive rhetoric, they deserve unequivocal condemnation. We must call out the hypocrisy of the political class whenever it attempts to weaponize identity. However, vigilance must not devolve into fatalistic fear. Kenya’s social fabric is demonstrably tougher than the fragile egos of its politicians. No community is ready to pick up machetes or stones to preserve a politician’s career, wealth, or collapsing legitimacy. The political class is attempting to frighten Kenyans with ghosts from 2007 because it has no convincing answers for the crises of 2026. But the ghost, as it turns out, has no teeth. Kenya has moved on, integrated, and grown up. It is time its politicians did the same. Ethnic animosity may still be useful to desperate leaders, but it no longer defines the people. Kenya’s present reality decisively defeats the old script, a testament to how far we’ve come, even if the leaders haven't.
