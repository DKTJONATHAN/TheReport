NAIROBI PIPELINE SALE: GOVERNMENT FORCED TO SHOW ITS HAND ON SH106 BILLION SCAM
WARNING: The High Court has ordered the government to cough up crucial documents related to the Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC) privatisation, after a petition questioning the billions pocketed from the sale was allowed to proceed. Expect more drama as the truth, if it ever surfaces, begins to unravel.

This whole song and dance about the KPC sale is just another classic Kenyan episode of "who's lining whose pockets." So, Justice Patricia Nyaundi, bless her for trying, wants the National Executive, AG, Privatisation Commission, and Authority to dump all their valuation reports, Cabinet memos, IMF deals - you name it - on the table within 21 days. They tried to sweep this under the rug, claiming it was already decided. As if these people ever learn. The government, patting itself on the back, cashed in Sh106.3 billion for a 65 percent stake, listing it on the stock market like it was some kind of nation-building miracle. They even had the audacity to revoke its national government entity status.

Now, Busia Senator Okiya Omtatah, along with a fraud risk consultant Bernard Muchiri Muchere and Naomi Nyakerario Misati, are the ones brave enough to ask the real questions. They’re not buying the narrative on the privatisation itself, the so called Privatisation Act of 2025, or the IMF’s heavy hand in pushing these reforms. The AG’s predictable whine about previous judgments fell flat. These petitioners are pointing out that this case is about the very constitutionality of privatisation, the validity of that shaky Act, those pesky IMF conditions, and a suspiciously large Sh97 billion financial black hole at KPC.

Justice Nyaundi, while letting the case go forward, wisely refused to grant the petitioners’ plea for interim orders to freeze things. Instead, she’s demanded the documents. Forget the fancy talk about sovereignty and national security needing a three-judge panel; the court sees this as a straight up document disclosure issue for now. The petitioners are convinced this whole mess was "externally driven by unconstitutional IMF conditionalities rather than sovereign public interest," done without any real public input and by entities that probably shouldn't have been involved. It’s a story as old as time, reminding me of the debt-saturated mirage of digital wealth we've seen before.

The affidavit from Mr Muchere is the real gut punch here. He claims to have found an "unexplained variance" of Sh97.18 billion in KPC's 2023/24 accounts. That's not just loose change; it’s "evidence of serious financial impropriety, misapplication, or concealment of public money." The respondents, naturally, are denying everything and fighting tooth and nail against any meaningful action. But the court’s order means the government now has to pull back the curtain on this Sh106.3 billion deal. We’ll see what secrets come crawling out.

This whole charade is just another reminder that when the government starts talking about "privatisation" and "IMF reforms," it's usually a cue for the public to brace for impact. The KPC sale, hyped as a triumph, is now facing scrutiny over billions of shillings and the very legality of the process. The court's order for documents is a small win for transparency, but the real battle for accountability is just beginning.
