So, President Ruto is out here telling us to dig into our own pockets for infrastructure. "He who pays the piper calls the tune," he says. Yeah, tell that to the millions of Kenyans who can barely afford to put food on the table, let alone fund a KSh5 trillion infrastructure binge over the next decade. It's a nice sentiment, but let's be real, where is this "domestic capital" supposed to magically appear from? It sounds like another one of those grand pronouncements that ends up benefiting a select few while the rest of us are left holding the bag, much like how [YOUR IDENTITY FOR SALE WHILE THE ELITE HIDE IN RUNDA](/posts/your-identity-for-sale-while-the-elite-hide-in-runda) highlighted the disparity in wealth.

He warns about foreign capital coming with "competing interests" and the risk of undermining Africa's long-term goals. What else is new? We’ve been here before. Every time a leader starts talking about self-reliance and domestic funding, it’s usually a prelude to more debt, more promises, and ultimately, the same old story. Africa’s resources are being plundered by foreign entities, and now we’re supposed to believe we can build oil refineries and fund ambitious projects with our own money when our pension funds, as President Museveni points out, are largely untapped? It's a slap in the face to the average citizen.

The idea of pooling mineral resources for "global green manufacturing" sounds appealing on paper, but can we even trust our own leaders not to sell these critical minerals for peanuts to the same foreign powers they claim to be wary of? The East African countries talking about a joint oil refinery is just another pipe dream, another way to line pockets with the promise of future prosperity that never quite arrives. We import $90 billion worth of petroleum products annually, yet we can't even get our own infrastructure sorted.

Ruto's call for regional integration and comparison to the European Coal and Steel Community is a bit rich. Europe has centuries of established institutions and a much more unified political will. Here in Africa, we’re still struggling with basic governance, corruption is rampant, and the idea of seamless movement of goods and resources feels like science fiction. Until we tackle the systemic issues of accountability and true leadership, these summits will remain just talk shops, as pointless as [SAMRO’s 2026 Music Business Lab: Teaching Artists to Beg for Their Own Royalties?](/posts/samro-s-2026-music-business-lab--teaching-artists-to-beg-for-their-own-royalties) was in its promise.

The real issue isn't a lack of ambition; it's a chronic lack of execution and a persistent tendency for leaders to preach one thing and practice another. We need tangible action, not just platitudes about domestic capital. Until our own leaders demonstrate genuine commitment to serving the people and not just their own interests, Africa will continue to be a continent of immense potential, perpetually held back by its own.
