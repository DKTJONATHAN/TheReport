Do not be fooled by the one-page memorandum of understanding currently being floated. Diplomatic sources may speak of cautious optimism, but the structural damage to global supply chains is already terminal. Japan and China are screaming for the taps to be turned back on because their industrial engines are seizing up. When even Maersk warns that energy costs will stay high regardless of a peace deal, you know the game is rigged. Refinery capacity and logistical bottlenecks are not solved by a signature on a piece of paper in Beijing or D.C.

The U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve is being cannibalized at a record pace. President Trump's order to dump 172 million barrels into the market in March 2026 is a short-term sedative for a long-term cancer. We are witnessing the largest single drawdown in history, a move that leaves the West's energy security naked for the next inevitable spike. Meanwhile, the UK is already flirting with jet fuel rationing and Goldman Sachs is whispering about extreme physical tightness in the market.

Iran claims it will control the Strait of Hormuz forever, a statement that carries more weight than any temporary ceasefire. Even if Iranian oil floods back into the market, the underinvestment in production capacity means the global economy is still one bad day away from 150-dollar barrels again. The current price drop is a brief moment of relief before the structural reality of a fractured world reasserts its stranglehold.
