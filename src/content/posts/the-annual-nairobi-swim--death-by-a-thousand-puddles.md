Here we go again. Nairobi is under water, and the body count is at 42 and counting. It is the same tired story every single rainy season: the heavens open, the drainage fails, and the poor in informal settlements pay the ultimate price. Our leaders act shocked every time, as if they did not know that April comes right after March.

President Ruto is out here talking about soldiers and strategic food reserves. It is the usual reactive theater designed to make the government look busy while people are literally being swept away. They wait for bodies to be pulled from the mud before they remember that [Kenya Is Rotten To The Core](/posts/new-post) when it comes to basic urban planning and accountability. Sending the military to coordinate rescues is just a PR band-aid on a gaping wound of systemic neglect.

Even the elite are not safe this time. When Kenya Airways has to divert flights to Mombasa because the capital is a swamp, you know the incompetence has reached the clouds. Roads are gone, vehicles are floating, and the only thing moving fast in this city is the rate at which our infrastructure is crumbling. We are paying taxes for world-class infrastructure and getting a water park we never asked for.

The government promises to pay hospital bills for the survivors. How generous of them to offer a band-aid after they allowed the building to fall on you. They talk about "lasting solutions" to perennial flooding while approving another concrete jungle without a single functioning sewer line. It is a script we have all memorized by now, and frankly, the performance is getting boring.

Scientists want to blame global warming and intense rainfall bursts. While the climate is definitely changing, our appetite for corruption and poor engineering remains a constant. We do not have a rain problem; we have a leadership problem that treats every predictable season like a surprise attack. Until the drainage actually works, all the relief food in the world is just seasoning for the next disaster.
