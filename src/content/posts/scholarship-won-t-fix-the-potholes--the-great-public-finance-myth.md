Lyla Latif is out here writing books about public finance like it is some high-brow intellectual justice, but let us be real for a second. While scholars debate "fiscal institutions" and the "interconnectedness" of revenue policy, the rest of us are busy swerving around potholes that could swallow a Probox. She says public money is ours. If it is mine, why do I have to pay a bribe at a government clinic just to get a Panadol? It is the same old story: the big bosses in the Treasury talk about "macroeconomic stability" while the ground reality is a constant struggle to keep the lights on.

The book goes on about how the British used the 1910 Hut Tax to force us into wage labor. Surprise, surprise: things have not changed much. We just traded colonial masters for international treaty regimes and sovereign debt markets that treat the Kenyan taxpayer like a bottomless ATM. We are stuck in a cycle where debt sustainability determines the budget, and the budget determines that you will never see a working street light in your lifetime. It is all connected, sure, but mostly it is connected to the pockets of people who do not even live in the same postal code as the people they are "serving."

Latif mentions the 2010 Constitution as if paper laws can stop a hungry politician. We have the "architecture of transparency," but the reality is just [The $3 Trillion Mirage: Ruto Sells Continental Dreams While Nairobi Suffers the Ground Reality](/posts/the--3-trillion-mirage--ruto-sells-continental-dreams-while-nairobi-suffers-the-ground-reality). The gap between what the Constitution promises and what we actually experience is wide enough to fit a fleet of luxury SUVs bought with "diverted" funds. These elite guys talk about "fiscal power" in air-conditioned offices, but for us, it is just higher prices at the petrol station and another "finance bill" that feels like a mugging.

The scholar says we need to look inward and build "professionally independent revenue authorities." Good luck with that in a place where corruption is the only thing that actually works with any efficiency. She wants "indigenous fiscal scholarship" instead of importing ideas from Washington or Paris. Fine. But until that scholarship stops the "illicit financial flows" that drain the continent faster than a broken tap, it is just more words on a page. The "fiscal contract" she dreams of is currently a one-way street where we pay, and they play.

Maybe she is right that African states are finally writing the rules at the UN, but wake me up when those rules actually reach the street level. "Fiscal sovereignty" sounds great in a lecture hall or at a book launch. In the real Nairobi, sovereignty means choosing which meal you are going to skip so you can pay for your kid-s "underfunded" school fees. We are told we are no longer "rule takers," yet we are still the ones paying for the party we were never invited to attend. Public money might be mine, but I have yet to see the receipt.
