Welcome to the Kenyan political circus, where "national unity" is just a fancy word for "who gets the biggest slice of the budget." We were told this broad-based government was the cure for our country’s fractures, but anyone with a brain in Nairobi knows it was just a temporary truce to keep the streets quiet. Now, the mask is slipping because ODM has realized that being a "partner" without a seat at the very top is just being an expensive flower girl.

President Ruto is currently trapped in a political bind that would make a seasoned escape artist sweat. On one hand, he has Kithure Kindiki, the man currently holding the briefcase and representing the ever-fickle Mt. Kenya vote. On the other, he has his new ODM "brothers" who did not come to the party just to eat crumbs. Much like [The Comedy of Errors: Why the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan), this whole political arrangement was never about love or shared values; it was always a cold, hard business transaction that is now failing its first audit.

The UDA wing is already sharpening their knives, terrified that their loyalty is being traded for a temporary peace with the Lake region. They see the Deputy President position as their birthright, a reward for standing by the Chief through the teargas and the chaos. If Ruto gives in to ODM, he loses the Mountain. If he keeps Kindiki, the broad-based government becomes just another failed experiment in Kenyan history, ending in a messy public breakup that leaves everyone bitter.

While these giants clash over who gets to fly the second flag, the rest of us are still stuck in the same Nairobi traffic, wondering if the price of unga will ever actually drop. We are expected to watch this soap opera with bated breath, as if the identity of the person sitting in that office will change the fact that our taxes are being swallowed by a black hole of debt and "administrative costs." It is all just a distraction from the reality that the common mwananchi is not even invited to the table where these deals are being cut.

Ultimately, this is the risk you run when you build a house on the shifting sands of political desperation. You can call it a broad-based government all you want, but without a clear division of the spoils, the vultures will eventually start eating each other. Do not be surprised when the whole thing disintegrates before the next election cycle even begins. In this city, the only thing you can trust is that a politician will always choose their own skin over the stability of the nation.
