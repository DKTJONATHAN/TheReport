Dr. Ruto entered Arusha in 2024 like the messiah of Pan-Africanism, promising us a borderless paradise where the East African shilling would finally mean something. Fast forward to today and the only thing that has successfully integrated is the shared sense of disappointment. While the so-called Pan-Africanist was busy making grand policy pronouncements and flying across the globe, the East African Community (EAC) was busy falling apart at its very thin seams. Intra-regional trade is stuck at a miserable 15 percent - or even lower according to the latest quarterly reports - proving that our leaders would rather protect their own small kingdoms than actually build a bloc that works for anyone other than the bureaucrats.

The recent summit in Arusha was less of a high-level meeting and more of a ghost town. When you have the big boys like Kagame, Tshisekedi, and Salva Kiir skipping class, you know the school is in trouble. It is hard to talk about regional unity when Rwanda and Burundi are busy accusing each other of sponsoring rebels, and Congo treats Kenya like a suspicious neighbor who just invited a thief to dinner. This is [the high-cost of staged smiles and lakeside revolutions](/posts/the-high-cost-of-staged-smiles-and-lakeside-revolutions) that we have come to expect from our political elite. They sign peace deals in Washington and Doha because they cannot even stand to look at each other across a conference table in their own backyard.

Let us talk about the money - or the absolute lack of it. The EAC is basically a broke club where the members refuse to pay their membership fees but still want to enjoy the executive lounge. As of January, the bloc was owed nearly 90 million dollars in arrears. The DRC is sitting on a 27 million dollar debt while their country burns, and the Secretariat had to fire nearly half its staff last year because the Council of Ministers could not agree on a simple contract extension. It is a classic Nairobi story: big dreams, zero budget, and a lot of diplomacy that is really just a polite way of saying we are too weak to enforce our own rules.

Instead of trading with each other, we are shipping in everything from China, India, and the UAE. We have spent over twenty years talking about a Customs Union, yet we still have non-tariff barriers that make it easier to move a container from Shanghai to Mombasa than a truck of maize from Uganda to Kenya. The experts call it unrealized potential, but on the streets of Nairobi, we call it incompetence. We are recording growth in international trade while our own regional brothers remain strangers, blocked by protectionist walls that no amount of presidential rhetoric can seem to tear down.

As Ruto hands over the chairmanship this Saturday, he is not giving his successor a baton; he is handing over a ticking time bomb. The next leader has to find money where there is none, stop wars that have lasted decades, and convince eight presidents to stop acting like petty landlords. They are launching a new Seventh Development Strategy, but if the last six are anything to go by, it will be another glossy document used to level an uneven table in an office that has had its electricity cut off for non-payment. Same script, different year.
