Kenya's Empty Promises on Fish: Another Workshop for the Elite

Kenya’s shiny pronouncements about “evidence-based fisheries governance” are just another talk shop for the suited types while the real issues - climate change and rampant illegal fishing - continue to gut our oceans. Cabinet Secretary Hassan Ali Joho, fresh from hobnobbing at the Travellers Beach Hotel, is urging African Union members to “strengthen cooperation” and adopt “science-driven policies.” Sounds good on paper, doesn’t it? But how much of this actually trickles down to the fishermen struggling to make ends meet when foreign trawlers are plundering their catches?

The usual suspects, the African Union Inter-African Bureau for Animal Resources (AU-IBAR) and the European Union, are back with their funding and their workshops. They want delegates to develop “practical solutions” and deliberate with “Pan-African solidarity.” It’s the same song, different verse. Billions of dollars are lost annually to illegal fishing, and climate change is a runaway train. Meanwhile, these meetings produce more paper than fish, and the only thing that gets stronger is the hotel’s bar bill.

Joho chimes in about the Blue Economy’s potential, but let’s be honest, the only people benefiting from this “potential” are those who can afford to attend these fancy conferences. He talks about safeguarding aquatic resources for “current and future generations,” a noble sentiment that rings hollow when IUU fishing is already depleting stocks faster than we can say “sustainable management.” We’ve heard all this before, remember when [Willis Otieno Explains Why Kenyans Should Judge Leadership on Policy, Not Ethnic Garbage](/posts/willis-otieno-explains-why-kenyans-should-judge-leadership-on-policy--not-ethnic-garbage)? Policies are great, but enforcement is the real boss, and that’s where the real problems lie.

The workshop aims to promote scientific evidence, better data collection, and coordinated policy-making. It’s a nice idea, but let’s not forget who is actually doing the fishing and who is being impacted. The millions who depend on fisheries are seeing their livelihoods threatened. When will these “policymakers, fisheries experts and development partners” actually implement something tangible, instead of just churning out more strategies and common approaches that seem to go nowhere?

Kenya, as always, is a “steadfast partner” to the AU. That means more official statements, more delegates flying in, and probably more selfies with the ocean in the background. The real crisis at sea, the one that affects food security and coastal communities, will continue unabated until someone decides that actions speak louder than words, and that maybe, just maybe, some of that money could be spent on actual patrol boats instead of more PowerPoint presentations.

Kenya's Blue Economy Talk: Evidence or Empty Vessels?

Kenya’s grand pronouncements about reforming fisheries governance are a classic case of talking a big game while the real problems - climate change and rampant illegal fishing - continue to devour our oceans and livelihoods.
