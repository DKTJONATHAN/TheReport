Another day in Nairobi, another list of shamed heroes to add to our collection of national disappointments. We wake up to find our "national pride" is actually just a group of people who are exceptionally good at playing hide and seek with the authorities. Wiseman Were and Rita Jeptoo are the latest to remind us that in this country, if someone is running that fast, they are probably just trying to outrun a drug tester.

Wiseman Were was supposed to be the golden boy of the hurdles, fresh from the Paris Olympics and military glory. But apparently, he could not find his way to a testing station three times in a single year. "Whereabouts failure" is just athlete-speak for "I knew you were coming and I was not ready to be seen." It is a pathetic end to a career that was finally picking up speed, but in Kenya, the only thing faster than our runners is the rate at which they break our hearts.

Then we have Rita Jeptoo, a woman who has more comebacks than a bad habit. She was already stripped of major titles years ago, served a long ban, and still had the audacity to come back and get caught with steroids. It is the same old script of greed and shortcuts that we see in every sector of this country. We keep watching these sports spectacles, much like how we deal with [Ruto’s Yellow Wave: A New Script for the Same Old Play](/posts/ruto-s-yellow-wave--a-new-script-for-the-same-old-play), hoping for something different while the actors just change their kits and keep the same tired routine.

The rot is no longer confined to the track. It has officially leaked into the football pitch and the basketball court, proving that the culture of cheating is a multi-sport discipline. Charles Ouma and the boys from Nairobi United are out here missing tests like they miss open goals. Even Rooney Onyango, all the way in Norway, cannot seem to keep his calendar straight for the investigators. When volleyballers and basketballers start getting flagged for whereabouts failures, you know the "get rich or die trying" mentality has fully corrupted the locker rooms.

We are a country of pretenders. We throw parades for these people, hand them flags, and then act shocked when the lab results reveal the truth. ADAK is currently suspending 27 people at once because they finally realized the whole house is on fire. But for those of us living the Nairobi reality, we know the truth: talent is often just a cover for chemistry, and the only clean thing in our sports industry is the bank accounts of the officials after the bribes are paid.
