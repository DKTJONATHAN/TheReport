Rising Air Ticket Prices Loom As Kenya Joins African Nations Drowning In Jet Fuel Crisis

Another day, another crisis for us poor souls trying to get anywhere. Our beloved airlines, already milking us dry, are now blaming the sky for their sky-high prices. Apparently, the Middle East's drama is our problem, and we're the ones footing the bill for their fuel tantrums.

So, the geniuses in charge, who can't even refine enough fuel to keep our planes in the air, are pointing fingers at global markets. Kenya, Madagascar, South Africa - we’re all in the same leaky boat, relying on imports that are now apparently as precious as gold. And don't even get me started on that Strait of Hormuz thing; sounds like a fancy way of saying we're at the mercy of whoever controls that choke point. It’s a classic case of [The $20 Trillion Shadow: How Global Elites Are Hiding Your Future in Plain Sight](/posts/the-20-trillion-shadow-how-global-elites-are-hiding-your-future-in-plain-sight).

Our local refineries, bless their tiny hearts, are about as useful as a screen door on a submarine. They can’t meet demand, so we’re left scrambling, paying exorbitant prices for fuel that’s getting more expensive by the day. And get this, jet fuel is now a massive chunk of our airlines’ operating costs, far more than what those European or Asian carriers are coughing up. Figures.

Meanwhile, crude oil is doing its usual dance towards the stratosphere, all thanks to some brouhaha in Iraq and Kuwait. Even with all those emergency reserves being "released" - which probably means a select few got a nice little bonus - prices are still climbing. And Donald Trump? He’s just over there, confidently declaring the Strait of Hormuz will remain open. Famous last words, probably.

The data from the Kenya National Bureau of Statistics and EPRA shows our jet fuel consumption is actually *up*. Of course it is. We’re flying more, apparently, trying to escape this mess, but only to find ourselves caught in another one. It’s a vicious cycle, and guess who's stuck in the middle, paying for it all? Us, the passengers.

So, get ready for more excuses and even higher ticket prices. This isn't some temporary hiccup; this is the new normal for us, the ones who just want to get from point A to point B without bankrupting ourselves. The promises of recovery and growth? Seems like they’re only growing when it comes to the costs we have to bear.







Kenya's air ticket prices are poised to soar, joining other East and Southern African nations grappling with a severe jet fuel shortage. The culprit? Our heavy reliance on imported refined products, a vulnerability amplified by escalating tensions in the Middle East that have sent global fuel markets into a tailspin.

According to CITAC, a consultancy specializing in African energy, these geopolitical jitters are directly translating into higher jet fuel prices for our airlines. In response, carriers are already adjusting fares, a predictable outcome for those of us who have to navigate these unpredictable cost increases. It’s another painful reminder that global conflicts have very local consequences, a concept well-covered in [The $20 Trillion Shadow: How Global Elites Are Hiding Your Future in Plain Sight](/posts/the-20-trillion-shadow-how-global-elites-are-hiding-your-future-in-plain-sight).

Compounding the problem is Africa's pathetic refining capacity. Our local plants are mere toddlers compared to their Asian and European counterparts, largely incapable of meeting domestic demand. This leaves the continent exceptionally exposed to supply shocks and price fluctuations, with a staggering 70% of jet fuel and kerosene imports passing through the volatile Strait of Hormuz, according to S&P Global.

African carriers are particularly vulnerable, as jet fuel constitutes a disproportionate 30% to over 40% of their operating expenses, far exceeding the global average of 20% to 25%, as noted by the African Airlines Association. Meanwhile, crude oil futures are flirting with $98 a barrel, driven by potential supply disruptions in Iraq and drone strikes in Kuwait. Even the International Energy Agency's release of emergency reserves can’t seem to bring prices down.

Despite this grim outlook, Kenya's jet fuel consumption actually rose significantly in 2025, reaching 347,180 metric tonnes, and showing a robust 10.8% increase between 2023 and 2024. This surge highlights the growth in air traffic, a recovery that is now directly threatened by these fuel supply woes. The numbers show a steady climb from 506,870 MT in 2021 to 661,100 MT in 2023, demonstrating a clear upward trend in demand that our fragile supply chain can no longer sustain.

The situation is made even more absurd by statements from US President Donald Trump, who has dismissed calls for a ceasefire in Iran, expressing confidence that the Strait of Hormuz will remain open. For those of us on the ground, facing ever-increasing ticket prices and the looming threat of flight disruptions, this confidence feels like a cruel joke. Get ready to pay more for less.
