Invest Kenya Flies to Rome to "Advance" Italian Partnerships, While We Here Count Kuku






So, Invest Kenya has been busy jetting off to Rome, looking all official at this Italy-Kenya Economic Forum. Apparently, they were "advancing strategic partnerships" and all that jazz. They met with over 220 Italian companies, talking about energy, food, innovation, tourism, and manufacturing. This follows that other conference last month that apparently hosted "over 70 Italian investors." One wonders if these numbers translate to actual jobs or just more free flights for some.

They're pushing this "pipeline of bankable opportunities," which sounds fancy, but let's be real, it usually means asking for money with a nice PowerPoint. They're even using the Mattei Plan for Africa and the Kenya-EU Economic Partnership Agreement as leverage. It's all about joint ventures and technology transfer, they say. They expect this to "catalyze new investments" and make Kenya a "regional hub." We’ve heard this tune before, haven’t we? It’s like waiting for the rain in a drought, you hope for the best, but mostly you just get dust.

Italy, bless their hearts, is our 16th largest investor. Nearly 50 Italian companies are already here, dabbling in food, energy, cars, and farming. Invest Kenya’s CEO, John Mwendwa, was in Rome, probably telling them how Kenya is the "preferred gateway" to East and Central Africa. He highlighted our "strategic location" and "improving business environment." Improving, yes, but is it enough to impress someone who can invest anywhere? It makes you wonder if all this talk of industrialization and export-led growth is just more hot air. We've seen how these things go, much like the endless discussions around [Police FC Coach Explains Abud Omar at Center Back - More Games, More Money, Same Old Story](/posts/police-fc-coach-explains-abud-omar-at-center-back---more-games--more-money--same-old-story).

The real gem they’re pushing is the leather industry. Currently worth a measly $80 million (KSh 10 billion), they want it to hit $850 million (KSh 115 billion) by 2040. With "over 80 million livestock," they’ve got the raw material. The plan is to stop exporting raw hides and start making finished goods, like 40 million pairs of shoes annually. Italy, being the leather capital, is the obvious partner. So, we’re going to send them our hides, and they’ll send us back fancy shoes? Sounds like a great deal for them.

Invest Kenya insists they are "promoting and facilitating investments" and providing "aftercare services." They even have a website for more information. Good for them. Meanwhile, back here, we’re still trying to figure out how to make electricity reliable enough for our existing factories, let alone attracting new ones. The promises are grand, the trips are frequent, but the tangible results for the average Kenyan often feel as distant as Rome itself.

The media contact is Njeri Kagwe, in case you want to ask her about the return on investment from these glamorous trips. They also mention using cookies to enhance your browsing experience, which is about as relevant to our economy as a penguin in a Nairobi heatwave. It’s all about looking good on paper, isn’t it?
