So, the matatus and trucks are off the road, and Nairobi looks like a ghost town. Thousands of commuters are left stranded, forced to trek to work because, surprise, surprise, the government decided to jack up fuel prices to record levels. It's the same old story: they raise prices, we suffer. And the "US-Israel conflict with Iran"? Please, spare us the global excuses when the real problem is domestic incompetence and a thirst for more taxes.

The so called "Transport Sector Alliance" is making noise about how this isn't just for them, but for "every Kenyan citizen." Well, they're right about that part. We're all paying for their fuel, and now we're paying for their strike too. Businesses are shut, schools are empty - it’s a masterclass in how to bring a country to its knees. And the police, busy with their tear gas and warnings, are just there to manage the fallout, not prevent it.

The Treasury Minister, John Mbadi, calls the strike "uncalled for" and asks why we're trying to solve a global problem with domestic means. Perhaps he should ask himself why domestic policies are constantly creating global-level problems for us. He admits it’s hurting the economy, but his solutions are as empty as these streets. Meanwhile, the price of everything from your ugali to your bus fare is going up, and the government's token VAT cut feels more like a slap on the wrist than a helping hand.

It’s a predictable mess. Every time the cost of living spikes, and it’s always spiking, it’s the ordinary Kenyan who gets squeezed dry. Remember when Ruto was talking about "stronger" ties? Seems like the only thing getting stronger are the chains around our wallets. This whole situation, the rising prices, the government's inaction, it’s a cycle that’s as reliable as a broken matatu on a rainy day. And nobody, least of all the people making these decisions, seems to care about the outcome.
