So, President Ruto has graced us with his wisdom from a church pulpit, no less, to explain why our fuel prices are doing their own version of climbing Mount Kenya. Apparently, we're a "middle-income country," a label that conveniently allows him to say we can't be compared to our "least developed" neighbors. Because, you know, everyone else being broke makes our own expensive fuel suddenly logical. It's like saying your broken-down car is superior to a bicycle because at least yours has four wheels, even if they're all flat.

He then trots out the old chestnut about fuel levies funding our roads. Twenty thousand kilometers of tarmac, he boasts, plus another six thousand under construction. He even tries to one-up our neighbors by claiming their combined road networks don't match ours. Fascinating. It makes you wonder if these roads are paved with gold, or if the "levies" are just fancy words for more money disappearing into the ether. This whole "development is a long-term investment" spiel sounds suspiciously like a justification for taking us for a ride, pun intended.

Ruto's argument that "higher standards come with increased costs" is, frankly, insulting. We're not asking for caviar and champagne; we're asking to afford basic transportation without selling a kidney. The man talks about transformation and rejecting mediocrity, but all I see is the same old song and dance. It's the same old excuses for the same old problems, just packaged in a slightly more pious language. Remember when we were worried about the [The High-Frequency Hustle: Why You’re Paying $100 for a Thigh Rash](/posts/the-high-frequency-hustle--why-you-re-paying--100-for-a-thigh-rash)? This feels like the national version.

He’s basically saying, "You want decent roads? Fine. But you’ll pay through your nose for the privilege." And who gets to enjoy these magnificent roads? The same people who can afford the fuel to drive on them, I suppose. For the rest of us, it's just another reason to stay put and watch the country transform from the sidelines, while our wallets shrink with every pump refill. It's a real masterclass in managing a beautiful country into a more expensive one.
