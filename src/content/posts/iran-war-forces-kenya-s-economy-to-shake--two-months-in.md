Kenya's Economy Shakes - Thanks, Iran War

Another global mess, and guess who gets to pay for it? Kenya. Two months of fighting in Iran has turned our economic outlook into a joke, with prices shooting up for everything from petrol to the fertiliser we need to grow food. Don't expect any miracles.

The war has messed with shipping routes, leaving our precious tea stuck in Mombasa. Millions of kilograms of it. This isn't just an inconvenience; it's a direct hit to our export earnings and the pockets of our farmers. And just like that, our economic forecast for 2026 has been downgraded by the World Bank. They now predict a pathetic 4.4 percent growth, down from a slightly less pathetic 4.9 percent. What does that mean for you? Fewer jobs and salaries that get eaten alive by inflation.

The real kick in the teeth is that countries like ours, the ones already struggling, are the ones who will feel the economic pain the most. We can't afford to subsidise fuel prices or anything else for long. And to top it all off, borrowing money, which we desperately need, is suddenly way more expensive. The International Energy Agency is calling the loss of global energy supplies due to Iran's attacks the "greatest global energy security threat in history." Honestly, it’s just another excuse for the rich to get richer while we’re left scrambling.

Remember those fuel price reviews? Petrol and diesel prices jumped by nearly Sh20 and Sh30 respectively. That's not some random act of God; that's directly linked to this Middle Eastern drama. Add to that disruptions in remittances from the Gulf, and a volatile shilling that's making our currency look like a joke against the dollar, and you've got a recipe for disaster. Our trade with the Gulf alone is worth Sh700 billion. Now imagine that getting choked. It's like we can't escape these geopolitical shocks. The Treasury is even begging the World Bank for a Sh38.8 billion bailout. Just to cushion us.

And don't even get me started on food. Fertilizer costs have doubled, which means the price of your ugali and sukuma wiki is going to climb even higher. It's a vicious cycle. Even the Nairobi Securities Exchange, which usually just sees tiny gains, has seen its growth slow to a crawl since the war started. We’re talking a 0.5 percent increase versus a 15.3 percent jump earlier in the year, even with a new company listing. It’s a clear sign that investors are getting spooked, and who can blame them? It makes you wonder if there's any point in investing when the world can just decide to mess everything up overnight. It’s almost as predictable as a poorly executed government announcement, like that Kisumu quarry ban.







The fallout from two months of war in Iran has stopped the bear run at the Nairobi bourse, helped shrink Kenya’s foreign currency reserves and ushered in increases in cost of items from petrol to fertilisers and freight.

Disruptions to shipping routes linked to Iran have left millions of kilograms of tea stuck in warehouses in Mombasa, threatening export earnings and farmer incomes. In just eight weeks- less time than it takes to finish a school term- the Kenyan economic outlook has been knocked sideways. The World Bank has downgraded Kenya’s growth forecast to 4.4 percent from 4.9 percent for 2026, weakening the economy’s ability to generate jobs and pay higher salaries as inflation is expected to eat into workers' earnings.

The worst economic pain will be felt in poor countries like Kenya, where consumers cannot afford higher energy prices, and governments cannot afford to provide aid or subsidies for prolonged periods to offset the costs. And as financing tightens, the cost of desperately needed borrowing for these countries increases. The loss of some 20 percent of the world’s energy supplies in the wake of the war, which has seen Iran’s attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure has already been called the “greatest global energy security threat in history” by the International Energy Agency.

In the April-May fuel price review, Kenya raised petrol and diesel prices by Sh19.32 and Sh30.09, respectively, to Sh197.60 and Sh196.63, reflecting the impact of the higher global crude prices. Besides higher pump prices, Kenya is also facing disruptions in remittances from the Middle East, impaired exports and imports to and from the region, and volatility for the shilling and the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE). Kenya carries out trade worth Sh700 billion with the Gulf, while remittances from the region account for about 10 percent of the annual flows of $5.1 billion.

Kenya’s exposure to these global geopolitical shocks has now forced the Treasury to seek emergency funding of $300 million (Sh38.8 billion) from the World Bank to cushion the economy. Food production will be damaged by fertiliser shortages, which will lead to further inflation on costly meals. Fertiliser costs have nearly doubled weeks into the war. In the financial markets, the war has nudged the shilling into increased volatility. In early April, the shilling slipped to the 130 level against the dollar for the first time since August 2024, but it has now regained some ground to trade at Sh129.27 to the greenback.

At the Nairobi bourse, investor wealth has grown 0.5 percent or Sh17 billion since the war began, compared to a growth of Sh453.5 billion or 15.3 billion in the first two months of the year. The slower growth in March and April came despite the listing of Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC) on March 11, which added Sh166 billion in new value to the bourse. It makes you wonder if any of these global dramas, like the Doha Diaries, are ever truly separate from our own struggles.
