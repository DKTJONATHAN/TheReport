West African Designers Eye Nairobi: Another Fashion Circus in Town

Another week, another summit, another bunch of hopefuls trotting into Nairobi with their designer wares. This time it’s West African fashion folks, pretending they’re going to conquer our little East African market. Bless their ambitious hearts. They’re linking up with this ‘Africa Forward 2026’ thing, which sounds like a bad sci-fi movie plot, and hoping we’ll ditch our beloved mitumba for their fancy threads. Good luck with that.

Professor Myriam Sidibé, founder of ‘Brands on a Mission,’ is spouting nonsense about ‘creativity is the new infrastructure’ and how these designers are building ‘new economies on their own terms.’ Please. They’re building collections, and hoping we’ll pay for them. The real economic force here is the guy selling second-hand jeans at Gikomba, not some Senegalese designer who probably thinks Nairobi is a village. They’re coming here because their own markets are saturated, and frankly, they need the cash.

This whole shindig is kicking off with a showcase and then some "creator and industry sessions." Translation: they’ll be schmoozing with local influencers and anyone who can get them a shelf space. They’re hoping to explore "collaboration and distribution opportunities." In other words, they want us to do the heavy lifting while they take the credit and the profits. And let’s not forget, this is happening right before the big France-Africa Summit, where real business will be discussed, not just who’s wearing what.

The numbers don’t lie, though. West Africa’s fashion scene is apparently bigger, fueled by a US$8.8 billion apparel market in Nigeria alone. Meanwhile, our East African market is a whopping US$14 billion, but let’s be honest, 80% of that is our trusty mitumba. They talk about West Africa capturing value in branded fashion, while we're stuck with low-margin resale. So, they’re bringing their designer duds to see if we’ll bite. It’s a classic case of the haves trying to sell to the have-nots, and hoping we haven't noticed the price tags.

They’re relying on these "curated activations" and pop-ups because, surprise, surprise, they have no real plan for sustained wholesale distribution here. It’s all about the flash and the bang, hoping to make a quick sale before packing up and heading to the next city. Meanwhile, the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is supposed to be making things easier, but here we are, still talking about tariff barriers that could cripple some of these designers before they even land. It’s a bit like watching Gor Mahia try to dominate a league they’ve never played in; a lot of hype, little sustained success.

So, while they’re busy setting up their displays and talking about ‘economic and cultural forces,’ remember what’s really going on. It’s a market test. They want to see if Nairobi can absorb their expensive clothes, or if we’ll just keep our heads down, buying what we can afford. It’s a nice thought, West African designers, but don't expect a revolution on our streets just yet. We've got bigger things to worry about, like whether we can afford that new Triumph Scrambler 400 XC.
