Months after Elon Musk's so called Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) imploded, its young, alarmingly inexperienced operatives are not languishing in obscurity. Instead, many have been rewarded with more responsibility, proving that in certain circles, causing chaos is merely a stepping stone to further power. The narrative of "waste, fraud, and abuse" Musk peddled to justify gutting agencies now seems to be the preferred justification for the Trump administration's ongoing purges.

Edward Coristine, who joined DOGE at a tender 19 with no prior government experience and an online handle of "Big Balls," is a prime example. His resume includes alleged attempts to upload sensitive data to unsecured servers at the Social Security Administration and participation in the dismantling of the US Agency for International Development (USAID). Now, he’s the engineering lead at the National Design Studio, conveniently helping right wing influencers target organizations for viral takedowns. Apparently, "efficiency" means making it easier to score political points.

Then there's Gavin Kliger, another early DOGE recruit. After stints undermining agencies like USAID and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, he's landed as the chief data officer for the Department of Defense. His involvement in the Pentagon's squabbles with AI companies like Anthropic, as revealed in leaked emails, suggests a continued pattern of navigating complex situations with questionable outcomes. The Pentagon's stoic response that its "DOGE team" continues to execute priorities is a masterclass in bureaucratic obfuscation, confirming that the wheels of government turn, albeit perhaps greased by incompetence.

And let's not forget Jeremy Lewin, the former lawyer and investment banker who was instrumental in effectively shuttering USAID. The tangible consequences of his "efficiency" - the halt of life saving treatments for diseases like tuberculosis and the slashing of food aid for refugees - are conveniently overlooked. These aren't minor inconveniences; they are human tragedies stemming from ideological crusades disguised as fiscal prudence.

The dispersal of these DOGE members isn't a victory for government efficiency; it's a testament to the enduring power of connections and the cyclical nature of bureaucratic upheaval. Whether they remain in government, cozying up to Silicon Valley giants, or move to the private sector, their influence persists, a stark reminder that the most disruptive forces often end up dictating the very systems they claim to fix. The damage they've inflicted is not merely statistical; it's etched in the diminished capacity of essential services and the erosion of public trust.
