Kenya's Parliament is currently "weighing" the diplomatic immunity of the Ford Foundation, and if you're expecting a transparent, principled debate, you haven't been paying attention. In Nairobi, when politicians "weigh" something, it usually means they are calculating the angles, the leverage, and what's in it for them or their benefactors. This isn't about fostering independent work; it's about control, plain and simple, dressed up in official jargon.

This latest deliberation comes as no surprise, given the larger tug-of-war for influence playing out across the country. Parliament just rubber-stamped a raft of new security partnerships, effectively placing Kenya squarely in the middle of Washington and Beijing's geopolitical boxing match. It's a classic move: invite the global heavyweights, then pretend to be surprised when their struggles for power spill over. Meanwhile, while the country grapples with genuine security threats and criminal enterprises, like when [Oyugis Police nabbed a phone hacker syndicate member in Homa Bay](/posts/oyugis-police-nab-phone-hacker-syndicate-member-in-homa-bay-bust), our leaders are busy playing chess with international organizations.

Take the curious case of the Global Centre on Adaptation. Despite supposedly having government backing, this research facility is on the verge of losing its diplomatic status, all thanks to "controversies" and the inconvenient truth that its leader is cosy with the head of state. It's a clear demonstration of how quickly "backing" can disappear when political winds shift or someone falls out of favour. Diplomatic status, it seems, is a privilege, not a right, and it can be revoked as easily as a weekend pass.

And while these internal battles rage, our diplomats are busy on the international stage, schmoozing with the French to promote a Nairobi communiqué to G7 states and planning the "Africa Forward" event. Paris, in its infinite wisdom, is even thinking of inviting non-African partners-because what's an "Africa Forward" discussion without more external voices telling us what's what? It's the usual song and dance, showcasing Kenya on the global stage while behind the scenes, every piece of legislation, every immunity, every partnership, is another pawn in a never-ending game of power and patronage.
