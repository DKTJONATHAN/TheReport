In the eyes of a multi-billion dollar sports franchise, a hungry human being is not a neighbor but a potential legal liability. Baker had to operate in the shadows of the Interstate-69 underpass to perform a basic act of humanity because the organization feared a lawsuit more than it feared the moral stain of wasting high-quality food. This is the global corporate standard. Whether it is a tech giant in Silicon Valley or a baseball team in Texas, the protocol remains the same: it is better to fill a dumpster than to risk a courtroom.

This systemic cruelty has international ripples. As MLB aggressively exports its brand to markets like Japan with the signing of Imai, it exports a specific brand of American wastefulness. The "official philanthropic projects" mentioned by the team are nothing more than tax-deductible PR stunts designed to look good on a social media feed. They are controlled, sanitized, and ultimately meaningless when compared to the thousands of unhoused people living in the literal shadow of the stadium.

The irony of the Astros' history is thick and bitter. A team that counts former homeless players like JR Richard and Evan Gattis among its alumni should, in theory, possess a shred of institutional empathy. Instead, the organization prioritizes a "winter reset" focused on player salaries while ignoring the surplus population created by the very economic systems that allow a baseball team to worth billions. It is a cycle of exploitation where the struggle of the poor is only valuable when it can be sold as a "comeback story."

Baker’s decision to finally speak out at age 80 reflects a growing fatigue with the "politically correct" gag orders that define professional sports. His focus on Jackie Robinson in the book serves as a grim reminder of how social justice is often commodified by leagues to sell jerseys while the actual, messy work of helping the marginalized is treated as a breach of contract. Robinson’s legacy has been polished for the Dodgers' brand, but the grit of his struggle is inconvenient for modern owners.

Ultimately, the Astros are just another cog in a global engine that views human life as an externality. They will likely find their way back to the postseason, and the fans will cheer for Correa and Imai, oblivious to the fact that the calories required to fuel those athletes are being guarded by lawyers and security guards. The lesson is clear: in the world of elite sports, you are either a valuable asset or a nuisance to be managed.
