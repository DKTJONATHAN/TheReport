Ruto Urges Land Preservation, Launches Projects - Because Who Needs Farmland When You Have Houses?

The President's grand pronouncements on protecting farmland by building houses in rural areas are as predictable as a rainy Tuesday in Nairobi. While he's busy doling out keys and laying foundations, let's talk about the fine print.

President Ruto, bless his cotton socks, has discovered that people build houses on land that was once for farming. Shocking, I know. So, his brilliant solution? Build *more* houses, but call them "affordable," and *then* people won't build on farmland. It’s a masterclass in circular logic, really. He’s busy playing Santa Claus with development projects in Migori and Homa Bay, throwing money at markets and hostels, as if that’s going to stop the inevitable urban sprawl or solve the real issues driving land subdivision.

Then there's the whole "education is the greatest equalizer" spiel. He’s wagging his finger at parents for not sending their kids to school, especially in the arid regions where children are supposedly herding livestock instead of soaking up wisdom. Apparently, the Sh700 billion the government throws at education annually isn't quite enough to make school attractive enough on its own. And let’s not forget the SHA, which has supposedly paid out a hefty sum to Migori hospitals. Let's see how long that lasts before the real bills come due.

The President also took time to condemn ethnic politics, urging unity and development. Coming from a man who often plays the divisive tune himself, it’s rich. While he’s busy inaugurating transmission lines and inspecting hospitals, back here in Nairobi, things are… well, they're always something. We've had DCI accusing Tuju of deception, and some court ruling that basically says spilling tea on someone is now legal. Honestly, it’s a bit like watching [The Comedy of Errors: Why the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan) but with more actual legal drama and less actual comedy.

So, while Ruto is off cutting ribbons and making grand pronouncements about food security and education, the reality on the ground is a bit murkier. The affordable housing might keep a few people off the farms for now, but it’s just another band-aid on a gaping wound. And the promise of universal healthcare and better electricity? We’ve heard it all before. Let’s just hope the Sh345 million market and Sh200 million market don’t end up being white elephants by next year.
