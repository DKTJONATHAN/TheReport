So, Chad's decided to jump into the Haiti dumpster fire, pledging 800 of their finest police and gendarmes to a so called "international force." Apparently, these guys are getting some fancy training from Europeans and Americans. It’s all very hush-hush, of course, because transparency is for suckers. Meanwhile, the Dominican Republic's foreign minister is gleefully announcing that Kenya’s boys, who were supposed to be the big heroes, should start packing their bags. Nice to see how these "partnerships" really work out.

The whole Haiti operation is shaping up to be a real clown show. They're talking about a "Gang Suppression Force" that’s supposed to reach 5,500 by October. But here’s the kicker: the State Department is denying Chad's troops are training on American soil, even though the Dominican Republic guy claims they are. Classic diplomatic tango, where nobody is really telling the whole truth, and everyone's got their own agenda. Just like back home when we hear about police funds disappearing into thin air, like in [The Blue-Uniformed Billionaires: Police SACCO’s Sh66 Billion Mirage](/posts/the-blue-uniformed-billionaires--police-sacco-s-sh66-billion-mirage).

Remember that Kenyan-led Multinational Security Support mission? Yeah, that was supposed to be the big solution. But since it got approved last September, not much has actually happened. It’s basically just a bunch of Kenyan police and a few token forces from smaller countries. Now Chad's stepping in, following similar pledges from Benin and Bangladesh. It’s like a revolving door of countries willing to send their people into a situation that looks like it's already gone pear-shaped.

What’s really going on is anyone's guess. Is this a genuine effort to bring order, or just another geopolitical chess game where nations are vying for influence and the Haitians are stuck in the middle? The promises are flying, but the reality on the ground is usually a lot grimmer. People are desperate, gangs are powerful, and these international forces often end up being a band-aid on a bullet wound, or worse, an open invitation for more corruption.

Honestly, I wouldn't hold my breath for a quick fix in Haiti. It's a complicated mess, and these deployments are often more about optics than effective solutions. We’ll see how long Chad’s enthusiasm lasts, and what happens when the cameras turn off. It’s the same old story, different country.
