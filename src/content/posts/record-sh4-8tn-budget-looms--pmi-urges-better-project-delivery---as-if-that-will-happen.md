So, the big boys in project management are out here, flapping their gums about stronger execution for this Sh4.8 trillion budget. Record breaking, they say. Like that's supposed to impress anyone. Billions being tossed around for infrastructure, housing, the whole shebang. You know, the usual song and dance to keep us all dreaming while the shillings disappear into thin air.

Maureen Mbithi Ochang, bless her heart, points out Kenya doesn't lack vision. Oh no, we've got Vision 2030, Bottom-Up, digital blueprints, climate commitments - a whole library of fancy words. The "real question," she asks, is "How do we execute better?" Darling, the real question has always been "Who is actually doing the executing, and are they lining their pockets more than building anything?" It's the same old story, just with a bigger cheque.

And let's not forget the International Monetary Fund, the official killjoys, dropping the bombshell that nearly half of government projects are stalled. Sh1 trillion needed to finish them. That's not a typo. Imagine that kind of money actually seeing the light of day for its intended purpose. It’s like asking a fox to guard the henhouse, then being surprised when there are no hens left. This constant cycle of grand plans and stalled projects feels like a perpetual arms race of digital vanity, where we chase the next shiny object while the foundations crumble.

George Asamani from PMI chimes in that governments are judged by results, not promises. Citizens are watching. Well, they’re watching alright. They're watching the potholes get deeper and the prices get higher. They see institutions that can’t translate "national priorities" into anything visible in their daily lives. It's almost as if the more complex the programmes get, the easier it is to hide the bungling.

PMI is projecting a 56 to 75 percent surge in demand for skilled project professionals by 2035. So, more people to manage projects that probably won't get finished. They're calling for more investment in government project management capacity. You know, to "improve implementation, reduce delays and maximise returns." It’s like bringing in more accountants to a company that's already haemorrhaging money. The only thing that will be maximised is the number of meetings.

At the end of the day, it's a lot of noise about a lot of money. They’ll talk about strategy, complexity, and capacity until they're blue in the face. But until the fundamental issues of accountability and genuine commitment to public service are addressed, this Sh4.8 trillion will just be another headline, another budget, and another pile of unfinished business.
