AFRICAN COMMISSION SHOUTS INTO THE VOID: PROTECT KENYA'S DIGITAL FREEDOM AND RIGHT TO PROTEST - ARTICLE 19 STILL WAITS

Don't get your hopes up. Another day, another continental body making noise about how things ought to be, while back home, the same old song and dance plays out. ARTICLE 19 Eastern Africa trotted out their usual spiel at some African Commission meeting, flapping their gums about freedom of expression and the right to assemble. They even brought up Kenya's periodic report, as if that piece of paper actually means anything to the folks in charge here.

They acknowledged Kenya's "milestones" - probably referring to the fancy new digital highways they've built so they can watch us better. But then they got to the real dirt: the police brutality, the arbitrary arrests, the way they shoot first and ask questions later when people dare to gather. It’s the same story we’ve heard a million times, whether it's about protests or the utter failure to protect anyone, like those poor girls in [Kenya's Dangerous Denial: How Government Officials Are Concealing Child Sex Trafficking Exposed by BBC Investigation](/posts/BBC-Kenya-Child-Sex-Trafficking-Scandle). They want the Commission to hold Kenya accountable. Good luck with that.

Then there's the digital front. Oh yes, Kenya's got all the tech. But it's not for our benefit, is it? It's for targeted surveillance, for threatening to throttle the internet when inconvenient truths start popping up, and for twisting those cybercrime laws to silence anyone who dares to speak out or investigate. Journalists, bloggers, human rights defenders - they're all fair game for intimidation. It's a constant battle, much like trying to get a straight answer about why our sports teams, like the [Kisumu All Starlets: Broke and Busted, While Police Bullets Take the Top Spot](/posts/kisumu-all-starlets--broke-and-busted--while-police-bullets-take-the-top-spot), are neglected while the powerful focus on other things.

And let's not forget the press. The fearless few who actually try to report on what’s going on get assaulted, harassed, and dragged through the courts. It’s a culture of impunity, pure and simple. ARTICLE 19 is calling for independent mechanisms to investigate police misconduct and to fix those computer misuse laws. Noble sentiments, I'm sure, but don't hold your breath waiting for them to be implemented. This is Kenya.
