So, President Ruto, bless his heart, decided to grace us with another social media spectacle this week. He's furious, apparently, with Standard Group and their owner, Gideon Moi, for daring to print headlines that aren't exactly fawning over his administration. Calls it "extortionist propaganda," "blackmail," and tells them to "do your worst!" Like we haven't seen this movie before, with a different lead actor, perhaps.

His complaint? Five days a week of "critical headlines and negative coverage." Imagine that - a media house actually scrutinizing government policies, public spending, and those ever-so-transparent graft allegations. It's almost as if it's their job. The Standard, bless their brave little hearts, has been pointing out things like the President's endless foreign trips and those little fibs about [Ruto Signs Finance Bill Into Law, Declares 'No New Taxes' - Tell That To Your Wallet](/posts/ruto-signs-finance-bill-into-law--declares--no-new-taxes----tell-that-to-your-wallet). They even have a whole 'Gallery of Ruto's Lies' segment. Someone's clearly hit a nerve.

Of course, the internet went wild. "Unpresidential," "compromised account," "bad grammar!" As if a President suddenly forgetting how to punctuate is the biggest problem we're facing. The actual message, though, was clear: 'Don't you dare question my divine right to rule, or I'll throw a public tantrum.' It’s less about a compromised account and more about a compromised ego, if you ask me.

This whole circus is just another round in the never-ending battle between government and media, where "press freedom" often gets trampled underfoot by presidential decrees and public scoldings. Ruto says Kenya belongs to all citizens, not just Moi. Funny, because sometimes it feels like it belongs only to the select few who get to decide what's "transformative" and what's "extortion." What a joke.
