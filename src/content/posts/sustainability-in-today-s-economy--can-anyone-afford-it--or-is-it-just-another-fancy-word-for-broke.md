So, they’re out here talking about "sustainability." Apparently, it means thinking about the future, not just grabbing what we can today. It's about using resources without wrecking them for the next generation. In our personal lives, this supposedly means picking products with a "positive environmental and social impact." Right. Like those reusable shopping bags we all have stuffed in drawers somewhere, only to buy another flimsy plastic one at Sarit Centre because we forgot. Great. Another thing to remember, another way to feel guilty.

Let's be real. Everything you buy, from that tiny sachet of sugar to that imported bottle of water, has a whole drama behind it. Raw materials, workers who are probably underpaid, trucks burning fuel. Sustainable options? They’re usually the ones with the hefty price tag. The ones that sit on the shelves while we eye the cheaper, more questionable alternatives. It’s easy for them to preach when they can afford the "eco-friendly" option without blinking.

But fine, they do throw us a bone. They say it doesn't *have* to cost an arm and a leg. Go to your local mama mboga. Support the neighbourhood. The produce is often fresher, and yes, cheaper than those sterile supermarkets. Less transport, less packaging - all that jazz. Plus, you're actually putting money into your own community. It’s the least we can do when the fuel prices are going up faster than you can say "transport fares." Remember that time fuel prices shot up and then food prices followed like clockwork? Yeah, that’s the "economy" they want us to be "sustainable" in.

And AI? Apparently, it’s a big energy hog. So, while we’re trying to save electricity by not leaving lights on, the geeks are busy powering data centers that are probably melting the planet. Makes sense. The practical stuff, though: turn off lights, unplug chargers. It might shave a few shillings off your electricity bill, which is more than you can say for worrying about carbon markets. And fashion? They want you to declutter your wardrobe. That dress from 2018 that doesn’t fit anymore? Donate it. Avoid fast fashion. Invest in "versatile, high-quality pieces." For whom? Most of us are just trying to get through the week without wearing the same two shirts.

Honestly, this whole "sustainability" thing feels like another trend. They’ll push it for a bit, then move on to the next buzzword. The truth is, when your rent is due and the price of sukuma wiki keeps climbing, you’re not thinking about the long-term impact on the polar bears. You’re thinking about surviving today. Maybe, just maybe, if they made sustainable options affordable and accessible, like that U.S. experimental Ebola treatment amidst Kenya quarantine resistance, we might actually pay attention. But until then, we’ll just keep reusing those shopping bags... sometimes.
