MT KENYA WORKERS URGED TO PRIORITISE SAVINGS AS ONLY 18% ACHIEVE FINANCIAL HEALTH DESPITE HIGH FINANCIAL INCLUSION

FINANCIAL WELLNESS IS A LUXURY MOST KENYANS CAN'T AFFORD, DESPITE ACCESS TO SERVICES.

Mt Kenya workers are being told to get their financial act together, but let's be real, when is that ever going to happen?

So, the big brains at Octagon Africa and wherever else these "experts" hang out are shouting from the rooftops about financial wellness, retirement readiness, and literacy. Apparently, these aren't optional anymore, but "essential life skills." Easy for them to say, sitting in their air-conditioned offices, probably not worrying about where the next bus fare is coming from. The FinAccess survey, bless its heart, tells us 84.8% of us have access to formal financial services. That's great. It means we can open accounts, get loans we can't afford, and maybe even have a mobile money wallet. But the kicker? Only 18.3% are actually financially healthy. That's less than one in five. The rest of us are just… surviving, I guess.

And guess who's barely scraping by even in the "prosperous" Mt Kenya region? The survey says their counties are only "slightly above the national average." Kiambu leads the pack at a measly 26.7%. That means even in the best-performing county, nearly three-quarters of people aren't financially healthy. It’s the same old story: access to money services doesn't magically translate into actual money in your pocket or a secure future. We can sign up for a thousand apps, but if the income isn't there, or it's all going towards paying off expensive credit, what's the point? It's like handing someone a fancy recipe book and a broken oven.

The "advice" from these forums? "Practical financial discipline." Oh, you mean like consistent saving? Emergency funds for six months? Reducing reliance on expensive credit? As if anyone earning minimum wage can just stash away a cool six months' worth of expenses. And don't get me started on pension schemes. The coverage has nudged up to 20.4%, but let's be honest, that's still a tiny fraction. Most people are too busy trying to make ends meet today to worry about a retirement that feels like it's a century away. It’s a bit like that time the Kenya Railways announced new fares, and we all knew our pockets were already crying before we even stepped on the train. [/posts/kenya-railways-new-fares--nyahururu-train-back--your-pocket-already-crying]

The experts are babbling about investment opportunities in pension schemes and capital markets. Great. So, while the rest of us are struggling to get by, we should apparently be playing the stock market? They keep talking about "long-term compounding," which sounds suspiciously like another way for the rich to get richer while the rest of us are left holding the bag. This whole "financial resilience" talk feels like a nice slogan, but for most Kenyans, it’s just another unattainable dream. Perhaps if the cost of living wasn't so insane, and wages actually kept up with inflation, we might have a shot. Until then, we'll keep accessing financial services and hoping for the best. It’s a tough market out there, and you have to wonder if it's all just a sophisticated way of saying "you're on your own."
