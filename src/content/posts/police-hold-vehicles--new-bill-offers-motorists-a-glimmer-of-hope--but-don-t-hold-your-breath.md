So, the politicians are at it again, pretending to care about the common mwananchi. This time it's a new bill, the Traffic (Amendment) Bill of 2026, apparently designed to stop our beloved police from holding onto your precious whip for an eternity. They say it’s about balancing law enforcement with private property. We’ll see.

Apparently, your car or motorbike can only be confiscated if it's "directly connected" to a crime. Big news, right? And get this, if it’s just evidence and a photo will do, they can only keep it for 48 hours. For investigations, it’s a generous 14 days. This is meant to stop "prolonged impoundments." Sounds lovely on paper, doesn't it? Like those promises we hear every election cycle. Remember how electronics get "refurbished" and sold as new in Luthuli Avenue? This feels like that same old song and dance. [The 'Second-Hand' Scam: How Refurbished Electronics are Being Sold as New in Luthuli Avenue](/posts/the-second-hand-scam-how-refurbished-electronics-are-being-sold-as-new-in-luthuli-avenue)

And if your vehicle *does* get impounded, the officer in charge is supposed to make sure it’s preserved. Because we all know how well things are preserved in police custody. Reports of damage and vandalism are frequent. This bill is supposed to offer "added protection." Let's just say I’m not holding my breath for pristine vehicles coming out of police garages.

The kicker is, this doesn’t actually take away the courts' power. They can still make whatever orders they deem fit. So, while this bill might limit the police's *own* arbitrary detention periods, the judiciary can still prolong your misery if they feel like it. It’s like putting a small bandage on a gaping wound.

They also admit this "limits the right to property under Article 40 of the Constitution to the extent that a police officer may detain a vehicle or motorcycle for purposes of investigation." So, your property rights are still up for grabs when the boys in blue decide they need to "investigate." It’s a "relief" for some, I suppose, a slightly less suffocating form of the same old system.

If this law actually gets passed and, more importantly, *enforced* without the usual bribes and loopholes, then maybe, just maybe, some motorists will see a change. But in Nairobi, we’ve learned not to get too excited by sweet talk from politicians. We wait, we watch, and we prepare for the inevitable reality.
