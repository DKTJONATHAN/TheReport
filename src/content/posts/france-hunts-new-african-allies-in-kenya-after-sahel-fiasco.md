France Flounders in Africa: Now Begging for New Pals in Kenya

This Africa Summit in Kenya? Just France's pathetic attempt to patch up a sinking ship. After getting kicked out of the Sahel, they're scrambling for new friends, hoping we Kenyans will forget their colonial baggage and pretend this is a fresh start. Don't be fooled.

The "Africa Forward Summit" is all about optics. Macron wants to look like he's still relevant, desperately trying to pivot from the West African disaster zone where, surprise surprise, people are tired of being told what to do. Ulf Laessing, bless his analytical heart, spells it out: France lost its mojo in Francophone Africa and is now sniffing around English speaking countries like Kenya, South Africa, and Nigeria, hoping we're less cynical. We are, apparently.

They're peddling sweet talk about security, investment, and green energy, as if that's going to erase decades of interference and the lingering stench of neocolonialism. And Ruto, our own president, is playing along, talking about a fairer global financial system while France pledges support. Let's see how much of that pledge actually materializes, especially when they're competing with China, India, and the Gulf states for scraps. It's a global game, and we're just the latest piece on the board for them. Remember how we’re all still paying for those Chinese rail debts? [Kenya, Uganda Double Down on Chinese Rail Debt, We're All Paying](/posts/kenya--uganda-double-down-on-chinese-rail-debt--we-re-all-paying).

The truth is, France is losing influence everywhere. Young Africans in places like Mali don't have the same "connection" to France; they've seen the manipulation and felt the disappointment. The military regimes in the Sahel are capitalizing on that anti-French sentiment, and even the stubborn CFA franc is a constant reminder of the past. France is trying to reposition itself as a partner, but their history is a heavy anchor.

Yves Ekoué Amaïzo, another analyst, points out France is shifting focus from raw materials to infrastructure and digital economy. Good for them. But they'll be fighting tooth and nail for these opportunities, facing stiff competition. France is already doing some business here, a fifth largest investor, supposedly supporting 46,000 jobs. But let's be real, it's a drop in the ocean compared to the real challenges we face.

Kenya, meanwhile, is using this summit to shout its own aspirations to the world stage. We want to shape our own destiny, not just be a stepping stone for France's geopolitical anxieties. It's a calculated move by Kenya to forge stronger alliances, and France, seeing its traditional markets in turmoil, is looking for any port in a storm. They think Kenya, with its EAC ties to Francophone countries like DRC, is a convenient entry point. We'll see how convenient it turns out to be for them, and more importantly, for us.







France's humiliating withdrawal from the Sahel has them clutching at straws, and this "Africa Forward Summit" in Kenya is their latest desperate grab for relevance. They're hoping to charm us Anglophone Africans into forgetting their colonial past and the messy fallout in West Africa, where their prestige has taken a nosedive. Don't expect any miracles; this is just damage control dressed up as diplomacy.

The whole event at the University of Nairobi is meant to signal a "fresh start," according to the talking heads. But what it really signals is France's strategic retreat from Francophone Africa, where anti-French sentiment is as thick as the Nairobi traffic. Analysts like Ulf Laessing openly admit France is trying to "offset the loss of influence" by pivoting to countries like Kenya, South Africa, and Nigeria. They're looking for new playgrounds, and we're the current attraction.

Macron and Ruto will be front and center, parading their "new partnership" focused on security, investment, and green energy. It’s a nice story, but let's not forget the history. France lost favor because of perceived neocolonial policies and interference, especially in the Sahel. Now, they're hoping a shiny new summit in East Africa will erase all that. It's a bold move, but history suggests it won't be an easy sell. We've seen this song and dance before, and the tune rarely changes in our favor. It reminds me of the perpetual debt cycle, like the one we're still entangled in with China. [Kenya, Uganda Double Down on Chinese Rail Debt, We're All Paying](/posts/kenya--uganda-double-down-on-chinese-rail-debt--we-re-all-paying).

The summit aims to showcase France's shift towards more diversified, economically oriented relationships, moving away from resource extraction towards infrastructure and digital economies. This sounds promising, but France will be up against some serious competition. China, India, the UAE, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia are all vying for influence and investment opportunities across Africa. France is just another player in a crowded market, and they'll have to fight for every inch.

Kenya, for its part, is using this summit to project itself as a global player, a gateway to Africa. President Ruto is talking big about Africa shaping its own destiny, and while that’s a noble sentiment, it’s happening on France's terms, at their summit. France, meanwhile, sees Kenya as a strategic hub and a less hostile entry point into Anglophone Africa. It's a mutually beneficial arrangement, sure, but let's not get too sentimental about it.

Ultimately, this summit is a testament to the shifting global landscape. With crises in the Middle East and Ukraine putting pressure on traditional markets, established powers like France are forced to realign their strategies. They’re looking towards emerging economies like ours, hoping to find new opportunities. We'll see if this "fresh start" actually leads to genuine partnerships or just another chapter in the same old story of foreign influence.
