Canadian firms are already folding under the pressure of reality. Expectations for inflation have jumped significantly, driven by the inescapable costs of fuel, freight, and fertilizer. This is the brutal reality of a world where energy security is a myth sold to the public by short-sighted politicians. The central bank claims it will look through the immediate oil price shock, but looking through a problem is just a polite way of saying they have no remaining tools to fix it. A borrowing cost of 2.25 percent is a toothpick trying to hold back a tidal wave of rising global input costs.

The human cost of this economic friction is manifest in the sudden retreat of the consumer. Nearly a third of households are already slashing their spending, canceling travel, and hoarding what remains of their purchasing power. This is the death rattle of the post-pandemic boom. The facade of a resilient domestic economy crumbles the moment supply chains in the Middle East are disrupted, proving that national sovereignty is an illusion when your grocery prices are dictated by overseas artillery and regional power struggles.

Adding insult to injury, the workforce is now forced to stare down the double-barrel threat of geopolitical instability and technological replacement. Workers already expect artificial intelligence to cannibalize their hours and prospects, even as the cost of basic survival climbs. In a world where war inflates the cost of living and software deflates the value of labor, the average citizen is caught in a pincer movement. The optimism seen in early February was a fluke, a brief moment of ignorance before the reality of a permanent global crisis reasserted itself.
