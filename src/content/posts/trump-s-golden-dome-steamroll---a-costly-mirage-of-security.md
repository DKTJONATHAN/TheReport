The Pentagon is apparently full steam ahead on Donald Trump's "Golden Dome," an initiative to create a space-based missile defense network. Two companies, satellite startup Impulse Space and defense tech firm Anduril, have reportedly been selected to develop prototypes for the core technology-space-based missile tracking and targeting. This grand plan, announced by Trump upon taking office in January 2025, aims to detect and neutralize aerial attacks on American soil. Curiously, this is a threat that hasn't materialized since World War II, making its urgency questionable at best.

The timeline for this project is as nebulous as its feasibility. While a Congressional Budget Office report from May 2025 suggested it could take two decades to build, a CNN report in August 2025 claimed a major test might occur just before the 2028 presidential election. The recent contract awards offer a rare peek into the administration's progression, but the foundational technology itself-space-based interceptors capable of finding and destroying incoming missiles and drones-does not yet exist. In fact, it's widely considered theoretically ineffective and impractical, with considerable doubt cast on its viability.

The Space Force has been conducting market research on these capabilities since last summer, with Bloomberg reporting that Impulse Space, a company founded by a SpaceX veteran, will subcontract to Anduril. This isn't the first time such a grand vision has been proposed; similar "moonshot" missile defense plans have been floated since Ronald Reagan's era, none achieving their promised invincibility. Critics decry the Golden Dome as scientifically impossible and astronomically expensive, with one study projecting costs as high as $3.6 trillion by 2045.

Beyond the financial drain, the plan is also seen as destabilizing. The Brookings Institution has warned that deploying space-based interceptors could provoke a new arms race with China and Russia, ultimately making the world less secure. The Golden Dome, therefore, appears to be a classic case of a high-profile, potentially ruinously expensive, and strategically dubious project driven by political ambition rather than sound defense policy. The steam is on, but whether it's powering progress or just a runaway train towards fiscal and geopolitical folly remains to be seen.
