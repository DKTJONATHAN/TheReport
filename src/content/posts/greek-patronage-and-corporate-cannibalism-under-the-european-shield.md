While the political class bickers over who gets to return to the ministerial table, the corporate landscape is being stripped for parts. The rebranding of the Athens Exchange to Euronext Athens is not a sign of progress but a surrender. Local brokerage firms are being handed a death sentence via technological costs and regulatory fees. They must merge or vanish, clearing the field for the global giants to harvest what remains of the Greek economy. It is the same story from Milan to Amsterdam; the homogenization of capital markets ensures that the small player is always the first one sacrificed to the digital altar of efficiency.

Even the so-called digital cut-off for children is a classic piece of populist theater. By leaning on artificial intelligence to police the age of users, the state is simply outsourcing its parental and ethical failures to the very tech giants it claims to regulate. This move has cross-party appeal only because it provides a convenient distraction from the OPEKEPE scandal and the soaring cost of living. The elite tell the public that everything has been more expensive since 1985, as if inflation were a natural weather pattern rather than a direct result of their own fiscal mismanagement and currency debasement.

The defense sector provides the final, darkest chapter. Hellenic Defence Systems is seeking investors just as the gears of the regional war machine begin to grind faster. In a world of unstable geopolitical environments, the restructuring of arms manufacturers is never about peace. It is about positioning for the inevitable profit that comes from shell and cartridge production. The global realist knows that when the defence industry is in the spotlight, the human cost is already being calculated in the backrooms of the boardroom.

This cycle of immunity, consolidation, and distraction is not unique to Athens. It is the blueprint for the modern state.
