So, Kenya Airways. Our beloved national carrier. Remember that brief flicker of hope last year, that tiny profit? Forget it. March 2026 rolls around, and BAM! KSh17.1-17.2 billion in net losses for 2025. Back to the familiar embrace of red ink, just in time for the government to start sweating about their KSh146 billion debt stake. Apparently, currency gains and "operational efficiency" don't pay for broken engines and supply chain hiccups. It’s like expecting a leaking bucket to hold water just because you’ve painted it a nice colour.

The core of this predictable disaster? Three Boeing 787 Dreamliners are grounded, coughing and sputtering instead of earning their keep. Fewer planes mean fewer flights, especially on those lucrative long-haul routes. And surprise, surprise, revenue takes a nosedive, down 14 percent. But the costs? Oh, they’re still there, stubbornly clinging on like a bad habit. Finance costs alone jumped to KSh12.3 billion. So you’ve got fewer people paying, more money going out, and planes sitting idle. Sounds like a solid business plan, right? It's almost as baffling as watching [wargames for the elites and empty pockets for the rest](/posts/wargames-for-the-elites-and-empty-pockets-for-the-rest) while people struggle to make rent.

Now, here's the kicker: the planes that *are* flying are practically bursting at the seams, with load factors hitting 99 percent on some routes. Apparently, due to global aviation chaos and Nairobi's "strategic" hub status, transit passengers are flocking. So, if the planes are full, why the massive loss? Because a full plane on a reduced schedule doesn't magically create more planes. It’s a classic case of showing off your fancy, nearly full, but very limited wardrobe while your bills pile up. Capacity, my friends. It’s not just about how many people fit in the seat, it’s about how many seats you actually have to sell.

And what's the brilliant new strategy? Forget one big investor. They're now looking for a whole committee of money people, each with their own little stake, debt, or revenue share. Sounds like a recipe for endless meetings and conflicting agendas. All this while the government, who already owns nearly half and is owed most of the debt, ponders turning that debt into more equity. More state control, more "policy considerations" masquerading as business decisions. It’s a delicate dance between pretending to be a commercial enterprise and begging for taxpayer bailouts.

At least they're trying to patch things up with partnerships, like with CemAir and JetBlue, to extend their reach without buying more metal. Plus, they're scrambling to get those grounded planes back in the air before the peak season - because, you know, flying is what airlines are supposed to do. Boardroom reshuffles are also happening, because when things go south, someone always has to lose their plush office. It's all part of the grand Kenyan tradition of rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic.

Ultimately, Kenya Airways is wrestling with the same old ghosts: how to be a national symbol *and* a profitable business. They're trying to juggle operational recovery, a financial overhaul, and the ever-present burden of being a government pet project. The next phase won't be about whether they’re making money, but whether they can finally cobble together a business model that doesn't constantly rely on the public purse. My money's on the public purse taking another hit.
