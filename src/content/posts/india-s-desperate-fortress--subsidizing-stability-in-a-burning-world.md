Internationally, this move signals the definitive end of the free-market fantasy. When a major emerging economy shifts toward state-backed insulation, it forces a domino effect. Global supply chains, already strained by geopolitical friction, are now being carved into subsidized silos. India is effectively telling the world that it can no longer rely on market forces to dictate survival, opting instead for a managed decline of risk through government intervention.

The proposed "trade cost management" is a polite euphemism for state interference in a failing global shipping market. By shielding its small businesses from the reality of a fractured West Asia, India risks creating a zombie economy. These MSMEs will survive on the taxpayer's dime while their competitors elsewhere face the raw, brutal reality of a de-dollarizing world. It is a temporary fix for a permanent structural shift that the current global order is ill-equipped to handle.

As noted in "The Golden Graveyard: How De-Dollarization is Burying the Old World Order," the old rules of engagement are being buried under the rubble of regional conflicts. India's latest maneuvers are merely a frantic attempt to avoid the funeral. By prioritizing internal stability over global integration, the world moves one step closer to a fragmented, hostile landscape where every nation is an island, and state-backed credit is the only remaining currency of hope.
