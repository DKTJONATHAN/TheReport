Our government has finally found a way to solve the unemployment crisis: just ship the youth off to a war zone and hope they send back enough dollars to keep the local economy breathing. According to the Principal Secretary for Diaspora Affairs, Roseline Njogu, we have over 500,000 Kenyans scattered across the Middle East. They are not there for the scenery; they are there because Nairobi has nothing to offer them but taxes and empty promises. Now that Iran and Israel are exchanging strikes, these "labor exports" have become collateral damage in a regional mess that they have absolutely no stake in.

It is a beautiful deal for the elites in the leafy suburbs. While [THE DEBT TRAP’S NEW TEETH: HOW TARIFFS ARE STRANGLING THE DOLLAR’S DOMINANCE](/posts/the-debt-trap-s-new-teeth--how-tariffs-are-strangling-the-dollar-s-dominance) keeps the National Treasury in a cold sweat, they rely on the KSh 650 billion sent home by the diaspora to keep the Kenyan shilling from doing a total face-plant. We have turned our sisters and brothers into human ATMs. We do not care about the "high-risk areas" or the military facilities nearby, as long as the money for school fees and unga keeps hitting the local bank accounts every month.

The government’s response to the escalating conflict is as predictable as a Nairobi traffic jam. They have activated "emergency communication channels" and are telling people to register with embassies. If a missile starts falling in Riyadh or Doha, do they honestly think a 24-hour hotline is going to save a domestic worker who is locked inside a villa? Registration is just a fancy way for the state to keep track of who is still alive to send money back. For a country that prides itself on "labor migration" as an economic pillar, we sure have a lazy way of protecting the pillars themselves.

We are quickly learning that when you export your labor force, you also export your economic stability. Like the Philippines, Kenya has realized that Middle Eastern conflicts are never just foreign affairs. They are local tragedies waiting to happen. If the Gulf goes dark, the "sluggish growth" we complain about now will look like a luxury. Our national economy is currently built on the backs of people working in places like Lebanon and Iraq, and yet the best we can offer them is a security advisory to "avoid non-essential movement."

While [BORED BISHOPS AND BUS COUSINS: THE ELITES ARE PANICKING OVER YOUR TIKTOK WAR MEMES](/posts/bored-bishops-and-bus-cousins--the-elites-are-panicking-over-your-tiktok-war-memes) might be the biggest concern for the high-society crowd in Nairobi, the 300,000 Kenyans in Saudi Arabia are facing a much darker reality. President Ruto condemned the strikes without naming names, playing the diplomatic game while his "hustlers" are stuck in the crosshairs. We have sold our youth to the highest bidder in the desert, and now that the desert is on fire, all we can do is sit back and hope the cash flow does not stop. It is not a policy; it is a gamble with other people’s lives.
