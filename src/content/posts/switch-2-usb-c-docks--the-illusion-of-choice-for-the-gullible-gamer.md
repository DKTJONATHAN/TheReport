So, Nintendo’s Switch 2 is out, and surprise, surprise, they’ve changed the rules of the game again. Apparently, fiddling with wireless protocols and how video spits out over USB-C was just too much for the third-party accessory vultures to handle without a complete overhaul. This, of course, means they all have to start from scratch, which conveniently translates into a glorious opportunity for them to reinvent the wheel and charge you premium prices for the privilege.

Now, after a considerable amount of effort and likely considerable consumer desperation, we have two so called "reliable" USB-C dock alternatives. Jsaux’s OmniCentro Dock, a mere $45.99, landed first. Then, Genki’s "crowdsourced" Covert Dock 3 hobbled onto the scene at a whopping $69.99. What’s the massive difference? Apparently, Genki’s dock features "translucent orange components on two sides." Groundbreaking. The price difference is clearly justified by… aesthetic choice. You don’t need to be a genius to see that one company is playing the budget game while the other is banking on the illusion of premium features.

Both of these glorified dongles claim to output up to 4K at 120Hz, which is a laugh considering the Switch 2 itself maxes out at a more pathetic 4K / 60Hz. They’ll also scale down to 1440p or 1080p at 120Hz, complete with HDR and up to 65W PD fast charging. Oh, and a spare USB-A port for those essential dongles or controllers. It’s all very exciting, assuming you believe the marketing.

The article waxes poetic about how these things are handy for travel, with their "foldable prongs" that won't snag your bag. How revolutionary. Or you can just use it at home, like a normal person, to avoid lugging Nintendo’s official dock around. The novelty, they say, has worn off since 2020. That's corporate speak for "we've been doing this for years, and you're still falling for it."

The real kicker, of course, is the firmware. Both of these devices boast "upgradeable firmware," because Nintendo has a track record of screwing over third-party accessory makers with system updates. A November 2025 update, we're told, "blocked some third-party docks." So, buy one of these now, and Nintendo could easily render it a useless paperweight with the next patch. The manufacturers are fully aware of this, hence the firmware updates, which means you'll be paying for a product that's essentially on a perpetual lease from Nintendo's goodwill.

In the end, it’s just another example of how consumers are caught in the crossfire of corporate power plays. Nintendo dictates the terms, and accessory makers scramble to comply, all while you, the gamer, are left to decide which overpriced piece of plastic offers the least amount of immediate disappointment. The choice, as always, is an illusion.
