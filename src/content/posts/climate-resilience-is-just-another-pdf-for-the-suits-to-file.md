Rain in Kenya used to be a blessing, but now it is just another way to go broke. While the Insurance Regulatory Authority (IRA) fusses over penetration rates stuck at a miserable 2.4 percent, the reality on the ground is even bleaker. We are told that climate change is an economic risk, yet the people most affected are the ones these big companies ignore. It is the same old story of [The Faux War on Corruption: A Critical Examination of African Leadership](/posts/the-faux-war-on-corruption--a-critical-examination-of-african-leadership) where the rhetoric is world-class but the actual delivery is nowhere to be found. 

The industry is sitting on over Sh1 trillion in assets, but agricultural insurance accounts for less than one percent of premiums. Let that sink in. In a country where we eat what we grow, the people growing the food are left to the mercy of the clouds. The suits at CIC and ICEA LION are busy talking about IFRS S1 and S2 climate disclosure standards. That is just fancy talk for "we have a nice office and our shareholders are happy." Meanwhile, the smallholder farmer is one drought away from losing everything because the products supposedly designed for them are either too expensive or too complicated to understand.

Reinsurers like Africa Re are at least honest enough to admit they are clueless. They talk about a lack of sophisticated modelling and weather data as if that is an excuse for leaving 83 percent of farmers uninsured. They say climate insurance is a "new concept" and they are still in "formative stages." We have been having droughts since the beginning of time, but suddenly because there is a global buzzword like ESG, they need more time to figure out how to take a premium and actually pay a claim. 

The Insurance Regulatory Authority wants innovation, but they are fighting a losing battle against corporate greed. When claims start rising because of floods or medical emergencies, the insurers do not run to help the vulnerable. They run back to the "safe" stuff like motor and health insurance where they can squeeze the middle class for every cent. They call it "prioritizing physical and transition risks," but we call it looking out for number one while the rest of the country drowns. 

Until these "sustainability roadmaps" actually put money back into the pockets of a farmer whose crops have withered, they are just paper. You can have all the digital transformation and bancassurance you want, but if the product does not work when the sky stays dry, it is a scam. In Nairobi, we know the drill: the big man gets a bailout, the corporation gets a tax break, and the rest of us get a lecture on "resilience" while we pick up the pieces of our lives.
