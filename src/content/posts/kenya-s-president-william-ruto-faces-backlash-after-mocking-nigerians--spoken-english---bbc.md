Ruto's Big Mouth: Kenyans Mocked for Bad English by Their Own President
WARNING: This story highlights a Kenyan President's condescending remarks about Nigerian English, sparking backlash and revealing a colonial hangover.
President William Ruto speaking to Kenyans in Italy.
Politics
Ruto, Nigeria, English
President

So, our esteemed President, William Ruto, decided to grace the Kenyan diaspora in Italy with his pearls of wisdom. And what wisdom it was! Apparently, according to him, listening to a Nigerian speak English is like trying to decipher alien transmissions - you need a translator. He even had the audacity to boast that Kenyans speak "some of the best English in the world." Because nothing screams sophistication like belittling your neighbours, right? It's the same old song and dance we hear from leaders, isn't it? You might remember how we discussed the "Hobbes, Ruto, Gachagua, and the Cheap Thrills of Political Theatre" not too long ago. This is just another act in that predictable play.

The backlash, predictably, was swift and brutal. Nigerians and other Africans online rightly called him out, accusing him of displaying a "deep inferiority complex rooted in colonial conditioning." It's almost laughable, if it wasn't so pathetic. Here's a man, leading a nation, bragging about English proficiency - a language forced upon us, mind you - as if it's some sort of divine gift. As if the Nobel laureates and literary giants Nigeria has produced are just speaking gibberish to him. It's a classic case of masters' tools, if you ask me.

This whole episode just exposes the rot beneath the surface. Instead of focusing on the skyrocketing cost of living and the endless unemployment lines back home, our leaders are busy engaging in petty online spats and displaying their colonial baggage. It’s a distraction, pure and simple. While these online barbs between Kenya and Nigeria are a common, albeit tiresome, occurrence, this time it's the head of state stooping to that level.

And let's not forget the context. This comes after Nigerian President Bola Tinubu made his own questionable pronouncements about Kenyans being "better off" than others. So, this isn't just about accents; it's a petty, back-and-forth squabble between two nations whose leaders seem more interested in ego-boosting than actual governance. Nigeria's English might have its own flavour, shaped by over 500 indigenous languages, just as ours is a mix of Bantu, Nilotic, and Cushitic influences. But to call it incomprehensible? That’s just plain ignorant, and frankly, embarrassing for us all.
