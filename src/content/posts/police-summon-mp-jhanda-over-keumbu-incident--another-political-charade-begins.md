So, the DCI in Kisii has finally gotten around to 'summoning' Nyaribari Chache MP Zaheer Jhanda over the Keumbu convoy attacks. What a surprise. As if we haven't seen this particular song and dance before, where a politician is "required to appear" and then, poof, the investigation either fizzles out or lands squarely on the heads of a few poor souls who were probably paid peanuts to throw stones. It's the same old script, just a different actor this time.

The National Police Service, in their infinite wisdom, released a statement Sunday, vowing to protect lives and property. Honestly, you'd think they were new to this country. The 'Linda Mwananchi' faction leaders' convoy was attacked, vehicles vandalized, people injured - a scene as common as a matatu breaking down on Thika Road. This isn't an isolated incident; it's a feature, not a bug, of our political landscape. Just another day where the strong arm of the state conveniently looks the other way until public outcry forces a minimal, performative response.

They've arrested seven suspects now, four of them 'additional.' Good for them. These low-level goons will likely be paraded for the cameras, serving as convenient scapegoats while the actual orchestrators - the ones pulling the strings and splashing the cash - remain comfortably out of reach. It’s a classic move, designed to show 'progress' without actually addressing the root cause of political hooliganism that seems to permeate every election cycle and every public gathering. It reminds you of how much of our public life is just a grand performance, much like [Sonko's KES 609,000 Phone Purchase Exposes Kenya's Political Theater of Greed](/posts/sonko-s-kes-609-000-phone-purchase-exposes-kenya-s-political-theater-of-greed).

The NPS statement also talks about 'establishing the full circumstances' and 'identifying any other individuals.' Bless their hearts. We all know how these 'ongoing investigations' typically end: with a whimper, a forgotten report, and everyone moving on to the next manufactured crisis or scandal. The commitment to 'decisive action against anyone who threatens public safety' rings hollow when the threats often come from within the very system meant to uphold that safety.

Outrage from political leaders and civil rights groups? Expected. Predictable. And ultimately, ineffective. They call for speedy investigations, but speedy doesn't mean thorough or just, especially when powerful names are involved. Until we demand real accountability, not just theatrical summons and token arrests, incidents like Keumbu will keep happening. And we'll keep reading about them, feeling that familiar knot of frustration, knowing the cycle will repeat.
