AFRICAN FLIGHTS: JET FUEL'S SCARCITY IS YOUR EXPENSIVE REALITY

WARNING: Brace yourself for flight surcharges and the grim reality of dwindling fuel supplies as geopolitical drama in the Middle East directly impacts your travel costs across Africa.

THE HIGH PRICE OF WAR IS LANDING ON YOUR BOARDING PASS.

GOSSIP
JET FUEL, AFRICA, WAR

PLANE

So, the Yanks and their mates are at it again with Iran, and guess who’s footing the bill? Us, of course. This "war on Iran," or whatever they're calling it this week, has messed with the global oil supply, and Africa, as usual, is getting the short end of the stick. Seventy percent of our precious jet fuel and kerosene gets choked through the Strait of Hormuz. Now that it's practically shut down, our flights are already feeling the squeeze. You think you're just flying from Nairobi to Mombasa? Think again. The price of fuel changes faster than the weather, and by the time you land, they might want more of your cash.

Jannie de Klerk from National Airways Corporation pretty much spells it out: "By the time you get there, the price has changed." He’s seen prices jump by Sh46 a litre in just ten hours. Ten hours! This isn't some abstract economic theory; this is your wallet getting lighter because some suits decided to play war games. He’s already having to rethink how far ahead he quotes jobs because you can easily end up losing money instead of making it. It's a mad scramble out there, and those who thought business was simple are learning a harsh lesson. It’s almost as chaotic as the circus around the [African music boom]( /posts/110-million-for-who--the-african-music-boom-is-just-a-fancy-way-to-go-broke) where everyone thought they’d get rich quick.

And it's not just small charter companies. Even the big players are feeling it. FlySafair, a low-cost carrier, is estimating an extra Sh260,853 per flight hour for each of their planes. That's on top of their usual costs, which are already inflated because jet fuel makes up a staggering 50-55% of their operating expenses. Meanwhile, coastal airports in South Africa saw prices shoot up by 70% in a single week. Seventy percent! You think that won’t translate to your ticket price? Think again.

The situation is so bad that some countries are down to just three to four weeks of jet fuel stock. Zambia is practically running on fumes, with only ten days left. Airlink is okay for now, but beyond April? Who knows. They’re already slapping on temporary fuel surcharges, and you can bet others will follow suit, or they'll just cut flights. It’s a classic case of "profits over people," or in this instance, profits over passengers. Africa's own refineries are a joke compared to the rest of the world, so we're stuck relying on these volatile imports, making us prime targets for this kind of global instability.

Europe is also in a panic, and when Europe panics about fuel, Africa feels it twice as hard. They’re looking at record highs for jet fuel, and we’re right behind them. China’s even banned exports of diesel and gasoline to protect their own supply. So while they’re scrambling to secure their own resources, we're left staring at a dwindling supply and ever-increasing prices. Get ready to pay more for less, because that’s the African aviation reality these days.
