So, this police officer, Siriri, a man who's supposed to *enforce* the law, gets married, and then gets transferred. Standard Nairobi hustle, right? His wife decides the rural farm life is more her speed, which, frankly, is a valid choice. He's sending money, she's farming, all good. Then, she disappears. Not a dramatic vanishing act, just… gone. Packed up and went to her parents, and never came back. Eight years of him making lonely trips and returning in tears. Because, you see, he's a police officer, and apparently, that means he has to be a saint. He couldn't remarry because, bigamy, felony, jail time. The usual threats to keep you in line.

But here's the kicker. While our law-abiding officer was busy being miserable, his wife, the one who vanished from their "sweetness of honeymoon," went and got herself another husband. Under customary law, of course. Because apparently, those laws are more like suggestions that can be bent or broken. She even stayed in that second union for four years before it went south and she decided to come home. So, while he was living in a self-imposed prison of marital fidelity, she was out there living her best, albeit temporary, second life. The hypocrisy is so thick you could spread it on ugali.

And the best part? He didn't report her. Why? Because he's a police officer, and he didn't want the "shame" of confronting her or reporting her second marriage. So, he just waited seven years for the legal dust to settle, then filed for divorce on grounds of denial of conjugal rights and intimidation. Because after all that, *he* needs to go through the legal rigmarole to be free. This whole situation screams of how the law is selectively applied, and how people, even those meant to uphold it, find ways to navigate around it when it's inconvenient. It’s almost as if certain individuals can operate with impunity, much like those we’ve seen in past stories about misappropriation of funds; [Same Forest, Same Monkeys: How Nick Mwendwa and Friends ‘Ate’ CHAN 2018](/posts/same-forest--same-monkeys--how-nick-mwendwa-and-friends--ate--chan-2018).

The data on bigamy cases is almost as baffling as the entire premise. Women committing bigamy one year, men the next, then it evens out. It’s a statistical mess, likely reflecting the reality that most of these cases never see the inside of a courtroom. The stigma, they say, is the big barrier. People would rather suffer in silence than admit their spouse is a cheat and a lawbreaker, or that they themselves are married to someone already taken. It’s easier to just accept the betrayal and the legal limbo, especially when the alternative is public humiliation and a lengthy, expensive court battle.

And let’s talk about the "recourse." You have to prove your spouse was already married, that there was no divorce, and that they entered into a subsequent marriage. You need a marriage certificate, a decree absolute, records from the Attorney General's office. It's a bureaucratic nightmare designed to deter anyone from actually seeking justice. Lawyers advise legal counsel, of course, because who else is going to profit from this mess? It's a system that’s meant to protect, but often ends up protecting the guilty by making the process of seeking justice so convoluted and expensive that most people just give up.

Ultimately, Siriri got his divorce and remarried, even having a child. So, eight years of loneliness, emotional turmoil, and legal paralysis, just to get back to where he should have been sooner. His story isn't just about one man's bad luck; it's a stark illustration of how the Kenyan legal system, particularly concerning marriage, is riddled with loopholes and societal pressures that render its laws almost meaningless for the average person. The Marriage Act might be on the books, but in practice, it’s often just a decorative piece of paper.
