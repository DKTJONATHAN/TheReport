The charade is over, at least for now, in O Smach, Cambodia. What was once a sprawling, six-story scam compound, a veritable factory of digital deceit, has been reduced to rubble and dust by Thai airstrikes. The BBC gained access, painting a grim picture of a sophisticated operation designed to bilk the unsuspecting worldwide. Inside, meticulously crafted replicas of Australian and Chinese police stations, complete with uniforms and scripts, served as stages for elaborate cons. Motivational slogans like "Money Coming From Everywhere" adorned the walls, a cruel irony given the discarded fake currency littering the floors.

This wasn't just a small-time operation. Thousands of foreign workers, lured by false promises of legitimate employment, were trapped in a brutal regime. Their lives were regimented, their efforts directed towards defrauding thousands of others of their hard-earned savings. The compound, attached to the Royal Hill casino, was abandoned in haste after Thai bombing raids in December, a consequence of what the Thais claim were Cambodian drone launches from the casino. The scene left behind was one of immediate abandonment: uneaten meals, half-finished drinks, and a pervasive stench.

The Thai military, now occupying the ruined compound, used the visit as a platform to highlight the scale of Cambodia's scam industry and appeal for international aid, a move that conveniently also justifies their aggressive actions. The Cambodian government's protests of Thai occupation are little more than a theatrical performance, given the mutually agreed ceasefire terms. What's truly striking is how this colossal operation remained largely unknown until the Thais seized it.

While the neighboring O Smach Casino, owned by the well-connected tycoon Ly Yong Phat, had garnered attention for worker abuses, Royal Hill's owner, Lim Heng, operated in relative obscurity. Despite his ties to the ruling Hun clan and the prestigious Neak Oknha title, he hadn't attracted international sanctions like Phat. His only notable eccentricity was his homage to Pol Pot, the Khmer Rouge leader, a chilling link to Cambodia's dark past that foreshadows the exploitation at play.

Cambodia's elite, enriched by land grabs and fueled by Chinese investment, have long profited from the country's lax regulatory environment. Border casinos, in particular, became lucrative havens, exploiting gambling bans in neighboring nations. Even after Cambodia banned online gambling in 2019 under pressure from China, the syndicates simply pivoted to online fraud, an easy transition exacerbated by the pandemic.

The recruitment tactics were insidious. Young workers, promised clerical or programming jobs, found themselves ensnared in a brutal reality. Documents recovered from the rubble detailed harsh punishments for failing to meet targets: five strokes of the cane for a single missed "lead," with escalating penalties for prolonged failure. Even casual conversation or a reluctance to share personal details for fabricated victim trust was grounds for punishment. Wilson, a Ugandan escapee, recounted tales of electrocution and torture in "The Black Room," his own experience a testament to the grim conditions and forced deception.

Workers were forced into 15-16 hour days, reciting scripts and using AI to impersonate wealthy women seeking husbands, all designed to exploit older American men through emotional manipulation and product sales. The bombardment didn't halt operations; workers were simply forced back to their posts after each explosion. The level of control was absolute, with even toilet breaks meticulously logged and timed.

The scams themselves were diverse and manipulative. Near a replica Brazilian police station, soundproofed booths housed scammers armed with scripts and fake legal documents designed to instill fear and elicit funds. For years, the Cambodian government turned a blind eye, earning condemnation from the US State Department for its inaction. Sanctions against figures like Ly Yong Phat were met with accusations of violating Cambodian sovereignty.

However, this year, under sustained international pressure, the government has suddenly changed its tune. Raids have become more frequent, and Prime Minister Hun Manet has vowed to shut down the industry by April, citing its damage to Cambodia's economy and reputation. The extradition of Chinese entrepreneur Chen Zhi, a formerly untouchable figure with deep ties to the ruling elite, was a dramatic signal of this shift. His public humiliation and subsequent extradition to China marked a symbolic sacrifice to salvage Cambodia's image. The extradition of Li Xiong, accused of laundering scam profits, further underscored this new, albeit likely temporary, stance.

While the government claims thousands of foreign workers have been repatriated and compounds shuttered, skepticism is warranted. These operations are notoriously difficult to dismantle permanently, often described as a game of "whack-a-mole." Workers can easily be relocated to less conspicuous locations, and many are likely still in Cambodia, perpetuating the cycle. And with the exception of Chen Zhi, the tycoons who facilitated these scams behind their casino facades have, as of yet, faced no meaningful consequences. This latest crackdown is likely just another performance for the international stage, a temporary reprieve before the next wave of exploitation inevitably surfaces.
