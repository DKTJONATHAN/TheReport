So, President Ruto is there, doing his usual presidential song and dance at a parliamentary group meeting. Normally, this is where everyone just nods along, pretending to be super interested, because interrupting Mheshimiwa is practically sacrilege. But then, Embu Governor Cecily Mbarire, bless her brave soul, decides to actually *ask* something. She’s looking at this report on the handshake deal with Raila’s ODM - a year later, mind you - and it’s completely silent on the two-thirds gender rule. Talk about blind spots.

This isn’t some minor detail, people. Article 27(8) of our shiny 2010 Constitution clearly states we need to make sure neither gender is more than two-thirds of any elective or appointive body. It’s been shoved down our throats since 2012, with the Supreme Court giving Parliament a deadline that came and went faster than a promised job. It’s supposed to be part of fixing our democracy and economy, all wrapped up in this “10-Point Agenda.” More like a 10-point excuse.

Ruto, naturally, tried to wriggle out of it, mumbling something about "constitutional amendment" in the report. Vague references, you know, the kind that sound important but mean nothing. The MPs, who apparently have brains despite everything, weren't buying it and gave the committee another two months to fix this mess. It makes you wonder if Ruto and his inner circle are scared of tackling anything that might actually upset the apple cart, especially if it means risking his re-election. Because let's be honest, anything requiring a constitutional amendment is about as popular as a tax increase.

Kenya’s Constitution, bless its heart, hasn't been changed since 2010. Every attempt has either been too unpopular, or Parliament and the courts have found ways to kick the can down the road. This two-thirds gender rule has been a hot potato for ages. Judges have threatened to dissolve Parliament, the Chief Justice himself has sent stern letters, and what happens? MPs boycott meetings to deny quorum. It’s a classic move, seen before with gender bills that mysteriously disappear. It’s almost like they enjoy the drama.

And it’s not just the gender rule. Ruto’s plans to change the power structure, maybe add a Prime Cabinet Secretary or an official opposition leader, are also facing a rough ride. These ideas sound suspiciously like the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) that Uhuru Kenyatta and Raila Odinga were pushing. Remember how that ended? Declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court. Ruto, back then, used it to bash them, calling them power-hungry dynasties. Now, he’s walking a tightrope, potentially having to make the same "gambles" he once criticized. It’s a classic Nairobi plot twist, and frankly, we’re tired of the sequels.
