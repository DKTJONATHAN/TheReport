If you want to know where your bank fees and the interest on your predatory mobile loans are going, look no further than the manicured lawns of Karen Country Club. While the rest of Nairobi is dodging potholes and trying to figure out if we can afford unga this week, the "decision-makers" are busy patting each other on the back at the NCBA Corporate Golf Day. It is the same old story: a few men in expensive polos deciding the fate of the country over eighteen holes, pretending that a 2026 outlook matters to a person who cannot afford charcoal today.

They call it "deepening capital flows," but any realist in this city knows a garage sale when they see one. Nedbank is swallowing NCBA, the government is offloading Safaricom crumbs to Vodacom, and even our beer is now being poured into the pockets of the Asahi Group. We are being partitioned and sold in chunks to the highest bidder while the suits call it "long-term value creation." It is not value for you; it is value for shareholders in Johannesburg and Tokyo who have never stepped foot in a matatu or felt the heat of a Gikomba fire.

Then there is the "UBUNTU Strategy 2026-2030." Seriously? Using a word that means "I am because we are" to describe a banking roadmap is the ultimate middle finger to the poor. It is all part of their move toward scale and digital integration, much like how [AI Agents Are Now Running the Back Office at Insurance Giants](/posts/ai-agents-are-now-running-the-back-office-at-insurance-giants) to ensure that the human element is completely stripped away from service. They want "mutual growth" only if that growth happens in their balance sheets and not in your meager savings account.

NCBA claims to finance 60 percent of the sugar industry and half of the tea sector. In plain English, they own the sweat of the rural farmer. They have their hands in the SACCOs, the diplomatic missions, and even the NGOs. When one bank controls that much of the "value chain," you are no longer a customer; you are a tenant in their financial empire. They are the ones holding the keys to the granary while we are told to be "dynamic" and "innovative" to survive their high-interest traps.

To top off the hypocrisy, they handed out 5,000 tree seedlings to the winners. It is the classic corporate move: extract everything from the economy, then plant a few shrubs to look like saints in the next annual report. Ten million trees by 2030 will not offset the carbon footprint of their luxury SUVs or the cold reality of a manufacturing sector that gets nothing from these banks but more debt. It is just green paint on a sinking ship, and we are all expected to clap while they sail away.
