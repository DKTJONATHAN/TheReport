So, the June 25 anniversary protest, eh? Seems like a perfect time for our esteemed police service to remind everyone that "demonstrating" can get you more than just a stern talking-to. Six people, who just wanted to commemorate some other poor souls, apparently got "arrested" outside Parliament. Next thing you know, they're missing for a couple of days, only to be found roughed up and chucked out like yesterday's garbage in various parts of this city. Lang'ata Road and Talanta Stadium - real scenic spots for being dumped after a good thrashing.

KHRC and Haki Africa, bless their persistent hearts, had to do the legwork of finding these folks. Irene Soila from KHRC basically spelled it out: these people were tortured. They disappeared without consent, without anyone knowing where they were. It’s almost like being a citizen in Nairobi means you're just waiting for the next "disappearance" or roadside chaos, a bit like those KeNHA stories we’ve seen.

Hussein Khalid from Haki Africa was even more direct, calling out the police for treating Kenyans in an "inhuman and degrading manner." Transported between stations, left in lorries, driven out to some random murram road, blindfolded, beaten, and then apparently taken to a forest for more interrogation about funding and recording officers. Seriously? This sounds less like law enforcement and more like a bad action movie, except the victims are real and the "villains" are wearing blue uniforms.

They were questioned about who funds them, where the money comes from, why they're filming the police. The usual script when the state doesn't like what you're doing. Then, after being held until 3 am on Friday, they were unceremoniously dumped. It’s a wonder any of them managed to contact anyone to get to a hospital. Khalid’s got a point - if you still had any doubts about the National Police Service being a brutal institution, this should clear them up. They're operating like they're above any law, a common theme when you consider the new wash wash scams that keep popping up.

Soila’s warning about worsening human rights violations is, frankly, stating the obvious. When people disappear and get tortured during protests, it’s not just a bad day; it’s a sign that our democracy is on shaky ground. And Khalid is demanding justice. Of course he is. Because demanding justice is what you do when the people sworn to protect you are the ones doing the beating. Let’s see how many officers actually get identified from those videos and held accountable. Don't hold your breath.
