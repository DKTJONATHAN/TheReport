So, Pema Chödrön, bless her heart, wants us to "bear" our loneliness. Apparently, we're all born alone, we all die alone, and the brief, fleeting moments in between are supposed to be filled with this thing called "trust and love." How much of that we manage to squeeze out, or how much we generously pour onto others, is supposedly dependent on how well we've learned to tolerate the crushing weight of our own solitude. It’s presented as some profound philosophical insight, this "fundamental fact of every human existence." As if we didn't already know.

Chödrön’s book, "When Things Fall Apart," which sounds less like a self-help guide and more like a prophecy, suggests we can cultivate a "nonthreatening relationship with loneliness." This involves transmuting the terror of the existential void into a "relaxing and cooling loneliness." Right. Because nothing says relaxation like staring into the abyss and deciding it’s not so bad.

The core of this "advice" is that suffering, in the Buddhist view, is just us kicking and screaming against reality. We want things to be a certain way, and when they’re not, we suffer. So, by "befriending our loneliness," we’re supposed to accept life's "as-is" nature, with all its messy uncertainty. We’re told that we are "fundamentally alone, and there is nothing anywhere to hold on to." And this isn't a problem, it's an opportunity to discover an "unfabricated state of being." This means dropping our illusions and realizing we "don’t ultimately know anything." This painful truth, the article suggests, is best met by "relaxing with something as familiar as loneliness."

The article paints loneliness as a mirror, reflecting our "greatest strength." By looking at ourselves "without aggression," we can "drop our ideals" and see "who we are." This "cool loneliness" doesn't offer resolutions or stability; it forces us to step into a world of "no reference point." This is what they call the "middle way" or the "sacred path of the warrior." A warrior who, apparently, is marching alone into a fog bank.

In essence, the message is: stop fighting it. Loneliness is here to stay. Instead of trying to escape it or find lasting happiness in relationships or external validation, just sit with it. Accept that there's no inherent meaning or certainty, and that's okay. Find comfort in the discomfort. It’s a rather bleak outlook, dressed up in spiritual platitudes. The value of life, it seems, is not in overcoming loneliness, but in learning to tolerate its constant, gnawing presence. A truly uplifting thought for a world already teetering on the edge.
