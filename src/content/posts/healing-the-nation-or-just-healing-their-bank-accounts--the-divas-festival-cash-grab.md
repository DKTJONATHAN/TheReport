Here we go again. Coronation Park is set to host the Divas International Music Festival on March 21, 2026, under the lofty and rather eye-rolling theme, "Voices That Heal." Apparently, the cure for gender-based violence (GBV) isn't systemic reform or actual police work, but a star-studded concert lineup. It is the same old script: wait for a national crisis to be declared - like President Ramaphosa did in 2025 - and then pivot every music festival into a "national instrument for awareness." We see you, TANK KOMS, and we see that government funding from the Mzansi Golden Economy initiative you are chasing.

The lineup is a predictable mix of legends and the "it-girls" of the moment. We have got Zee Nxumalo, Makhadzi, and Nkosazana Daughter ready to "empower" us from the stage. Zee is already out here giving us the PR-approved "full-circle moment" quotes, claiming this is about meaning and not just music. Please. It is about a paycheck and a chance to stand next to Abigail Kubeka for some inherited street cred. While Lwah Ndlunkulu talks about her village roots and how her music is a "safe space," one has to wonder if that safe space includes the VIP section where the real "economic participation" happens.

The organizers are leaning heavily on the GBV angle, trying to make this look like more than just a party. It reminds me of the same empty posturing we saw during the [High-Level Hallucinations: Nairobi Prepares for Another Digital Talkfest](/posts/high-level-hallucinations--nairobi-prepares-for-another-digital-talkfest), where big words like "measurable social action" were thrown around to hide a lack of actual results. Calling a music festival a "platform for healing" is a stretch even for the most seasoned publicist. If songs could stop the scourge of GBVF, South Africa would have been the safest place on earth decades ago.

Then there is the mandatory tribute to the late Dr. Miriam Makeba. It has been 17 years since she passed, yet her name is still being dragged out to add "activism" weight to every local production. Zenzi Makeba Lee and the Makeba Band will be doing a set to celebrate "Mama Africa," but let us be honest - it is a convenient way to make a standard music gig look like a historic event. It is a formula that works: mix one part legitimate legend, two parts trendy TikTok stars, and garnish with a heavy dose of social justice buzzwords.

The Department of Sports, Arts and Culture is footing the bill through the MGE initiative, which means your tax rands are paying for this "empowerment" session. They claim the festival creates opportunities for women in production and marketing, but we all know the real money stays at the top. While the "Divas" are singing about hope and peace, the rest of the industry will be back to its usual messy self by Monday morning. It is a glamorous distraction from a grim reality, but hey, at least the outfits will look great on Instagram.
