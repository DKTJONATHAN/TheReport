Another tragedy, another round of politicians lining up to give eulogies that sound suspiciously like performance reviews. The late Captain George Were is being defended by the heavy hitters - National Assembly Speaker Moses Wetang’ula and Majority Leader Aaron Cheruiyot. Apparently, our leaders aren't just busy with legislation; they have moonlighted as expert flight evaluators. They’re telling us the pilot was disciplined and "astute" to silence the whispers on social media. We get it, he was your favorite driver in the sky, but the sudden rush to "protect his legacy" from the "trolls" feels a little too rehearsed for my liking.

Wetang’ula was particularly poetic, describing how the pilot "swung from one cloud to another" during a flight just two weeks ago. Honestly, if I wanted a weather report mixed with a Hallmark card, I’d watch a different channel. He’s asking us to "appreciate the will of God" and stop the blame game. It’s the classic Kenyan political move: when things go wrong, invoke the heavens so nobody looks at the maintenance logs or the age of the machinery. It reminds me of the usual smoke and mirrors we see in this country, much like [The Great Trek to Nowhere: Eric Omondi’s 486KM PR Stunt for a Dying Nation](/posts/the-great-trek-to-nowhere--eric-omondi-s-486km-pr-stunt-for-a-dying-nation), where a lot of noise is made to distract us from the actual rot in the system.

Then we have Aaron Cheruiyot joining the "I flew with him once" club. It’s become a status symbol now, hasn’t it? The logic seems to be: "I survived a flight with the deceased, so clearly, nothing could have gone wrong on his watch." It’s incredibly cynical to use a requiem mass to build a defensive wall against online critics when families are mourning more than just a reputation. MP Johana Ng’eno and several others are gone, and instead of real answers or a wait for a technical report, we’re getting a collection of five-star reviews for a helicopter.

Let’s be real - the internet is a messy place, and people will talk, but the government’s obsession with "correcting the record" before the investigation is even cold is telling. They want us to ignore the smoke and focus on the "precision" and "professionalism" they witnessed from the passenger seat. It’s the same old script of protecting the elite circle and their associates. If you’re a pilot for the big boys, you’re a hero beyond reproach; if you’re anyone else, you’re just another tragic headline.

As the burials approach this Friday, the PR machine will likely go into overdrive. We’ll hear more about "the will of God" and "unfriendly weather" maneuvers. But while the leaders are busy swinging from one cloud of rhetoric to another, the rest of us are grounded in a reality where things just don't add up. Stay tuned, because in this town, the truth usually crashes long before the aircraft does. When the elite start defending someone this hard, you have to wonder what they are really trying to keep from falling out of the sky.
