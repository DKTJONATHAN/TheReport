So, Kenya Airways is suddenly swimming in passengers, almost hitting 100% capacity. Their acting CEO, George Kamal, is chirping about how the “US-Israeli war on Iran” has somehow magically boosted their load factors from a measly 70% in January to nearly full. Apparently, the global aviation chaos, with other airlines scrambling and cancelling flights, has pushed everyone onto KQ’s doorstep. Convenient, isn't it?

Most of these newfound fans, according to Kamal, are coming from Europe, the US, and Asia. Yes, those long-haul routes that usually cost a fortune and require you to sell a kidney just to afford a ticket. Now, KQ is raking it in. It's almost as if people are so desperate to avoid trouble zones that they’ll pay anything to fly with us. Let's not pretend this is about better service; it's pure, unadulterated geopolitical opportunism.

But hold on, before you start celebrating KQ's supposed success, let's talk about the small print, the stuff they conveniently gloss over. While they’re bragging about full planes, their flight operations head, Paul Njoroge, is busy scrambling for jet fuel. They’ve got about 56 days' worth, and they’re desperately trying to get more from India. So, while the load factor is high, the ability to actually *fly* those planes seems to be a secondary concern, or at least, a less glamorous one to trumpet.

It’s the classic Kenyan story, isn't it? All flash, no substance. They'll highlight the bumper demand, but conveniently omit the precarious fuel situation. It’s like watching someone boast about their expensive new car while whispering about how they can’t afford to fill the tank. One wonders how long this fuel crisis will last, and what happens when the Middle East situation eventually calms down, or when KQ’s current fuel supply runs dry. We've seen this kind of shaky foundation before, remember when we talked about [Exporting Souls and Empty Bonds](/posts/exporting-souls-and-empty-bonds)? It always comes back to the basics.

And let's not forget, this surge in demand is a direct result of human conflict and suffering. While KQ sees it as a business opportunity, it’s a grim reminder of how interconnected and fragile our world is. They’re benefiting from a war, plain and simple. So, next time you hear about KQ’s soaring numbers, just remember the context. It's not a triumph of brilliant management, it's a reflection of a world in turmoil, and KQ is just one of the many entities trying to cash in on the chaos.
