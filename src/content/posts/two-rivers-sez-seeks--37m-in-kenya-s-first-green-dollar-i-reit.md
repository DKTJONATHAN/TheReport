TWO RIVERS SEZ'S $37M GREEN DOLLAR I-REIT: MORE HOT AIR THAN CASH FOR NAIROBI?

Warning Summary: Trific Ltd is pushing Kenya's first green dollar I-Reit on the NSE, promising stable USD income from eco-friendly real estate. But don't expect it to solve your rent problems anytime soon.

Caption: Another day, another dollar dream peddled on the Nairobi Securities Exchange.





So, Trific Ltd, operating out of the so-called Two Rivers International Finance and Innovation Centre - sounds fancy, doesn't it? - is trying to shake loose $37.3 million from unsuspecting investors. This isn't just any money grab; it's a "green, dollar-denominated Income-Real Estate Investment Trust" (I-Reit) on the Nairobi Securities Exchange. Apparently, it's all about buying their own North Tower and building more "premium, environmentally sustainable commercial towers." Because Nairobi desperately needs more glass boxes when half of us are still wondering how to get 15 Kenyans pulled from Iran's mess via Turkey, because what else is new?.

Brenda Mbathi, Trific's CEO, chirps on about investors earning a "stable share of the export revenues" from global service firms. Translation: you're basically betting on call centres and outsourcing companies paying their rent on time. This "future-oriented" product offers USD income in a market that's mostly playing in Kenyan shillings. It’s supposed to be the first of its kind, a real estate income stream in dollars from eco-friendly buildings. Sure, why not.

They’re calling it a "Project of Strategic National Importance" and lining it up with Vision 2030. Vision 2030, that old chestnut. Meanwhile, the North Tower is "fully developed, fully leased," and 92 percent occupied by these "blue-chip service export enterprises." Which sounds great on paper, but let's be honest, it’s just another office block with a fancy new label. The offer is open from May 13 to June 12, with a listing set for June 23, 2026. Plenty of time for things to go sideways.

The kicker? You can get in for a minimum of 1,000 units, which works out to $1,000, or about Ksh129,000. Maurice Opiyo from KCB Investment Bank claims this is "within reach of most investors." For those struggling to make rent, maybe this is the solution, or maybe it's just another way for the already well-off to park their dollars while the rest of us navigate the local currency chaos. The Two Rivers Land Company is holding at least 20 percent of the securities initially, which is supposed to show commitment, but they can ditch it all by the second anniversary. Sounds like a plan to get cash in the door and then bail.
