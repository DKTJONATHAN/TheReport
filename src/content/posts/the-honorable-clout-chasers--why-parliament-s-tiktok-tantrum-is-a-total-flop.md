The "Honorable" members of the National Assembly are back at it, acting like they just discovered the internet yesterday. They spent their week debating whether to ban TikTok, clutching their pearls over "youth exposure" and "misinformation" as if their own political campaigns aren't built on a foundation of WhatsApp rumors and empty promises. It’s hilarious to watch men in expensive suits pretend to be the moral police when we all know they are probably the biggest consumers of those late-night "live" sessions that definitely aren't about "digital economic growth."

In a shocking twist that surprised absolutely nobody, the Public Petitions Committee decided an outright ban is a no-go. They claim it would "infringe on fundamental rights," but let’s be real - they just don’t want to be the ones responsible for a nationwide influencer uprising. Can you imagine the chaos if the dance-challenge brigade and the tea-spillers were forced to actually find real jobs? The committee is now calling for "robust regulation," which is just government-speak for "we want a seat at the table so we can feel important while the kids keep scrolling."

The technical reality of this whole circus is the funniest part. These boomers are talking about firewalls and gateways as if Kenya isn't an open-market digital playground. Our internet is so decentralized that trying to block one app would be like trying to stop rain with a sieve. Even if they tried to pull the plug, every tech-savvy Gen Z in Nairobi already has three different VPNs ready to go. Trying to enforce a ban here would turn into a mess faster than [the Mzungu dream is just a low-budget horror movie in Nairobi](/posts/the-mzungu-dream-is-just-a-low-budget-horror-movie-in-nairobi) where everyone realizes the hero has no plan.

Our local "content creators" were probably shaking in their boots at the thought of losing their precious brand deals. From the ones selling questionable skincare to the "motivational speakers" who film in rented Airbnbs, TikTok is the only thing keeping the delusion alive. The committee’s report noted that small businesses rely on the platform for reach, but they forgot to mention that half of that "reach" is just people arguing in the comments about who cheated on who in the latest celebrity breakup. 

At the end of the day, this isn't about security or data protection; it’s about control. The government realized they can’t win a war against an algorithm that knows us better than our own mothers do. So, instead of a ban, we get "periodic compliance reviews." Translation: expect more boring government ads appearing between your favorite transition videos. TikTok stays, the influencers keep chasing clout, and the MPs get to pretend they did something useful for once. Same script, different platform.
