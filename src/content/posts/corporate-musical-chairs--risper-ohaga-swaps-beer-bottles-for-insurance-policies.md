Risper Ohaga is moving from the CFO desk at East African Breweries PLC to become the Group CEO of APA Apollo Group. It is the same old Nairobi story where the elite simply rotate seats while the rest of us watch from the sidelines. She spent years at EABL "optimising" capital structures, which is just corporate-speak for making sure the dividends kept flowing while the price of a cold Tusker became a luxury for the average hustler. Now, she is heading back to the insurance world to sell us policies we probably cannot afford to claim.

The announcement says she is not even leaving EABL until June 2026. It must be nice to have your life planned out two years in advance while the rest of the city does not even know if they will get to work on time. You could be trying to drive your overpriced import to a meeting only to find that [KeNHA Announces Eight Day Night Closure of Uhuru Highway Section for Maintenance](/posts/kenha-announces-eight-day-night-closure-of-uhuru-highway-section-for-maintenance), reminding you that planning is a privilege reserved for those with "CEO" in their title. For everyone else, Nairobi is just one big traffic jam.

Ohaga's resume is a map of the private clubs that run this country. From Barclays Zambia to I&M Bank and now back to APA, the governance footprint is deep. These people sit on each other's boards, audit each other's risks, and then act surprised when the economy feels rigged against the small man. While she was busy with "debt refinancing initiatives" to enhance liquidity resilience, most Kenyans were just trying to survive the latest tax hike without losing their minds.

It is not just the corporate sector playing this game. We see Princess Zahra Aga Khan getting an EGH from the President and Naphtaly Kipchirchir Rono being handed the keys to the Financial Reporting Centre. Even Winnie Odinga is keeping the family name alive in EALA. It is a closed loop of the same surnames and the same faces, whether they are climate activists like Truphena Muthoni or high-flying bankers. The titles change, the offices get bigger, but the reality for the person on the street remains exactly the same.

This "strategic leadership shift" at APA Apollo is being hailed as a milestone, but let us be real. It is just another day of the "haves" securing their future. Ohaga handled the EABL-Asahi deal, reshuffling ownership for global players who do not know where Luthuli Avenue is. Now she will be at APA, likely "optimising" premiums so the company stays resilient while your household budget collapses. In this city, the only thing that actually trickles down is the bill.
