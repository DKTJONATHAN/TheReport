Only in Kenya do we treat a political document like a sacred relic while the ink is still wet with the tears of taxpayers. This Saturday, the so-called 10-point Memorandum of Understanding between Ruto’s UDA and the late Raila Odinga’s ODM hits its one-year mark. They called it a national reset back in March 2025, a way to calm the youth-led protests that actually had the government sweating. But let us be real - it was not a reset for the country; it was a life jacket for a sinking presidency and a buffet ticket for an opposition that was tired of shouting in the rain.

Now the vultures are circling the corpse of the agreement. You have Edwin Sifuna and James Orengo shouting that the deal is dead because it lacks an extension clause, while Gladys Wanga and Oburu Oginga are trying to convince us that this "political marriage" is eternal. Sifuna says you need "Baba’s signature" to extend it, which is a neat trick considering the man is no longer with us. It is the peak of Kenyan political comedy: fighting over the ghost of a signature to justify staying in a government that most of their voters currently loathe.

While the bigwigs argue over commas and clauses, the "broad-based" experiment has been a masterclass in elite capture. John Mbadi and Hassan Joho swapped their protest whistles for ministerial flags, and suddenly the "struggle" became a lot more comfortable. They claim 60 percent of the agenda is done, but if you look at your wallet, that percentage feels more like zero. This kind of institutional betrayal is not new; it is the same spirit we see when [The Pulpit For Sale: Kiengei’s Holy U-Turn](/posts/the-pulpit-for-sale--kiengei-s-holy-u-turn) showed us how quickly principles are traded for a seat at the high table.

The reality is that the cost of living has not budged, and the "conflict-of-interest" laws they brag about are just new ways to ensure the right people get the right bribes. Ruto got the political calm he needed to keep taxing us into the ground, and ODM got a front-row seat to the looting. This Saturday is not a deadline for reform; it is just the anniversary of the day the opposition decided that if you cannot beat the oppressors, you might as well join them for lunch.

If you are waiting for this "joint parliamentary meeting" to produce anything other than a bloated lunch bill and a press release full of hot air, you have not been paying attention. Whether they extend the MoU or let it expire does not matter to the person in Kibera or Githurai. The pact was never about the people. It was a 10-point plan to make sure the elite stayed relevant while the rest of us tried to figure out how to pay for unga. The marriage continues because, in Nairobi, nobody walks away from a feast until the plates are licked clean.
