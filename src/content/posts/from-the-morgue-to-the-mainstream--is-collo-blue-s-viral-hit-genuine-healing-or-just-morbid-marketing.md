Listen, I have seen some desperate attempts at rebranding in this city, but Collins Njuguna - or "Collo Blue" as he wants us to call him - really took "dead end job" literally. Our latest national obsession is a man who spends his 9-to-5 around corpses and his 5-to-9 teaching us how to "fly" with our hands in the air. While the PR machine is working overtime to call this "profound psychological healing," let’s be real: it is a catchy tune and a simple dance that even a corporate CEO with two left feet can manage. It is morbid, it is weird, and it is exactly the kind of trauma-glamorization that Kenyan social media thrives on.

The narrative being shoved down our throats is that "Up Burukanga" is some sort of spiritual release for a nation in crisis. They want us to believe that shaking our shoulders is a "unifying catharsis." Please. We are talking about a country where [the great divorce between reality and marketing scams](/posts/the-great-divorce--why-global-unity-is-a-marketing-scam) is at an all-time high. Just because a grief psychologist tells you to dance doesn’t mean your rent is paid or the economy is fixed. It just means you are distracted for fifteen seconds while he racks up the streaming royalties. 

I find it hilarious how quickly the "Suits" in Nairobi jumped on this. Watching a bank executive try to look "authentic" and "unpretentious" by doing a dance born in a mortuary is the peak of cringe. It is the same old story: someone from a humble, albeit slightly creepy, background creates something raw, and the middle class immediately sanitizes it so they can feel "connected to the streets." Collo Blue says working with death gives him an appreciation for life, but I bet the sudden influx of brand endorsements doesn’t hurt his "spirit" either.

The music industry is patting itself on the back because you "no longer need a massive studio budget" to blow up. Translation: record labels are getting lazy and hoping the next viral sensation falls out of a funeral home and into their laps. They call it "the democratic nature of internet culture," but I call it a race to the bottom where the loudest gimmick wins. If the "Up Burukanga" trend proves anything, it is that Kenyans will latch onto anything that promises a temporary escape from the daily grind, no matter how bizarre the origin story.

As we head further into 2026, expect more of this "authentic" content that is carefully curated to look accidental. Collo Blue is currently the darling of the digital space, but the internet is a fickle mistress. Once the novelty of the "dancing mortician" wears off, will we still be "flying," or will we be looking for the next shiny object to distract us from our problems? Stay tuned, because the only thing that moves faster than a viral dance is the speed at which we forget the person who started it.
