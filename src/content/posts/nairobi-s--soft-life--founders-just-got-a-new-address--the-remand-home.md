Darling, the "manifestation" girls and the "passive income" boys are shaking. While half of Nairobi was busy posting screenshots of their fake crypto dashboards and claiming they "started from the bottom," Interpol was actually doing the math. On Thursday, the global police body announced they have scooped up 27 "entrepreneurs" right here in Kenya for allegedly running fake investment scams. It turns out that those "global corporate" partnerships they were bragging about on their WhatsApp status were as real as a socialite's original nose.

The operation spanned 16 African countries, because apparently, being a fraudster is the only pan-African dream that actually works. These geniuses were using messaging apps and social media to lure victims with buy-ins as low as fifty dollars. Imagine being so desperate for a "soft life" that you give your hard-earned six thousand shillings to a stranger behind a generic stock photo. It is the classic "fake it till you make it" strategy, but instead of making it to the Forbes list, they made it to the booking room.

We all know this is just a spin on [The New Wash Wash 2.0: How Nairobi Scammers Trade Fake Gold for AI Deepfakes](/posts/new-wash-wash-20-nairobi-scammers-ai-deepfakes-mulot) but with better branding and worse execution. While the Nigerian syndicate was busy building actual mansions as operational hubs, our Kenyan brothers were allegedly just blocking withdrawal requests and showing people fabricated account statements. It is the ultimate toxic relationship: they take your money, show you pretty pictures of what you could have had, and then ghost you the moment you ask for a commitment or a refund.

Interpol seized over two thousand devices and took down thousands of malicious IPs across the continent. It is honestly exhausting to watch. While we are out here trying to figure out if our favorite influencers are actually rich or just really good at using Canva to make fake bank statements, the police are reminding us that the hustle is often just a heist. If your "mentor" is promising you high returns from a fifty dollar investment, they are not a financial genius; they are just a bored person with a SIM card and a script.

The hypocrisy is the delicious cherry on top. These syndicates talk about "empowering communities" while they are out there harvesting sensitive personal data and imposing abusive debt-collection practices. It is the same energy as a politician promising a road that never appears. At the end of the day, whether it is a fake investment or a fake lifestyle, the only thing being "multiplied" in this city is the audacity. Stay broke and safe, kids, because the "high-yield" life is currently sitting in a cell.
