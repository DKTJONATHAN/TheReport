So, the State Department of Industry, led by Dr. Juma Mukhwana, is at it again, waving Kenya's flag and shouting about being a manufacturing and industrial hub. They're inviting the whole world to come and "invest" in local production, "transfer" technology, and "develop" skills. It sounds lovely on paper, doesn't it? Like a fairy tale where everyone gets a job and Kenya magically transforms into Singapore. The PS himself assures us Kenya is "open for business" with a "stable, innovative environment." Right. Stable for who, exactly?

Apparently, our economy grew by a whole 4.6% in 2025, with the construction sector doing a bit of a comeback and mining expanding. This all translates to "increasing demand for industrial equipment." Funny how that demand doesn't seem to be creating enough jobs to go around. The manufacturing sector is still stuck at a measly 7.1% of GDP, and the grand plan is to nudge it up to 15% by 2027. That's like trying to push a hippo uphill with a toothpick. And all this talk about electric mobility and smart manufacturing? It's the same song and dance, just with shinier buzzwords. It reminds me of when Nairobi celebs started spending millions to escape the 'wealthy' look, thinking a new trend would solve deep-seated issues.

The Kenya National Chamber of Commerce and Industry, bless their optimistic hearts, is also on the bandwagon, calling the expo a "strategic platform." They're encouraging businesses to adopt modern tech. Sure, if businesses can afford it and if the infrastructure actually supports it. Meanwhile, KenInvest is busy pitching Kenya as "Africa's most attractive investment destination." They trot out the usual lines about strategic location and expanding infrastructure. And then there's the juicy bit: over 350 Chinese companies are already here, and bilateral trade is booming. They even attracted $243 million in Chinese FDI. So, while they're telling us Kenya is open for business, it seems China already got the memo and is doing brisk business.

This three-day expo at KICC, with its theme "Smart Manufacturing Africa, Shared Future," is supposed to be a catalyst for partnerships, tech transfer, and market linkages. It’s a gathering of over 200 enterprises, government officials, investors, and business leaders. They'll talk, they'll shake hands, they'll pose for photos, and then they'll go back to their respective corners. We'll be left here, waiting for the promised transformation, the jobs, and the value addition. Let's just hope this isn't another one of those fancy spectacles that leaves the ordinary Kenyan even further behind.
