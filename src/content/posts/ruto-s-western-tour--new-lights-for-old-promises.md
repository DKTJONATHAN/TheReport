Ruto's Western Tour: More Hot Air Than Light for the Hustlers

WARNING: Another presidential tour, another round of promises and photo ops. While the President claims to be bringing light to rural Kenya, it's worth questioning how much of it actually reaches the pockets of the "hustlers" he so readily invokes.

The President's five-day trip through Western Kenya, conveniently ending on Friday, was plastered with announcements of electrification projects. The "Last Mile Electricity Connectivity Projects" sounds grand, right? They even wheeled out the REREC CEO to spout the usual platitudes about "touching Kenyans" and "bringing life." But let's be real, folks. For every household that suddenly sees a bulb flicker on, how many more are still in the dark, struggling to make ends meet? This isn't about empowerment; it's about ticking boxes for a press release.

We're told there were "emotional scenes" as beneficiaries shared stories of "socio-economic empowerment." Ah yes, the classic tear-jerker. Suddenly a welder can set up shop at home, or a mama mboga can afford a blender. It’s a nice narrative, but is it sustainable? Or is it just enough to get the cameras rolling and the headlines singing? Remember, Nairobi Is A Crime Scene And Nobody Is Coming To Save You, and this kind of superficial progress won't change that fundamental reality for most.

The numbers are impressive, of course: Ksh 6.3 billion for 889 projects, aiming to connect nearly 90,000 new customers. Sounds like a lot, doesn't it? But break it down. That's roughly Ksh 70,000 per connection. When you factor in the ongoing corruption and mismanagement that plagues this country, you have to wonder how much of that money actually ends up powering homes and how much ends up in someone's offshore account. The Ksh 32.9 million for 325 households? That's even more per household.

And let's not forget the usual entourage of ministers and governors, all basking in the reflected glow of the presidential tour. They pose for pictures, shake hands, and probably share a few hearty laughs, all while the real issues facing ordinary Kenyans - like the ever-rising cost of living and the lack of genuine economic opportunity - continue to fester. It's a well-worn script, and frankly, it's getting tired.

So, while the headlines scream about lights turning on, let's keep our eyes open. Is this a genuine step towards progress, or just another expensive PR exercise designed to make the powers that be look good? History suggests the latter.













President William Ruto wrapped up his five-day development blitz through Western Kenya on Friday, leaving behind a trail of commissioned projects and, no doubt, a hefty bill. The star of the show? The Last Mile Electricity Connectivity Projects, rolled out by the Rural Electrification and Renewable Energy Corporation (REREC). They're selling it as bringing power to the people, turning promises into reality for homes and businesses in remote corners of the country.

The official narrative paints a picture of emotional beneficiaries, their lives transformed by the magic of the national grid. We're hearing about mama mbogas upgrading their stalls and welders setting up shop in their backyards. It's a heartwarming tale, perfect for photo opportunities. But let's be clear: in a country where basic necessities are a luxury for many, a single light bulb isn't going to solve systemic poverty. This is about creating a visual narrative, not necessarily a lasting change.

The numbers are thrown around like confetti: Ksh 6.3 billion allocated for nearly 900 projects, connecting over 88,000 new customers. Sounds impressive, but when you consider the vast sums of money that disappear into the ether through corruption and mismanagement, you have to wonder how much of that actually materializes into working power lines and affordable electricity. For every home that gets connected, how many remain in the dark, forgotten by the system? This feels like another iteration of the same old song and dance.

The accompanying ministers and governors, of course, were all smiles and handshakes, lending their gravitas to the presidential spectacle. They touted the economic inclusivity and industrial growth that electricity supposedly unlocks. But for those of us who’ve seen countless such tours come and go, the reality is often far less glamorous. It’s a performance, a carefully curated display designed to project an image of progress, while the fundamental issues that plague places like Nairobi Is A Crime Scene And Nobody Is Coming To Save You, continue unabated.

The REREC CEO, Dr. Rose Mkalama, waxed lyrical about "touching Kenyans" and "bringing life and meaning." It's a noble sentiment, but one that often rings hollow when juxtaposed with the daily struggles of survival for most. While the intent might be there, the execution and the follow-through are what truly matter. And based on past experience, it's wise to approach these grand pronouncements with a healthy dose of skepticism.

Now the President is off to Nyanza. Let's see if the electricity they promise there will be enough to illuminate the stark realities of economic inequality, or if it's just more smoke and mirrors.
