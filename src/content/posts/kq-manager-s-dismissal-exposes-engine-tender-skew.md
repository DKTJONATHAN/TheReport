KQ Manager's Dismissal Unpacks Engine Tender Mess

This whole drama at Kenya Airways, the national carrier-if you can even call it that-is just another episode in the Kenyan comedy of errors. Turns out, a top manager got the boot, and it's all because someone decided to pay more for a German engine part instead of going with the cheaper Israeli option. Because, obviously, why save money when you can just burn it? It's the usual song and dance: officials making questionable decisions, and then someone else taking the fall when it all blows up.

The court, bless its heart, declared the dismissal of Daniel Okello procedurally flawed. Apparently, the supervisor who pointed the finger also sat in on the disciplinary hearing. Shocking, I know. So, while the airline had grounds to fire him for defying orders - because who dares question the bosses, right? - the way they did it was a mess. Bias and lack of transparency, they call it. I call it business as usual in this country.

Okello, the brave whistleblower, tried to argue that steering clear of the best deal for a more expensive third-tier supplier was a violation of everything from procurement manuals to ethics codes. He saw the financial strain on KQ - which, let's be honest, is a permanent fixture these days - and tried to do the right thing. But in Kenya, doing the right thing often just means getting yourself in trouble. It’s a bit like [The Broad-Based Buffet: When the Vultures Start Biting Each Other](/posts/the-broad-based-buffet--when-the-vultures-start-biting-each-other), just with less feasting and more firings.

The airline, naturally, backed its superior, claiming the MD and COO had ordered the halt. Okello, the stubborn one, kept negotiating, leading to accusations of insubordination and a general "insolent attitude." He was given a chance to explain himself, but his defence that he was protecting the company from bad deals apparently wasn't convincing enough. The disciplinary hearing, the appeal - all just rubber stamps to get rid of the inconvenient truth-teller.

In the end, Okello gets a paltry six months' salary, a slap on the wrist compared to the Sh161.7 million he was asking for. The court deemed his claims speculative, and frankly, who can blame them? Proving illegality and financial loss in this circus is like finding a unicorn. So, the engine tender dispute is "exposed," but the real rot - the systemic corruption and the abuse of power - remains comfortably hidden. Just another Tuesday at the national airline.
