The Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) has finally decided to "note with grave concern" what every woman in Nairobi with a social media account has known for months: we are being hunted for content. This time, the villain of the week is a suspected Russian national who reportedly spent his "holiday" recording and circulating intimate videos of Kenyan women without their consent. It’s the same old script - a foreigner arrives with a passport and a GoPro, treats the local population like a buffet, and the authorities only wake up once the Telegram links start hitting the group chats of the "moral" elite.

This isn't just a "serious violation of privacy," as the DCI’s PR machine puts it; it’s the inevitable endgame of a city that has sold its soul for foreign exchange. As I’ve noted before in [Kenya’s Oldest Hustle in 5G: How the Digital Nomad Visa Supercharged a Quiet Sex Economy](/posts/kenyas-oldest-hustle-in-5g), we’ve spent years rolling out the red carpet for "investors" and "digital nomads" without checking what they’re actually doing behind their MacBook screens. We built the high-speed infrastructure for the world to see us, and now we’re acting shocked that the view includes the inside of our bedrooms.

Gender CS Hanna Cheptumo is talking big about an "aggressive manhunt" and "international law enforcement partners." It sounds great in a press release, doesn't it? But let’s be real - while the DCI is "activating specialized units" to trace digital footprints, the suspect is likely already sipping vodka in a jurisdiction that doesn't care about Kenyan "constitutional protections." The government loves to talk about "national values" only after those values have been packaged, uploaded, and monetized on some dark-web forum.

The DCI is now urging victims to "come forward" to their headquarters, promising "confidentiality, dignity, and sensitivity." In a country where the police are better known for extortion than empathy, that’s a tall order. Asking a victim of technology-facilitated GBV to walk into a station and explain her trauma to a sergeant who probably thinks "the cloud" is something that brings rain is a special kind of joke. We tell women to #FichuaKwaDCI, but the system is designed to make the victim feel like the criminal for being in the room in the first place.

And then there’s the warning to the public: don't share the videos or you’ll face "criminal liability." It’s a classic move - threaten the small fish while the shark is already halfway across the ocean. The digital footprint is a permanent scar, and no amount of "coordination with foreign authorities" is going to hit the delete button on the internet. We’ve turned Nairobi into a playground for predators with high-speed internet, and now we’re surprised that the swings are broken and the kids are being filmed.

In the end, this isn't just about one Russian guy with a sick hobby. It’s about a state that protects "investor" status more than its own citizens. The DCI can "reiterate its commitment" all it wants, but until we stop treating foreign predators like VIPs, our women will continue to be nothing more than "harmful content" in someone else’s cloud storage. Welcome to the new Nairobi, where the only thing cheaper than the data is the dignity of the people providing the "engagement."
