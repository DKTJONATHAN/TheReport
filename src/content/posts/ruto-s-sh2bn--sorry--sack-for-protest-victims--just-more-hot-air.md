RUTO'S SH2BN 'SORRY' SACK FOR PROTEST VICTIMS: JUST MORE HOT AIR?

President Ruto unveils a Sh2 billion package for over 1,000 victims of state brutality, calling it a "beginning of the end." Yeah, right. After years of promises and more pain, Kenyans are expected to swallow this as genuine atonement. Let's see how long this chapter actually stays closed before the next one begins.

So, the government has "approved" a compensation program. Sh2 billion for more than a thousand people who suffered under the boot of the state. We're talking right to life, sexual violence, torture, freedom and security of the person, property rights, and the ever-popular enforced disappearance. It’s a sad list, but is this money actually going to bring back lives lost or heal the scars? Don't hold your breath. This is the same government that unleashed its agents on peaceful protesters, including the "Gen Z protests" that surely reminded some of us of the darker days, like those documented in [Ruai's Human Cargo: Another Day in the Trafficking Hub of Africa](/posts/ruai-s-human-cargo--another-day-in-the-trafficking-hub-of-africa).

President Ruto waxes lyrical about a "painful chapter" and how Kenya can't keep going "protest after protest, tragedy after tragedy." He even admits the "excessive use of force" and "loss of life" during demonstrations. But let's be real, this isn't some sudden epiphany. It’s a response to pressure, a move to quiet the noise before it gets louder. The "reasonable basis to believe" standard for verification sounds official, but we all know how "reasonable" things can get when the powerful are involved.

The KNCHR chair, Claris Ogangah, talks about "voiceless children" and the constitution being ignored. And Prof Makau Mutua, the expert, speaks of "atonement" and the state accepting its "duty to repair." It’s a lot of fancy words, isn't it? They’re acknowledging the suffering, sure. But is this compensation a genuine act of restorative justice, as MP Kimani Ichungwah suggests, or just another political maneuver to tick a box and move on? Experience tells us the latter is far more likely.

Junet Mohammed, bless his heart, notes that no regime has been "bold enough" to tackle this. Perhaps they’ve been too busy creating the problems in the first place. The Sh2 billion sounds like a lot, but when you divide it among over a thousand victims, it starts to look less like a life-changing sum and more like a token gesture. Especially when you consider the scale of violations, which the KNCHR hints might be even larger than what they've managed to document.

The government wants us to believe this is the end of a dark era. But until the police brutality stops, until accountability is real and not just for show, and until the victims can actually see their lives meaningfully improved, this Sh2 billion compensation package will just be another headline, another promise that’s more about optics than actual change. We’ve heard it all before.
