So, you’ve felt that prickle of unease, that sense of a presence lurking just beyond perception. Turns out, it’s probably not Casper’s cousin, but rather the hum of the refrigerator or the distant rumble of traffic. A new study out of Canada suggests that those supposedly supernatural chills are more likely the result of infrasound - sound waves below the range of human hearing. Apparently, our delicate constitutions are easily rattled by these unseen vibrations, leading to increased stress hormones and a general feeling of "the heebie jeebies."

Researchers exposed volunteers to these imperceptible low frequencies and, surprise surprise, they reported feeling more irritable and stressed. The study’s lead scientist, Rodney Schmaltz, clarifies that infrasound doesn't conjure apparitions, but rather provides an "unexplainable discomfort" that fertile imaginations then readily label as a haunting. This is hardly groundbreaking; it’s merely a scientific validation of what many have suspected: humans are remarkably adept at projecting their own anxieties onto ambiguous stimuli.

The fact that 60% of Americans claim to have experienced some form of paranormal event, with "feeling a presence" being the most common, just underscores how easily our minds can be swayed. We’re not talking about genuine spectral encounters here, but rather the predictable outcomes of a suggestible mind in an environment primed for spookiness. The backstories of famous hauntings are often fabricated, and the "evidence" is usually a mishmash of misinterpretation and outright fabrication.

Infrasound, defined as sound at or below 20 hertz, can be physically felt as pressure or vibration, even if we can't technically "hear" it. While some researchers have explored its potential link to hauntings with mixed results, this latest study provides a more controlled, albeit still limited, demonstration. Volunteers exposed to infrasound, without even realizing it, exhibited increased irritability and higher cortisol levels. Crucially, their perceptions didn't change whether they consciously believed infrasound was present or not, suggesting the effects are subconscious.

This isn't exactly a paradigm shift. We already know that our environment influences our mood. Ventilation systems, industrial equipment, and even heavy traffic generate these low-frequency sounds. The implication is that prolonged exposure to these common environmental factors can subtly shift our emotional state, making us more prone to interpreting ambiguous sensations as something more sinister.

The researchers themselves admit the study's sample size is small, but they point to other existing research that supports their findings. The idea that infrasound can induce "unusual experiences" isn't entirely new. What's more interesting is the potential for this research to be applied beyond the confines of haunted houses. If common, everyday sources of infrasound can negatively impact our mood and stress levels without us even noticing, it suggests a widespread, albeit often ignored, factor influencing our well-being.

Ultimately, this study doesn't debunk hauntings entirely, but it does offer a convenient, scientifically plausible explanation for a significant portion of them. It’s a comforting thought for those who’ve had a "ghostly encounter": you weren't necessarily hallucinating, you were just subjected to a bit of low-frequency sonic manipulation. Expectation and misperception remain key players, but infrasound appears to be another predictable ingredient in the recipe for manufactured spookiness.
