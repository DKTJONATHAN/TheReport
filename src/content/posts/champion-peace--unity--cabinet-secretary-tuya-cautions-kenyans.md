CABINET SECRETARY TUYA URGES PEACE, NATIONAL UNITY - AS IF WE HAVEN'T HEARD IT ALL BEFORE











When Cabinet Secretaries start rambling about peace and unity, you know election season is just around the corner, or maybe they just want us to forget about our empty pockets for a minute. Soipan Tuya, bless her heart, has been out in Narok South, inspecting what I assume are functioning health facilities - a rare sight these days, apparently. Alongside Governor Ole Ntutu, she’s pleading with us to ditch the ethnic squabbles and embrace this magical "peaceful coexistence." It's like they think we're the ones stirring the pot, not the politicians who profit from it.

Tuya reckons the Kenya Kwanza administration is showering development projects everywhere, no discrimination. She’s also quick to remind us how blessed Kenya is with peace and stability, especially compared to our "neighbouring countries." Yes, because complaining about the potholes on Lang'ata Road is *exactly* the same as grappling with actual political instability. It’s almost as if they haven't heard the whispers about the mounting debt or the everyday struggles most of us face.

She's particularly worried about leaders pushing narratives that divide us, especially with 2027 looming. She warned against "ethnic balkanization," as if it’s a new disease and not the same old song and dance. She’s even recalling the violence of the past, probably hoping we’ll be too scared to even *think* about asking for better. It’s a classic move: scare them with ghosts of the past so they don’t notice the present rot. Just last week, I was reading about the unsettling parallels of unrest in historical contexts, a stark reminder that some patterns are hard to break, especially when leaders are actively creating them. Tuya wants us to believe Narok is some kind of model of unity. Sure, some places might be a bit calmer, but let's not pretend ethnic differences are just a minor inconvenience. Leaders, she says, should foster unity, not exploit differences for their "narrow political gains." Revolutionary stuff, really. Meanwhile, she’s also lamenting that some leaders are busy "painting our country black" to the international community. Well, maybe if the country wasn't being painted with the colours of corruption and broken promises, foreigners wouldn't see the shades so clearly.

Her plea for patriotism and for us to "love our country more than anybody else" sounds nice on paper, especially when she asks what kind of country we’re leaving for our children. It’s a valid question, but it rings hollow when the very people demanding this love are the ones slowly dismantling the foundations. She’s worried about our kids copying us, but what about them copying the leaders who seem more interested in grandstanding than good governance? She’s right, though: the praise Kenya gets abroad often feels like a distant dream compared to the reality on the ground.

Governor Ole Ntutu chimes in with his own calls for justice and respect for democratic processes. He wants leaders to focus on "wananchi" and avoid divisive actions. All very noble sentiments. He also jumps to the defence of the Kenya Kwanza administration’s "difficult policy decisions." Apparently, these reforms are for our own good, even if they’re making life harder right now. It’s the usual script: endure the pain now for a brighter future that never quite arrives.
