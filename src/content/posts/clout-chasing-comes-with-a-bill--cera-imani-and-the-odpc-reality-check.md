Oh, honey, the "Main Character Energy" is finally hitting a very expensive wall. Our favorite local content creators have spent years acting like the internet is their personal playground where other people’s lives are just props for their aesthetic. Well, the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC) just walked into the party and turned off the music. It turns out that posting someone's face or their private documents without a signed contract isn't "networking" - it is actually a legal nightmare that carries a price tag.

Take Waithera Imani, or Cera Imani as she’s known to her TikTok followers who thrive on her promotional drivel. She thought she could just slap Ian Itolondo Mutoro’s name and face onto a video for some app called Eshe Community without his permission. She claimed she had consent, but when the Data Commissioner, Immaculate Kassait, asked for the receipts, Cera had absolutely nothing to show. Now she is out Sh50,000. For an influencer, that is a lot of ring lights and overpriced lattes down the drain just because she could not be bothered to send a simple email.

Then we have Mwachere Shuma, a blogger from Taita Taveta who apparently thinks he is a private investigator. This man actually went out of his way to find John Mwang’ombe’s degree certificate from Kampala International University and post it on Facebook and WhatsApp. Why? For the "tea," I suppose. But the tea was bitter, and now he owes Sh25,000 for his little stunt. It is embarrassing, really. These people are so desperate for engagement that they have forgotten that regular citizens actually have rights to their own information.

This whole situation just screams amateur hour. These influencers treat their followers like pawns in a game, much like how some critics argue [Rita Ora’s missing ring](/posts/rita-ora-s-missing-ring--just-another-scene-in-the-never-ending-pr-play) was just another scene in a never-ending PR play designed to keep her relevant. In Kenya, the PR play is becoming a legal liability. You cannot just "process" someone's data - which is a fancy way of saying "stalking them for content" - and expect to get away with it anymore. The ODPC is making it clear: if you are not serving the public interest, you better have a signature on the dotted line.

The irony is delicious. These creators spend so much time curate-ing their "perfect" lives, yet they cannot even follow basic privacy laws. They want the fame and the brand deals, but they do not want the responsibility that comes with being a "data controller." It is time for a reality check. If you are going to build a career out of being in everyone’s business, you might want to make sure your own legal house is not made of very thin, very transparent glass. Stay humble, or stay broke - the choice is yours, "celebs."
