So, EPRA, the folks who supposedly keep our wheels greased, have decided that for the March to April 2026 period, things are… stable. Nairobi motorists, bless their optimistic hearts, will still be coughing up Sh178.28 for a litre of petrol. Diesel is a mere Sh166.54, and kerosene, for those still clinging to that primitive light source, is Sh152.78. And before you start dreaming of faraway Mombasas or Kisumus with their slightly less punishing prices, know that the difference is pocket change when you’re already being fleeced.

EPRA’s excuse? Some bureaucratic mumbo jumbo about "February-priced cargoes" arriving and the "effect of the situation in the Middle East has not affected the prices yet." Haven't affected them *yet*. That's the kicker, isn't it? They're essentially telling us, "Enjoy it while it lasts, because the dam is about to break." All this while the Strait of Hormuz is apparently playing dodgeball with ships and major shipping firms are having a collective panic attack. Kenya, being the proud importer of almost everything we consume, is as exposed as a politician caught in a bribery scandal.

Now, enter the saviour, Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi, reassuring us that we have "sufficient stocks." This sounds suspiciously like the same empty promises we hear every time there's a whiff of trouble, reminiscent of how some folks handle scandals, like that whole Amas Obasogie situation, where "technical errors" seemed to cover up more than they revealed. He claims the Ministry has "reviewed the supply and stock situation." Reviewed it, sure. But can they *control* it when global powers are playing war games with oil routes?

The truth is, "unchanged" doesn't mean "good." It means "we're waiting for the storm to hit." It means the prices we see now are a temporary lull before the inevitable global shockwaves find their way to our struggling economy. So, go ahead, fill up your tank. Just remember this moment of deceptive calm the next time you're staring at a pump price that makes your eyes water. Because, darling, the real story isn't that prices are stable; it's how fragile that stability truly is.
