The fourth contingent of 150 Kenyan police officers has returned to the country after their deployment to Haiti as part of the Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) Mission. The officers arrived at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport on Tuesday night and were met by senior government and security officials.

The returning team was led by Deputy Inspector General of the Administration Police Service, Gilbert Masengeli, who had been in Haiti to oversee the drawdown. Inspector General of Police Douglas Kanja, Acting National Security Advisor Joseph Boinett, and Deputy Inspector General of the Kenya Police Service, Eliud Lagat, were among those who welcomed the officers.

This return signifies the ongoing transition of the MSS mission into a United Nations-backed Gang Suppression Force (GSF), established under United Nations Security Council Resolution 2793 (2025). The GSF is intended to address the persistent security challenges posed by armed gangs in Haiti. The Kenyan-led deployment, initiated in mid-2024, aimed to work with Haitian authorities to restore order and enhance local policing capabilities.

During his visit, Masengeli met with Contingent Commander Godfrey Otunge and other international representatives, receiving briefings on operational successes such as joint security operations with the Haitian National Police, protection of critical infrastructure, and efforts to stabilize gang-affected areas. This latest group of officers adds to the 208 repatriated in March, bringing the total number of Kenyan officers who have exited the mission to 803.

Officials highlighted the MSS mission's role in assisting Haitian authorities in combating organized criminal gangs, securing key installations, and facilitating humanitarian aid. The forthcoming GSF is expected to involve broader international participation, with an estimated 5,500 personnel from contributing countries. This wider framework aims to achieve long-term security restoration in Haiti. The current operational achievements, while presented as successes, must be viewed against the backdrop of the persistent instability and the ongoing, costly international involvement. It highlights a continuous cycle of intervention rather than a definitive solution, much like Kenya's climate risk insurance: another fancy idea for the same old mess.
