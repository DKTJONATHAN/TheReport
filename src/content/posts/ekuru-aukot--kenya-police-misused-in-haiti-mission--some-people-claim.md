Ekuru Aukot: Kenya Police Misused in Haiti Mission, Some People Claim

Ekuru Aukot is out here on Citizen TV, doing his usual "I told you so" routine about this Haiti mission. And honestly, who can blame him? He’s saying the whole thing is a spectacular failure, and our police, bless their uniforms, were just pawns in some poorly planned game. He's spitting facts, or at least his version of them, that we "violated our own Constitution." Sounds about right for Nairobi these days, doesn't it?

According to Aukot, the mission didn't even achieve its basic goal. Gangs are still running wild in Haiti, and our boys were sent in to… what exactly? Look pretty? He's harping on the High Court ruling that this whole deployment was unconstitutional. Apparently, we "misused our National Police Service against the law." It’s like watching a bad Nollywood movie where everyone knows the script is terrible, but they go ahead and film it anyway.

Then there's the whole legitimacy issue. Aukot claims Haiti's request for intervention wasn't from a "duly elected authority." Apparently, a prime minister who "was not properly in office" can't just invite foreign troops. This is the kind of technicality that makes you wonder if anyone in government actually reads the fine print, or if they just assume the rules don't apply to them, much like how we navigate [NTSA’s New Price List: How Much It Costs to Breathe on Nairobi Roads](/posts/ntsa-s-new-price-list--how-much-it-costs-to-breathe-on-nairobi-roads).

Meanwhile, back home, the government is all smiles and pomp for the returning police contingent. Principal Secretary Raymond Omollo is out there, praising their "sacrifice" and "dedication" in "difficult and often dangerous conditions." They "uphold the highest standards of professionalism, discipline and courage," apparently. It’s a nice narrative, but when you hear the same story about politicians and their supposed "achievements," you start to develop a healthy dose of skepticism.

This whole Kenya-led mission, backed by the UN and a smattering of other countries, began in 2024 with promises of restoring order. But if Aukot is to be believed, and his arguments have a certain ring of truth to them, it was more of a political stunt than a genuine intervention. Sending our police into a quagmire based on questionable authority and then celebrating their return with platitudes? Classic Nairobi logic.
