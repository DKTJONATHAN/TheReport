The Ol Kalou by-election, called after an MP kicked the bucket, was always going to be a spectacle. You had Ruto's UDA versus his former deputy Rigathi Gachagua's new outfit. These two, once thick as thieves, are now spitting venom at each other, ever since Gachagua got the boot in 2024. Ol Kalou, 150km from Nairobi, is "strategically important" for 2027, which in plain language means it's a testing ground for all the usual dirty tricks.

Voters turned out in droves, initially peaceful, they say. But we all know how long that lasts. Soon enough, police vehicles were being blocked and then, *surprise, surprise*, masked men in unmarked 4x4s started firing tear-gas. Other footage showed men with sticks doing their job - terrorising voters and, of course, journalists. Apparently, their equipment was "stolen." It's almost predictable, isn't it? Just another day, another hustle, as they say. Over 1,000 police officers were deployed, but you have to wonder if they were there to keep order or just provide background scenery for the chaos. The Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC), in its usual performative outrage, reported vote-buying and intimidation, even threatening to cancel the whole thing. As if that ever truly happens. Then Interior Minister Murkomen pops up, promising a "comprehensive investigation" and not ruling out police involvement. "Anyone found guilty must be arrested," he declares. We've heard that song before, haven't we? It's just noise.

Gachagua, predictably, jumped on X to accuse "rogue police officers" of causing chaos to rig results. He called it "a test of what will happen next year" and declared it "now clear that we will not have peaceful general elections next year." Well, wasn't that already clear to anyone who's been paying attention to Kenyan politics for, oh, more than five minutes? The Law Society of Kenya, bless their hearts, chimed in with their "concern" over intimidation, calling for investigations and declaring Ol Kalou a "crucial test." It's a test we've failed repeatedly since the deadly violence of 2007, and it seems we're just setting ourselves up for another round.
