Our "diplomatic genius" at the top has done it again. While the Middle East turns into a literal tinderbox, President Ruto decided to play favorites, backing the US and Israel before his own Ministry of Foreign Affairs had to run behind him with a mop. They call it "clarification," but we know it as panic. It is the classic Nairobi hustle: talk big to the bosses in the West and then act surprised when the 500,000 Kenyans working in the Gulf suddenly find themselves in the crosshairs of a regional war.

The statistics are being tossed around like they do not represent actual human beings. We are told 300,000 Kenyans are in Saudi Arabia and thousands more are scattered across Qatar and the UAE. Diaspora PS Roseline Njogu is on TV telling people to "limit movement," as if you can just hide in a closet when missiles are flying and airspaces are slamming shut. It is becoming painfully obvious that [the global order is dying and we are paying for the funeral](/posts/the-global-order-is-dying-and-we-are-paying-for-the-funeral) with the safety of our own citizens.

Then there is the fuel. If you thought the post-Ukraine spike was bad, wait until you see the "Hormuz Special." Oil prices have already jumped 13 percent, and CS Opiyo Wandayi is trying to calm us down by saying we have supplies until April. That is government-speak for "start budgeting for a bicycle." We are a net importer, which means every time a tanker gets stuck in the Gulf, the price of unga in downtown Nairobi takes a flight. We are not "monitoring a fluid situation"; we are drowning in it.

The economic fallout is not just about the pump. Our tea exports and fresh produce are sitting in limbo while 3,000 ships are stranded. Treasury CS John Mbadi is already warming us up for another "Covid-style" hit to the economy. It is the same old script: the elite make the "strategic" blunders, and the ordinary Kenyan is told to "manoeuvre" through the wreckage. Remittances are the only thing keeping our foreign exchange from flatlining, yet we are watching our workers get trapped in a war zone with no clear way out.

In the end, this "market diversification" talk from the Trade Ministry is just a fancy way of saying they have no plan. You do not diversify your way out of a global supply chain collapse in a week. While the big powers trade fire, our leaders are busy trying to look important on the world stage, ignoring the fact that their citizens are one missile strike away from a total catastrophe. It is cynical, it is predictable, and as usual, the bill is coming straight to us.
