California reggae band Stick Figure, after two decades in the business, finds itself in a predictable quagmire. Their track "Angels Above Me," a seven year old deep cut, inexplicably shot to the top of iTunes charts in multiple countries. This surge, however, wasn't fueled by organic fan adoration but by a swarm of unauthorized AI generated remixes. Lead singer Scott Woodruff expressed dismay, noting that the explosive popularity was derived from "stolen and generated in one click" versions, for which the band receives no compensation.

This isn't a novel problem, merely an accelerated and democratized version of old exploitation. For years, the music industry has grappled with unauthorized remixes and mashups. The advent of AI tools has transformed this into a high volume, low effort operation. Deezer reports a dramatic increase in AI detected songs, with a significant percentage estimated to be fraudulent attempts to steal royalties. Companies are actively marketing tools to facilitate this ersatz music creation, effectively turning creativity into a commodity to be plundered.

Stick Figure's management team is engaged in a futile game of whack a mole, issuing copyright takedown notices and contacting streamers and offending parties. While some platforms have complied, others remain defiant, with one remixer even attempting to pass off their AI creation as a cover, offering a pittance of royalties. This highlights the systemic rot: the technology is enabling theft, and the legal and ethical frameworks are woefully inadequate to police it.

The trajectory mirrors past events, such as the sped up TikTok remixes of Steve Lacy's "Bad Habit." The trend became so pronounced that Lacy's label encouraged an official release to capitalize on the unauthorized popularity, a testament to the industry's tendency to embrace and monetize its own degradation. Stick Figure's plight is a stark reminder that in the current digital landscape, an artist's success can be easily manufactured by machines and weaponized against them, with little recourse and even less justice. The future of music appears to be a constant battle against a tidal wave of digital detritus designed solely to siphon off revenue.
