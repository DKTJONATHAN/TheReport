In this city, "human resources" is usually just a fancy name for the department that protects the company from you, not the other way around. We keep pretending that workplace sexual harassment is a simple "policy violation" instead of the crime it actually is. Interns and junior staff are told to file a report and trust the process, but the process is built to silence you. While [AI agents are now running the back office at insurance giants](/posts/ai-agents-are-now-running-the-back-office-at-insurance-giants) to make things efficient, the Kenyan system for reporting predators remains stuck in a loop of endless paperwork and zero accountability.

The Employment Act 2007 is a joke when it comes to real justice. It treats a boss grabbing you like a missed deadline or a late report. Meanwhile, the Sexual Offences Act 2006 actually has teeth-it calls this behavior a crime. But have you ever seen a CEO in handcuffs for what they do behind closed doors? No. We keep it internal. We keep it quiet. We treat a violation of bodily autonomy as a "management issue" because heaven forbid we disrupt the corporate flow or the company’s reputation.

Look at the John Chebochok situation. A BBC documentary had to do the heavy lifting because our own systems were asleep. Even after being exposed for exploitation on tea plantations, the man almost waltzed into a director seat. If it wasn't for a civil case in Kericho, he would still be calling the shots. It is the same story at the Law Society of Kenya. When the very people who literally write and interpret the law are the ones preying on pupils and interns, you know the rot is deep.

The state expects survivors to be heroes. They want you to navigate the HR maze, go to the police, deal with the trauma, and somehow not get blacklisted in an industry where everyone knows everyone. It is a suicide mission for your career. We are told to report, but the system is designed to break you before you even get to a courtroom. The power imbalance is so skewed that most people just quit and move on, leaving the predator to wait for the next "junior associate" to walk through the door.

The Ministry of Labour can talk about "mandatory reporting" all they want, but until the boardroom door stops acting as a shield for criminals, nothing changes. Employers need to stop acting like parallel justice systems that can settle crimes with a handshake or a non-disclosure agreement. If it is a crime on the street, it is a crime in the office. Until the police are called before the "internal investigation" starts, Nairobi’s offices will remain hunting grounds for those with a title and a corner office.
