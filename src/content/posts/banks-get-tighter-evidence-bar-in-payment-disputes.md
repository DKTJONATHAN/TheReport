Banks Face Tougher Proof in Payment Bust-Ups

The courts have finally woken up and told banks they can't just wave their internal paperwork around like a magic wand to prove they've actually paid someone. If a client says they didn't get their cash, the bank better have more than just their own scribbled notes to show for it. It's about time someone cracked down on these financial giants who think their internal mumbo jumbo is gospel.

So, Consolidated Bank thought they could pull a fast one on Muteithia Kibira Advocates LLP, claiming they'd paid a Sh72,460 deposit for legal defense. The law firm, naturally, said "Uh, no." The bank's defence? A fancy "Maker Checker" stamp and a payment schedule, the kind of stuff they cook up internally. The High Court in Nairobi, thankfully, saw through this charade. They pointed out that internal approvals are just that - approvals. It doesn't mean the money actually left the bank and landed in the advocate's account. It's like saying you've baked a cake because you've got the flour and eggs out, but you haven't actually put it in the oven.

Justice Benard Murunga, bless his pragmatic soul, essentially said that the real proof of payment is the *receipt* of the money, not the *preparation* of it. He’s basically told banks to get with the program in this age of Real Time Gross Settlements and Electronic Fund Transfers. Where’s the remittance advice? Where are the electronic transfer records? Banks should be handing over signed and stamped proof of transfer, just like they do for a simple cash deposit. This whole argument about "unjust enrichment" is a joke if they can't even prove they paid in the first place. Imagine, claiming someone got paid twice when you can't even show they got paid once. It's the kind of logic that makes you wonder about the intellectual capacity of some of these financial wizards.

The court highlighted that if Consolidated Bank had any shred of evidence beyond their internal dockets, they should have produced it when the advocates were hounding them for payment for years. Their silence and lack of response to those demands speaks volumes, a deafening silence that screams, "We didn't actually pay." This whole debacle is a stark reminder that in business, like in life, actions speak louder than internal memos. Relying on internal processes when faced with a dispute is just lazy, and as this ruling shows, it's a losing strategy. You can’t just hide behind your own paperwork. It makes you wonder if banks are just perpetually stuck in a kind of [THE COLLEGIATE CIRCUS: EXPLOITING YOUTH IN THE SHADOW OF A CRUMBLING EMPIRE](https://example.com/posts/the-collegiate-circus--exploiting-youth-in-the-shadow-of-a-crumbling-empire) of internal bureaucracy, detached from the realities of actual transactions.
