So, Kenya’s travel agencies are getting all dressed up with nowhere to go, financially speaking. On one hand, the airlines are finally realising that maybe, just maybe, they should make it easier for these agents to connect. It’s about time, honestly. Digital distribution is getting slicker, and soon it’ll be as easy to book a flight through your local agent as it is to complain about traffic on Wahome Mutahi Road. This is supposedly going to make things better for everyone, especially the big players who can now integrate their fancy new systems without breaking a sweat.

But hold your horses, because while that’s happening, the actual money - the lifeblood of any business - is getting harder to get your hands on. It’s like they’re handing you the keys to a fancy new car, but then telling you there’s no petrol station for miles. The Tourism Regulatory Authority has decided to get all official, making it tougher to even get a license. They want to weed out the "under-resourced operators," which is just a polite way of saying they’re making it harder for the little guys to survive. So, you’ve got rising compliance costs and then this whole NDC integration and digital retailing thing demanding more investment. It’s a classic Nairobi move: pile on the pressure and see who buckles. This whole formalisation push is happening separate from the tech changes, like a bad marriage where nobody talks to each other.

Kenya Airways, bless their ambitious hearts, is trying to do a bit of everything. They’ve got this NDC strategy that’s layered like a badly made ugali. First, they partner with Verteil, then they’re on the Amadeus platform, and now they’ve opened the gates to non-IATA accredited agencies. This last bit is actually a big deal. Apparently, a good chunk of Kenyan travel businesses don’t have the full IATA accreditation, so this finally lets them get in on the modern airline retailing action. It’s like they realised they were leaving money on the table and decided to grab it, even if it means a few more headaches for everyone else. They've built a whole system, which is commendable, but it's still within a system that’s fundamentally changing how money flows.

And that’s where the real pain is. The global travel scene is tightening the screws on how agents get paid. IATA’s decided everyone needs to get paid on a strict weekly cycle, meaning agents have to cough up cash to airlines before their own clients have even paid them. This is a disaster for businesses that rely on credit from corporate and government clients, who, let’s be honest, pay slower than a broken-down matatu. KATA, the local association, is already raising alarms, and rightly so. They’re talking about exposure, which is a polite way of saying agencies are about to be on the hook for cash they haven’t received. This whole thing is making me think of how companies in the Middle East are constantly looking for handouts, [like the begging bowl of industry](/posts/the-begging-bowl-of-industry--socializing-risk-as-the-middle-east-burns), except here, the risk is being pushed down to the smallest players.

Now, the clever local boys like Triply are trying to build something that actually makes sense - a system that combines distribution with the financial tools to actually operate. They’re not waiting for the global giants to sort things out; they’re building it themselves. This is the kind of ambition that could save these agents, but it’s still a gamble. Will it work at scale? We’ll see. Meanwhile, the safari operators are still stuck in the email and phone era, which is a joke when you compare it to how fast aviation is moving. It’s like the country’s tourism is a body with a super-fast heart but legs that refuse to move. They’re modernising in fits and starts, and the agents are caught in the middle, trying to keep their heads above water while the rules of the game are being rewritten by people who clearly don't understand how they actually make a living.
