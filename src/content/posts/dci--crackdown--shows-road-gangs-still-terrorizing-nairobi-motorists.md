HIGHWAY ROBBERY HORROR: DCI "CRACKDOWN" JUST ANOTHER DAY ON THE ROAD

Another week, another headline about the DCI "cracking down" on criminals. This time it's highway robbers on the Mombasa-Nairobi route. Because, you know, before this, everyone was just driving around singing Kumbaya. The good news? Three "masterminds" were nabbed after a midnight chase. The bad news? For every one they catch, ten more are out there, probably planning their next hit while you're stuck in traffic.

These bandits, the "armed" kind, mind you, are apparently targeting public transport and those poor, overloaded trucks. It's not exactly a secret society, is it? Anyone who's ever been stuck in a matatu after dark knows the score. We're talking about desperation meeting opportunity, a classic Nairobi cocktail. It's a miracle it doesn't happen more often, given the state of things.

And let's not forget Wanyoike, the chap who apparently played hide-and-seek with the police for three years. Three years! Makes you wonder about the efficiency of the "crackdown" in the first place. Was he just on holiday, or were they too busy with the tea and mandazi at headquarters?

This whole spectacle of arrests and chases is just the usual song and dance. It’s meant to reassure us, I suppose, that somebody's actually doing something. But really, it’s just a reminder that the infrastructure we’re so proud of, the very arteries of our economy we hear about at events like [The Africa We Build summit and Kenya’s bold infrastructure blueprint](/posts/the-africa-we-build-summit-and-kenya-s-bold-infrastructure-blueprint), are also the hunting grounds for those with nothing to lose.

The wife's recollection of a last conversation before death? A grim reminder of the human cost. But let's be honest, for the average Nairobi commuter, this is just another Tuesday. You lock your doors, clutch your phone, and pray you don't become the next statistic on the DCI's report card.

So, yes, a "crackdown." Three arrests. But the fear on our highways? That's not going anywhere. It's as reliable as the next power outage or the rising cost of maize flour.
