The Recording Academy finally woke up from its decades-long nap to hand the late Fela Anikulapo-Kuti a Lifetime Achievement Award at the 68th Grammys. How incredibly sweet of them to honor the man nearly thirty years after he passed away. It is the ultimate "better late than never" move that reeks of an organization trying to fix its diversity PR nightmare by clinging to a legacy they ignored for half a century. While the Kuti clan-Yeni, Femi, and Seun-put on their best faces to accept the trophy in Los Angeles, one has to wonder if they were holding back a collective eye-roll. It is a full-circle moment, sure, but mostly it is a reminder that the West only validates African genius once it is safe, dead, and buried.

Meanwhile, back in reality, the rest of Nigeria had to swallow a very bitter, trophy-less pill. Despite all the social media noise and the "Afrobeats to the world" hashtags that have been clogging our feeds, the country left the 2026 ceremony with exactly zero wins. After years of Burna Boy and Tems making us believe the Grammys were our new playground, this year felt like a cold shower for the "New Nigeria" ego. It is almost as depressing as [The Luxury of Hitting Rock Bottom](/posts/the-luxury-of-hitting-rock-bottom) when you realize that being "in the building" does not actually mean you are leaving with the hardware. The "global influence" we keep hearing about seems to have hit a very expensive glass ceiling this time around.

Of course, Davido was spotted lurking around the Kuti family during the festivities. If you cannot win a Grammy of your own despite the global craze, I suppose the next best thing is to stand close enough to a Lifetime Achievement Award and hope some of that legendary aura rubs off on your Instagram feed. Made Kuti spent his time reminding everyone that his grandfather was "on the same professional level" as the world's best. We already knew that, Made. The real question is why the Recording Academy took until the 68th annual show to admit it. The hypocrisy of the industry is louder than a Fela horn section on a Friday night at the Shrine.

The irony reached peak levels when President Tinubu joined the praise-singing from afar, calling Fela a "fearless voice" and a "philosopher of freedom." It is hilarious how the establishment loves a revolutionary once he is no longer around to call them out for their nonsense. Back home, Nigerians are split between popping champagne and asking why we are still begging for Western validation. If Fela was the "originator of a style" that lives on its own, why do we need a gold-plated gramophone from a committee in Los Angeles to prove it? It is a performance of recognition that feels more like a marketing strategy for the Academy's newly "accommodated" African categories.

At the end of the day, the 2026 Grammys were just another high-fashion charade where the past was used to mask a mediocre present. Africa is supposedly "leading," but we are still fighting for scraps in specialized categories while the major awards stay behind closed doors. Fela's legacy does not need a museum piece or a late-to-the-party shoutout to remain relevant. His music already soundtracks Hollywood and gets sampled by everyone from Beyonce to J. Cole. The Recording Academy did not honor Fela; they just finally admitted they were late to a party that everyone else has been enjoying for fifty years.
