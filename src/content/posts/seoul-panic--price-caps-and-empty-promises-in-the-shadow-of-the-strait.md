History is a cruel teacher for those who ignore the mechanics of supply and demand. Price caps almost universally lead to the very outcomes they claim to prevent: hoarding, black markets, and eventual shortages. While Lee talks of "swiftly" introducing these measures, the international community sees a manufacturing powerhouse admitting it has no defense against a closed Strait of Hormuz. When 1.7 million barrels of daily crude are held hostage by regional warfare, a government mandate on the price of a liter of petrol is akin to using a paper shield against a monsoon.

The international impact of this maneuver extends far beyond the Korean Peninsula. South Korea is the canary in the coal mine for energy-dependent nations. If Seoul, with its sophisticated industrial base and 208-day reserve, is forced to cannibalize its fiscal reserves to keep the lights on, the outlook for less-prepared emerging markets is catastrophic. The promise of "alternative sources" for natural gas is equally hollow. Every major economy is currently hunting for those same non-Middle Eastern molecules, and the bidding war will be predatory.

The financial markets have already voiced their verdict. A six per cent drop in the KOSPI and the Won staggering toward the 1,500 mark against the dollar suggests that investors have zero confidence in the government's stabilization programs. Throwing 100 trillion won at a systemic geopolitical blockade is like trying to extinguish a forest fire with a spray bottle. The capital flight has begun, and no amount of "market stabilization" will stop the exodus of reality-based wealth.

We are witnessing the slow-motion dismantling of the global trade order. Lee’s rhetoric about "diversifying" away from the Strait of Hormuz is three decades too late. As the Middle East burns, the manufacturing hubs of East Asia are discovering that their "economic miracles" were always built on the fragile foundation of open sea lanes and the benevolence of distant petrostates. The price cap is not a solution; it is a confession of helplessness.
