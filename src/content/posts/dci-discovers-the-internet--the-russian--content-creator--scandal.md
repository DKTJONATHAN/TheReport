They’re calling it "technology-facilitated gender-based violence." In real-world terms, it’s just another Tuesday where a predator exploits the "hospitality" of this city while the law sits at a desk in Kiambu Road drinking tea. The DCI says they’ve activated "specialized cybercrime units" to trace digital footprints. Let’s be real - half the time, the government can’t even keep their own websites running, yet we’re supposed to believe they’re going to outrun a tech-savvy foreigner hiding behind a VPN and international borders.

Then we have the Cabinet Secretary for Gender, Hannah Wendot, weighing in with the usual "deeply disturbing" and "no one is above the law" rhetoric. It’s a nice sentiment for a press conference, but it does nothing for the women whose lives have already been auctioned off on Telegram. These statements are just PR band-aids on a gaping wound. We talk about protecting "personal dignity" while the system is designed to victim-shame anyone who dares to report a crime at a police station where the officers still use carbon paper.

The DCI is promising "confidentiality, sensitivity, and respect" for those who come forward to record statements. If you’ve ever tried to report a crime in this town, you know that "sensitivity" usually involves a bored officer asking you what you were wearing or why you were there in the first place. The "cross-border" element is the perfect excuse for this investigation to eventually disappear into a black hole of "international coordination" that never actually coordinates anything.

And let’s not ignore the hypocrisy of the public. The same people "outraged" by the DCI’s statement are probably the ones hitting "forward" on WhatsApp. We live in a society that consumes trauma as entertainment and then acts shocked when the DCI warns us about "secondary victimisation." The law might say sharing is a crime, but in the digital wild west of Nairobi, a "trending" tag is often valued more than someone’s constitutional rights.

At the end of the day, this isn't just about a Russian national; it’s about a country that treats its women like disposable assets. Whether it’s a foreigner with a camera or a local with a platform, the result is the same. The DCI can pledge to "uphold justice" all they want, but in a city where the "premier investigative agency" is always three steps behind the latest tweet, we know better than to hold our breath for a conviction.
