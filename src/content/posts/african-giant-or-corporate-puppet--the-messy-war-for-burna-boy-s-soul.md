We all love the narrative of the self-made African Giant, but the reality is usually buried in a dusty folder in a lawyer's office. Burna Boy and his mother, Bose Ogulu, thought they were playing chess when they moved his early masters to their Spaceship Music imprint in 2024. They wanted "Like to Party" and "Tonight" safely under the family umbrella. But in this industry, no one lets go of a cash cow without a fight. Now, 960 Music Group has surfaced like a predatory shark, claiming the whole deal was a backroom hustle done without their consent.

It is the classic Nairobi-style "private deal" where the left hand sells what the right hand didn't even know it owned. 960 Music, which holds 40 per cent of Aristokrat, says they were completely ghosted while the label's "crown jewel assets" were being moved. This entire saga smells like the same brand of administrative incompetence we see in government projects. Just like [The Mandera PR Circus: Big Words, Small Checks, and the Usual Script](/posts/the-mandera-pr-circus--big-words--small-checks--and-the-usual-script), this legal battle is less about the music and more about who gets to keep the change from a multi-million-dollar heist.

The drama has shifted from the boardroom to the police station. Piriye Isokrari, the Aristokrat CEO, is now facing criminal charges for fraudulent conversion. The investigators are alleging that the money from this "strategic" sale never actually reached the company accounts. Instead, it seems to have taken a detour into private pockets. It is a predictable script: big egos, fast money, and a complete lack of corporate governance. In the end, the artist is just the bait used to lure in the next round of investors.

If the Federal High Court in Port Harcourt decides the sale was invalid, Burna Boy will be forced to hand back the keys to his own history. All that talk about independence and owning one's masters will evaporate. It is a cold reminder that you can call yourself a king all you want, but if the paperwork is signed in a dark corner, the suit-and-tie brigade will eventually come to collect their pound of flesh. In the world of high-stakes music rights, there are no heroes, only creditors.
