So, the Africa Forward Summit happened. Big deal, right? Macron jogging with Kipchoge, Bien dressed like a dictator, Ombachi making ugali. It’s all a show, a carefully crafted performance for us, the gullible public. The article talks about how governments used to ban art because it expressed the soul of the people. Now? They just slap a celebrity endorsement on it and call it a day. This whole "reimagining Africa's relationship with Europe" is just a fancy way of saying France wants to keep its grip, and Kenya is happy to be the compliant gatekeeper for international capital. Don't expect any real talk about imperialism here; that’s so last century.

Remember when artists were supposed to speak truth to power? That’s ancient history. Now, our beloved creatives are the government’s personal branding consultants. They’re not challenging power; they're softening it, making it palatable. The government even co-opts protest songs, turning "wantam" into "tutam." It’s pathetic. They take any political statement and water it down until it’s as bland as government-approved ugali. It makes you wonder if art ever had any real political teeth, or if we just fooled ourselves into thinking it did.

The article tries to make a distinction between "socially conscious aesthetics" and "genuinely disruptive political work." But let's be real, most of what passes for "socially conscious" these days is just a well-marketed image compatible with corporate sponsorships and state partnerships. It’s all about looking good while demanding nothing that might actually rock the boat. If a government facing a crisis can just hire a few famous faces to make them look good, what threat are they really posing? It’s like trying to fight a tank with a selfie stick.

Perhaps the real problem isn't that the government has neutered art, but that the art itself has become so diluted. The article mentions Ngugi wa Thiong’o, a writer who actually understood Marxism and clearly identified the oppressors. You won't find that kind of intellectual depth among the popular artists today. They're more interested in looking good and getting paid. If art is to ever be a threat again, artists need to get serious about political grounding. Until then, they're just dancing puppets for the powers that be. It’s a predictable collapse of greed, but this time, the artists are complicit.
