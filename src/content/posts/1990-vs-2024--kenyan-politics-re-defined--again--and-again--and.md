So, July 7, 1990. Big speeches, big crowds, all yelling "End Kanu dictatorship!" Martin Shikuku, Oginga Odinga, James Orengo - names from a history book. The regime, as expected, didn't exactly break out the party poppers. Instead, they unleashed the usual Nairobi special: bullets, beatings, and a one-way ticket to the government's holiday resorts masquerading as prisons. But hey, that blood, those tears, they apparently bought us something, right? Dismantled torture chambers, chucked out archaic sedition laws, and stopped the constant fear of being labelled a parrot for not singing the government's tune. Progress, they call it.

Fast forward to June 25, 2024. The kids are out, not for democracy this time, but for their pockets. Punitive taxes, they say. But we all know it’s deeper than that. It’s about the same old story: a fat oligarchy living it up while everyone else is just trying to scrape by. Oxfam’s latest masterpiece of misery confirms it - half of Kenya in poverty, while a handful hoard more than the rest of us combined. Access to education and health? A joke only the rich can afford. And how did the current regime respond? With the same old playbook: snipers on rooftops, painting the pavements with the brains of their own children. Some things never change, except maybe the weaponry.

And the disappearances, the hooded thugs whisking people away to secret torture dens. Even Moi’s ‘Nyayo’ regime, bless its tyrannical heart, had the sense to see when history was breathing down its neck. This lot? They’re actively trying to fight history. They’re so busy polishing their own opulent lifestyles, they’ve forgotten that the people are the ones who are supposed to be in charge. It’s a constitutional right, apparently, to demand development, not have it handed down like crumbs from a very well-fed table.

Now, the similarities are there, sure. Blood, suffering, the same streets echoing with the same demands for change. But the difference? In 1990, it was the big names, the opposition leaders. In 2024, the youth proudly declared themselves "leaderless, tribe-less and party-less." Which, frankly, is either incredibly naive or brilliantly strategic. Back then, they wanted constitutional tweaks. These days, they're talking about a whole new beginning, a "national renaissance." Sounds nice, doesn't it? Just like the talk of investment that never seems to trickle down, even from fancy [Italy-Kenya Economic Forum](posts/invest-kenya-advances-strategic-investment-partnerships-at-italy-kenya-economic-forum-in-rome) meetings.

The truth is, 1990 changed Kenya. And 2024, no doubt, changed it again. It’s a constant reassertion that power belongs to the people, even if the people are constantly being reminded that their voices are often just whispers against the roar of the powerful. We demand overhaul, we demand rights, we demand a new beginning. And then? We wait for the next trigger, the next wave of "brutality that shocked the world," and the next round of brave souls willing to pay the price for a country that seems determined to repeat its own mistakes. The cycle, my friends, is a well-oiled machine.

What hasn't changed, though, is the fundamental disconnect. The same system that allows a few to amass unimaginable wealth while the majority languish in poverty, that’s the beast. And until that beast is slain, not just poked at, these rebellions, these "days that re-defined Kenyan politics," will just be footnotes in a longer, sadder story. We march, we demand, we suffer, and the wheel keeps turning, grinding us all a little finer.
