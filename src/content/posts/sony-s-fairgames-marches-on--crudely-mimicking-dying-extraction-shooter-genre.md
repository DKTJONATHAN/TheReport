It's been nearly three years since "Fairgames," formerly known as "Project Havendale," first graced the PlayStation Showcase, and the multiplayer heist shooter somehow continues to exist. Now, the project is reportedly venturing into the crowded extraction shooter space with a mode codenamed "Cargo Heist." This borrowed concept, where players load into a map to complete objectives and extract with their ill gotten gains, directly mirrors the gameplay loop of Bungie's struggling "Marathon."

Insider Gaming reports that the internal description for "Cargo Heist" outlines a process of "Break In" to find vault codes, a "Drill" to breach the vault and secure the cargo, and an "Extract" phase to call for extraction. This level of detail is more than what was offered in the game's initial vague promises of "emergent sandbox gameplay" that rewards "creativity and mastery."

The decision to pivot towards extraction shooters, a genre already overflowing with titles, raises serious concerns about "Fairgames'" viability. This move comes at a time when "Marathon" itself is failing to capture significant player interest, and Sony appears to be scaling back its overall live-service ventures. The acquisition of Haven, the studio behind "Fairgames," back in 2022, now looks like another potentially misguided investment, especially considering recent cost cutting measures that have impacted other Sony-owned studios.

The game's development has been plagued by setbacks. A reported delay last year coincided with the departure of studio head Jade Raymond. Shortly thereafter, game director Daniel Drapeau also announced his exit. Despite these significant leadership changes, "Fairgames" persists, with recent job listings for key production roles suggesting the project is still moving, however uncertain its direction may be. This continued development, while admirable in its persistence, appears to be a desperate attempt to salvage a project by mimicking trends rather than forging a unique identity. The question remains whether this strategy will lead to anything other than another casualty in Sony's increasingly questionable push into the live-service arena.
