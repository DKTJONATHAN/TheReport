So, Raila’s ODM, the party that’s been dictating Kenyan politics for longer than some of us have had proper jobs, has decided to throw a public tantrum. Two parties, two shows, same old drama. Jamhuri Grounds, all neat and tidy, everyone in their orange regalia, looking like they’ve got it all figured out. Meanwhile, Ufungamano House is the other side of the coin - the real messy, shouting match where the actual power struggles are happening. It's a circus, plain and simple, and we're all paying for the tickets.

At Jamhuri, they’re pretending it’s business as usual. Delegates are bussed in, the speeches are rehearsed, and the "orange wave" is still supposed to be crashing. The Secretary General is out there daring anyone to try and oust him - as if that’s the biggest problem. The new "party leader" is yapping about factionalism, which is rich coming from a party that’s practically a faction itself.

And then there’s the family affair. Winnie Odinga is backing her uncle, Dr Oburu Oginga. It’s all about who’s related to whom, not about actual policies or what the common mwananchi needs. Simba Arati and Abdulswamad Nassir are apparently the new deputy leaders. Great. Another two names to remember for the next round of political musical chairs. It’s like watching the same old movie, just with slightly different actors and a new coat of paint.

The whole charade about attendance being pegged to the National Delegates Convention? That’s just more political manoeuvring, the kind of bureaucratic nonsense that keeps these parties afloat while actual progress stalls. It reminds me of that time Germany discovered the Thika Tunnel and thought it was some kind of engineering marvel, when really it was just a poorly executed fix for a much bigger problem. [First World Problems, Third World Solutions: Germany Discovers the Thika Tunnel](/posts/first-world-problems--third-world-solutions--germany-discovers-the-thika-tunnel). This ODM situation is no different.

They’re all so busy fighting over who’s got the loudest voice and the most delegates that they’ve forgotten what ODM even stands for anymore. It’s less about ideology and more about preserving the status quo, about keeping the gravy train running. You see it with other tech and media companies too, a constant leadership transition and internal squabbles that distract from their core purpose. It’s the same old song, different tune.

So, here we are, watching the ODM’s identity crisis unfold in public. Two events, one divided party, and the same old struggle for ‘pawa’. The only difference is, this time, the cracks are showing a bit too much, and it’s hard to ignore the rot underneath the orange veneer.
