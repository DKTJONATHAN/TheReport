So, the junior officers are getting a "sweeping" salary review. We are supposed to clap because a constable might finally afford something more than a dented tin of blue band. But let us be real for a second. This is not about dignity; it is about keeping the boots on the ground loyal enough to ignore the hunger in their own neighborhoods. When the government starts backdating pay hikes to July, they are not rewarding service-they are paying a retainer for the next time things get messy on the streets.

The Maraga taskforce thinks extra paramilitary training and "expanded security powers" are what we need. Because, obviously, the problem with the Kenyan police was that they were not scary enough. Now, instead of a guy with a baton asking for "kitu kidogo," we get a tactical unit with more authority to ruin your day. It reminds me of how we already pay a [Digital Judas and the Sh10,000 Justice Fee](/posts/digital-judas-and-the-sh10-000-justice-fee) just to get out of a trumped-up charge, and now the state is giving them more tools to squeeze us.

CS Murkomen promised a ceasefire that has not arrived, which is typical for this administration. They talk about peace while sharpening the knives. This ten percent raise coming in 2025 is just a carrot on a very long, dusty stick. By the time that money hits the accounts, inflation will have eaten the bread, the butter, and the knife used to spread it. It is a classic Nairobi shell game where you watch the cup, but the pea was never there to begin with.

Meanwhile, the big shots in the National Assembly are fighting over the payroll system. The Constitutional Implementation Oversight Committee wants everyone to appear before them next week because they cannot decide who gets to hold the wallet. It is never about the officers' welfare; it is always about who controls the "source" of the funds. When two elephants fight over a payroll system, it is the taxpayer who gets trampled while the officers wait for a digital miracle.

We are being told this is "reform." In this city, reform usually just means the bribe price is going up to match the new salary scale. You cannot fix a broken system by just throwing a few extra coins at the people holding the rungu. Until the culture of the uniform changes, all we are doing is financing a more efficient machine of state-sponsored harassment. Enjoy your ten percent, boys-we know we will be the ones paying for it at the next roadblock.
