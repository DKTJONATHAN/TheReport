DP KINDIKI, CS WANDAYI'S G-TO-G FUEL DEAL DEFENSE: A GLOBAL CRISIS OR A SHADY SCAM?

This is how they spin it: blame the whole world for our empty pockets. While the rest of us are choking on rising prices, our esteemed leaders are busy pointing fingers at Iran and Israel, hoping we'll be too busy watching the fireworks to notice our own wallets disappearing.

PROFESSOR KINDIKI, the man who probably sleeps on a bed of tax receipts, wants us to "remain patient." Patient for what, exactly? For the global oil market to magically sort itself out? He's even dismissing calls for protests, as if a few angry Kenyans waving placards will suddenly make crude oil cheaper. Apparently, demonstrations won't bring prices down, but neither will this G-to-G deal, if we're being honest. He’s asking for time, as if time is something we have in abundance when every refill feels like a luxury. And let's not forget the Sh6.2 billion subsidy and VAT reduction; that’s just a band-aid on a gaping wound, designed to make us *think* they're doing something.

ENERGY CS OPIYO WANDAYI chimes in, defending the current pricing by rattling off statistics about "global landing costs" and a "jeopardised world petroleum products market." Kenya, he reminds us, "does not operate on an island." No, we operate on an island of high taxes and questionable deals, apparently. The numbers he throws around - 42% increase for petrol, 69% for diesel - are supposed to shock us into submission, to make us grateful that petrol isn't Sh217 per litre. But let's be real, the kerosene price being "deliberately maintained" to protect the "very low in society" sounds more like a calculated move to distract from the bigger picture.

And then there's the sacred G-to-G framework, the government's golden goose. Wandayi claims it has "stabilised costs" by fixing premiums and freight. Fixed at what, exactly? Eighty-four dollars per metric tonne for petrol and seventy-eight for diesel. Sounds like a nice, neat little package for someone, doesn't it? This whole G-to-G saga smells suspiciously like the kind of opaque deals that fuel corruption, a problem we've seen before, even in seemingly unrelated areas like [Malindi’s Smallest Export: The Child Trafficking Economy](/posts/malindi-s-smallest-export--the-child-trafficking-economy). The opposition is calling it a "fuel scandal," and frankly, who can blame them when the burden of global shocks is being passed directly to us, the people who actually have to pay?

While the government insists its "interventions" have cushioned us, the reality on the ground is that fuel prices are feeding into everything. Transport fares are up, commodities are up, and our bank accounts are going down. Industry players are warning of slowed economic activity and households pushed into financial distress. But hey, at least the DP and the CS are confident. Confident that the current pressure won't "destabilise our economy." Easy for them to say when their fuel tanks are being filled by the taxpayer.
