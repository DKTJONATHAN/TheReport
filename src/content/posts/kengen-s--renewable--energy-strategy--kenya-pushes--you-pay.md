KenGen, our dear "state-linked" electricity giant, is out here outlining its grand "energy strategy" as Kenya supposedly "pushes renewables." Sounds progressive, doesn't it? Geothermal, hydropower- all the green buzzwords. But let's be real, whenever a state-linked entity starts talking about long-term strategies and "regulated returns," the cynical alarm bells start ringing. It's less about illuminating homes cheaply and more about guaranteeing steady revenue streams for those in the know, shielded from the messy realities of a free market.

They talk about geothermal requiring "high upfront drilling and development costs." Who do you think shoulders that bill? Us, the taxpayers, directly or indirectly through inflated tariffs and national debt. And then they mention "limited fresh market data" for investors. Convenient, isn't it? Makes it harder for outsiders to really scrutinize the deals, the costs, or how those "long-term power purchase arrangements" are structured. It's a closed shop, much like how various public entities in this country manage funds, often leading to shambles for those who rely on them, like when [IEBC Commissioner Aduol Got Caught Up In The Sh39 Million TUK Retirees Shambles](/posts/iebc-commissioner-aduol-caught-up-in-sh39-million-tuk-retirees-shambles).

Hydropower sounds lovely until you realize "operational decisions around water use affect not only power generation but also agriculture and local ecosystems." Translation: when there's a choice between powering Nairobi's skyscrapers and ensuring a farmer's livelihood downstream, guess who wins? They also have thermal plants, the dirty ones, still playing a "role." They'll "seek to reduce reliance" eventually, but don't hold your breath. It's always about managing the "transition carefully" - carefully for their pockets, that is.

New projects? Oh, don't worry, there's a "mix of domestic funding, international development finance institutions, and commercial lenders" ready to jump in. More debt for Kenya, more leverage for foreign entities, and more opportunities for tenders that somehow always seem to benefit the usual suspects. And this talk about "regional initiatives" and exporting power? Sounds like another layer of bureaucracy and "joint studies" that rarely materialize into anything tangible for the average mwananchi, but certainly involve plenty of travel allowances for the "experts."

So KenGen is "central to the country's long-term energy planning." Central, indeed. Central to a system where stability means predictable profits for a select few, where "growth investment" often means taxpayer-backed risk, and where "renewables" is a good PR slogan. Don't let the greenwash fool you. It's the same old Nairobi story, just with bigger turbines.
