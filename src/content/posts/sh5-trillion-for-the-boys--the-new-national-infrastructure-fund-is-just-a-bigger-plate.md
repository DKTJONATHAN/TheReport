So, Zakayo has finally put pen to paper on the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF) Bill 2026. We are told this magic pot is going to mobilize Sh5 trillion over the next decade. Sh5 trillion. That is a lot of zeros for a government that still struggles to keep the lights on or fix a single drainage pipe when the clouds decide to sneeze. They call it an investment-led approach, but for anyone who has lived in Nairobi long enough, it sounds like just another way to repackage the same old appetite for debt under a fancier name.

Majority Leader Kimani Ichung-wah is out here talking about how the journey to Singapore has been crystallized. It is funny how every politician in this country thinks we are one signature away from becoming a first-world nation. We have been on the roadmap to Singapore since the sixties, yet here we are, still stuck in a perpetual traffic jam on Mombasa Road. If words could build bridges, we would have reached the moon by now, but in reality, these grand visions usually end up as half-baked projects that benefit the same elite circles while the rest of us fight for space on a crowded matatu.

The law says that if you steal from this fund, you have to repay twice the amount. It sounds tough on paper, but when was the last time a big fish in this city actually coughed up stolen loot? The "independent" board is another joke. Six independent members appointed by the President. In Nairobi, we know that "independent" usually means someone who knows how to nod in the right direction when the big man speaks. It is just more bureaucracy designed to give the illusion of oversight while the real decisions are made behind closed doors at State House.

They are targeting highways, railways, and "strategic commercial investments." We have seen how these strategic plays turn out. Often, they are nothing more than excuses for the well-connected to flip land and secure tenders. It reminds me of how international investors sometimes try to mask their failures; much like [The Rising Sun Feeds on Rust](/posts/the-rising-sun-feeds-on-rust--nippon-s--15-billion-toxic-real-estate-play) where billion-dollar plays are nothing but toxic real estate dressed up in a suit. This NIF thing feels like the Kenyan version-a massive pile of money waiting to be mismanaged under the guise of "national development."

While the Treasury Cabinet Secretary and the Central Bank Governor sit in their air-conditioned boardrooms discussing "strategic direction," the city is literally drowning. The same news cycle reporting on this Sh5 trillion fund is also telling us about 27 people killed by flash floods in Nairobi. It is the classic Kenyan duality: we have billions for "future infrastructure" that may never materialize, but we cannot find the money to fix the sewers that are currently washing away our neighbors. 

The NIF is supposed to reduce our reliance on debt, but with private sector "participation" and more loans on the table, it looks like the same trap with a new coat of paint. We are being told to wait for the first world while we struggle to survive the third-world reality of City Shuttle buses crashing on Valley Road. It is a cynical cycle, really. They sign the papers, the elites toast with champagne, and the common mwananchi is left holding a bill for a "Singapore" they will never see.
