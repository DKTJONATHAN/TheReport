NAIROBI'S MILITARY AI SUMMIT: ANOTHER EXPENSIVE TALK SHOP FOR AFRICA, 2027

Kenya gets to host some big global shindig in 2027 about "responsible" military AI. Apparently, this is a "first for Africa." Great. So, while we're still trying to figure out how to get basic services to everyone, we're going to spend time and money talking about how robots can kill people more efficiently. Because that's what nations do, right? They plan for war, and now they're getting fancy new toys.

This REAIM thing sounds like a fancy way for governments and defense contractors to hobnob and make promises they'll probably break. They’ll talk about "ethical safeguards" and "international cooperation," but let's be real, when it comes to military tech, it's always about who has the biggest, baddest weapon. Kenya's suddenly a "leading voice" because they're good at attending meetings and nodding along, just like how some politicians are good at making grand pronouncements about electric dreams while the rest of us struggle with fuel prices. It's all about appearances, isn't it?

They’re boasting about Kenya’s role in UN committees and national strategies, which sounds impressive on paper. But what does it mean for the average Kenyan struggling to pay rent? Probably nothing. This Moran AI and Cyber Center of Excellence sounds important, but it’s more likely to churn out more tech-savvy soldiers to operate these new AI gadgets, rather than solving our everyday problems. It’s all about keeping up with the global arms race, disguised in diplomatic language.

And of course, they'll be promoting "African perspectives." You know what that means? A lot of talk, a few carefully selected delegates, and the same old song and dance about how the Global South needs a seat at the table. Meanwhile, the real decisions will be made by the usual suspects, the big players who actually build and fund this stuff. Kenya is just the stage for their global theatre.

They say this summit will focus on practical action and strengthening capacities. But when has a global summit ever truly translated into tangible relief for the common person? We’ll hear about strengthening regional capacities and public-private partnerships. Translation: more money flowing to defense industries, more government contracts, and more talk about transparency and accountability - words that often get lost in translation when the military is involved.

So, Kenya gets to host another international conference. It’s a chance for some politicians to travel, some diplomats to network, and for us to get a nice big bill at the end of it all. The real impact on warfare will be decided elsewhere, and we’ll be left to clean up the mess, or at least talk about it very responsibly.













Kenya is slated to host the Fourth Summit on Responsible Artificial Intelligence in the Military Domain (REAIM) in Nairobi in April 2027. Touted as a major coup for the continent, this event positions Kenya as the first African nation and the first from the Global South to lead discussions on the burgeoning field of military AI. The announcement, made in Geneva, suggests a growing Kenyan influence in global technology and security governance. However, for many Kenyans, this grand pronouncement on "responsible governance of emerging technologies" likely sounds like another expensive international gathering while domestic issues remain unresolved.

The summit aims to bring together governments, defence establishments, and tech companies to develop norms and safeguards for military AI. Ambassador Philip Thigo, Kenya’s Special Envoy on Technology, emphasized translating commitments into practical action. But one has to wonder what practical action will truly emerge from such a forum, especially when the nation is still grappling with its own economic realities. It’s easy to champion ethical innovation when you’re not the one worried about where the next meal is coming from, much like how some leaders focus on grand plans while the population struggles with the cost of basic necessities, a situation not unlike the ongoing issues surrounding [Ruto's Sh10 Diesel 'Relief' and Electric Dreams: Nairobi's Wallet Still Bleeds](/posts/ruto-s-sh10-diesel--relief--and-electric-dreams--nairobi-s-wallet-still-bleeds).

Kenya’s selection as host is presented as a reflection of its broader leadership in global AI governance, citing its involvement in UN bodies and its national AI strategy. Yet, the reality on the ground often tells a different story. While institutions like the Moran AI and Cyber Center of Excellence (MAIC-CoE) are being established, the focus remains on military advancement. The question is whether this sophisticated technological push will trickle down to benefit the average citizen, or simply enhance the nation's capacity for future conflicts.

The organizers highlight Kenya's efforts to champion African participation, including regional workshops. This is commendable, as it ensures a voice for the continent. However, the inherent power dynamics of global forums mean that while Africa may have a seat at the table, the decisions made are often influenced by the established military powers. The discourse around "strengthening regional capacities" and "public-private partnerships" could easily translate into lucrative contracts for foreign tech firms, with little tangible benefit for the local populace beyond diplomatic photo opportunities.

As nations grapple with the implications of AI in warfare, Nairobi is set to host a summit that promises to shape the future of military technology. The focus on transparency, accountability, and human dignity is crucial, but history suggests that in the realm of defense, these principles often take a backseat to strategic advantage. While Kenya positions itself as a leader, the true impact of REAIM 2027 on global security, and more importantly, on the lives of ordinary Kenyans, remains to be seen. The hope is that it’s more than just another expensive talk shop.
