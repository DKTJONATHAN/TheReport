In a world where influencers are selling tea and fake lifestyles, we finally have a new character in the Kenyan scene: a literal nun in a courtroom. Sr. Immaculate Muthoni is out here trying to convince us that she can balance a vow of poverty with the billable hours of a lawyer. She claims she was inspired by the 2007-08 post-election violence to make justice "lived," which sounds great for a LinkedIn bio, but let us look at the fine print before we canonize her as the next legal shark.

Here is the kicker: Sister Immaculate will not touch a divorce or a criminal case. She says it is because of church teachings on the sanctity of life and marriage. How convenient! While every other lawyer in Nairobi is getting their hands dirty in the trenches of messy breakups and high-stakes murder trials, she is sticking to the "safe" civil cases and institutional compliance. It is giving "curated legal career" vibes. If you are going to be a crusader for justice, why stop where the paperwork gets uncomfortable?

She loves a good entrance, though. Imagine the drama in the corridors of justice when she drops the "May it please your court, I am Sister Immaculate" line. It is a total power move that probably leaves judges too confused to hold her in contempt. She claims people associate her habit with integrity and compassion, but in this town, we know that a uniform is often just the best camouflage for someone playing the long game for a seat on the bench.

While she is busy giving free legal training on social media and talking about "generosity as justice," the rest of the country is struggling with reality. It is hard to swallow her "justice for all" rhetoric when we see things like [Free SHA for Inmates While You Starve? Zakayo's Newest PR Stunt Is a Prison Break for Logic](/posts/free-sha-for-inmates-while-you-starve--zakayo-s-newest-pr-stunt-is-a-prison-break-for-logic) happening right under our noses. It seems everyone in leadership, from the politicians to the religious elite, has a very specific, very limited definition of what fairness actually looks like for the common mwananchi.

Sister Immaculate says she spends her time reading "Becoming" by Michelle Obama and hanging out with her seniors. It is all very wholesome, very "main character energy." She envisions sisters as ethical anchors in governance, which sounds like a fancy way of saying she wants a Ph.D. and a promotion. We will see if her brand of "selective justice" actually changes the system or if she is just another lawyer with a better-than-average Sunday outfit.
