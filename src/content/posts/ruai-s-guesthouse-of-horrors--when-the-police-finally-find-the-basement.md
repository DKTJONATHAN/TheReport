Seventy people. Imagine seventy human beings squeezed into a single house in Ruai like bags of charcoal waiting for a delivery truck. The Kenya Police Service wants a round of applause for busting this syndicate on a Thursday morning, rescuing 66 Ethiopians and four Eritreans from the shadows. They caught one Kenyan national, probably a low-level caretaker whose only job was to make sure the door stayed locked. It is the usual Nairobi theater: a dramatic raid, a stern statement, and a warning for the rest of us to stay vigilant while the real architects of these pipelines are probably sitting in air-conditioned offices sipping tea.

This Ruai discovery is just a small, messy leak in a dam that is already bursting. Only a few days ago, Majority Leader Kimani Ichung’wah was shouting in Parliament about how our own immigration and security officers are the ones greasing the wheels for these syndicates. It is not just about people coming in from Ethiopia; it is about our own being sold off to fight in the Russia-Ukraine war. When you have the Directorate of Criminal Investigations and the National Employment Authority allegedly colluding to ship Kenyans off as cannon fodder, a raid in Ruai feels less like a victory and more like a PR distraction.

We are living in a country where the system is designed to fail you unless you have the right last name. While these 70 victims were being hidden in a basement, thousands of Kenyan students were finding out that their futures were already auctioned off in [The Cluster 1 Hunger Games: 90,000 Kenyan Kids Just Lost the Lottery](/posts/the-cluster-1-hunger-games--90-000-kenyan-kids-just-lost-the-lottery). Desperation is the only thing we produce in surplus these days. Whether it is a student cheated out of a university spot or an Eritrean migrant hoping for a better life through a Ruai transit point, the currency is always the same: human suffering managed by rogue state officials.

The United States Department of State recently kept Kenya at Tier 2 in its Trafficking in Persons report. Apparently, we are making progress but have persistent gaps. That is diplomat-speak for saying the government knows exactly who is running the trade but is too busy taking a cut to shut it down. If you are Tier 2, it means you are just good enough to keep the donor money flowing but bad enough that human beings are still being traded like counterfeit sneakers in Eastleigh.

Do not expect this raid to change anything. The one Kenyan suspect will likely be the fall guy while the bigger fish continue to coordinate with foreign missions and immigration desks. The police tell us to report suspicious activity, but who do you call when the person taking the report is the one who signed the traffickers' transit permits? In Nairobi, the only thing more common than a tip-off is the silence that follows once the cameras are turned off.
