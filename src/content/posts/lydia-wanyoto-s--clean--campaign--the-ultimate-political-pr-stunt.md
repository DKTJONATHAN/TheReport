Welcome to the latest season of "Who Wants to Be a Speaker," where the stakes are high but the fashion is consistently questionable. Lydia Wanyoto just threw her wig into the ring, claiming she is the "clean and tested" alternative for the 12th Parliament. In a world where every public figure has a digital trail of messiness longer than a grocery receipt, Lydia is out here pretending she has spent 32 years in the system without ever being caught in a scandal. It is giving "I have never used a filter" energy while standing directly under a high-definition ring light.

She admitted during her appearance on UBC TV that she does not exactly have the numerical backing-the kind of support that actually wins elections-but she is hoping her "integrity" will act as a magic wand. She is basically the contestant on a reality show who thinks talent will win her the crown while the popular girls are busy buying followers and making backroom deals. It is the same kind of blind optimism we see in [The Kangemi Circus: 16 Classrooms and 80 Billion Reasons to Stay Skeptical](/posts/the-kangemi-circus--16-classrooms-and-80-billion-reasons-to-stay-skeptical), where we are expected to believe that a lack of visible noise equals a job well done.

Let us talk about the competition, because this cast is stacked with drama. She is going up against the incumbent Anita Among, who has more tea surrounding her than a high-society brunch, and Norbert Mao, the veteran political actor who refuses to let the credits roll on his career. Wanyoto is positioning herself as the "fresh start," which is hilarious considering she has been a fixture in the NRM since the dawn of time. You cannot be a "fresh start" when you have been in the same room for three decades; at that point, you are just the furniture that finally got a new coat of polish.

The audacity to say "nobody points at a negative thing about you" in 2024 is either the ultimate flex or the ultimate delusion. In this era of social media investigators and leaked WhatsApp screenshots, a "clean record" usually just means your PR team is working overtime or you have been hiding in the background for thirty years. While she waits for the party to "negotiate" her into the top spot, the rest of us are watching this political pageant with the same cynicism we reserve for a celebrity apology video. Honey, the 12th Parliament needs more than a clean record; it needs a miracle and a much better wardrobe department.
