DECIMALIZING CONSENSUAL ADOLESCENT RELATIONSHIPS IN KENYA? DON'T HOLD YOUR BREATH.

The Sexual Offences Act was supposed to protect kids. Instead, it's turning them into criminals for doing what teenagers do. The law doesn't care if it's consensual; if you're young and in love, you're a potential convict.

Another government screw-up. The Sexual Offences Act, meant to shield children, is being used to jail them. These kids, just trying to navigate their youth, are branded criminals for relationships that are mutually agreed upon, with no coercion or exploitation. It’s a perfect example of how laws designed with good intentions can become blunt instruments of injustice in this country.

This isn't some rare occurrence. We're talking about thousands of adolescents across Kenya facing the same nightmare. Arrested, detained, with no decent legal representation, and shoved through a justice system that’s about as equipped to handle their realities as a fish is to climb a mango tree. It’s no surprise then, that these cases are piling up. It’s just another way Nairobi can sell your loneliness, pushing people into desperate situations with no support, not unlike how [pets in Kenya are another way Nairobi is selling your loneliness](/posts/pets-in-kenya--another-way-nairobi-is-selling-your-loneliness).

Take HSO, a 17-year-old, and CNK, 16. They were a couple, trying to build a life. Police raided them, arrested them, and boom - defilement charges for HSO. The bail was astronomical, and he sat in custody until some organization finally bailed him out. Then there's AMO and TA. Their consensual relationship led to pregnancy, and TA’s family, instead of support, orchestrated AMO’s arrest. He was charged with defilement, spent years under a dark cloud, and only got off when they confirmed he was a minor. Now they’re together with their kids, a testament to their resilience against a system that tried to break them.

The Center for Reproductive Rights and RHNK have filed a constitutional petition. They're calling out the SOA for these draconian provisions and the systemic failures that deny adolescents access to basic SRH information and services. It’s a long shot, of course. When has the system ever truly prioritized the best interests of the child, especially when it involves the messy realities of adolescence?

This fight is about more than just a few cases. It's about challenging laws that are fundamentally flawed and fail to acknowledge the developmental stages of young people. It’s about ensuring that adolescents aren't criminalized for consensual relationships, but are instead provided with the support and information they need. But let's be real, change in this country is slower than a sloth on Valium.







The Sexual Offences Act in Kenya was supposed to be a shield for children, a weapon against predators. But as is typical with anything this government touches, it's become a weapon against the very people it was meant to protect. Adolescents, navigating the choppy waters of youth and attraction, are finding themselves branded criminals for consensual, non-exploitative relationships with their peers. The law, in its infinite wisdom, doesn't differentiate between actual abuse and what amounts to teenage romance. It’s a classic Nairobi special: good intentions paved with the road to hell, or in this case, police cells.

We've got Sections 8, 9, and 11 of the SOA, and Section 43(4)(f) to thank for this mess. These sections, in their rigid, unthinking application, lump together serious exploitation with mutual, consensual peer relationships between adolescents of similar ages. The result? Prosecutions, detentions, and stigmatization. All this without a shred of consideration for the best interests of the child, fair trial guarantees, or the simple, messy psychosocial realities of adolescence. It's a system designed for a different era, and it's crushing the current one.

This isn't an isolated incident. We're talking about thousands of young people across Kenya suffering the same fate. Arrested for normal peer relationships, often without proper legal representation, they're pushed through a criminal justice system that was never built for them. It’s a vicious cycle, and frankly, it’s exhausting to even write about. It’s just another way Nairobi can sell your loneliness, pushing people into desperate situations with no support, not unlike how [pets in Kenya are another way Nairobi is selling your loneliness](/posts/pets-in-kenya--another-way-nairobi-is-selling-your-loneliness).

The Center for Reproductive Rights and their local partners, RHNK, have finally thrown a legal grenade. They’ve filed a constitutional petition on behalf of affected adolescents and NAYA, a youth organization. They’re challenging the SOA provisions that criminalize consensual peer relationships and exposing the systemic failures that leave adolescents vulnerable, pushing them into the jaws of the criminal justice system instead of providing them with safe, confidential sexual and reproductive health information and services.

Look at the examples: HSO, 17, in a consensual relationship with 16-year-old CNK. They rented a room for stability. Police raid, arrest, detention, and defilement charges for HSO. The bail was KES 50,000, a fortune for them, and he languished in custody until NAYA intervened. Then there's AMO, 17, and TA, who got pregnant from their consensual relationship. TA’s family, instead of support, had AMO arrested and charged with defilement. He was only cleared after years of legal battles once his age was confirmed. Now they are raising their children together, a testament to their survival.

The petition names the usual suspects: the Director of Public Prosecutions, the Attorney General, the Inspector General of Police, and the Makadara Law Courts. These are the faces of the system that criminalizes young love and vulnerability. It's a long fight, and the odds are stacked. But sometimes, you have to at least try to shout into the void, hoping someone, somewhere, might actually listen.
