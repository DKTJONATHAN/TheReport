So, the Adani deal got a bit too hot for the kitchen, and now the chefs at State House have cooked up a new recipe. They are calling it the National Infrastructure Fund. It sounds professional, almost patriotic, until you realize it is just a fancy way of saying they have run out of foreigners to borrow from and are now coming for your NSSF and pension contributions. After the public screamed about the Indian tycoon taking over our strategic national asset, the government decided to "localize" the exploitation.

President Ruto is out here signing bills and talking about Sh20 billion in equity participation for the JKIA expansion. They want us to believe this is a shield against exchange-rate risks. In reality, it is a shield against accountability. When the money comes from a domestic fund, they do not have to explain themselves to the IMF or some grumpy lender in Beijing. They just need to convince a "professional board" that they handpicked themselves to release the billions.

We have seen this brand of "innovation" before. Whether it is a new fund or a new manual, the outcome is always the same for the common Kenyan. Just like the empty promises in [Old Radios, New Lies: KBC’s AI PR Stunt Won’t Save a Sinking Ship](/posts/old-radios--new-lies--kbc-s-ai-pr-stunt-won-t-save-a-sinking-ship), this infrastructure fund is just lipstick on a very expensive pig. They are promising to turn Sh1.2 trillion into Sh5 trillion over a decade, which is the kind of math you only hear from pyramid scheme kings in downtown Nairobi.

The most hilarious part is the talk of "parliamentary oversight." Since when did our MPs oversee anything other than their own salary increments and the size of their fuel guzzlers? They are inviting pension funds and collective investment schemes to the table, but let us be real. If you are a Kenyan worker, your retirement is now being bet on a project that has been a magnet for controversy for years. If the project stalls or the money disappears into "technical expertise" fees, you will be the one retiring in a polythene shack while they fly out of the very airport you built.

This shift to domestic institutional investors is just a desperate pivot. They have hit the debt ceiling so hard they have a permanent headache, so now they are looking at your "long-term local savings" with hungry eyes. They say it is about reducing reliance on public debt, but all I see is a government that has found a way to gamble with your future without having to ask for your permission first. By the time the "independent professional board" finishes its work, the only thing left of JKIA will be the same old buckets catching rainwater in the departure lounge.
