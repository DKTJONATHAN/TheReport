Welcome to the annual Nairobi swimming season, where the entry fee is your life and the lifeguards are nowhere to be found. Last night, the heavens opened and, as usual, the city folded like a cheap umbrella. At least 23 people are dead because our "world-class" infrastructure has the drainage capacity of a soda straw. While rescue teams pull bodies from the muck in Mukuru and Mathare, the rest of us are left wondering if we should invest in a canoe or just accept that our living rooms are the new riparian lands.

The tragedy hits the same spots every time, a predictable map of misery covering Kibra, Pipeline, and Githurai. It is almost poetic how the water ignores the gated community walls and reminds everyone that, in a flood, we are all just obstacles for the silt. Dozens of cars were swept away, proving once again that a German engine is no match for a Kenyan pothole filled with three feet of stagnant rainwater. 

The government claims they have deployed 4,000 personnel to clear the rivers and drains. It is the same tired script of reactive panic we saw in [Smart Vodka and Stupid Risks: The DCI’s Latest Performance](/posts/smart-vodka-and-stupid-risks--the-dci-s-latest-performance), where the authorities only show up once the cameras are rolling and the damage is irreversible. Cleaning a drain in the middle of a flood is like trying to dry the ocean with a paper towel. It is high-octane theater for a public that stopped believing in fairy tales a long time ago.

City Hall has also kindly informed us that the water supply is disrupted. The irony is so thick you could swim in it: you are currently drowning in water that is too filthy to wash your hands with. Pipelines along Outering Road and Brookside Drive have decided to quit, leaving residents to watch the floodwaters rise while their taps stay bone-dry. If you were looking for a sign that this city is managed by people who couldn't organize a two-car funeral, this is it.

Even the high-fliers at Kenya Airways were brought down to earth, or rather, diverted to Mombasa. When the sky turns gray, Nairobi becomes a no-fly zone, proving that our incompetence extends from the sewers all the way to the clouds. If you are stuck in a terminal or watching your furniture float toward the Indian Ocean, just remember that the Met Department says there is more to come. 

So, buckle up for a weekend of landslides and water contamination. The authorities will keep giving us warnings we already see out of our windows, and the search-and-rescue teams will keep counting the cost of decades of neglect. In Nairobi, we do not solve problems; we just wait for the sun to come out so we can forget they exist until the next cloud appears.
