Twelve AK-47s. Let that sink in. In a region where every teenage herder treats a rifle like a mandatory rite of passage, the National Police Service is popping champagne over a dozen rusted tubes handed over at Marich Police Station. They call it "community cooperation" and a "voluntary surrender," but let’s be real - this is just a spring cleaning of the weapons that probably don't shoot straight anymore. It is the usual PR exercise designed to make us believe Operation Maliza Uhalifu is doing something other than burning through fuel and daily allowances.

While the NPS brags about "growing trust," the rest of the country is effectively falling apart. We are being told to celebrate a few surrendered magazines while Transparency International reminds us that our political elites treat Chapter Six of the Constitution like a polite suggestion rather than a law. It is the same old story: the small fish in Pokot hand over their tools of survival while the big sharks in Nairobi continue their [cashless corruption](/posts/cashless-corruption--the-new-digital-toll-on-nairobi-roads) under the cover of digital transformation and "strategic" initiatives.

President Ruto is busy signing off on Sh5 trillion for the National Infrastructure Fund, promising "strict governance" to prevent misappropriation. We have heard that script before, and we know how it ends. Usually, "strategic projects" is just code for another round of looting while the actual infrastructure - like the roads where City Shuttle buses lose control and kill commuters - remains a death trap. One person dies on Valley Road, twenty-seven drown in Nairobi flash floods, and the government's response is to sign a bill for more concrete we cannot afford.

They want us to partner with the police and share information on "suspicious activities" while they ignore the most suspicious activity of all: how a country can mobilize trillions for "strategic projects" but cannot manage a city drain or protect a minor without "specialized training" for 160 prosecutors. It is all a game of optics. Twelve guns off the street in West Pokot won't stop the rot when the system itself is the primary weapon being used against the Kenyan taxpayer.

The reality of Nairobi is that we are always one rainstorm away from a funeral and one "voluntary surrender" away from another empty government promise. As long as the vetting processes remain weak and the political class remains untouched, these disarmament initiatives are just a distraction. They are painting the fence while the house is being eaten by termites. Wake me up when they actually start disarming the people in suits who are stealing our future.
