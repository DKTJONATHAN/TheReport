Seventy million shillings. In Nairobi, that barely buys you a decent townhouse in Lavington or pays for one benchmarking trip for a parliamentary committee. But out in West Pokot, the Kenya Development Corporation is calling it a wholesale financing facility. They are patting themselves on the back for dropping what amounts to loose change into the livestock sector, promising to unlock value chains as if they have finally found the key to the desert. We have heard this script so many times it feels like a tired rerun of a bad local drama.

The money is being funneled through Eco Pillar Sacco under something called the DRIVE program. It sounds fancy on a press release, but let us be real. By the time this money trickles down through the layers of bureaucracy, the interest rates and processing fees will have eaten the meat, leaving the farmers with nothing but the hooves. It is the same old story of government-led inclusion that usually ends with a few well-connected individuals in Nairobi buying new SUVs while the people in the north continue to pray for rain.

They claim 170 farmers have already received 35 million. That is about 200,000 shillings per person. In this economy, where the price of fuel and veterinary services is skyrocketing, that is barely enough to keep a herd alive during a dry spell, let alone build long-term resilience. It is another example of the government’s obsession with optics over impact, much like how [Ruto’s Gen Z Problem](/posts/ruto-gen-z-problem-2027-election-won-on-the-feed) proves that the youth are tired of seeing these staged photo ops while their economic reality remains bleak.

The irony of discussing value addition for hides and leather in 2024 is not lost on anyone who knows the state of our local manufacturing. We talk about processing meat and milk while the infrastructure in these counties remains a nightmare. It is easier to get a cold drink in a Nairobi dive bar than it is to transport fresh milk from a remote village in Turkana to a viable market without it spoiling. This facility is just a small band-aid on a gaping wound of systemic neglect and poor planning.

Meanwhile, the national debt is sitting at 11.8 trillion shillings. Every development loan we take is just another brick in the wall of our economic prison. KDC talks about de-risking, but the biggest risk in Kenya remains the massive gap between what is signed in air-conditioned boardroom meetings and what actually reaches the ground. We are being fed a diet of press releases and mega dams while the basic cost of living continues to suffocate the very pastoralists they claim to be saving.
