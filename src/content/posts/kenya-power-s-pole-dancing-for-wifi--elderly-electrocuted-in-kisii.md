So, two people, including an 84-year-old man, decided to get fancy and connect a WiFi cable in Sengera, Kisii. Joseph Mosoti, bless his technologically adventurous soul, was the first to find out that electricity and faulty connections are a deadly cocktail. His neighbor, Esther Oirere, 60, a woman who clearly hadn't read the memo about not rushing into situations involving live wires, apparently tried to help and, surprise, surprise, ended up joining him in the afterlife. Honestly, the sheer lack of common sense is astounding.

Of course, the police and Kenya Power officials were called, because someone has to catalog the mess and point fingers. They're "investigating," which is code for "we'll find some poor sod to blame, probably a quack who wired it up, and then we'll go back to sleep until the next one." This is the latest in a long line of incidents blamed on illegal connections and shoddy work. You'd think by now, people would understand that trying to cut corners on something that can kill you is a spectacularly bad idea. It’s almost as if some people never learned from the stories of [Muddy Wigs and Flooded Subarus: The MAM Rain Season Is Here To Humble Your Faves](/posts/muddy-wigs-and-flooded-subarus--the-mam-rain-season-is-here-to-humble-your-faves) - when you mess with forces you don't understand, you get… well, you get electrocuted.

But it’s not just electrocution making headlines. Over in Kilifi, a 25-year-old named Osman Ali got himself stabbed to death in an argument with a "friend." The motive? Unknown. Classic. Then, in Siaya, a mob, tired of waiting for the slow wheels of justice, decided to play judge, jury, and arsonist, burning down houses belonging to a murder suspect. Because why not add more destruction to an already grim situation?

And the grimness doesn't stop. An eight-year-old girl in Mandera drowned in a water tank while playing. In Gatundu, Kiambu, a man named Lucas Kinuthia decided to go for a swim in the Karumeru stream and, you guessed it, drowned. His clothes and gumboots were found on the riverbank, a testament to his last, fatal dip. It’s a wonder anyone survives in this country without a daily dose of paranoia and a healthy skepticism towards anything that looks remotely like progress or a shortcut.
