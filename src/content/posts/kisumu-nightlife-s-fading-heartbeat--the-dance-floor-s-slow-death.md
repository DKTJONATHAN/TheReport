Leone Frank Doso, bless his heart, still remembers when Kisumu’s nightlife had a pulse you could feel from the sidewalk. He talks about music pulling people onto the dance floor, about rhythm, style, and this thing called "carefree abandon." For his generation, the dance floor wasn’t just an accessory; it was the whole point. Cute.

But let’s be real. For most of us, growing up meant realizing that "love" is a concept best left to rom-coms and that "rejection" is the real MVP of human experience. So, if Mr. Doso became an empath because he never experienced love, I suppose I'm a cynic because I've experienced *plenty* of everything else. It’s not a choice, it’s a survival skill in this city.

The idea of a "dance floor" as a focal point feels as quaint as asking if there are rules for wearing a bin bag to State House. We’re too busy navigating the syndicate’s illegal parking schemes or wondering how a credit firm can admit they have no record of a loan, yet still manage to ruin someone’s employer’s details. That’s Nairobi for you - where the music has died and the concrete is sinking.

Maybe the slow death of the dance floor is just a symptom of a larger malaise. When you’re dodging potholes that could swallow a Land Rover and worrying about the latest City Hall grift, the urge to boogie on down seems… a little misplaced. It’s hard to feel carefree abandon when you’re constantly bracing for the next Nairobi’s Annual Drowning.

The truth is, the "carefree abandon" he talks about probably only existed for a select few. For the rest of us, the dance floor was just another place to try and impress someone, to forget the daily grind, or to just get away from the constant buzz of things that are fundamentally broken. Like the EAC, for instance. That whole thing is a house of cards waiting to tumble.

So, while Mr. Doso cherishes his memories of a vibrant Kisumu, the rest of us are left with the echoes. The real rhythm of Nairobi is the sound of sirens, the crunch of tires on questionable roads, and the endless pursuit of a little bit of sanity in a world that’s determined to take it all away. If you’re looking for a heartbeat, you’re better off checking your own pulse before it flatlines from sheer exhaustion.
