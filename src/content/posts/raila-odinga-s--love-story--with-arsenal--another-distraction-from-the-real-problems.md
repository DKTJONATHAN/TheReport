So, Baba is gone. And the world, or at least Philip Etale and Arsenal FC, are falling over themselves to mourn his supposed undying love for a football club. Apparently, the Premier League giants finally got around to sending their condolences, a whole bloody three months late, mind you. January dispatch, March arrival. Classic. You'd think a club with their global reach could manage a bit of timely post, but then again, maybe they were just waiting to see if the fuss would die down. Easier to send a generic pamphlet than actually engage with anything real.

And what a pamphlet it is. "Arsenal Remembers Rt. Hon. Raila Amolo Odinga." How touching. They even dedicated a 0-0 draw between them and Liverpool to honour their "most prominent Kenyan supporter." Because when you're dealing with the death of a major political figure, what you really need is a football club to send a photo mount and a booklet. It’s almost as if they’re trying to paper over the cracks, just like the endless stream of politicians who suddenly found their voice to laud him. It’s all a bit performative, isn't it?

The whole "die-hard football fan" angle, as if that’s the defining legacy. He played a bit, he watched a bit, he became an ‘Invincible’ fan. Great. Meanwhile, the country he was supposed to be leading is still grappling with the same old issues. Remember the endless debates about youth employment and national unity? He championed them, supposedly. But did his passion for Arsenal ever translate into actual tangible change on the ground? Or was it just another convenient narrative, another way to connect with the masses while the real work, the dirty, unglamorous work of governance, got pushed aside? It’s a bit like people who brag about their investments in Nairobi’s property market while their own houses are falling apart, a topic we touched on briefly in [The Dollar Bouquet Delusion: Paying a Premium to Look Rich While Going Broke](/posts/the-dollar-bouquet-delusion--paying-a-premium-to-look-rich-while-going-broke).

Let's be honest, this whole "tribute" feels like a convenient distraction. While presidents and rivals send their formal letters, Arsenal swoops in with their little parcel, adding a touch of "relatability" to a man who spent decades in the political arena. It's a nice story, sure, a humanizing element. But at the end of the day, it’s just football. A game. And while it might have mirrored his political battles, it certainly didn't win them. We’ll be left with the same old song and dance, the same struggles, and probably another delayed delivery from somewhere in North London.
