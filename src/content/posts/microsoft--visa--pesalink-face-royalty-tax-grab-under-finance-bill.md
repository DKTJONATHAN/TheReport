So, the government's brilliant plan to cover their ever-growing debt? Tax the very digital tools we rely on daily. This Finance Bill 2026 is poised to slap a royalty tax on payments made to digital platforms and software providers. That means every time you use your Visa, Mastercard, or even that fancy Microsoft software for your business, expect the cost to go up. They're calling it 'royalties,' but we all know it's just another way to get more of our hard-earned cash.

This isn't just about big global players; it's about the everyday hustle. Fintech firms, banks, telcos - they're all in the firing line. Those processing fees for card payments, the license fees for essential software like Kaspersky, even switching systems like Pesalink, are now fair game for the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA). It’s like they’re punishing us for embracing digital progress. Remember how we discussed the "blood money" involved in certain dealings? Well, this is just another layer of financial burden, making everything more expensive.

They're expanding the definition of royalties to include pretty much any payment for digital services. Whether it’s a license, development, training, or support fees for software, it’s now a royalty. And for payment networks and card schemes? Transaction costs are now on the taxman's radar, regardless of what they're called - service fee, processing fee, you name it. So, those interchange fees and merchant service charges that banks and payment processors deal with? Get ready for them to be reclassified and taxed.

Kenya's pride in its vibrant fintech and mobile money market is about to get a rude awakening. With billions of shillings flowing through digital transactions daily, this tax move could send ripples across the entire ecosystem. Thousands of local businesses relying on foreign software and payment systems will likely see their operating costs skyrocket as these suppliers pass on the tax burden. It’s a classic move: hit the businesses, and they hit the consumers.

The KRA needs to hit Sh2.985 trillion in tax revenue, a hefty increase from last year. This Finance Bill is expected to deliver the lion's share of that, with the Treasury projecting Sh120 billion from these new measures. It’s all about widening the tax base, they say, while conveniently ignoring the fact that they’re choking the very engines of commerce they claim to support. Just another day in Nairobi, where innovation meets the taxman's insatiable appetite.
