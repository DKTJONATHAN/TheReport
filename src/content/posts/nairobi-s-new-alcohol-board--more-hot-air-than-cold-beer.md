NAIROBI'S ALCOHOL BOARD: MORE HOT AIR THAN COLD BEER

Don't hold your breath, Nairobi. The county government has "inducted" a new Alcoholic Drinks Control and Licensing Board. Supposedly, this is a "renewed drive to restore order and strengthen regulation in the liquor sector." Yeah, right. It's just another committee, another meeting at the Kenya School of Government, another way to shuffle papers and pretend they're doing something about the mess. They talk a big game about improving coordination and enforcing compliance, but we've heard it all before.

The usual suspects are on this board: police bosses, health officials, and NACADA folks - the same people who've been failing to sort this mess out for years. Lydia Mathia, the Chief Officer for Business and Hustler Opportunities, is spouting the usual platitudes about "starting right" and members needing to "fully understand the Alcoholic Drinks Control Act." It's all about "tightening oversight" and "addressing enforcement gaps." We'll see how much actual tightening happens when the bribes start flowing.

Mathia is "extremely confident" and promises Nairobi residents will "begin to see a difference in a few weeks." Bless her optimistic heart. They're pushing everyone to register through this "Liquor Pay system" and warning of "strict enforcement actions" against illegal operators. They're calling it a "season of compliance." This is the same old song and dance, just with a new dance troupe. They’ll clamp down for a week, then get bored, and the illegal dens and the kids drinking their lives away will be back in full swing. We’ve seen this drama play out before, much like the predictable cycle of how Nairobi's short-term rentals have become the new [Airbnb brothel crisis](%2Fposts%2Fairbnb-brothel-crisis-nairobi-short-term-rentals-koinange).

Regional Commissioner S.K. Mahano is also on board, talking about "collaboration" and "shared challenges." It’s all sounds very official and very pointless. They want to "professionalise the liquor sector," "promote responsible alcohol trade," and "protect public health." Meanwhile, the real problems - the cheap, dangerous brews, the gangs running the unlicensed joints, the kids on the streets - will probably remain exactly where they are. This is just another attempt to look like they're in control, a classic Nairobi move.
