In this town, if you are not being fleeced by a landlord, you are being scammed by a man in a polyester suit claiming to have a direct line to heaven. We call it vibrancy, but let us be real: it is a marketplace. While the youth are out here trying to survive, the pulpit has become a stage for dramatic miracle claims and constant appeals for tithes. Whether it is the horror of Shakahola or the infamous Victor Kanyari and his KSh 310 seed scandal, the script is the same. They prey on the desperate who are just looking for a way out of the mud, promising heaven while delivering a shallow grave or an empty bank account.

It is funny how these church founders equate international travel and flashy suits with spiritual authority. They parade their divine favor while their followers skip meals to keep the church lights on. This obsession with status and wealth is just another version of how [the 'soft life' just got harder: interpol crashes the fake billionaire party](/posts/the--soft-life--just-got-harder--interpol-crashes-the-fake-billionaire-party) for those who think appearance is everything. In Kenya, the gospel is often just a business venture, and business is booming because hope is the only thing we have left to sell in a country marked by unemployment and inequality.

Across the Atlantic, the Americans have their own brand of madness. They have traded the marketplace for the ballot box. Instead of selling miracles, they are selling nationalism. You have preachers sanctifying bombing campaigns and treating political figures like the second coming of the Messiah. It is no longer about love thy neighbor if that neighbor happens to be an immigrant or a refugee. They have turned the cross into a campaign slogan, proving that white-washed politics is just as toxic as a staged miracle in a Nairobi tent.

Whether it is the financial power grab in Kenya or the political power play in the United States, the core is rotten. We see pastors like Ezekiel Odero or James Ng-ang-a making headlines for all the wrong reasons, while American leaders like Franklin Graham justify war in the name of faith. They all quote the same book but conveniently forget the parts about humility and mercy. When prayer accompanies a drone strike or a seed purchase, you know the faith has officially lost its center. Faith has become a tool for the powerful to maintain their grip on the masses.

The tragedy is that while these religious giants fight for influence and wealth, the actual mission is dead. We have 85 percent of Kenyans identifying as Christians, yet our society is still riddled with greed and corruption. The churches run schools and hospitals, sure, but the spectacle is what keeps the cash flowing. If Christianity is supposed to be about self-giving love, then most of what we see today is just a well-funded ego trip. The soul is gone; only the collection plate remains. In the end, it is all just a performance for a crowd that is too tired to ask for their money back.
