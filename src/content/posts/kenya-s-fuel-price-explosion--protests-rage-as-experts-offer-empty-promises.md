Kenya's Record Fuel Prices: Protests and Paltry Solutions

Kenya, you are being fleeced. Again. As if the ever-increasing taxes and the looming spectre of corruption weren't enough, now our diesel is more expensive than gold. And who's surprised? Nobody. We all knew this was coming. The government, bless their hearts, decided to slap another 23.5% onto diesel prices for May, pushing us to a ridiculous $1.87 a liter. This isn't just a price hike; it's a slap in the face to every Kenyan trying to get by.

The immediate fallout? Chaos, naturally. Taxis, the lifeblood of this city, decided they'd had enough and suspended services. Nairobi plunged into a "deadly frenzy," which is just a polite way of saying we all went a bit mad. The government, scrambling as usual, managed to broker a temporary ceasefire, but let's not pretend this is over. It’s just the calm before the next storm, especially with more taxes, including some for those struggling SMMEs, being pushed through. Honestly, it's like they *want* us to fail.

The irony? We’ve got oil sitting in Turkana, just waiting to be extracted. But of course, that’s years away. In the meantime, we're still beholden to the Gulf states, paying nine - yes, NINE - domestic taxes and levies on every drop of fuel. They even tried to soften the blow by halving the VAT on fuel a while back, but look where that got us: nearly 50% price increase in two months. President Ruto’s "bargaining power" is about as strong as a wet tissue in this global energy crisis.

Experts, bless their theoretical hearts, are suggesting more VAT reductions and subsidies. Groundbreaking stuff. Meanwhile, businesses are either cutting back or shutting down entirely, which is exactly what happens when your entire economy is built on a foundation of expensive fuel and a thirst for more taxes. It’s a vicious cycle, and frankly, it’s exhausting. It reminds me of how these big corporations always seem to win, no matter what - like with [The Semaglutide Shakedown: Why Corporate Monopolies Always Win](/posts/the-semaglutide-shakedown--why-corporate-monopolies-always-win).

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz was supposed to be a wake-up call, but it seems our leaders are still hitting the snooze button. If this conflict continues, and let’s be honest, it probably will, we might actually have to consider looking elsewhere for fuel. Imagine that. But for now, we’re stuck with the same old song and dance: higher prices, public outrage, and promises that will likely evaporate faster than a puddle in the Nairobi sun.
