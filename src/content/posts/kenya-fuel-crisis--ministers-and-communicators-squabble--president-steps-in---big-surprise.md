So, the big shots in government are having a spat. Ministers and their communication teams are apparently "clashing" over how to spin the latest fuel crisis. You know, the one where ordinary Kenyans are footing the bill for someone’s questionable dealings. Africa Intelligence, bless their subscription-only hearts, tells us the president has finally noticed that Chinese businesses are complaining about delays. Imagine that. It’s not like we haven't seen this movie before.

Meanwhile, the police are moving slower than a herd of turtles with that substandard fuel investigation. Naturally, the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission, bless their limited powers, has decided to jump in. They're not even afraid of ruffling feathers of cabinet members who, surprise, surprise, are backed by the president. It’s all very dramatic, isn't it? You almost forget that at the end of the day, nothing much will change for us.

The president's inner circle, a cozy collection of political, military, and police types, are all gathered around to address "terrorist threats." Because, you know, that's the pressing issue when your fuel reserves are questionable and your economy is sputtering. While the army might be all sunshine and rainbows internally, the security apparatus looks like a broken Jenga tower, ready to topple.

It’s a classic Kenyan tale: scandals erupt, officials get shuffled, but the real players, the ones with the presidential backing, seem to skate by. Those allies of the president are fuming about the "double standard" of removing junior officials while ministers implicated in the mess - the very same mess that’s probably linked to global price surges, as some like DP Kindiki and CS Wandayi would have you believe [DP Kindiki, CS Wandayi defend G-to-G deal, link fuel price surge to global crisis](/posts/dp-kindiki--cs-wandayi-defend-g-to-g-deal--link-fuel-price-surge-to-global-crisis) - remain in their plush offices. It’s not exactly inspiring confidence, is it?
