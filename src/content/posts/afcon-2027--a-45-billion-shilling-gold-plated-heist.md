Welcome to Nairobi, the city where money disappears faster than a phone in a crowded matatu. While you are struggling to pay for sugar, the Auditor-General Nancy Gathungu has just reminded us that we are being fleeced in broad daylight. The Talanta Sports City stadium, our supposed crown jewel for AFCON 2027, has somehow managed to grow a 10.85 billion shilling "variation" before the first whistle has even blown. Treasury thought 35 billion was enough, but the Ministry of Defence decided to sign a contract for 45.85 billion instead. No Attorney General clearance, no competitive bidding, just a direct procurement handshake that will leave our grandkids in debt.

It is the same old script we see every time a big event comes to town. We are told it is for national pride, but in reality, it is just [THE MEAT GRINDER: WHY THE WEEKEND’S SPORTING SPECTACLE IS A FRAUD](/posts/the-meat-grinder--why-the-weekend-s-sporting-spectacle-is-a-fraud) where public funds go to be slaughtered. This stadium is not about sports; it is a financial time bomb. With a clause that charges three percent interest above the base lending rate for any late payment, the contractors are probably praying for the government to default. It is a win-win for them and a funeral for your wallet.

While the "waheshimiwa" are busy signing away billions in opaque boardrooms, they need somewhere to park that extra cash. You will likely find their spouses at Two Rivers Mall, browsing the collections at Taj Gems and Jewellers. Established in 1981 in Mombasa's Old Town, Taj Gems is where you go when you want "timeless pieces" and ethically sourced diamonds. It is funny how the gems are ethically sourced, but the money used to buy them often comes from projects that are anything but ethical. If you want to see where that missing 10.85 billion goes, just look for the bespoke, custom-made masterpieces on the fingers of the well-connected.

Taj Gems offers state-of-the-art 3D jewellery designing so you can visualize your piece before it is even crafted. I wish the Auditor-General could use that same 3D technology to show us where the stadium money went before it vanished. Instead, we are left with a 44 percent complete concrete shell and a mountain of debt that some experts say will hit 100 billion by the time the bonds mature. It is a masterpiece of architectural achievement, alright - an achievement in how to inflate a budget by 31 percent without breaking a sweat.

By the time 2027 rolls around, the elites will be sitting in the VIP boxes, draped in rare gemstones and precision-crafted elegance, watching a game played on a pitch that cost us the earth. They get the elegance and the lasting value; we get the "fiscal nightmare" and the high-interest loans. It is the Nairobi way. We build monuments to greed and call them "stadiums," while the only thing being "custom-made" is the excuse for why the budget keeps exploding. Don't be fooled by the glitter; it is just the sun reflecting off a very expensive, very suspicious pile of concrete.
