So, the so-called "economists" are wailing about a "next global food crisis." Shocking, I know. Apparently, those little squabbles in the Strait of Hormuz are causing urea fertilizer prices to jump by a frankly insulting 20 to 60 percent. Because, you know, why should anything be affordable?

And it's not just the fertilizer. Oh no. Farmers, the backbone of this whole charade, are now looking at inflated transport, irrigation, and fuel costs. It’s the same old story, isn't it? Trouble brews somewhere far away, and suddenly your pocket is getting lighter and your plate is getting emptier. Remember when fuel prices went through the roof? This is just the sequel, but with more dire consequences. It's not Nairobi's fault, of course, it's just "global chaos hitting your pocket" yet again.

These price hikes are not some abstract economic theory; they are the direct consequence of a world that seems determined to shoot itself in the foot. While some corporate bigwigs at places like Alphabet are busy raising money for AI expansion with 100-year bonds, the people actually trying to grow our food are drowning in costs. It’s a familiar tune of inequality.

The economists warn of "supply shock risks." What they mean is your supermarket shelves will soon look as bare as Stevo Simple Boy's future child count, if Kibe's nonsense was anything to go by. It’s a predictable domino effect. Disruptions here, price hikes there, and suddenly, people are scrambling for basic necessities. It’s not a warning; it’s just Tuesday.
