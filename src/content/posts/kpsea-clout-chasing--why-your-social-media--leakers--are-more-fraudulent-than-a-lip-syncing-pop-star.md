Honestly, if I had a shilling for every time someone on X tried to "expose" the government with a blurry PDF, I would finally be able to afford that designer handbag I have been eyeing. This weekend, the clout-chasers decided to target the 2025 KPSEA results, circulating a fake slip that looked like it was designed by someone who misses the 8:4:4 system as much as we miss decent Kenyan pop music. KNEC had to jump out of bed on a Sunday morning to tell everyone to stop being so gullible and stay alert for agents of misinformation.

The fake slip was out here doing the most, listing numerical scores and percentages like it was 1998. It even claimed that "8:4:4 is back," which is basically the educational equivalent of a washed-up boy band announcing a "reunion tour" that no one actually asked for. KNEC was quick to clarify that the real learner performance reports are all about "performance levels." Translation: nobody is getting a "400 marks" sticker on their forehead anymore. It is all about "Exceeding Expectations," which is a phrase I only use when my Uber driver actually finds my house without calling me six times.

This whole situation just proves that Kenyans are addicted to drama and misinformation. Whether it is a fake exam slip or [The AFCON 2027 Billion-Shilling Ghost: Why Ruto's Stadium Promises Might Be Kenya's Biggest Sports Scam](/posts/the-afcon-2027-billion-shilling-ghost-why-rutos-stadium-promises-might-be-kenyas-biggest-sports-scam), we seem to love a good fairytale more than the actual truth. The desperation to see "marks" instead of "descriptors" shows just how traumatized we are by a system that graded our entire worth on a three-day marathon of shade-darkening circles.

The real irony? These "agents of fake news" are probably the same people who spend their days arguing about which Gengetone artist has the most organic views. KNEC is begging us to stay on verified platforms, but we all know that the "verified" checkmark on social media is now just a badge of honor for anyone with eight dollars and a dream. If you are getting your child's future from a viral WhatsApp forward, you might as well start taking fashion advice from a blindfolded ostrich.

So, let us put this to rest. The CBC is here to stay, and your child's results will continue to look more like a personality test than a math grade. Stop looking for numerical validation in a system that has moved on. If you want to see something fake, go watch a filtered Instagram reel of a socialite "living her best life" in a rented Airbnb. Leave the exam results to the people who actually have the passwords to the portal and the dignity to tell the truth.

In a world where everyone is chasing a headline, remember that the truth is usually a lot more boring than the lie. KNEC has confirmed that there is no SMS code, no secret 8:4:4 revival, and definitely no shortcut to being "Exceeding Expectations" without actually doing the work. Stay alert, stay cynical, and for heaven's sake, stop clicking on PDFs sent by "Agent 001" on Telegram. It is embarrassing for all of us.
