Welcome to Kenya, where the national pastime is running from teargas and the politicians are the primary coaches. They are calling the recent Kakamega rally a third revolution, but let us be real for a second. It is just another day at the office for Edwin Sifuna. He is out there talking about extinguishing teargas like bhang in prison, while the rest of us are just trying to find a way to breathe through the fumes. The Gen Z uprising of 2024 was supposed to change the foundations of the nation, but here we are, watching the same people use the same old scripts to stay relevant while the police play their usual role as the state's heavy-handed bouncers.

In Kitengela, the state showed its usual hospitality with live bullets and a body count that no one wants to take responsibility for. Then comes Kakamega, where the crowd decided to stay and play tag with the police instead of running home. Sifuna is positioning himself as the institutional anchor for a generation that is tired of being lied to. It is a clever move, really. He is bridging the gap between the streets and the Senate, but you have to wonder if the youth are being led to the promised land or just into another coalition agreement that only benefits the elite. 

The government is stuck in a loop of its own making. They deploy police, lob canisters, and then act surprised when the public gets even angrier. They call protesters armed infiltrators to justify the violence, which is as predictable and hollow as the [Kipchoge Keino Stadium](/posts/kipchoge-keino-stadium--a-decade-of-dust-and-33-percent-progress) project that has seen a decade of dust and almost no actual progress. This administration is gambling with a generation that has nothing left to lose, and the Wantam president label is sticking like glue because the social contract has been shredded for everyone to see.

We are being told this is a paradigm shift, a cultural awakening where digital platforms meet street chants. Sifuna and his ODM cronies are painting a tapestry of resistance with their speeches, talking about economic critique and regional solidarity. But to a realist in Nairobi, it all smells like a 2027 campaign strategy disguised as activism. The youth are fluent in hashtags, but the politicians are fluent in one thing only: survival. Whether Sifuna is the actual savior or just a better-dressed opportunist remains to be seen, but the teargas is definitely real.

In the end, the third and final revolution sounds great on a placard or a TikTok video. But in the real world, where the rent is high and the jobs are non-existent, slogans do not fill stomachs. If Sifuna thinks he can turn teargas into fuel, he better hope the fire does not burn the whole house down. Ruto's rule is being tested by a youth-led movement that refuses to fold, but if history teaches us anything in this city, it is that the people at the top usually find a way to stay there while the rest of us are left washing the sting of chemicals out of our eyes.
