Politics in Kenya is not a career path; it is a contact sport where the weak are trampled for sport. If you are a woman, the men in power already view you as a "flower girl" meant to decorate the party list. If you are a woman with a disability, you are essentially a target for public ridicule. Hamisa Zaja learned this the hard way in Mombasa. Instead of debating her policies, her own family told her to sit down and "be disabled" in private, while bullies on Facebook used photos of her physical disability to mock her. It is a nasty, spirit-crushing game where your manifesto matters less than your ability to run away when the stones start flying.

The physical environment is just as hostile as the voters themselves. While some analysts are busy discussing [strategic recalibration and defense surges](/posts/strategic-recalibration--india-s-defense-surge-and-the-evolution-of-south-asian-markets) on the global stage, our local candidates are struggling just to reach a podium. Hamisa found herself abandoned in a cloud of tear gas during a rally, waiting for her team to carry her off the stage like a piece of luggage because the terrain was a death trap. Imagine being visually impaired like Sara Mueni, trying to navigate a campaign trail where you cannot even trust the person leading you. It is not just about the lack of ramps; it is about a society that thinks a disabled woman contesting a seat is a sin against religion and nature.

Then there is the cold, hard reality of the "political tax." You cannot run for an MCA seat in Changamwe on "vibes and insha." Sara Mueni ended up drowning in debts that took years to clear because being disabled makes campaigning twice as expensive. You need trusted handlers to ensure you are not robbed or led into a ditch, and you need transport that does not exist in the "voter-rich" slums. The political parties are the biggest scammers in this equation. They promise nomination slots like they are handing out free sweets, only to demand sixty thousand shillings for a seat that was never actually available. It is a pyramid scheme dressed in party colors and empty promises.

The statistics are a slap in the face to anyone dreaming of "inclusive leadership." In the 2022 elections, out of six hundred candidates with disabilities, only seven managed to win. Only one of those winners was a woman. That is not progress; that is a statistical error. We can talk about gender-based violence policies and dedicated funds until we are blue in the face, but the system is working exactly as intended. It is designed to keep the marginalized on the sidelines, begging for "favors" from party leaders who treat them like charity cases. As long as Kenyan politics remains a "vurugu mechi," visibility is just another word for being a bigger target for the bullies.
