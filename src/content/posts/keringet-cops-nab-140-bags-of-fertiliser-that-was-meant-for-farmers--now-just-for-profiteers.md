So, the boys in blue, with their Ministry of Agriculture buddies in tow, finally caught a few rats in Keringet. 140 bags of subsidised fertiliser, pilfered and repackaged to squeeze more shillings out of farmers who can barely afford to eat, let alone grow food. Of course, it was "repackaging for sale to innocent farmers" - because nothing screams innocence like robbing people blind. They nabbed the lorry driver and the main man behind the operation, but you know there are bigger fish swimming in this murky pond.

Deputy County Commissioner Malak Namai, bless his heart, is all puffed up about cracking down on these "unscrupulous businessmen." He says these jokers were selling the repackaged stuff for Ksh6,000 to Ksh7,000. Meanwhile, actual farmers who are supposed to get this fertiliser at a subsidised rate are left scratching their heads, wondering where their inputs went. It’s the same old story, isn't it? Government promises, followed by greedy hands reaching into the pockets of the already struggling. It's almost as if [The 'Soft Life' Is A Scam: Your Favorite Influencer's Airbnb Is Probably Illegal](/posts/the--soft-life--is-a-scam--your-favorite-influencer-s-airbnb-is-probably-illegal), but with dirt instead of designer furniture.

Abdullah Osman from the Ministry of Agriculture is going around the country doing "spot checks" and "intelligence gathering." How novel. I guess they’ll be surprised when they find more middlemen lining their pockets instead of fertiliser reaching the actual farms. He’s calling on the public to report suspicious activities. Because clearly, the system can’t be trusted to work on its own. We need the very people being robbed to act as the informants. Brilliant.

The Principal Secretary, Paul Kiprono Ronoh, droned on about how this fertiliser program is a "key government intervention." Yes, a key intervention that seems to be expertly hijacked by those who know how to play the corruption game. Denying farmers access to essential inputs to line their own pockets is a direct attack on food security. But hey, at least they confiscated 140 bags this time. Last year it was 233 bags from a few kilometers away. Progress, I suppose.

So, the suspects are cooling their heels at Keringet police station, waiting for "investigation and subsequent prosecution." Let's not hold our breath for any major scalps. They'll probably get a slap on the wrist, pay a small fine, and be back in business before the next planting season. The real crime here isn't just the theft of fertiliser; it's the constant, brazen disregard for the people who actually do the hard work.

This whole subsidy program is just another elaborate theatre for the well-connected to siphon off public funds. The government touts its support, but the reality on the ground is that these "interventions" are just feeding the greedy. Until we see actual, sustained consequences for these kinds of scams, farmers will continue to be squeezed, and the cycle of poverty will remain unbroken.
