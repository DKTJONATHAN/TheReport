The 25th of June rolls around, and what do you expect? Police sealing off Nairobi's key roads, of course, ahead of these Gen Z "demonstrations." It's a national holiday, not for celebration, but for the annual spectacle of government muscle-flexing against its own citizens. Two years since the last lot of protests, and the script remains unchanged: the youth demand justice, the state delivers tear gas and roadblocks. Central Nairobi, once again, becomes a ghost town, not because people chose to stay home in solidarity, but because the uniformed men made sure they couldn't even get there.

While Deputy Police Chief Gilbert Masengeli blissfully declares that "there are no blocked roads" and "the country remained calm," the reality paints a different, soot-stained picture. Commuters stranded, businesses shut, schools closed - all for the sake of "checking if they are carrying weapons." What about the police carrying water cannons and horses to disperse crowds? Or the footage from Githurai showing flames and smoke, tear gas canisters flying? It's the usual song and dance, where the authorities gaslight the public into believing their eyes are lying. They say they're ensuring safety, but it feels a lot more like ensuring submission.

The memory of more than 80 people killed and dozens injured in past demonstrations isn't just a statistic; it's a festering wound that refuses to heal. We're talking about deliberate attempts to kill, abductions, and extrajudicial killings - the kind of rot that makes you wonder if our security forces are any different from the [fake police stations in Cambodian scam compounds](/posts/cambodian-scam-compounds-exposed--fake-police-stations-and-the-rot-beneath-the-surface). Parents, heartbroken and exhausted, simply ask to remember their dead peacefully. But "peace" in this context always seems to involve running battles and tear gas. President Ruto, in his infinite wisdom, offers a $15 million compensation fund for victims, but human rights groups have already called it out for what it is: inadequate, opaque, and frankly, insulting. It's a political band-aid, not true accountability.

Ruto's administration is facing "growing public discontent," apparently. And just like clockwork, his former deputy-turned-rival Rigathi Gachagua jumps into the fray, urging Gen Z to stay home. It's not about genuine concern for the youth's safety; it's about political posturing ahead of the 2027 elections. Everyone's playing their own game, using the blood and sweat of the common mwananchi as pawns. The President warns against "destroying property," but conveniently forgets the lives destroyed by his own forces. The cycle repeats, endlessly, with promises broken and justice deferred, all while the elite jockey for position.
