Ruto's 'Broad-Based Pact': Another Kenyan Story of Unity and Empty Pockets

The President's pronouncements from Kisii State Lodge, promising unity and development for Nyamira and Kisii counties, sound like the same old song sung in a new key. Apparently, this "broad-based government" wasn't a political manoeuvre, but a deliberate move to ensure "no Kenyan is left behind." Right. And I suppose the Ksh.900 million stadium, miraculously finishing in nine months, is for cheering us all up.

He’s dishing out promises like cheap handouts - Ksh.27 billion for housing and markets in Kisii, an expanded airport. All part of the grand plan to make Kenya a "first-world country." This is the same rhetoric we’ve heard from every leader since independence. They promise the moon and deliver dust, especially when it comes to development in regions outside their immediate political strongholds. It's a classic bait-and-switch, designed to keep the grassroots leaders nodding along while the real decisions are made elsewhere.

The President claims he's not doing an easy job, and I believe him. It's never easy to convince people to believe in the same tired promises. He's also on a four-day "development visit," which usually translates to a few photo ops, some ribbon-cutting for projects that were likely started years ago, and a whole lot of speeches about collaboration. Remember Ruto's Western Tour? It felt like [New Lights For Old Promises](/posts/ruto-s-western-tour--new-lights-for-old-promises) then, and it feels exactly the same now.

He's urging everyone to work together, to not underestimate what they can do. It's all very inspiring, if you ignore the gaping hole where actual accountability should be. The idea that investing in housing and markets will magically transform Kenya into a first-world nation is, frankly, insulting. We've seen billions poured into projects that disappear into the ether, leaving the common mwananchi with little to show for it.

The President is projecting a vision of a united Kenya, fast-tracking development through broad-based governance. But let's be real, these pacts are usually about consolidating power and ensuring political survival. The "unity" they preach often dissolves once the elections are over, and the development promised to these counties will likely be dissected and delayed by bureaucratic red tape and the usual suspects lining their pockets.

So, yes, President Ruto is on a visit. He's promising big things for Kisii and Nyamira. But as a Nairobi realist, I've seen this movie before. It’s a story of hope peddled by politicians, a story that ends with more questions than answers and a bank account that remains stubbornly empty for the majority.
