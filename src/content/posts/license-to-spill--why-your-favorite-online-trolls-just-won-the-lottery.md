Harrison Nyende Mumia, the man who lives to give the religious right a migraine, is finally getting his flowers-or at least a "get out of jail free" card. After being dragged to court for allegedly posting that President William Ruto had kicked the bucket, Mumia was facing the wrath of the Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act. But the Court of Appeal just handed him a lifeline that has every clout-chaser in Nairobi screaming. Judges Patrick Kiage, Aggrey Muchelule, and Welson Korir basically told the government that they cannot jail people just for being "creative" with the truth on Instagram.

The judges took one look at Sections 22 and 23-those pesky little clauses MPs "sneaked" in to stop us from calling out their bad behavior-and declared them unconstitutional. They called these laws "unguided missiles" that were designed to hunt down anyone who dared to speak out. For the gossip industry, this is huge. It means the "social media warriors" who spend their nights forwarding unverified tea without reading a single word are finally safe from the long arm of the law. It is open season on the timeline, and I for one am here for the chaos.

It is honestly hilarious to watch the state try to "police" the timeline when they cannot even manage their own scandals. This ruling proves that our leaders were more interested in protecting their fragile egos than actually protecting the public. It reminds me of [The Great TikTok Takedown: Why Our Leaders Are Suddenly Terrified of Your For You Page](/posts/the-great-tiktok-takedown--why-your-leaders-are-suddenly-terrified-of-your-for-you-page) where the panic over what we say behind our screens reached fever pitch. Now, the court says that "false information is better than no information at all," which is basically a green light for every keyboard hero to keep the receipts coming without fear of a cold cell in Milimani.

The judges even got a bit poetic, bringing up Galileo and the "naked king" to explain that "truth" is a slippery concept. In a world where filters and AI-generated lifestyle goals are the norm, trying to criminalize a lie is like trying to catch smoke with a fishnet. They realized that these laws were being used to silence satire and opinions under the guise of "national cohesion." If we cannot joke about a politician's dry chest or pot belly anymore, then what is the point of being online? The hypocrisy of trying to jail a man for a Facebook post while the big fish swim free is finally being called out.

Of course, do not get it twisted-you still cannot be a total creep. The court kept the laws against cyberbullying and child pornography, because even in this lawless digital jungle, there have to be some boundaries. But for the rest of us who just want to point out the messy reality of the elite, the muzzle is off. Harrison Mumia might be an atheist, but for the gossip community, this ruling is nothing short of a miracle. Get your screenshots ready, babes; it is going to be a very messy season on these streets.
