Just when we thought the influencer era had reached its peak of delusion, Olajide Olatunji-better known as KSI-has decided to buy himself a football club. On a random Tuesday, the 32-year-old Arsenal superfan announced he is taking over a stake in Dagenham and Redbridge. Let us be real: this is not about "spiritual returns" or local passion. It is the classic millionaire move of treating a struggling sports team like a shiny new toy in a high-stakes game of Sims. Drowning in National League South mediocrity, the club is currently sitting at 13th place, which is exactly where most of these influencer-led vanity projects end up once the cameras stop rolling and the Prime energy drink hype dies down.

KSI claims he is ready for the "grit" of the lower leagues, promising to take a team with a measly 43,500 Instagram followers to the Premier League. It is cute, really. He is banking on the same "Wrexham effect" that has every celebrity thinking they can buy their way into sports history. While he is busy selling the dream of a rapid ascent, the actual fans are left wondering if he knows the difference between a tactical substitution and a YouTube thumbnail. He says he is "reachable" and that fans can just tweet him their frustrations. Right, because a man worth £80 million definitely spends his weekends scrolling through angry mentions from East Londoners instead of sipping cocktails on a private jet.

The hypocrisy is thick enough to choke on. He is preaching about hard work and his "never-say-die" attitude, citing his broken noses and thumbs in the boxing ring as proof of his resilience. But football is not a solo sport where you can just out-shout your opponent. It is a business, and often a predatory one. We have seen how the pursuit of the [The 'Soft Life' Debt Trap: The Rising Rate of Loan App Suicides Among Young Professionals](/posts/the-soft-life-debt-trap-the-rising-rate-of-loan-app-suicides-among-young-professionals) destroys those who buy into the illusion of overnight success, and this feels like the elite version of that same hustle-culture fever dream. Buying a club is easy; keeping it from going bankrupt when the "content" stops being profitable is the real fight.

At the end of the day, Olatunji is a brand, not a savior. He is trading the virtual world for Victoria Road, a stadium that holds about as many people as a medium-sized shopping mall. He is promising to be a "hands-on" chairman, but we all know how this ends. Once the novelty wears off and he realizes he cannot use a FIFA "healing card" to fix a striker’s blown-out ACL, he will probably pivot to the next trend. Dagenham and Redbridge fans better hold onto their hats, because this "rollercoaster" he is promising usually ends with the passengers feeling sick while the owner walks away with a new documentary deal.
