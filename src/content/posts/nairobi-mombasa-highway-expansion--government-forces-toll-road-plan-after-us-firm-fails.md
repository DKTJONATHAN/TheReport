NAIROBI DROPS US FIRM, FORCES FOUR-LANE TOLL ON MOMBASA HIGHWAY

Another day, another grand government plan that’s really just a dressed-up rehash of something that was already broken. Forget that fancy new expressway the Americans were peddling, the one that would’ve cost an arm and a leg in land acquisition and probably still ended up a mess. No, the Treasury, in its infinite wisdom, has decided to take the existing Nairobi-Mombasa highway, slap on two more lanes, and charge us good and proper for the privilege. Because that’s what Kenya does best: take existing problems, add a toll, and call it progress.

This whole song and dance about a private investor building a new Sh468 billion road? Dead in the water. Apparently, the Americans, Everstrong Capital, couldn't even get their financial structure straight, let alone convince anyone they had the technical chops. The Treasury boys, bless their bureaucratic hearts, finally saw the writing on the wall - buying up all that land would have been a nightmare, and who do you think would end up paying for that? Us, of course, through exorbitant tolls. They even refused to force trucks and buses onto the new road, which, let's be honest, is the only way these massive projects ever make sense on paper.

Then there’s the whole Mota-Engil drama. The Portuguese firm, part-owned by a Chinese outfit that also built our beloved SGR (remember that white elephant? [Kenya and Uganda Launch SGR to Link Mombasa to Great Lakes, But Who's Picking Up the Tab?](/posts/kenya-and-uganda-launch-sgr-to-link-mombasa-to-great-lakes--but-who-s-picking-up-the-tab)), got spooked by American financiers and bailed. So, Everstrong was left with its trousers down, unable to replace them, and the Treasury committee, not surprisingly, told them to take a hike. The tribunal, in its infinite fairness, agreed, essentially saying that just because you’ve been encouraged to waste your time and money doesn’t mean the government owes you anything. Classic.

Now, the Treasury’s PPP Directorate is busy finding someone to advise them on how to make this expansion happen. They’re talking about "cost-effective design solutions" and "minimising impact on local connectivity." That's government-speak for "we'll try not to completely destroy your neighbourhood, but don't hold your breath." The plan is to use the existing corridor, which sounds good on paper, but we all know how these "minimised impacts" tend to play out. Expect endless roadworks, more traffic chaos, and eventually, a fatter toll booth.

This highway is supposedly crucial for trade and tourism, linking Mombasa to all those landlocked neighbours who are probably wondering when they’ll ever get a decent road. But let’s not get sentimental. It’s a cash cow for the government, a way to keep the coffers full and the contractors happy. And for us, the motorists? We get to pay more to drive on a road that’s been widened, not built from scratch, and which will probably still have potholes by the time it’s finished. Nairobi realism, people.







Forget the glossy brochures and promises of a brand new superhighway. The government has officially thrown the American investor’s expensive dreams out the window and decided to patch up the existing Nairobi-Mombasa road, slap on two more lanes, and then charge us a hefty toll for the privilege. This isn't about improving our lives; it's about finding a new stream of revenue, and conveniently, the Treasury's Public-Private Partnerships (PPP) Directorate has already received bids for a transaction advisor to make this happen.

The original plan by US firm Everstrong Capital to build a separate 419-kilometre expressway, costing a cool Sh468 billion, was deemed too ambitious and flawed. The Treasury’s PPP Committee shut it down, citing weaknesses in its financial structure and technical capacity. A major stumbling block was the massive land acquisition required for a "greenfield" alignment, which would have undoubtedly driven up costs and, you guessed it, toll fees. They also weren’t about to give Everstrong the special concessions they wanted, like forcing all heavy commercial vehicles onto the new road.

Adding to the project’s woes, Portuguese firm Mota-Engil, which was meant to provide construction expertise and funding, backed out. Their ties to a Chinese firm, the parent of the SGR builder, apparently spooked US financiers. Without them, Everstrong couldn't demonstrate enough financial clout or find a suitable replacement. The tribunal eventually sided with the government, dismissing Everstrong's petition and confirming the project's lack of viability. It's a familiar story of grand plans crumbling under the weight of reality and finances.

Now, the focus is back on the existing 461km Nairobi-Mombasa (A8) road. The plan is to expand it to at least four lanes and make it an "access-controlled tolled road." The PPP Directorate is touting "cost-effective design solutions" and minimising impact on local connectivity. This "brownfield" approach, they claim, will save money and avoid the land acquisition headaches. It sounds sensible, but we've seen this song and dance before. The goal is to leverage the existing transport corridor, which is vital for trade with neighbouring countries.

The Nairobi-Mombasa highway is a critical artery for the Northern Corridor, moving goods and people to Uganda, Rwanda, and beyond. While the government spins this as a move to boost trade and tourism, it's also a clear strategy to generate revenue. The transaction advisor will now work out the technical details, commercial structure, and financing model before private investors are invited to bid. So, get ready to dig deeper into your pockets, because this upgrade, while necessary, comes with a mandatory toll.
