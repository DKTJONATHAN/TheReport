So, Sam Kitiki, the guy who used to give Nairobi's "in crowd" their fancy trims, is now out here begging on camera. Apparently, GIST cancer is doing its thing, and his pockets are as empty as a politician's promise. He’s gone from "powerfully built" to looking like he's been through the wringer, all while some gospel music plays in the background. It’s a real tearjerker, I guess, if you're into that sort of thing.

His "kinyozi scene" reputation meant nothing when the illness hit. Now his shop is drier than the Serengeti in a drought, with customers choosing to stay away. Imagine that - no income to buy medication or even put food on the table. This is Nairobi, my friend. You get sick, you’re basically screwed unless you’ve got serious cash stashed away or a long line of suckers willing to open their wallets.

The sickness isn't just hitting his bank account; it's visibly messing up his skin and lips. Daily life is a battle, and on top of that, he’s got the emotional baggage. He claims he’s still got hope, drawing strength from faith and community. Funny how faith always seems to come up when the money runs out.

He’s asking everyone to chip in, even suggesting a haircut at his place is a direct contribution. It’s a smart move, I’ll give him that. Turn a service into a charity drive. It’s a shame that people only seem to pay attention when someone is on their deathbed, especially when you consider how much hot air is usually blown about "partnerships" and "innovation" in this city, like those [Kenya, France Deepen Youth Innovation Partnership, More Hot Air Than Opportunity](/posts/kenya--france-deepen-youth-innovation-partnership--more-hot-air-than-opportunity).

Meanwhile, across town, some TikToker is dropping hints about HIV symptoms. Because, you know, it’s not enough to just worry about cancer, now we need to be scanning ourselves for other nasties. The message is always the same: get tested, prevent, treat. Groundbreaking stuff, really.

And then there’s the whole Pastor Kanyari drama, which is just your typical Nairobi soap opera. Custody battles, bitterness - same old story, different faces. Makes you wonder if anything truly changes here, or if we’re all just stuck in a loop of suffering and desperate pleas.
