Walk long enough with a stone in your shoe and you stop feeling it. The pain becomes rhythm. You learn to limp without appearing injured. That’s the Kenyan spirit for you, especially in places like Gikomba. This market, a sprawling testament to our national habit of making do, has weathered fire, floods, demolitions, and outright violence since the 1950s. It’s a stubborn old goat, refusing to be put down. But make no mistake, resilience doesn’t mean it’s not hurting.

The latest jab - Tuesday’s demolition of market sections - has left thousands of traders scrambling for air, their livelihoods scattered like dust. The Chairman of the shoe market, bless his ever-so-informed heart, claims traders were aware of the planned relocation. Aware, yes. Powerless to stop it? Absolutely. It’s the same old song and dance. Promises of relocation, vague reassurances, and then, BAM. Another day, another eviction.

And then we have the President, stepping in with his pronouncements. "Gikomba is public land... we must have documents to prove that Gikomba is public property." Oh, is that what it takes? Documents? Because for years, this "public land" has been a private battleground for survival for countless Kenyans. It’s almost as if acquiring land for development, or whatever the current excuse is, is easier than getting a straight answer about where the money goes. Perhaps if the government focused on boosting local garment production with actual incentives, instead of this perpetual cycle of destruction, we wouldn't need Gikomba in the first place. It's a concept as alien to our leadership as sound financial planning, unlike the predictable flow of cash in certain [Money Market Funds](/posts/safety-of-money-market-funds).

The narrative is always the same: disruption, displacement, and then the inevitable call for official pronouncements. It’s a tired script, one that plays out across the country, much like the endless cycles of exploitation seen in the [Petro-Illusion](/posts/the-petro-illusion--why-global-conflict-only-enriches-the-few-while-starving-the-many). Gikomba, like many other markets, is just another casualty in the ongoing war between the government’s grand plans and the everyday struggles of ordinary people. It’s not about national wardrobe; it’s about a national wardrobe that’s constantly being torn apart and then pieced back together by those who have no other choice.
