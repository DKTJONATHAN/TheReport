On the international stage, these demands signal that India is far from the self-reliant titan it claims to be. The reliance on state-mandated trade cost management reveals a nation that cannot compete when the global supply chain stops being subsidized by cheap transit and stable energy prices. By asking for moratoriums, the industry is essentially asking for permission to delay the inevitable reckoning of its bad debts. It is a classic move to socialize the risks of global instability while privatizing any meager gains.

The focus on small and medium enterprises is a clever rhetorical shield, but the outcome remains the same: more debt in a world already drowning in it. As the West Asia conflict threatens to become a permanent fixture of the geopolitical landscape, the Indian strategy of using credit as a bandage will only lead to a more painful infection later. This is not about bolstering stability; it is about keeping the lights on in a factory that can no longer afford the fuel.
