So, the 2025 Missing Voices report is out, and guess what? It’s the same old story, just with more numbers to prove it. Ninety percent. That's how many men are getting zeroed out by our dear law enforcement. Out of 125 lives snuffed out last year, 112 were men. Thirteen women. It's not exactly rocket science, is it? The boys are definitely getting the short end of the stick, or perhaps more accurately, the short end of the bullet.

And the real kicker? Nobody’s screaming bloody murder when it’s a man. The report, bless its heart, points out that while women’s deaths spark outrage, men just seem to fade into the background noise. It’s like we’ve all collectively decided that a dead man is just another casualty of the streets, not a human being whose life had value. This quiet acceptance just makes them more vulnerable, more expendable.

This isn't some random glitch in the system; it's the damn pattern. This lopsided ratio, 112 to 13, screams systemic issue. Young men, especially, are finding themselves in the crosshairs. It’s a tragic, predictable outcome when the consequences for taking a life are so consistently muted. Maybe if there was a fraction of the fuss we saw around fuel rationing, [Frustrations As Kenyan Petrol Stations Start Rationing Fuel Ahead Of EPRA Review](/posts/frustrations-as-kenyan-petrol-stations-start-rationing-fuel-ahead-of-epra-review), things might change. But alas, that’s wishful thinking.

So, as these numbers circulate, don’t expect a revolution. Expect more reports, more quiet nods, and more young men meeting their end with barely a ripple in the public consciousness. It’s the Nairobi reality: grim, predictable, and with a healthy dose of societal indifference.
