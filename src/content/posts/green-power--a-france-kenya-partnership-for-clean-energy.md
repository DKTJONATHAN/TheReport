Green Power? France and Kenya's "Partnership" for Clean Energy Is Just Hot Air









So, Paris and Nairobi are teaming up again, this time with a fancy name: the Africa Forward Summit. The talk is all about "green industrialisation" and "energy sovereignty." Sounds lovely, doesn't it? Like a fresh breeze blowing through a dusty Nairobi street. But let's be real, this is just another diplomatic dance, a performance for the world stage, while the actual issues - high energy prices crippling ordinary Kenyans and the persistent threat of global shocks - remain largely unaddressed. They’re saying they want to “refuse new dependencies,” but after decades of relying on fossil fuels, it feels more like swapping one master for another, hopefully one that’s less… smelly.

They’re crowing about how renewable capacity has almost tripled and solar costs have plummeted. Good for them. Kenya’s geothermal is apparently a “transformative” marvel. But while they’re busy celebrating their nearly 100% renewable electricity generation (which, by the way, a lot of us can’t even afford to connect to), nearly 600 million Africans are still in the dark. And those poor women and children breathing in toxic fumes for cooking? Yeah, that’s not exactly a priority when you’re busy signing deals. It’s like building a mansion on a shaky foundation.

The real kicker is this obsession with "energy sovereignty" through local value chains. Brilliant idea, except Africa is still getting less than 2% of global renewable energy investment. So, the resources are here, but the capital isn't. They’re talking about de-risking and guarantees, which sounds suspiciously like the same old song and dance to get foreign money flowing in, probably with strings attached. Remember that Sh100 million cash deal Ruto was involved in? This smells like a similar kind of "investment" where the real beneficiaries are rarely the ones on the ground.

And then there's the acceleration of electrification. Great. More electricity for those who can pay. But what about the cost? What about the infrastructure that’s barely holding up as it is? They want to power industries of the future, but what about powering the present for the majority? Electric mobility is touted as an example, but tell that to the boda boda rider struggling to keep his fuel costs down. This whole "clean energy is the foundation for industrial transformation" mantra is fine on paper, but the reality on the streets of Nairobi is a lot more complicated.

Ultimately, this partnership, like many before it, is a strategic move for France and a development agenda for Africa, as they so delicately put it. They want to reduce their fossil fuel exposure and turn Africa’s potential into industrial strength. It sounds good, it looks good on paper, and it’s definitely good for the people signing the agreements. But for the average Kenyan, it’s just more talk, more summits, and the same old struggle for reliable and affordable energy. Let’s just hope this "partnership" doesn't end up being another case of "we come to you" with nothing substantial to show for it.
