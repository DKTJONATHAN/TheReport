The usual suspects are clapping for the President because he managed to beg for a little more time in Washington. They call it "decisive engagement" and "professional diplomacy." I call it a stay of execution. While the Kenyan Embassy is busy patting itself on the back for a job well done, the reality on the ground in our EPZs is as bleak as a dry Monday morning in Nairobi. We got an extension, sure, but we did not get any more competitive. We are just waiting for the next crisis to hit.

Let us be real. This AGOA deal is like putting a fancy weave on a woman who has not eaten for three days. It looks good in the photos, but the structural hunger remains. We are celebrating "market access" while our competitors in Bangladesh are laughing all the way to the bank. They are already 20 percent cheaper than us at the factory gate. Now they have zero-tariff access to the US, while we are staring at a 10 percent disadvantage if we lose our footing. Arithmetic does not care about your "diplomatic clarity" or your political capital.

This government loves the optics of rescue. Whether it is a trade deal or the way we see [Martha Njoki And The Politics Of Rescue: Mercy, Optics, And Power Outside Nairobi City Hall](/posts/martha-njoki-politics-of-rescue), the pattern is the same. We wait for a crisis, perform a frantic miracle for the cameras, and then ignore the rot that caused the problem in the first place. For ten years, our apparel exports have been stuck in the mud. Why? Because electricity is expensive, transport is a nightmare, and the cost of doing business is basically a tax on existence.

Buyers in the US do not shop with their hearts. They shop with calculators. If Kenya remains 30 percent more expensive than the next guy, no amount of "strategic partnership" talk will keep those factory doors open. We are becoming an "opportunity-buy" market. That means we are the side-chick of global trade, used only when the main supplier has a headache. That is not how you build a sustainable industry; it is how you run a desperate hustle.

Ruto talks about job creation for the youth, but you cannot create jobs in a factory that cannot afford its own power bill. We are being outpaced by Egypt, Vietnam, and even Pakistan. While we waste time on high-level discussions and "bilateral flows," the seasonal orders are moving elsewhere. We are losing our relevance season by season, order by order, while we convince ourselves that we still have time. We do not. The global market does not wait for countries that prefer talk over execution.
