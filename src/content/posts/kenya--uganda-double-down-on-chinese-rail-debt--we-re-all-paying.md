Kenya, Uganda Double Down on Chinese Rail Debt, We're All Paying

WARNING: Kenya is burying itself deeper in Chinese debt to fund a railway extension, a project that's already costing us a billion dollars a year to service, while generating a fraction of that in revenue. This is the usual song and dance, promise of progress, and the reality of crippling debt.

This isn't about progress, it's about politicians looking good for the cameras while lining someone else's pockets. They call it "defining generations." I call it mortgaging them.

Kenya and Uganda, led by their respective presidents, are practically high-fiving near their border, celebrating the "multi-billion dollar" extension of a railway that's already a financial albatross around Kenya's neck. We're shelling out $1 billion a year just to pay interest and penalties on this Chinese loan - a sum that dwarfs the railway's pathetic revenue of $165 million last year. Apparently, being late with payments costs us an extra $260 million. Great planning.

President Ruto, with his grand pronouncements about "slashing logistics costs" and creating jobs, is clearly not the one crunching the numbers that show we're hemorrhaging cash. The ambition is to push this line all the way to Kisumu by 2027 and then Malaba. Meanwhile, the auditor general's report found millions were "wasted." But hey, who cares about wasted millions when you can have pomp and circumstance? It’s the same old story we saw when we discussed how climate resilience plans often just end up as [another PDF for the suits to file](/posts/climate-resilience-is-just-another-pdf-for-the-suits-to-file).

The excuse? Shifting cargo from roads to rail will "reduce transport costs and improve efficiency." Museveni is chuffed about rationalizing his "irrational and wasteful" transport system. It’s a nice thought, if it weren't for the fact that we’re borrowing against future cargo taxes for this phase, and China, bless their hearts, stopped lending from 2020 to 2023 because we were struggling to pay them back. They’re still partnering with Chinese firms, though. So much for independence.

The whole point, they say, is to strengthen trade with landlocked neighbours like Uganda, Rwanda, South Sudan, and the DRC. But at what cost? Kenya’s Treasury estimates this whole affair will top $3.9 billion. We've already sunk $9.7 billion into Chinese loans between 2000 and 2019, with half of that going to this railway. It’s a classic case of the predatory state, bleeding the public dry for vanity projects while capital flees.
