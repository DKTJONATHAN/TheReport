The timing is particularly grim. With the shadow of the Adani indictment looming over India Inc, the cost of overseas fundraising is set to skyrocket. Foreign investors are not looking for stable environments promised by bureaucrats; they are looking for exits. The central bank is essentially setting a trap with higher interest rates, hoping that international capital is blind enough to overlook the systemic risks of elite impunity that have long defined the Indian market.

There is also the Trump factor to consider. If H-1B visas are slashed, the reliable stream of remittances that bolsters the Indian economy will begin to dry up. This forced repatriation of talent might sound good on paper, but in reality, it puts more strain on a domestic infrastructure that cannot even keep its own deposits from fleeing. The RBI is trying to build a dam with sand while the tide is coming in.

Furthermore, the global financial order is fracturing. As China courts Saudi Arabia with dollar bonds, the traditional hegemony of Western-aligned central banks is being challenged. India's attempt to signal stability is a performance for an audience that is already looking at the exits or moving to more aggressive alternatives like Huawei's independent ecosystem. Like the situations described in The High Price of Elite Impunity, the cost of maintaining this facade of order will ultimately be paid by the common citizen whose savings are sacrificed to maintain the global standing of a few.
