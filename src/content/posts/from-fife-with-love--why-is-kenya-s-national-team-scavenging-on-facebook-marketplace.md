If you thought Facebook Marketplace was only for selling crusty sofas or haunted Victorian dolls, think again. Apparently, it is now the official procurement office for the Kenyan national paracycling team. Dr. Carrie Ruxton, a gym owner in St Andrews, was just trying to offload her daughter's old tandem bike when she got a hit from Alice Miring'u. You know, a professional athlete who somehow had to scroll through Scottish local ads because our own sports ministry is too busy doing whatever it is they do with their massive budgets. It is the ultimate "one woman's trash is another woman's international racing gear" story, and frankly, the optics are embarrassing.

The bike has been named "Valentine" because it arrived in Nairobi on February 14th, but let's not get too misty-eyed over the sentimentality. While the media is busy painting this as a "heartwarming" cross-continental miracle, the reality is a cynical slap in the face to our athletes. Alice Miring'u is a pilot for blind cyclists, a job that requires elite precision, yet she is out here relying on the kindness of strangers in Cupar, Fife. It is a classic case of the "Hustle Culture" being forced upon people who should be focused on training, not logistics and begging for hand-me-downs.

This DIY approach to national pride is exactly the kind of chaos we see when the systems designed to support us actually fail. It reminds me of the underlying decay in our modern structures, much like how [The 24-Hour Economy is a Mirage and Your Subscription is a Ransom Note](/posts/the-24-hour-economy-is-a-mirage-and-your-subscription-is-a-ransom-note) proves that we are all just one missed payment or one generous donation away from total irrelevance. We are celebrating a used bike with a tartan ribbon while the people in charge of our sports funding are likely laughing all the way to the bank in their tax-payer funded SUVs.

The bike itself is not even race-ready yet. It needs "upgraded components" and "testing" before it can actually hit the track. So, basically, we accepted a frame that a child outgrew and now we have to spend more money to make it usable for an actual grown-up athlete. It is like getting a free car that has no engine and being told to win a Formula 1 race with it. But sure, let’s focus on the "wonderful feeling" of the donation rather than asking why our national team does not have a budget for basic equipment.

The final irony? The Kenyan team is heading to Glasgow this summer for the Commonwealth Games, but the bike is staying in Nairobi because it is too much of a mess to travel back. So, our athletes will be in Scotland, the home of the bike, while the bike itself sits in Kenya getting a makeover. It is a messy, low-budget comedy of errors that highlights the massive gap between the "glamour" of international competition and the reality of being a para-athlete in a country that treats your equipment like a charity project. Stay classy, Kenya.
