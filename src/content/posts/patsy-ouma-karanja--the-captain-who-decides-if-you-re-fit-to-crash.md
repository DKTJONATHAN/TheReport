PATSY OUMA KARANJA: THE CAPTAIN WHO DECIDES IF YOU'RE FIT TO CRASH

So, Captain Patsy Ouma Karanja. Used to dream of being a pharmacist, apparently. Dispensing pills, no doubt, a lot less pressure than this flying gig. But then, life happened, as it tends to do when you're a Nairobi girl with a bit of restless energy. One minute she's eyeing a white coat, the next she's telling her folks she'll "try being a pilot." Her mum, bless her worried heart, fretted about women in the cockpit. Big surprise there. It's a man's world, and the skies are no different.

Her path wasn't exactly a smooth takeoff. Couldn't afford the fancy overseas training, so she humbled herself, knocking on hangar doors at Wilson Airport, offering to work for free. Ended up in the Kenya Police Airwing, where apparently flying hours were as rare as an honest politician. Then a stint with Kenya Airways, 15 years of it, probably staring out the window, wondering if this whole "conquering the skies" thing was worth it, especially when the pandemic hit and she was stuck at home with kids.

Now she's a big shot at RwandAir, a bunch of titles that sound impressive, but at the end of the day, she's the one with the power to ground you. Boeing 737 fleet training captain, line training captain, designated pilot examiner, type rating instructor, synthetic flight examiner, crew resource management trainer. It's a mouthful, and frankly, it sounds like a lot of office work and paperwork masquerading as high-stakes aviation. She's overseeing 104 colleagues, deciding who gets to fly and who gets to stay on the ground. All this after a little detour into teaching, which, let's be honest, is probably where the real satisfaction lies, especially when you're dealing with the egos of pilots.

She talks about resilience and training for the worst-case scenario, which, let's face it, is just a polite way of saying "things go wrong, and sometimes women are blamed." She even recalls a hydraulic failure en route to Lagos where passengers panicked, doubting her *because she was a woman*. Honestly, the entitlement. As if a woman's competence is up for debate when the plane is dropping. Still, the passengers refused a replacement crew, which is a nice touch, a bit of validation in a world that often denies it. She's trained a few women, too, probably trying to nudge the needle on that abysmal 4-6% global statistic, while also juggling the "administrative demands" of her many roles. It’s a lot of pressure, trying to be a pioneer while also keeping the whole operation from turning into a circus, much like the kind of political maneuvering you see in [THE DP SEAT IS JUST A MUSICAL CHAIR FOR THE ELITE](/posts/the-dp-seat-is-just-a-musical-chair-for-the-elite).

She wishes aviation was as accessible as a matatu or boda boda, which is a laugh. Imagine the chaos. Drones and flying taxis are appearing, apparently. Great. More things to break, more things for the government to mess up. She's hopeful, though. Says it's possible, it's doable, you just have to keep knocking on doors. Or kicking them open, as she puts it. Because at the end of the day, it's not just about flying planes, it's about shaping the people who fly them. And deciding who is ready to take to the skies, and more importantly, who isn't.

And her family, her daughters, her husband - that's what truly makes her heart move. Good for her. Must be nice to have a personal life to retreat to after a long day of judging grown men and women based on their ability to handle a metal tube hurtling through the air. She also found fulfillment in a business she built during her break. Because apparently, one career wasn't enough. She's got to be a jack of all trades, a master of some, and a gatekeeper of the skies, all while juggling the administrative demands and hoping nobody crashes their plane because the training was just a little too... *basic*.
