So, there's this new research out, courtesy of the International Rescue Committee's Re:BUiLD initiative. It basically confirms what any Nairobi matatu conductor could tell you: running a business here is a struggle, and for refugee women, it's an even bigger one. They're raking in a measly $52 a month, while the men are pocketing $88. And guess what sectors they're stuck in? Food and clothes. The highly competitive, lowest-margin dumpsters of the urban informal economy. It's almost like they're being set up to fail.

They even threw cash grants and mentorship at these women, hoping it would magically fix things. Apparently not. It helped them own more chairs and tables, but did it help them make more money or spend more time actually *doing* business? Nope. Meanwhile, the aggregate data shows cash transfers *do* help overall. So, it's not about the money itself; it's about who gets it and what they're allowed to do with it. Typical.

The report bangs on about capital being the "single most binding constraint." Of course it is. In this city, if you can't restock your stall or buy new tools, you're finished. You're just another casualty of the relentless hustle, whether you're a Kenyan mum selling mandazi or a refugee trying to make ends meet. It’s a brutal cycle where a bad trading week can mean you shut down for good. We’ve seen similar struggles play out in other tech-related spheres, like the ongoing debates around platforms that promise connection but often deliver disparity, as hinted at in discussions about the [Silicon Savannah or Digital Slaughterhouse?](/posts/silicon-savannah-or-digital-slaughterhouse).

And let's not forget the Nairobi refugee numbers - over 118,000 registered souls by April 2026. The policy sharks are finally noticing that refugees aren't just stuck in camps anymore; they're here, in the thick of it, navigating the same chaotic systems as everyone else: licensing, markets, rent, transport, the whole lot. But for refugees, the rules are often murky, and they end up in the shakiest corners of the economy, with less security and zero chance of climbing the ladder.

The real kicker? The weaker outcomes for refugee women aren't just about money. It's the whole package: they can't move around freely, they're stuck with childcare duties, they're cut off from useful networks and market intel, and sometimes they face downright discrimination from landlords and suppliers. It’s a compounding disadvantage that makes surviving, let alone thriving, a near impossibility. This whole situation just screams of systemic neglect, proving that the "rot" isn't just about temporary scares, but a deeper, persistent issue in how we manage our communities, much like the undertones in the [Kenya 'Cleared' Ebola Scare, But The Rot Runs Deeper Than Seven Negative Tests](/posts/kenya--cleared--ebola-scare--but-the-rot-runs-deeper-than-seven-negative-tests) piece.
