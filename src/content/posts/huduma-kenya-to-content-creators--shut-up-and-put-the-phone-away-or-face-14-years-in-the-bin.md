Darling, if you thought your ring light and a cheap tripod gave you the keys to the city, Huduma Kenya is here to snatch your wig. Our local content creators, who seem to think every square inch of this country is a backdrop for their mediocre TikToks, just got a reality check that tastes like dry bread. Apparently, some delusional "educational" vloggers had the audacity to ask if they could film their little skits inside Huduma Centres. The response? A big, fat, legal "No."

Huduma Kenya didn't just say no; they brought the whole recipe book of laws to the table. According to them, filming inside government offices is a "highly regulated activity." You need permits from the Kenya Film Classification Board (KFCB) and special clearance from whatever ministry is running the show. It turns out the Films and Stage Plays Act isn't just a dusty book on a shelf; it is a weapon they are ready to use against anyone trying to turn a passport queue into a "Day in My Life" aesthetic for the views.

Let's talk about the 14-year prison sentence, because that is the kind of drama we usually only see in high-stakes courtrooms. While some industry bigwigs are busy spinning narratives, much like how [LINDELANI MKHIZE PLAYS THE INNOCENCE CARD AMID JOYOUS CELEBRATION FINANCIAL DRAMA](/posts/hallelujah-for-the-heist--lindelani-mkhize-plays-the-innocence-card-amid-joyous-celebration-financial-drama), your favorite influencer might end up behind bars for over a decade just for a 15-second Reel. The Official Secrets Act is very clear: police stations, military camps, and even Huduma Centres are off-limits. If you think your "right to information" protects your camera lens, the state is more than happy to show you the inside of a cold cell.

The irony is truly delicious. These creators spend half their lives complaining about privacy when their "secret" sponsors are exposed, but they think it is perfectly fine to shove a camera in the face of a public officer just trying to do their job. The Data Protection Act of 2019 is finally being used for something other than annoying cookie pop-ups on websites. Filming someone without consent is a violation of Article 31, because, shocker, public officers are people too, not just props for your engagement metrics.

So, to all the aspiring "educators" and clout-chasers currently planning their next viral hit at the GPO: maybe stick to filming your fake morning routines in your rented Airbnbs. The government is not interested in your "content," and the KFCB is not handing out permits like candy at a parade. If you want to stay out of a jumpsuit that definitely won't match your aesthetic, keep your phone in your pocket and pay for your services like the rest of us peasants.
