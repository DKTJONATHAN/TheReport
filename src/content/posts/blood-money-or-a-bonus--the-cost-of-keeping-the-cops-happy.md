The Ministry of Interior just dropped the news that our brothers in blue are getting a fat pay rise. A whole 18,000 shillings extra for some constables. They call it a transformation framework, but in this city, we know better. While you are struggling to pay for unga and praying the landlord does not see you on the stairs, the people who spend their Tuesdays throwing teargas at your head are getting a 48 per cent salary hike. It is funny how money suddenly appears when the government needs to make sure the boots on the ground stay loyal to the hand that feeds them.

A fresh graduate from the police college will now walk home with over 29,000 shillings. Meanwhile, a seasoned constable can reach a basic salary of 57,700 shillings. The government wants us to believe this is about dignity and reforms. But let us be real: will this stop the "kitu kidogo" culture on our roads? Will an extra few thousand shillings stop a traffic cop from hiding behind a bush to extort a matatu driver? It is the same old story of [Manners for the Elite, Bills for the Rest of Us](/posts/manners-for-the-elite--bills-for-the-rest-of-us) where the state ensures its enforcers are comfortable while the common mwananchi continues to foot the bill for their survival.

The real winners in this reform are the senior officers at the top. While the foot soldiers get crumbs that barely cover the rising cost of charcoal, the big fish in the Prisons Service are looking at monthly paychecks of up to 584,903 shillings. It is a staggering amount of money in a country where the minimum wage is a joke. They claim these cumulative adjustments are the highest since independence, which is just a fancy way of saying they have never been this desperate to keep the security sector happy while the rest of the economy bleeds out.

Principal Secretary Raymond Omollo and his National Steering Committee are patting themselves on the back, claiming over 50 per cent of the planned reform actions have been implemented. They talk about institutional capacity and logistical preparedness as if those words mean anything to someone whose relative disappeared in a police cell. These reforms are just a cosmetic layer of paint on a crumbling wall. Increasing the pay does not change the culture of impunity that has defined the National Police Service for decades; it just makes it more expensive to maintain.

The timing is also suspiciously perfect. Effecting these raises in phases since July 2024 ensures that the officers feel just enough gratitude to keep the peace for the ruling class. We are told the National Police Service leads the way with 57.2 per cent implementation. For the rest of us living in the real Nairobi, the only thing that has been implemented is a higher tax burden to fund these raises. It is a cynical cycle: we pay more so the police can be paid more to make sure we do not complain too loudly about having nothing left.
