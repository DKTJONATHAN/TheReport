For decades, the myth of central bank independence has served as a convenient shield for politicians who lack the courage to balance a budget. Now, with fiscal spaces narrowing and polarization reaching a fever pitch, the shield is being melted down. The argument that a dual mandate - balancing employment with price stability - results in better outcomes is a fairy tale for the gullible. In reality, whether a bank has one goal or two, the result is the same when the system breaks. The poor get poorer, and the elite preserve their assets.

The mention of a divine coincidence in monetary policy is particularly galling. It suggests that by some cosmic alignment, stabilizing prices naturally stabilizes the soul of the economy. The 1970s proved this was a lie, yet here we are, watching the same script unfold. Central banks are being asked to ignore the lessons of stagflation because the current generation of leaders cannot survive an honest recession. They want the printing presses to run until the demographic winter sets in, hoping that a technological miracle will save the day.

The sudden pivot to artificial intelligence as a panacea for aging populations and shrinking labor pools is the ultimate sign of desperation. The technocrats are betting the house on a productivity boom that has yet to materialize in any meaningful way for the average worker. They are using the ghost of future innovation to justify present-day recklessness. If the AI revolution fails to deliver, the global economy will be left with nothing but devalued currencies and a mountain of unserviceable debt.

Ultimately, the debate over mandates is a distraction from the fundamental truth: the institutional framework of the global economy is failing. Independence is being traded for political survival. The vulnerable, who were hit hardest by the last surge of inflation, are being prepared as the first sacrifices for the next one. The priesthood knows the storm is coming, but they are too busy debating the semantics of their mission statements to actually fix the roof.
