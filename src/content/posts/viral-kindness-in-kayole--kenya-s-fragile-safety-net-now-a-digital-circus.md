So, another day, another sob story from Kayole hitting the internet like a rogue matatu. This time it's 19-year-old Elizabeth Nzisa, apparently playing mom to her younger siblings after being abandoned. Her story goes viral, the nation gets a collective dose of feel-good charity, and a Nairobi makeup artist, Rose, steps in. Sounds nice, right? Wrong. Because this is Kenya, and anything that gets popular online quickly turns into a feeding frenzy.

As soon as the digital spotlight shines, the wolves come out. Suddenly, Elizabeth isn't just a struggling girl; she's a public commodity. The well-meaning act of kindness from Rose turns into a nightmare as Elizabeth's inbox gets flooded with… well, let's just say the worst of Kenyan internet culture, especially from the "respectable" gentlemen. It’s a sad, predictable pattern: show a need, get some pity, then expose the vulnerable person to a parade of predators hiding behind their screens. It's like we’ve replaced actual social services with a digital hunger games, and the prize is more harassment.

This whole fiasco screams of a deeper problem. You see these informal settlements, like Kayole, where people are just trying to survive with zero support from the government. Then some brave soul, like Rose, tries to fill the massive gap left by absent state programs. But instead of celebrating her, and more importantly, ensuring Elizabeth has long-term, structured help, we turn her into a viral meme. It's a shame, really. We're so quick to offer a temporary band-aid through social media, but we forget to build the actual hospital. It’s a lot like how the glossy brochures about development in places like Karen can hide the grim reality on the ground; this viral charity does the same for systemic neglect.

The truth is, our "digital philanthropy" is a hot mess. We're brilliant at spotting a sob story and showering it with likes and maybe a few shillings, but we're terrible at protecting the people we claim to help. The internet has become a place where trauma is currency, and anyone unfortunate enough to be in need becomes a spectacle. Rose, the makeup artist, is now a digital bodyguard, not just a benefactor, all because our systems are too lazy to function properly.

We need to get real. This isn't about celebrating resilience; it's about acknowledging a massive failure in our social welfare. While it’s nice that people are giving, relying on viral hits for aid is a joke. We need actual institutions, not just fleeting internet fame, to support these children and young women. Until we demand better from social media platforms and our leaders, these vulnerable people will continue to be the collateral damage of our digital age. It's a dangerous game we're playing, and the ones who can least afford it are paying the price.
