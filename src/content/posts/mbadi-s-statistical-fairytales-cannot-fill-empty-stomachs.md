National Treasury CS John Mbadi stood before the Senate on Wednesday like a man reading a bedtime story to children who have already missed dinner. He wants us to believe that the poverty rate dropping from 42.9 percent in 2020 to 38.9 percent in 2026 is some kind of miracle. In Nairobi, we call that a rounding error. While he sits in his air-conditioned office citing "tangible results," the rest of us are watching the price of unga and electricity turn our bank accounts into crime scenes. 

The pandemic is the gift that keeps on giving for these politicians. Six years later, they are still using COVID-19 as a shield for every failed promise and every missing shilling. Mbadi says the crisis reversed development, but let’s be honest: the development was mostly happening in the pockets of the tenderpreneurs. To suggest that the current "intentional fiscal actions" are safeguarding at-risk groups is a stretch even for a seasoned politician. If this is what being safeguarded looks like, I’d hate to see what neglect feels like.

We are told that subsidies and social protection programmes are the cure-all for our misery. It is the same old script-throw a few crumbs to the elderly and the farmers while the middle class gets squeezed dry by "reforms to increase the efficiency of revenue collection." That is just a fancy way of saying they have found new ways to tax the air we breathe. It makes you wonder if every government initiative, like [Msaki’s Musical Summer Camp: Is AmaGroove 2026 Innovation or Just a Tax-Funded Vacation?](/posts/msaki-s-musical-summer-camp--is-amagroove-2026-innovation-or-just-a-tax-funded-vacation), is just another way to burn through public funds while the "informal workers" Mbadi mentions continue to drown.

I am particularly tired of the "resilience" narrative. Calling Kenyans resilient is just a way for the state to dodge its responsibilities. It is a polite way of saying, "We know we are making life impossible, but we admire how you refuse to die." Mbadi talks about "proper management of state funds" as if we haven't seen the reports of how those funds actually get managed. Between the state debt and the "missed incomes," the average Kenyan is running a marathon on a treadmill-lots of movement, zero progress.

If 38.9 percent of the population is still trapped in poverty after all these "recovery strategies," then the strategy is the problem. You cannot fix a broken house by just painting the front door and calling it "post-pandemic gains." The youth aren't looking for pillars of reinforcement; they are looking for jobs that don't require a godfather in the civil service. Until the math in the Treasury matches the math in our wallets, Mbadi can keep his percentages. We know the truth, and it isn't "slow but steady improvement"-it is just survival.
