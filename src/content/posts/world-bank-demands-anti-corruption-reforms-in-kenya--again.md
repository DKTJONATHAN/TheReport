World Bank Demands Anti-Corruption Reforms in Kenya, Again

The World Bank, bless their well-meaning hearts, have once again decided to lecture us on corruption. Apparently, our economic growth is taking a hit - down to 4.3% for 2026, they say, blaming the Middle East for some reason. But let's be real, the real culprit is the same old story: our government’s love affair with dodgy dealings. They’ve finally approved a loan, but only after dragging their feet for over a year because, shocker, they weren't keen on cleaning up their act.

Qimao Fan, the World Bank director, helpfully pointed out that every shilling lost to corruption is a shilling not spent on our schools, roads, or clinics. groundbreaking stuff. He’s pushing for four anti-corruption reforms, but good luck with those. Implementing conflict-of-interest rules will be a headache, and don't even get me started on the whistleblower bill. Parliament will likely fight tooth and nail to protect their gravy train. It’s like expecting a hyena to guard a carcass.

Fan reckons we have no choice if we want growth and to stop citizens from revolting. Funny, the 2024 protests over tax hikes, which saw people storming parliament, already showed that Kenyans are fed up with dishonest fiscal management and shoddy public services. You can't expect people to tighten their belts when they see money vanishing into thin air. It’s the same old song and dance we’ve heard before, like the empty promises made during [Ruto and Italy’s President Mattarella Meet in Rome - More Handshakes, Same Old Promises?](/posts/ruto-and-italy-s-president-mattarella-meet-in-rome---more-handshakes--same-old-promises).

Despite the World Bank’s pronouncements, our economy managed 4.6% growth. But that’s just a number. We’re still drowning in debt, with not enough formal jobs for our booming population. And thanks to the Middle East war, fuel and food prices are soaring, likely pushing another million people into poverty. So, while the World Bank gives us a lecture, we’re left with the same old problems, just with higher price tags.
