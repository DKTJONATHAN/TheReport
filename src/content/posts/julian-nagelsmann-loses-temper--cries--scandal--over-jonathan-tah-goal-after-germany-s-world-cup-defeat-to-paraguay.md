So, Germany, the mighty Germany, crashed out of the World Cup in the last 32. Not with a bang, but with a whimper and a penalty shootout against Paraguay - because nothing says 'elite football' like a game decided by pure luck. Their manager, Julian Nagelsmann, was left "furious." Furious? More like throwing a toddler's tantrum because VAR dared to apply the rules. It seems the world stops for European giants when things don't go their way.

The defining moment, apparently, was a disallowed extra-time goal from Jonathan Tah. VAR, that much-maligned technology, spotted a foul on the Paraguayan goalkeeper by Waldemar Anton. Nagelsmann, naturally, branded it a "scandal." Because it couldn't possibly be that his team wasn't good enough, could it? It's always someone else's fault. He's bleating about slow build-up play and then switches to a "brute-force approach," as if that's some genius tactic for a team full of giants.

His post-match interview was a masterclass in deflection. While acknowledging "shortcomings" - a nice, vague word for 'we were terrible' - he insisted the disallowed goal was the real problem. He saw a replay and declared it not just a "scandal," but an "absolute scandal." As if shouting louder makes it less true. It's a classic tale, really. People always find a scapegoat, always screaming 'foul play' when their own house is crumbling, much like how the talk of [Kenya's New Bangkok & Jakarta Envoys: More Than Just Pretty Faces Amidst Scam Scandals?](/posts/kenya-s-new-bangkok---jakarta-envoys--more-than-just-pretty-faces-amidst-scam-scandals) can overshadow the real issues.

The interview spiralled into a visible meltdown, with Nagelsmann losing patience as reporters dared to ask about Germany's decline after that delusional 7-1 opening win against Curacao. "Yes, as I already said, our build-up play today was simply too slow," he repeated, three times, visibly frustrated. Because clearly, repeating it makes it less his responsibility. Germany's World Cup campaign is over, leaving Nagelsmann and his squad with a "lengthy period of reflection." Or, as we call it here, time to figure out who to blame next, because clearly, the problem is never in the mirror.
