So, President Ruto is out here at State House, all pomp and circumstance, launching Justice Njoki Ndung'u's bid for the International Criminal Court. He's talking about her being an "accomplished jurist," a "constitutional reformer," and how this is "not about a person, but our nation." Right. Because when has a Kenyan politician ever done anything that wasn't primarily about their own legacy or political maneuvering, neatly wrapped in the flag? It's the usual script, dusted off for an international audience.

He's praising her "lifelong contributions to constitutionalism, gender justice, and the protection of the vulnerable," and her role in "democratic transitions." Fancy words, aren't they? Meanwhile, back in the real Nairobi, the vulnerable are still struggling, justice is often for sale, and our "democratic transitions" feel more like a revolving door for the same old faces with new promises. The 2010 Constitution, which she supposedly helped craft, is a beautiful document on paper. But principles are one thing, practice is quite another when you look at how institutions are bent daily.

Then there's the grand declaration that "Africa must help shape international justice," not just be its subject. A noble sentiment, no doubt, especially coming from a country whose leaders once rallied against the ICC itself. Now, suddenly, we're all about putting our people on that bench. It almost makes you wonder if this isn't just another play for influence, another way to secure a seat at the big table, perhaps even to quietly influence future proceedings. After all, the global stage, much like the local one, can often feel like [The Sh5.8 Billion Hustle: Africa’s Global Seat at the Scammer’s Table](/posts/the-sh5-8-billion-hustle--africa-s-global-seat-at-the-scammer-s-table).

He speaks of her "courage, intellect, and commitment to justice," highlighting her work on the Sexual Offences Act and the Maputo Protocol. Good work, yes, but let's be honest. How much has that truly translated into safety and dignity for the average Kenyan woman facing daily threats, especially when police response and judicial processes remain riddled with issues? It's easy to laud legislative contributions from State House, but the impact, or lack thereof, hits different on the streets. This isn't about her individually being unqualified, it's about the cynical framing around it.

Ultimately, this feels less like a genuine push for global justice and more like a carefully choreographed political exercise. A launch event with all the usual suspects, Chief Justice Koome and Prime Cabinet Secretary Mudavadi applauding on cue. It's about projecting an image, securing another "Kenyan" in an international body, and ensuring that our nation's "influence" extends. What that influence actually *does* for the ordinary mwananchi, beyond a photo opportunity, remains the usual unanswered question.
