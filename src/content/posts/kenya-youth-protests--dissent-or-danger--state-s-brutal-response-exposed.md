STATE CRACKS DOWN ON YOUTH PROTESTS: SECURITY THREAT OR LEGITIMATE GRIEVANCE?

Kenya’s youth are fed up, and they’re making noise. But instead of listening, the government’s just reaching for the tear gas. This article breaks down how the state sees these protests - either as a bunch of kids whining or a full-blown national security crisis. It’s the same old song and dance, really, where the state prioritizes its own skin over the actual problems facing its people.

The whole song and dance about "peaceful dissent" versus "security threat" is just that - a show. These kids, mostly Gen Z, are hitting the streets because the economy’s a dumpster fire. High cost of living? Check. No jobs? Check. Taxes strangling them? Double check, thanks to that ridiculous Finance Bill. They're using social media to shout, and the government’s response is to silence them, labelling them criminals or puppets of some shadowy force. It’s easier than admitting they messed up, right? We've seen this kind of heavy handedness before, like when the French Navy decided to make a big show in Mombasa - all about projecting power, not solving real issues.

From where I stand, it’s a human security issue, plain and simple. These young people are asking for a future where they can actually live, not just survive. They want a say in how things are run because, let’s be honest, the current lot aren’t exactly inspiring confidence. But the state’s response? Abductions, killings, the works. They're so busy “securing the nation” that they're trampling on the very people who should be the nation's future.

Then there’s this "bottom-up peacebuilding" nonsense. The idea is that if the government actually listened and included these voices, things might calm down. But no, it's easier to label it a security threat and bring out the heavy artillery. This just breeds more distrust, more anger. It’s a vicious cycle, and the youth are caught in the middle, their rights ignored while the state polices its own fear.

The numbers don’t lie. More protests, more police intervention. It’s a clear signal that the state isn’t interested in dialogue. They’re interested in control. So, while the official line is about protecting property and order, the reality is a brutal crackdown on legitimate grievances. The media and civil society are trying to shine a light on the human rights abuses, but it feels like shouting into a hurricane.

Ultimately, this whole debacle is about the state’s inability to adapt. They’re stuck in an old playbook, viewing any challenge as an attack. The youth are demanding inclusion and accountability, and the government’s response is to fortify its walls. It’s a recipe for disaster, and it’s happening right here, right now.







The streets of Kenya have been buzzing, and not with the good kind of news. From 2023 to 2024, we’ve seen an explosion of youth-led protests, all shouting about how the country’s going down the drain. But the government? They’re not hearing pleas for a better future; they're hearing sirens. It’s a classic case of the state seeing a security threat where its own citizens see a desperate cry for help.

These aren't just random outbursts; they're fueled by real issues - the economy’s in the mud, jobs are scarcer than honest politicians, and the cost of living is making everyone’s eyes water. The Finance Bill, that monument to financial mismanagement, really lit the fuse. Gen Z, armed with their phones and hashtags like #RejectFinanceBill2024, has been loud and clear. But instead of a listening ear, they’re getting tear gas and the cold shoulder. The state’s narrative is all about "criminals" and "subversive elements," a convenient way to avoid admitting their own failings. It reminds me a bit of how certain powers operate, like those French Navy displays in Mombasa - all show, no substance for the average Mwananchi.

The frameworks they trot out, like "human security," are supposed to mean the state cares about people. But when protests erupt, suddenly it’s all about protecting state actors and institutions. The real human security deficit - the lack of jobs, the exclusion, the sheer struggle to survive - gets ignored. The state views these protests not as a call for inclusion but as a threat to be neutralized, pushing these kids further into the margins.

This whole "bottom-up peacebuilding" thing sounds nice on paper, but the reality in Kenya is a top-down crackdown. Instead of dialogue and addressing the root causes of discontent, we're seeing abductions, arrests, and worse. The state’s preferred method is fear, hoping to stamp out dissent before it can gain traction. This only deepens the distrust and widens the chasm between the government and the governed.

The numbers paint a grim picture. More demonstrations, more aggressive state intervention. The rhetoric from the top is sharp, labelling protests as "treasonous" and "hijacked by criminals." Meanwhile, organizations like Human Rights Watch are documenting a disturbing pattern of excessive force, including live ammunition, water cannons, and widespread arrests. It’s a brutal response to what many see as a legitimate expression of frustration.

So, are these youth protests a sign of a healthy democracy finding its voice, or are they a ticking time bomb demanding state suppression? The state clearly leans towards the latter, prioritizing order over the rights of its citizens. This creates a dangerous situation where legitimate grievances are met with force, further eroding trust and stability in a country that desperately needs neither more division nor more crackdowns.
