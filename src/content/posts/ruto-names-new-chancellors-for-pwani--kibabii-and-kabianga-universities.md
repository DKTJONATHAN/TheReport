Ruto's University Top Jobs: More Names, Same Old Game
The President is shuffling the deck at public universities, appointing new chancellors. Don't expect miracles, just more connections.
President William Ruto's latest Gazette notices are out, and surprise, surprise, he's appointed new chancellors for Pwani, Kibabii, and Kabianga universities. Rita Kavashe is off to Pwani, Paul Russo (the KCB money man) is heading to Kibabii, and Kevit Desai, the engineer-turned-policy-guy, will be overseeing Kabianga. Five years each, just long enough to collect a title and maybe some favours.

This isn't about improving education, let's be real. It's about placing loyalists and seasoned operators in strategic positions. Kavashe, with her automotive and corporate board experience, is meant to strengthen links with industry. Russo, currently juggling KCB and a new state infrastructure fund role, is clearly a man of many hats - and perhaps many connections. Desai, who's bounced between vocational training and the East African Community, is another familiar face in the corridors of power. It's the usual Kenyan cycle of rewarding the connected, a bit like the [Mangoes, Mandates, and Premium Tears: The Kenyan Cycle of Theft](/posts/mangoes--mandates--and-premium-tears--the-kenyan-cycle-of-theft) we keep seeing.

The roles sound grand: presiding over graduations, promoting institutions, forging partnerships, and providing "oversight at the highest level of governance." In reality, these are largely ceremonial positions, often used to reward those who've been useful elsewhere or to bring in individuals with strong industry ties, who might be more interested in the prestige and networking opportunities than the actual heavy lifting of academic reform. After all, who needs actual academic leadership when you can have a corporate titan or a well-connected administrator?

It’s a classic move. You see it time and again: prominent figures from banking, industry, or even former public servants getting these high-profile, often advisory, roles. It gives the impression of progress and collaboration, but often it’s just a way to keep the wheels greased, ensuring that the right people are in the right places. We’ve seen similar moves with infrastructure projects, where the promises are grand but the reality is often more debt and less delivery, like the [Kenya SGR to Kisumu: More Tracks, More Debt, Same Old Story](/posts/kenya-sgr-to-kisumu--more-tracks--more-debt--same-old-story).

So, congratulations to Kavashe, Russo, and Desai. Enjoy your five years. We’ll be here, watching to see if any actual tangible improvements emerge, or if it's just another round of musical chairs for the Kenyan elite. Don't hold your breath for a revolution in higher education; it's more likely to be the same old tune, just played by slightly different musicians.
