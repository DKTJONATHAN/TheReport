So, the former Attorney General, Justin Muturi, is out here shouting from the rooftops about this new National Infrastructure Fund. Apparently, it's just another Kenyan masterpiece waiting to become a grand corruption scandal, a carbon copy of Malaysia’s disastrous 1MDB saga. He’s saying the whole setup, from the structure to the so-called legal framework, is practically begging for billions of shillings to disappear, with about as much oversight as a toddler in a candy shop.

Muturi is making it clear: this isn't about avoiding borrowing, it's about cleverly repackaging theft. He’s pointing out how this fund, chaired by the Treasury Cabinet Secretary, hands over too much power to the executive. These folks get to decide where to invest, how to invest, and who to partner with, all with "returns" as their primary goal, not the actual public interest. It’s the same old song and dance, where the end supposedly justifies the means, but for us taxpayers, the means are always a rigged game.

He’s quite rightly concerned that the Kenyan taxpayer, the one footing the bill for all these grand infrastructure dreams, will be the one left holding the bag. This whole charade is a deliberate move away from the constitutional financial controls we're supposed to have. Proceeds from selling off parts of Safaricom or Kenya Pipeline should go through the Consolidated Fund, where Parliament and the Auditor General can actually do their jobs. Instead, they want to funnel it all into this opaque fund, shielded from any real scrutiny. It’s a familiar story, much like how some might argue the **[The Silent Felony: How Kenya’s Penal Code Hunts Male Sex Workers for Sport](/posts/male-sexworkers-in-kenya)** highlights the exploitation of vulnerable groups when oversight fails.

Muturi isn't saying infrastructure is bad, far from it. What he's saying is that the way they're going about it is rotten to the core. He’s warning that without some serious safeguards, this fund will become a gaping hole for financial mismanagement. The risk is on ordinary Kenyans, while private investors are set to cash in. It’s the classic Kenyan playbook: we take the hit, they take the profit. And don't even get me started on the Constituencies Development Fund, which he also flags as ripe for abuse. It's all a bit too convenient, isn't it?
