So, after decades of letting Kenya's police training college in Kiganjo rot, someone's finally decided to throw some money at it. Not us, mind you - but the Americans and the UN. For years, recruits have been clanging "old barbells" in "modest gyms made from iron sheets covered in fading paint." Inspector Jendricks Nelima, who's been at this for nearly two decades, sums it up perfectly: "Our spirit keeps us going, but our facilities have struggled." Struggle is an understatement. The main gym hasn't been touched since 1967. Let that sink in.

It's not just the gym. We're talking about dining halls that are "old and overcrowded," shooting ranges with "barely functioning equipment," and libraries stuck in the last century. Officers are expected to tackle cybercrime and terrorism with resources that can't even keep up with basic physical training, forcing them to "improvise and innovate." It's a sad joke, really. The 2022 assessment finally woke someone up to the fact that "modernization is essential." What took so long for this blinding flash of the obvious?

Now, with UNODC and U.S. Embassy cash, they're promising a "long-term transformation." They'll renovate structures, install new equipment - all the shiny things that should have been there years ago. Luz Maria Guzman from UNODC calls it "just the beginning." Well, we've heard that song before. Chargé d’Affaires Susan Burns chimes in with the usual rhetoric about "security enables economic development" and allowing "young people to dream big." It all sounds very grand, but let's be honest, it's a Band-Aid on a gushing wound that our own government conveniently ignored. We've seen similar grand statements about progress before, often designed to [silence critics with roads and rhetoric](/posts/ruto-s-development-promises--silencing-critics-with-roads-and-rhetoric) rather than address root causes.

Inspector Nelima also manages a youth football club - a group of young men from nearby communities who flock to the dilapidated college grounds because, let's face it, there aren't many other opportunities. Kevin Biketi Mabonga, a 24-year-old with a diploma but no job, found "direction and purpose" thanks to the club. They train with "logs in place of weights and sledges made by a local welder." This isn't just community engagement; it's a stark reminder of systemic failure, where the police are left to fill the vacuum of jobs and positive alternatives that the state should be providing.

So, when Kevin says he's happy about a "renovated and equipped gym" coming "sometime in the future," it speaks volumes about how low the bar has been set. He believes it "shows the country is investing in us, too." But is it really *us*, or just a convenient project funded by foreign goodwill after years of neglect? "Trust grows," Nelima says, "when the community sees that police officers are invested in their future." True, but that trust shouldn't have to start from zero because we couldn't even keep our own training facilities in working order for half a century. Let's hope this "ambitious plan" isn't just another coat of paint on a crumbling structure.
