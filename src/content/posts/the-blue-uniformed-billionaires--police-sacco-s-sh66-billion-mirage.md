In a city where the average person is surviving on vibes and predatory mobile loans, the Kenya National Police DT SACCO is out here flaunting Sh66.6 billion in assets. They call it "strong financial results," but anyone with eyes in Nairobi knows that when the people with the boots and the badges start bragging about 17 percent dividends, it is time to check your pockets. It is a classic move-distract the rank and file with a Sh4.1 billion payout so they do not ask where the rest of the money is actually sitting.

CEO Solomon Angutsa is already telling members to "plough back" their returns. It is the oldest trick in the book. Do not take your money out, just leave it here so we can keep the numbers looking pretty on a spreadsheet. They talk about "wealth creation" like it is a spiritual calling, but in a place like [Nairobi: Where the Only Thing That Trickles Down is Blood](/posts/nairobi--where-the-only-thing-that-trickles-down-is-blood), we know that wealth for the institution rarely translates to dignity for the corporal on the street.

Then there is the Sh2.49 billion "capital investment plan" for ICT modernization and cybersecurity. Two and a half billion shillings for mobile access? In this town, that kind of budget for "systems upgrades" is usually code for a procurement feast that would make a hyena blush. We have seen it all before-shiny new apps that go offline the moment you actually need to withdraw money for an emergency, leaving you stranded while the "joint administrators" write more reports.

The government is also sticking its nose in, with talk of "Stability Funds" and "Deposit Guarantee Funds." CS Oparanya says the era of unsupported dividends is over, which is rich coming from a system that survives on air and promises. When they start talking about "non-negotiable compliance" and "accountability," it usually means they have found a new way to tax the only pot of money left standing in the country.

They are targeting Sh100 billion in assets within five years. It is a nice, round number for the bosses to chase while the city rots. While the global markets are twitching over trade wars and corporate valuations, the local reality remains the same: the house always wins, especially if the house is run by the people who carry the guns. Saving might be the foundation of wealth, but in Kanairo, it is also the quickest way to get your pockets picked by someone in a suit.
