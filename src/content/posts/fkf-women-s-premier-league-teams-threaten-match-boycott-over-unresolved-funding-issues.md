Teams Blame FKF Over Money, Threaten Match Boycott With A Twist

A classic Nairobi drama is unfolding, isn't it? The Football Kenya Federation Women's Premier League (FKF-WPL) is threatening to go on strike this weekend, and surprise, surprise, it’s all about the money. Eleven out of twelve teams are fed up, claiming FKF is playing games with funds meant for developing women’s football. They’ve gathered, they’ve resolved, and they're ready to throw a spanner in the works. It sounds like a broken record, but here we are again.

The minutes of their meeting are a litany of broken promises and ignored concerns. We're talking about FIFA and CAF grants that are supposed to be nurturing local talent, but instead, they seem to be disappearing into some shadowy FKF budget. Broadcast rights, prize money, awards - the usual suspects when money is involved. Even Kenya's bid for the 2026 Women’s Africa Cup of Nations is mired in accusations of the clubs being sidelined. It's the same old song and dance, just with more shinny jerseys.

Chairman Richard Teka is quoted saying FKF officials haven't responded since January, despite promises made last year. Their emails go unanswered, and a request to suspend the boycott for a promised Tuesday discussion was met with silence until a last-minute meeting offer for next week. It’s a tactic as old as time: a deaf ear until the threat becomes too loud to ignore. And now, with a meeting scheduled after the intended boycott, the timeline is too tight to be believable. It’s almost as if they’re masters of the last-minute scramble, just like some of those who chase the [SGR Travel Hacks and the Audacity of Clout: Why Kenyans Love a Good Scam](/posts/sgr-travel-hacks-and-the-audacity-of-clout--why-kenyans-love-a-good-scam) advertised on social media.

The FKF, in its infinite wisdom, has sent out a letter urging the clubs to honour the fixtures, claiming they have a "structured approach" and a meeting planned for Tuesday. Oh, the sincerity! Meanwhile, they’ve scrambled to secure venues, which is rich considering teams like Kisumu All Starlets and Kisped Queens have already pulled out due to logistical nightmares. Teka points out the absurdity of expecting teams to travel and play on short notice when they can barely afford to get to the stadium. It’s a pity the same energy isn't put into ensuring these women get the resources they deserve from the start.

And then there’s the cherry on top: Teka questioning why local female players aren’t being scouted, while FKF sends delegations to Europe to scout players for the men's Harambee Stars. It's a stark reminder of the inherent inequality that plagues women's sports, a sentiment that echoes sentiments of [LGBTQ+ Visibility and Ethical Governance Reshape the Global Sports Landscape](/posts/lgbtq--visibility-and-ethical-governance-reshape-the-global-sports-landscape), where fairness and inclusion are still a battle. This boycott isn't just about this weekend's matches; it's a cry against a system that seems to value photo ops over genuine development.

So, here we are. The clubs are threatening a boycott, FKF is offering hurried meetings, and the players are stuck in the middle, their development hanging in the balance. It’s a predictable cycle of neglect and last-ditch efforts. Let’s see if this “boycott threat” is just another performance or if it actually forces some real change. My money, however, is on the usual outcome: more talk, less action, and a lot of finger-pointing.
