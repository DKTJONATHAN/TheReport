So, the latest budget is out, and guess what? It's all about building things. Roads, railways, power lines - the whole nine yards. President Ruto is apparently betting his entire re-election campaign on whether he can pave his way back into office. Never mind the global jitters and the rising cost of chai, the big man's priority is "capital-intensive developments." Because nothing screams economic stability like a shiny new expressway while the cost of flour keeps creeping up, right?

They'll be trotting out these shiny projects like prize-winning chickens, pointing to every tarmac patch and every new pole as proof of their "tangible development outcomes." Because when the global economy is doing the cha-cha slide, and inflation is doing its best impression of a runaway matatu, what people really want is a smoother commute to work. It's the oldest trick in the book: distract them with concrete and steel, hoping they forget about the empty pockets.

KSh 750 billion here, KSh 220.4 billion for roads there - the numbers are staggering. And of course, the county governments get their usual crumbs, mostly from loans and grants, which means someone else will be footing the bill down the line. We've got billions for road maintenance, which sounds lovely, except we all know how that usually ends up - more potholes than pavement by the time the next rainy season hits. And the Nairobi Bus Rapid Transit? A nice thought, but let's see if it actually makes a dent in the traffic jams that swallow our lives. It reminds me of the endless talk about fixing things in other parts of the continent; sometimes, the pronouncements are just noise. Now, they're talking about fancy funds - the National Infrastructure Fund and the Sovereign Wealth Fund - to supposedly "protect future generations from unsustainable debt." Sounds noble, doesn't it? Except these funds are being seeded with money from IPOs and divestitures, which is just a fancy way of saying they're selling off what's left to fund the next election cycle. The Kenya Pipeline Company IPO and the Safaricom stake sale are the "seed capital" for this grand infrastructure show. We'll see how much of that actually trickles down to projects that benefit the ordinary mwananchi, and how much gets funnelled into the pockets of the well-connected.

And the grand vision? "Work, production, and exports," supported by all this infrastructure. It's the same song, different budget. The Rironi-Nakuru-Mau Summit Expressway is the poster child for this "approach," along with the promised Nairobi-Mombasa and Mau Summit-Eldoret-Malaba highways. More roads, more trade, more jobs - the eternal promise. But when you’re juggling soaring global prices and the ever-present threat of economic collapse, these grand pronouncements start to sound a little hollow. Let's just hope the rain doesn't wash away all these new roads before the votes are counted.
