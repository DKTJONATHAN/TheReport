So, the holy fathers, bless their cassocks, have finally noticed what the rest of us Nairobians have been enduring daily: our politicians sound like uncivilized toddlers throwing tantrums in public. Archbishop Maurice Muhatia Makumba of Kisumu, President of the Kenya Conference of Catholic Bishops (KCCB), has apparently had enough. He's begging these "leaders" to stop their endless insults, because apparently, the children are "shocked" and "traumatised." Give me a break. The kids are probably used to worse coming out of their own social media feeds.

He also mentioned that adults are "embarrassed." Well, that’s a step up from outright disgust, I suppose. It’s almost as if they’re admitting that these clowns are a "poor representation of our generation." No kidding. When these "leaders" publicly spew venom at each other, they're not just disrespecting each other; they're disrespecting all of us who have to listen to their nonsense. It’s a clear sign that not every thought rattling around in their empty heads needs to be broadcast to the nation.

And the gall of it all! These politicians, who are supposed to be serving the people, are acting like they own the place. Muhatia correctly points out that "this country belongs to more than 50 million Kenyans and not only to yourselves." But do you think that sinks in? These are the same characters who engage in the kind of political theatre that makes the 2027 elections look like a foregone conclusion of more of the same. It's a predictable cycle, much like the scripting we see in local football.

The bishops are speaking out because, with the 2027 elections looming, the rhetoric is getting uglier. Personal attacks, mockery, and outright hate speech are the norm. The recent spat between President Ruto and his former deputy, Rigathi Gachagua, is just the latest exhibit A in this circus of incompetence. Religious leaders and civil society groups are wringing their hands, worried about "institutional credibility" and "national cohesion." Honestly, I'm more worried about the traffic on Thika Road than their lofty pronouncements.

Still, they offer prayers. "We pray for you, that you may lead the people to the goal indicated by God." Ah, yes, the divine guidance. Because clearly, the direct application of common sense and basic decency has failed them so spectacularly. Kenya is "loved by God," they say, and they expect "only the best." Well, if you're expecting "the best" from this lot, you're probably setting yourself up for a spectacular disappointment. Like expecting a clean pothole.

One can only hope these bishops' words cut through the thick skulls of our esteemed politicians, but frankly, I wouldn't hold my breath. It's more likely to be another news cycle, another headline, and then back to the same old song and dance. Just another Tuesday in Kenya, really.
