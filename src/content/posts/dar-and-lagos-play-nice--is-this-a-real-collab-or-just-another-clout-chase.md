So, Lagos just hosted the ultimate "let's be friends" mixer for the suits from Tanzania and Nigeria, and honestly, the cringe was palpable. They called it a trade and investment forum, but it felt more like a desperate attempt to stay relevant while the rest of the world ignores us. Engr. Leye Kupoluyi was up there talking about "strategic economic interventions" and "parallel reform paths." That is just fancy talk for "we are both struggling and need a miracle." It is giving the same energy as a washed-up pop star announcing a "world tour" that only hits three cities and a local mall.

The theme was "Accelerating investment," but let's be real - the only thing accelerating is the cost of living. While they are busy "anchoring Africa's growth trajectory," the rest of us are trying to figure out how to pay for data. Dr. Chinyere Almona mentioned the global chaos with Russia and Israel as the reason we need to stick together. It is funny how these "leaders" only want to collab when the international "A-list" economies stop calling back. It is almost as chaotic as when [it-s-been-a-big - but-rocky - week for AI models from China](/posts/it-s-been-a-big----but-rocky----week-for-ai-models-from-china--here-s-what-s-happened) reminded everyone that even the most hyped "revolutions" can stumble out of the gate.

They kept mentioning the "long history of political solidarity," which is code for "we have been ignoring each other's DMs for decades." Now, suddenly, diplomatic goodwill has to be "commercially quantifiable." If my landlord accepted "diplomatic goodwill" as rent, I would be a happy woman, but here we are. Nigeria is out here doing "macroeconomic rebalancing" - which is a cute way of saying the exchange rate is a total disaster - while Tanzania is acting like the stable, disciplined sibling who finally got invited to the party.

Of course, the real stars of the show got their shoutouts. Ambassador Selestine Kakele made sure to name-drop the heavy hitters like Dangote and Sahara Energy. It is always the same old names getting the VIP treatment while the small businesses they claim to love are left outside the velvet rope. They want us to invest in "agriculture and fintech," but unless your last name starts with a "D" and ends with "angote," good luck getting a seat at that table. The hypocrisy is so thick you could cut it with a knife.

In the end, it is just another day of suits shaking hands and promising "deliverables" that we will probably never see. They want to "institutionalize business-to-business platforms," which sounds like a very expensive way to have more meetings about having meetings. Wake me up when these "meaningful partnerships" actually lower the price of a flight to Dar es Salaam or make it easier to buy a bag of rice in Lagos. Until then, it is just more corporate cosplay for the cameras.
