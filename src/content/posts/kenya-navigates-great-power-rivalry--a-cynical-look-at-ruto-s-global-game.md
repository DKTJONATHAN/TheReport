KENYA'S GRAND BALANCING ACT: GREAT-POWER RIVALRY MEANS MORE CHOICES, MORE TROUBLE

Nairobi's puffed-up chest about its "geopolitical junction" status is just that, puffery. Yes, they're getting invites to France and getting calls from America while China's digging tunnels. It's all a show, a carefully orchestrated dance by William Ruto and his crew. The Finnish President Stubb might call Kenya a "swing state," but let's be real, it's just a middleman trying to play all sides. This triangular world he’s peddling sounds nice on paper, but for us ordinary Kenyans, it just means more promises and less delivery.

The G7 invitation isn't some grand prize; it’s a strategically placed prop. France is looking for friends because its old buddies in West Africa are tired of being dictated to. So, they trot out Kenya, the dependable Anglophone kid in the class. A summit in Nairobi and a defence pact? It’s a courtship, sure, but one driven by Paris’s own convenience, not a sudden love for our soil. Being invited to the "tent" in 2017, 2024, and now 2026 doesn't magically transform us into global power brokers. As Obama himself admitted, these things are often just "talking shops" with long communiqués and zero immediate results.

Kenya’s strategy of "hedging" sounds smart when spoken by policy wonks, but it’s just a fancy word for playing both sides. China’s giving us railways we might not even need, and America’s funding health systems - a good thing, no doubt. But the real question is, what’s in it for us beyond the photo ops and the bragging rights? We see the same tired script playing out, with our leaders talking tough internationally while domestic expectations remain stubbornly unmet. It's a classic case of [Performative Romance: The Heavy Cost of Kenya's Influencer Industrial Complex](/posts/performative-romance--the-heavy-cost-of-kenya-s-influencer-industrial-complex---streamlinefeed-co-ke), all flash and no substance.

This global shift, from a US-led order to a chaotic free-for-all with rising China and assertive middle powers, is hardly something to celebrate. Conflicts are flaring up everywhere, and Kenya is expected to be a "balancer"? We're barely balancing our own budget. This idea of "swing states" is just another way of saying we're the playground for bigger powers to flex their muscles. We're not making the rules; we're just the pieces on the board that can be moved around.

So, Kenya is "oscillating across all three poles," engaging the West, accommodating the East, and asserting itself in the Global South. It’s a rational strategy, perhaps, for those who benefit from the diplomatic dance. But for the average Kenyan, this intricate choreography needs to translate into tangible benefits, not just more headlines about our president’s “manic energy.” The real test will be whether this calculated balancing act can actually deliver measurable gains for the people on the ground, or if it's just another elaborate distraction.

The world is indeed fragmented, and Kenya is being positioned as a key player in this new, uncertain landscape. But let's not get carried away with grand pronouncements. The reality is that while our leaders are busy hobnobbing with global elites, the everyday struggles of Kenyans - the rising cost of living, unemployment, and corruption - remain largely unaddressed. This "swing state" status is a double-edged sword, offering opportunities but also exposing us to greater risks and the whims of international politics.
