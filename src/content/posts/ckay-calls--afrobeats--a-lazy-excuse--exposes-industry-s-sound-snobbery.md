CKay Throws Shade: Why ‘Afrobeats’ Is Just Another Lazy Label For African Music

So, CKay, the man who practically force-fed us "Love Nwantiti" (no complaints, but let's be real), has decided to drop some truth bombs about the industry's obsession with the word "Afrobeats." Apparently, our darling singer thinks it's about as accurate as calling all Western music "white music." Honey, the audacity! But honestly, he's not wrong. It’s the same old song and dance, repackaged for a global audience that needs its music spoon-fed.

CKay, bless his Emo-Afrobeats heart, is calling out the oversimplification that comes with lumping over 50 diverse cultures and countless ethnic groups under one convenient umbrella term. He points out that even Nigeria alone has more than 36 ethnic groups, each with its own distinct musical flavor. It’s like trying to describe the entire human race as just "two-legged creatures." But hey, who has time for nuance when you can slap a catchy label on it and sell it to the masses? It reminds me of how some of these Kenyan "artists" try to pass off watered-down versions of other people’s sounds as original, much like what we saw with [Borrowed Light: Kenya’s 'Independence' is Now a Subscription Service to Ethiopia](/posts/borrowed-light--kenya-s--independence--is-now-a-subscription-service-to-ethiopia).

Of course, CKay admits that "Afrobeats" might be the palatable entry point for clueless Westerners dipping their toes into African sounds. It’s a gateway drug, if you will, designed to reel them in before they discover the real complexity. And he’s not the only one feeling this way. Burna Boy, the "Afro-fusion" advocate, has been singing this tune for ages, trying to save us from the suffocating embrace of a single genre. Wizkid, the king of switching it up, has also made it clear he’s not about being pigeonholed.

But let's not forget the commercial clout. Davido, bless his heart, acknowledged the global branding power of "Afrobeats" while subtly pushing his own "Afrofusion" narrative. It's all about finding that sweet spot between artistic integrity and cashing those international checks. And Omah Lay chiming in about the Lagos-centric bias? Darling, tell us something we *don't* know. The industry is always going to center itself, whether it’s a city or a sound.

Still, CKay is doing his own thing with this "Emo-Afrobeats" vibe. Apparently, it involves emotional chords and poetic lyrics about heartbreak - because nothing screams African originality like a good old dose of romantic turmoil, right? He’s aiming for innovation, not imitation, and honestly, good for him. While the rest of them are busy arguing about labels, he’s out there creating hits that defy categorization, proving that true artistry can’t be contained in a box, no matter how convenient the label.

The legacy of Fela Kuti, the OG, is being twisted and repackaged for mass consumption. From D’banj to Tems and Rema, everyone’s riding the wave. But CKay’s got his eyes on the prize, aiming to be remembered as an innovator, not just another voice in the chorus. He wants to build on the past, advance the culture, and, you know, make the world a better place. Bless.
