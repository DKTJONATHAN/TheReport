So, the word on the street is that Kenya’s creative sector is swimming in venture capital, thanks to everyone glued to their phones. They're calling it a "new frontier" for investors. What a joke. It's just another shiny distraction while the real issues, like why the cost of basic things keeps climbing and why some of us are still stuck in the same old grind, are conveniently ignored. They talk about "untapped potential," but what they really mean is untapped wallets - namely, yours and mine, being squeezed dry.

This whole "creative economy" boom is apparently saving us from the usual economic doom and gloom. Suddenly, animated series and catchy tunes are the new oil. Investors, those vultures, are swooping in because these digital ventures supposedly scale better than, say, building a decent road. They're more interested in intellectual property than actual infrastructure. It's easy to chase digital dreams when you don't have to deal with the messy reality of keeping the lights on for everyone else.

And the coffee? Treadville Coffee Kenya. Sounds fancy, right? Premium Kenyan Arabica from the slopes of Mount Kenya. They're talking about "distinctive terroir" and "direct farmer partnerships." But let's be real. While they're busy exporting fancy beans and making pretty packages, how much of that premium price actually trickles down to the farmer? It’s the same old story: the middlemen get rich, the producers scrape by, and we get to pay an arm and a leg for a cup of coffee that’s probably not even that much better than what my auntie brews back in the village. It reminds me of how [the give to gain delusion keeps people stuck in cycles of unfairness](/posts/the-give-to-gain-delusion--why-nairobi-s-glass-ceiling-is-made-of-reinforced-concrete).

They're touting the "Silicon Savannah" vision, trying to differentiate from Nollywood's volume or South Africa's established markets. Kenya's supposed strength is "high-quality, niche-market strategy." Translation: they're aiming for the wealthy international crowd, leaving the bulk of the local population behind. They might be creating jobs for a select few animators and sound engineers in refurbished warehouses, but for the millions of graduates struggling to find meaningful work, it’s just more of the same - an economy that prioritizes flashy digital exports over fundamental needs.

The real problem, the one no one wants to admit, is the persistent payment gap and the lack of actual infrastructure for these creators. They can churn out content, but can they actually get paid properly across borders? Without that, it’s just a bunch of talented individuals playing in a sandbox, not a serious industry. Investors are circling the "middle-mile" problems, the bits that actually make money by smoothing over the rough edges. It's all about the profit margins, not about building a sustainable ecosystem for everyone.

So, while Nairobi basks in the glow of venture capital and international attention, remember this: the "creative surge" is just another wave in a sea of economic disparities. It’s a convenient narrative for those looking to make a quick buck. Don't be fooled by the glossy packaging and the talk of a digital future. For most of us, it’s still about navigating the same old concrete jungle, hoping for a trickle of something real to fall our way.
