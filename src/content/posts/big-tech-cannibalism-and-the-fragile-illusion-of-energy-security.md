Energy security has become the new euphemism for geopolitical panic. David Rosenberg notes that the renewable energy theme remains resilient despite a temporary lull in Middle East tensions. This trend is not driven by an ecological awakening but by the stark realization that energy is a weapon of war. Europe and Asia are not buying into renewables to cool the planet; they are buying into them to hedge against the next time a regional hegemon decides to choke a shipping lane. The 48 percent jump in energy storage ETFs reflects a world that is hunkering down for a long-term siege.

The profitability of cybersecurity ETFs is perhaps the darkest indicator of our current trajectory. An investment in cybersecurity is a bet on the continued proliferation of state-sponsored chaos and digital extortion. As AI technology advances, the barrier to entry for crippling a nations infrastructure drops significantly. We are witnessing a market where the only reliable growth comes from the escalating threat of total systemic failure. Investors are not buying innovation; they are buying insurance against the inevitable breakdown of digital trust.

Canadian banks continue to outperform global peers not through superior management, but through the comfort of a protected oligopoly. The Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions recently lowered capital buffers, essentially telling banks they can hold less rainy-day cash to absorb losses. This allows for higher dividends and buybacks that keep shareholders sedated while valuations remain dangerously disconnected from the reality of a debt-laden populace. It is a house of cards maintained by regulatory decree, masquerading as financial stability.

The oil sector remains a playground for extraction and exit strategies rather than long-term development. Companies like Suncor and Cenovus are pivoting toward returning 100 percent of free cash flow to shareholders via buybacks. This is the ultimate cynical move: refusing to reinvest in long-term capacity or a genuine energy transition, and instead choosing to hollow out their own balance sheets to appease a fickle market. They are liquidating the energy future to pay for the present, betting that the world will remain addicted to their product just long enough for the executive class to cash out.
