The hike in inflation expectations from 3 per cent to 3.8 per cent is merely the opening act. As fuel, freight, and fertilizer costs climb, the global supply chain begins to choke in a way that no domestic policy can fix. This is not a localized Canadian problem; it is a planetary contagion. When the Middle East destabilizes, the cost of moving goods from one side of the planet to the other spikes, and the "transitory" lies of central bankers are exposed for the desperate fictions they are.

Businesses claim they are absorbing costs for now, but that is a temporary shield designed to prevent a total collapse in consumer confidence. No corporation acts as a charity for long. Eventually, the pressure from higher input prices will break the barrier of "limited pricing power," and the consumer will be the one left holding the bag. The mention of AI in the survey is a particularly bleak touch - even as people worry about paying for bread, they are also bracing for their jobs to be automated away by the very systems promised to save them.

This cycle is as predictable as it is depressing. For a deeper look at how global players manipulate these narratives and maintain the facade of control, see [Trump-s-pope-squabble-and-jesus-image-blunder-predictable-dysfunction-continues](/posts/trump-s-pope-squabble-and-jesus-image-blunder--predictable-dysfunction-continues). It is all part of the same theater of dysfunction where the common citizen is the only actor who actually pays for their ticket.

The realist understands that "supply and demand" is just a sanitized term for "scarcity and suffering." While the Bank of Canada holds its breath until the April 29 rate decision, the rest of the world is watching the oil pumps. If the conflict spreads, no amount of fiscal tinkering will stop the bleeding. The global economy is not a controlled environment; it is a wild animal that is currently being cornered by its own greed and geopolitical incompetence.
