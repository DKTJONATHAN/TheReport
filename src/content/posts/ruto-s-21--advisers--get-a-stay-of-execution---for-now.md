So, the big news from Nairobi is that President Ruto’s 21 handpicked buddies, the ones calling themselves ‘advisors’ and apparently costing us a fortune, have been given a temporary pass. The Court of Appeal, in its infinite wisdom, decided that booting them out immediately would be too disruptive. Apparently, the machinery of the presidency grinds to a halt if David Ndii and his pals can't pontificate from their plush offices. It's a temporary reprieve, mind you, until they figure out what’s really going on.

This whole circus started because some pesky activists and civil society types, bless their idealistic hearts, dared to question why the President thinks he can just create jobs for his friends, bypassing all the proper channels. They argued these roles were just duplicates of what Cabinet Secretaries are already doing, costing taxpayers more money for no reason. Sound familiar? It’s the same old song and dance in this country. We’re just adding more mouths to feed at the public trough.

The Attorney General’s office, naturally, swooped in to defend the status quo, claiming these advisors are crucial for the President's 'day-to-day work'. They even argued that the High Court failed to grasp the 'separate but complementary functions'. Translation: they get paid to look busy while the real work, the kind that might actually benefit us, gets stuck in bureaucratic limbo. It’s a convenient argument, isn't it? Especially when you consider how much a good lawyer can twist words. This whole situation feels like another chapter in the never ending story of Kenyan governance, not unlike how [Runda Is Just A Fancy Name For A Crime Scene](/posts/runda-is-just-a-fancy-name-for-a-crime-scene) - different players, same game.

The judges themselves admitted that the underlying constitutional questions remain unresolved, but they've urged for the appeal to be fast-tracked. Fast-tracked for them, probably. For us, it just means more waiting, more uncertainty, and more money being spent on people who were already deemed unconstitutional. The status quo at State House remains, which means the gravy train keeps chugging along.

While this legal drama unfolds, remember that somewhere in Embakasi North, teams are actually trying to unclog drains and sweep streets, basic stuff that seems to be a challenge in this city. And Nairobi County continues to rack up deaths, with over half the national casualties coming from our beloved capital. But don't worry, the President's advisors are safely in their seats, ready to offer their invaluable, and apparently constitutional, opinions.

This whole thing is a stark reminder of how the powerful find ways to protect their own. The Court of Appeal has bought them time, a temporary shield against accountability. But the questions about presidential power and the proper use of public funds are still hanging in the air, and it’s unlikely they’ll be answered in a way that benefits the common Kenyan. We’re left watching, waiting, and probably paying.
