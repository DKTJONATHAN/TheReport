The much-vaunted phrase "not fit for purpose," now a staple in the British political lexicon, is a testament to the enduring power of convenient shorthand and the perpetual failure of state institutions. Coined not by a fiery minister seeking public accountability, but by a civil servant, Sir David Normington, in a private memo to then Home Secretary John Reid in 2006, the term was initially a specific critique of the Immigration and Nationality Directorate's technological and managerial failings. Yet, like a virus, it mutated, shedding its nuanced origins to become a broad-brush indictment of the entire Home Office and, by extension, any government department deemed to be underperforming.

The irony, of course, is that the very people who now deploy this phrase with such self-righteous abandon are often the ones responsible for its perpetuation. Sir David himself, now a Lord, was forced to endure the spectacle of his carefully worded private assessment being broadcast to the world, painting all 70,000 civil servants with the same brush. His attempts to clarify the original intent - that it was about systems, not people - have been drowned out by the clamor for political theater.

The sheer volume of its use, nearly 3,000 times in Parliament since 2006, is staggering and speaks volumes about its utility. It allows politicians to appear decisive and critical without necessarily committing to concrete, long-term solutions. From armed forces housing to hospital sewerage, the phrase has become the go-to descriptor for anything that falls short of an undefined ideal, a rhetorical Swiss Army knife for those who wish to sound tough without doing the hard work of actual reform.

The consequences, while ostensibly stemming from this phrase, are often superficial. The creation of a new Ministry of Justice, while a significant administrative shift, does little to address the fundamental issues that plague large government departments. It’s a rearrangement of deck chairs on a perpetually sinking ship, a cosmetic change that allows the players to claim they've "done something."

Even the current Home Secretary, Shabana Mahmood, has embraced the phrase, echoing the sentiment of her predecessor, Suella Braverman, and declaring the Home Office "not yet fit for purpose" and "set up for failure." This is a familiar refrain, a self-fulfilling prophecy delivered by those who are supposedly in charge of fixing it. The candid admission from former special adviser Hannah Guerin about the relentless pressure and lack of long-term focus within the department underscores the futility of these pronouncements. The daily scramble for survival leaves no room for genuine improvement.

The cyclical nature of this failure is evident in the admissions of former Labour advisors, who concede that the priority was always winning the next election, not the painstaking work of policy development. The focus on immediate political gain eclipses any serious consideration of systemic reform.

The one area that miraculously escapes this damning indictment is counter-terrorism. Here, the narrative shifts to one of "heroes" working tirelessly in the face of existential threats. This stark contrast highlights a convenient selective application of the "not fit for purpose" label, reserving it for areas where blame can be easily assigned and public frustration can be conveniently channeled.

Ultimately, "not fit for purpose" is not a call to action; it is a declaration of perpetual dissatisfaction, a phrase that allows successive governments to signal their awareness of problems without ever truly solving them. It is the sound of a broken system, not being fixed, but merely being described, over and over again, in the same tired, cynical language.
