Ruto's Ksh.129B Microsoft Data Centre Project Still ON, Ambassador Thigo Clarifies - But Can We Even Power It?

Warning: Billion-dollar tech dreams are great, but when we can barely keep the lights on, this sounds like another grand pronouncement destined for the dustbin of broken promises.

Ambassador Thigo tries to spin it, but the truth is, our power grid is about as reliable as a politician’s campaign pledge.

Politics

Microsoft Data Centre, Electricity Crisis, Digital Infrastructure

Grid

So, President Ruto apparently *didn't* actually halt the Ksh.129 billion Microsoft-G42 data centre project. What he *did* do was point out the blindingly obvious: Kenya’s current electricity capacity of around 3,000 megawatts is pathetically insufficient to power this massive digital behemoth. It’s like planning a five-star banquet with a gas burner and expecting to feed an army. This is our usual song and dance, isn't it? Grand announcements followed by the cold, hard reality that we haven't even sorted out the basics.

Ambassador Thigo is out there trying to do damage control, claiming it's all about expanding generation. They're promising to hit 10,000 megawatts by 2030. Right. And I'll be president by then. It’s the same old story, using buzzwords like "transformative investments" and "economic growth" to mask the fact that we’re still struggling with the foundations. Our renewable energy, especially geothermal, is touted as our saviour, but let's be real, infrastructure challenges are always the elephant in the room, as we've seen with other grand tech promises that never quite materialize.

This whole scramble for data centres across Africa is just another rat race we're entering, probably late and underprepared. Kenya wants to be East Africa's digital hub, but we're still grappling with chronic power shortages that threaten to stall any real innovation. It's ironic, isn't it? We’re chasing the future with AI and cloud computing, while our present is defined by flickering lights and overloaded grids. Remember the "Second-Hand" Scam in Luthuli Avenue? We can't even get the simple stuff right; how are we supposed to manage something as complex as a hyperscale data centre?

The upcoming Africa Forward Summit 2026 is framed as a test of Africa’s capacity to power its digital future. Frankly, it sounds more like a test of our ability to talk a good game. Leaders will confront the need for resilient energy systems, but the reality is, we're always playing catch up. The demand for compute capacity and anchoring AI ecosystems sounds impressive, but it all hinges on one thing: electricity. And if the President himself is admitting we don’t have enough, then what exactly are we celebrating?

Thigo insists the Microsoft-G42 project is "firmly on course." On course to what? To become another expensive white elephant that drains our already stretched national grid? We're so busy trying to attract global investors with shiny digital ambitions that we seem to forget the practicalities. It’s not about the technology itself; it’s about our ability to sustain it. And right now, our capacity to power and scale our digital future looks about as robust as a banana leaf in a hurricane.
