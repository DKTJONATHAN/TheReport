If you have been following the pre-BAFTA press cycle, you might have caught Jessie Buckley trying her absolute hardest to convince us that she is just another frazzled mom who happens to own a Chanel bag. She is out here talking about stuffing cereal bars into five-thousand-dollar clutches and the struggle of Spanx. It is the classic Hollywood play-book: look as expensive as possible while talking about things that make you seem like you shop at a discount supermarket. We get it, Jessie, you are "real."

The Hamnet star really leaned into the "messy" narrative by sharing her past breastfeeding woes and the relief of rushing to the toilet to pump. It is the kind of manufactured vulnerability that celebrities use to build a brand. Much like how we exposed the truth behind certain local power couples in [The Comedy of Errors: Why the Njugush-Wakavinye ‘Fairy Tale’ Was Always a Business Plan](/posts/the-comedy-of-errors--why-the-njugush-wakavinye--fairy-tale--was-always-a-business-plan), Buckley is curating a persona that balances high-fashion glamor with relatable "human" moments. It is all very strategic, is it not?

Then comes the inevitable name-dropping of her "heroes." According to Buckley, Kate Winslet is a "rock" and Teyana Taylor is "mischievous." It must be so nice to have an emotional breakdown and have an Oscar winner there to hold your hand while you wait for your private car. She calls these "pinch-me moments," but let us be honest: this is just the A-list circle-jerk at its finest. Everyone is "bloody lovely" when you are all wearing the same designer and chasing the same golden statues.

Speaking of designers, Buckley’s new "friendship" with Matthieu Blazy at Chanel is the most transparent corporate marriage of the year. She calls him a "vibrant artist" who marries the past with the present. In the real world, we call that a brand ambassadorship and a massive paycheck. The "blue velvet" dress inspired by David Lynch sounds fancy, but at the end of the day, it is just another costume for the victory lap she thinks she deserves.

The most eye-rolling part of the whole interview? Her insistence that the "ladies' room" is the place to "get real with your girls." Because nothing says authenticity like a group of women in couture whispering about their Spanx in a marble-clad bathroom while a hired assistant waits outside. Buckley wants us to believe she is scampering home to "early motherhood" after the party, but she is doing it with a Chanel-sponsored glow that most moms will never see. It is a good performance, Jessie, but some of us can see right through the velvet.
