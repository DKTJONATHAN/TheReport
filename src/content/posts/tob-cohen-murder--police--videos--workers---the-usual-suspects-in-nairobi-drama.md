Seven years. Seven years since Tob Cohen, a Dutch businessman, vanished from his fancy Lower Kabete digs. Now, the prosecutors are rolling out their evidence, hoping to convince anyone who’ll listen that his widow, Sarah Wairimu Kamotho Cohen, is the one to blame. It’s the same old Nairobi song and dance, really - police records, sad stories from the former help, and forensic details so grim they could make a hyena gag. They finally found the poor chap’s body in a septic tank, because where else would you hide a body in a place like that?

The whole high-end residence is still under lock and key, a monument to a life cut short and a scandal that’s proving as stubborn as a Nairobi traffic jam. The court, bless its heart, is getting an earful of the gory details from the crime scene. Imagine, the recovered body, the septic tank - all laid bare for public consumption. It’s not exactly Shakespeare, but it’s what passes for entertainment in this city when the rains aren’t causing havoc, like that time [torrential rains in Kenya killed 81 this month](/posts/torrential-rains-in-kenya-kill-81-this-month--officials-confirm-waterlogged-reality).

Then, of course, the pièce de résistance: the accused faints in court. Classic. Just when the prosecution thinks they’ve got a handle on things, down she goes. Convenient timing, wouldn't you say? It’s the sort of drama that keeps the scribes busy and the public guessing, a welcome distraction from the daily grind.

And it’s not just this case. All over the country, from Nairobi to Eldoret to Mombasa, judges are being asked to authenticate wills and signatures. It seems when money and death are involved, everyone wants a piece of the pie, or at least to make sure their name is on the right document. It’s a tangled web, this life, and the courts are just the spiders trying to sort it out, or perhaps just another cog in the machine.
