WHY NAIROBI FIRMS MUST NOW REALISE EMPLOYMENT PRACTICES RISK IS MORE THAN JUST A BAD DAY

WARNING: Your company might be a ticking time bomb. The days of handshake deals and "it's just how we do things" are over. Kenyan employers are facing a legal minefield, and ignoring it means bankruptcy or a public shaming.

It’s not about what you *think* you're doing right; it's about the paperwork, the process, and the fact that everyone, from the tea lady to the CEO, knows their rights. The courts are watching, and they're handing out cash like confetti.

The old ways of sorting out employment beefs - a quiet chat, a small payout, or just hoping it goes away - are as useful as a dry umbrella in a Nairobi downpour. The 2010 Constitution basically handed employees a legal toolkit, and the Employment and Labour Relations Court is now the busiest place in town, thanks to everyone rushing to sue. You might think you're being fair, but if you skipped a step, forgot to write it down, or sent a WhatsApp message about someone's dismissal, you're toast. It's all about the process, not your good intentions. We've seen cases where a company got hammered for cutting off insurance too soon, and another for leaving an employee out of the office WhatsApp group. [HR Folders And Dead-End Dockets: Why Your Boss Is Getting Away With It](/posts/hr-folders-and-dead-end-dockets--why-your-boss-is-getting-away-with-it) used to be the norm, but not anymore.

This isn't just about getting sued; it's about your company's reputation. Investors and customers are watching how you treat people, and if they see a mess, they'll run. That's why these fancy Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) things are popping up. It's not just for paying out when things go wrong; it's supposed to help you avoid trouble in the first place. Think of it as paying for a lawyer to tell you not to do stupid things *before* you do them. This also ties into the whole ESG thing; look good on paper, get the money.

Companies that are actually managing this risk are the ones that treat communication like a serious business strategy, not an afterthought. They apply their policies consistently - no favouritism allowed, or you’ll be paying for it. And crucially, they document everything. Every decision, every warning, every policy change. It’s the only way to prove you weren’t just making it up as you went along. Because in Nairobi, if it's not written down, it never happened, or worse, it happened and you're getting sued for it.

The days of "it's just business" are over. Now, it's "it's employment law, and you're going to pay."
