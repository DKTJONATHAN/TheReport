Our "Queen of Afrobeats" is at it again, folks. Tiwa Savage has decided that being a global superstar isn’t enough, so she’s launching the eponymous Tiwa Savage Foundation. According to her Forbes Africa interview - because where else would you announce you’re saving the continent? - she’s out to discover the "next generation" of creatives. It sounds lovely on paper, but we all know that when an A-lister starts talking about "investing in the ecosystem," they’re usually just looking for a tax write-off or a way to stay relevant while the younger girls take over the charts.

She’s partnering with her alma mater, Berklee College of Music, for a four-day intensive program in Lagos this April. She’s calling it a "responsibility" to build structure behind the Afrobeats sound. It’s funny how these stars only feel that "responsibility" once they’ve already secured the bag and a couple of international awards. Much like how we saw [Redsan and Willy Paul go begging for validation again](/posts/irawma-nominations--redsan-and-willy-paul-go-begging-for-validation-again) in the latest award cycles, Tiwa seems desperate to pin the "educator" badge to her chest to prove she’s more than just a pop star.

Here’s the kicker: out of the millions of talented, struggling kids across Africa, they are only picking 100 participants. And while Tiwa brags that there’s "no tuition cost," the small print says attendees have to cover their own travel and accommodation in Lagos. Have you seen the price of a decent hotel in Ikeja or Victoria Island lately? By the time a kid from Kano or Nairobi pays for flights and a week of lodging, they might as well have paid for a semester at a local college. It’s "free" education for people who can already afford a luxury stay in Nigeria’s commercial capital.

Tiwa claims she wants to eventually build a "brick-and-mortar" institution that rivals Berklee. That’s a bold dream for someone whose main job is flying private jets and shooting high-budget music videos. She says she wants to train producers, engineers, and even music therapists. It all sounds very noble until you realize that "success" will be measured by how many of these kids get internships or "African-led ownership." It’s a lot of corporate buzzwords for a four-day workshop that mostly serves as a massive photo op for the Berklee suits visiting Lagos for the first time.

Don’t get me wrong, we love to see African music grow, but let’s stop pretending these "foundations" are purely about the art. Between the partial scholarships to Boston and the "world-class standards," this feels like a gateway to keep African talent funneling back into Western institutions. If Tiwa really wanted to change the game, she’d be funding existing local music departments that are currently crumbling, rather than slapping her name on a "flagship" program that only serves the elite few who can afford the trip.
