**Nairobi Police Brutality Victims: Compensation Scheme Launches, Fraudsters Warned**

**Fraudulent Claims Warned As KNCHR Starts Police Brutality Victim Compensation**

**KNCHR Kicks Off Police Brutality Victim Identification Process, Warning Against Scammers**

**CRIME**
**Police Brutality, Kenya, Corruption**
**Compensation**

So, the Kenya National Commission on Human Rights (KNCHR) has decided to finally get around to identifying victims of police brutality for compensation. They’re developing a whole framework, mind you, a whole fancy plan. And of course, they’re already shouting from the rooftops about fraudulent claims. Because, let's be honest, the moment there's a sniff of public money, our Kenyan brethren are already sharpening their pencils for some creative accounting.

They want victims to report to their offices in Nairobi, Kitale, Kisumu, Mombasa, Nyahururu, Wajir, Kajiado, Isiolo, and Garissa. And you better have your P3 forms, medical reports, OB reports, and postmortems ready. Apparently, they're mapping out "most affected areas" and engaging "key stakeholders." Sounds like a lot of bureaucratic jargon to mask the same old story of pain and struggle. It’s not exactly a surprise that issues of leadership and integrity are a problem, not just for the government but for us citizens too, as one official pointed out. It’s almost as if we've seen this script before, like in [Kenya Football is Just a Scripted Soap Opera for Asian Bookies](/posts/kenya-football-is-just-a-scripted-soap-opera-for-asian-bookies).

President Ruto, bless his heart, promised compensation by June, and Sh2 billion has been magically found in the supplementary budget. This whole compensation gig was one of those 10-point agendas he signed with Raila. Now, both the KNCHR and the implementation committee are singing the same tune: don't even think about faking documents, or you'll face prosecutions. They're so serious about it, they've got "experts" - doctors and police officers - ready to sniff out any falsified reports. Because clearly, the only thing more abundant than police brutality in Kenya is the ingenuity for squeezing money out of the system.

They claim they’ve got data from 2017, 2024, and 2025, and they’re calling on government agencies to cough up their lists. And for those poor souls whose relatives vanished during those "anti-government protests" and are still missing, the commission will "reach out." They'll even make recommendations for inquests and inquiries. It's a "holistic process," they say. Sounds like they're trying to cover all their bases, but let’s just hope this isn't another exercise in ticking boxes while the real issues fester.

The committee is giving everyone 21 days to compile names for verification. They'll consolidate lists from civil society and the public, hoping to create one "full list." Let's see how that pans out. If that Sh2 billion isn't enough, they'll just "allocate additional funds in the next financial year." Oh, the optimism! I’ll believe it when I see it.

And don't forget, even the police officers who got roughed up while doing their jobs are apparently on the list for compensation. Because, you know, they’re also victims. It's a real pity party, isn't it? So, bring your documents, bring your stories, and let's see if any of this actually translates into justice, or just another round of grand pronouncements and carefully worded warnings.
