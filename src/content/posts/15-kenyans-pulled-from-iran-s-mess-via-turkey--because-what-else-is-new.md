So, fifteen Kenyans decided to pack their bags and hightail it out of Iran. Apparently, things are getting a bit too spicy in the Middle East for their liking. The government, in its infinite wisdom and with enough fanfare to make a wedding blush, coordinated their safe return. Landed in Nairobi Sunday morning, after a little detour through Türkiye. Because direct flights are apparently too straightforward for these delicate operations.

They were met by some officials, probably more interested in ticking boxes than genuinely understanding the fear these folks must have felt. Then came the "debriefing" and "psychosocial support." Translation: telling them to forget about it and get back to their lives, which, let's be honest, is probably going to involve them dealing with their own problems anyway. This whole song and dance was apparently facilitated by our stellar diplomatic missions in Tehran and Ankara. Bravo.

This whole repatriation gig comes as the government is "monitoring" the safety of over 500,000 Kenyans scattered across the Middle East. A number that makes you wonder if they even know where most of them are. Apparently, no Kenyan casualties have been reported yet. Which is great, I guess. Most are just carrying on with their daily grind, working and studying remotely or in person, depending on whether their war-torn neighborhood is still standing.

Prime Cabinet Secretary Mudavadi assures everyone that their safety is a "top priority." Funny, that's the same priority they claim to have when dealing with anything else that goes wrong in this country, like the chronic lack of basic services or the fact that even getting robbed feels like a taxable event. He even suggested that if you *really* want to leave, just hop on a commercial flight or find a licensed travel agent. Because, you know, that's always an option for everyone.

And get this, Kenya Airways is apparently "repatriating some Kenyans" after securing "safe air corridors." Negotiations with relevant authorities, no doubt. Meanwhile, back home, the usual circus continues: floods in Molo, amnesty for Kenyans joining foreign armies, drivers with bad eyesight, and the ever-present whiff of mass graves being dug up. Just another day in paradise.

This whole affair highlights the precariousness of many Kenyans working abroad, a situation not unlike those who might find themselves caught in unfortunate circumstances. The government’s intervention, while commendable on the surface, often feels like a reactive measure, a band-aid on a gaping wound. We're all just trying to get by, aren't we?
