KENYA MOURNS EDUCATIONIST RAPHAEL MUNAVU AS RUTO LEADS TRIBUTES - OR WHAT'S LEFT OF HIM

The usual song and dance. Another "distinguished scholar" gone, and the President, who apparently sat in his lectures, is out there with the platitudes. Prof Raphael Munavu, a name now etched in that long list of Kenyans who served diligently only to get their send-off with flowery speeches and the promise of remembering their "legacy." Legacy? Let's see what that actually means when the cameras are off and the next committee appointment is up for grabs. It’s a sad affair, of course, but the way these things are handled in Nairobi, you’d think we’d never seen a funeral before.

President Ruto, a man who knows a thing or two about making promises - remember Kenya's 2027 Race Reopens Old Fault Lines: Another Round of Empty Promises? - is leading the charge. He’s talking about a "steadfast public servant" and a "profound legacy." It’s almost touching, if you didn't know the score. Munavu was tasked with reforming education, a task so big it’s practically a black hole for good intentions. Did they actually *listen* to his "thoughtful, forward looking recommendations"? Or was it just another box to tick before moving on to the next shiny project, like this "Silicon Savannah" they're always hyping up at Konza.

Wavinya Ndeti and Johnstone Muthama are also in on the act, calling him an "illustrious son" and a "dear friend." It’s the standard playbook: praise the departed, comfort the mourners, and subtly remind everyone of the deceased's connections. We’re told he was a "lover of Kamba traditions" and an "accomplished international scholar." All true, no doubt. But the real impact of his work, the tangible change in the lives of ordinary Kenyans struggling with the education system, that's a story that rarely makes it into the official eulogies.

At 80, the professor was still urging retired academics not to "waste their knowledge." A noble sentiment, but let's be real, how much of that knowledge actually translates into policy that benefits the masses? We’re drowning in committees and task forces, producing reports that gather dust while the system creaks along. His passing is a "great loss," they say. For the academic fraternity, perhaps. For the rest of us, it’s just another reminder that even the brightest minds often end up as footnotes in a system that seems determined to stay the same.

As for his "vision of an education system that is equitable, innovative, and responsive," well, good luck with that. We've been hearing variations of that line for decades. It's the kind of vague promise that sounds good on paper but crumbles under the weight of reality. The Competency Based Curriculum, the very thing he was reviewing, is still a headache for many parents. So, while we mourn the man, let's not get too carried away with the "indelible legacy" narrative. We’ll see what actually changes, or if this is just another chapter in The Total Erosion of Individual Anonymity, where the powerful wax lyrical while the system remains stubbornly intact.

This is how it is. A good man, a respected scholar, gone. And the politicians and academics circle like vultures, each picking out the bits that serve their narrative. The education system needs more than tributes; it needs genuine change, something far more difficult to deliver than a few kind words.
