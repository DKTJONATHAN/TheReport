Let's cut through the feel good nonsense. A new study from the Brookings Institute purports to identify workers "most at risk" of AI-driven layoffs and, in the same breath, declares them "well positioned to adapt." This is the kind of spin that makes you wonder who funded this research - likely the same people who stand to profit from mass displacement.

The study, bless its heart, looked at things like how easily AI can do a job, age, skills, local job markets, and even union membership. Apparently, 26.5 million out of 37.1 million U.S. workers in high AI exposure jobs have "above-median adaptive capacity." That's a lot of people who are supposedly going to just waltz into new careers while their old ones evaporate. Don't hold your breath.

This drivel arrives as companies like Amazon, Vimeo, Pinterest, and Block are busy shedding thousands of employees, all while touting the wonders of AI. Jack Dorsey himself practically admitted Block is betting AI will replace a huge chunk of its workforce, preferring a brutal "now" to a drawn out "later." This isn't about worker well-being; it's about maximizing profit margins by any means necessary.

The grand pronouncements about AI's economic impact are a joke. Goldman Sachs says AI investments have added "basically zero" to U.S. GDP growth. Yet, here we are, being told that those in fields like writing, customer service, and translation, which are prime targets for AI, are somehow going to thrive.

The real kicker? The study *does* identify a segment of the workforce facing a grim outlook: 6.1 million workers with high AI exposure and low adaptive capacity. Guess who's in this group? Clerical and administrative workers, a field where roughly 86% of the workforce is women. Add to that workers in university towns and midsize cities in the Mountain West and Midwest, who are apparently too geographically or demographically challenged to pivot.

Meanwhile, the darling of the tech world - web development, marketing, IT - are deemed more likely to adapt, especially in "large cities with stronger labor markets." Translation: the already privileged will continue to ride the wave, while others are left to drown.

The researchers claim their goal is to help policymakers focus resources on the most affected. That's a noble sentiment on paper, but in reality, this study feels more like a preemptive balm for the consciences of those orchestrating the layoffs. The "good news" is a smokescreen for the inevitable fallout, and the "adaptive capacity" of millions is about to be tested in the harshest way possible.
