So, the news is out. Diesel has decided to join the billionaires club, hitting a staggering Sh242.92 per litre. Petrol isn't exactly taking a holiday either, nudging up to Sh214.25. Apparently, the US-Israel war on Iran is the new excuse for why we're all paying more to get around, or to get anything delivered for that matter. Never mind that kerosene is apparently too humble to get caught in this global drama, staying put at Sh152.78.

And you think the government is doing us a favour with those "subsidies"? Please. We're talking Sh15.67 for diesel and a whopping Sh98.60 for kerosene. Petrol? Not a chance. Apparently, the Exchequer is too busy swimming in Sh17 billion of subsidy arrears to spare a dime for the fuel that actually keeps this country moving. It’s like offering a beggar a single grain of rice when his stomach is rumbling for a whole ugali.

This whole mess is apparently linked to the Strait of Hormuz and some "escalation" in the Middle East. Brent crude, the darling of global oil markets, decided to grace us with a four-year high of $126.41. It’s the same old story, isn't it? A conflict halfway across the world, and suddenly, your daily commute becomes a luxury you can barely afford. This is why we keep talking about the [Petroleum Shackle on Digital Dreams](/posts/the-petroleum-shackle-on-digital-dreams) - everything is tied to this volatile market.

And don't expect any relief. This fuel shocker is just the prelude to more pain. Inflation, already dancing at 5.7 percent, is about to do a full-blown jig. Diesel is the lifeblood of our economy, so when it costs an arm and a leg, farmers, manufacturers, and those poor matatu touts will, of course, pass the buck to us. It’s a never-ending cycle of misery.

We’re importing fuel under some "Government-to-Government" deal, which sounds fancy, but basically means the suppliers, like Aramco Trading Fujairah, are warning us of higher prices. They’re already scouting for fuel from alternative markets like India because of supply disruptions. Because why source locally when you can pay extra for a longer journey, right?

Meanwhile, countries like South Africa, Namibia, and Zambia are actually *waiving* taxes to give their citizens a break. But here in Kenya? We’ve got seven levies and two taxes on fuel. We’re practically drowning in them. Halving VAT to eight percent last month was a joke, a pathetic attempt to placate us while the real money is still being made on the back of our desperation. It's not a fuel crisis; it's a government revenue strategy.
