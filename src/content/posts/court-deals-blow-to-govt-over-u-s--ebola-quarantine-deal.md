Court Deals Blow to Govt Over U.S. Ebola Quarantine Deal, But Don't Celebrate Yet

WARNING: The High Court has temporarily stopped the U.S. funded Ebola quarantine centre in Laikipia. Katiba Institute, as usual, is screaming about public health and constitutional issues, while the government, also as usual, appears to have been caught with its pants down. This is Kenya, folks, where plans are made and unmade faster than you can say "Ksh1.7 billion."

CAPTION: Another day, another court order. This time it's about an Ebola centre, not about the usual drama.







So, the big American Ebola quarantine centre planned for Laikipia? Yeah, that's on ice. The High Court, thanks to the ever-vigilant Katiba Institute, has slammed the brakes on it. Justice Nyaundi has decreed that no U.S. government or any foreign agency can set up shop for Ebola exposure, quarantine, isolation, or treatment. Apparently, bringing in potential Ebola cases is a bit too much, even for this administration.

It seems our esteemed government, led by President Ruto, was a bit too eager to please Uncle Sam, approving this whole setup just hours before the court stepped in. Ksh1.7 billion is a lot of money, and when it's dangled in front of you, sometimes you forget to check if the paperwork is even in order, or if it aligns with actual public safety concerns. A 50-bed field hospital, expandable to 250, with U.S. Public Health Service personnel training in Maryland - sounds like a serious operation, until a court order arrives.

Katiba Institute, bless their persistent hearts, argues this poses an "imminent threat" to life and public safety. They've filed their urgent petition, and the court, in its infinite wisdom, agrees it's urgent enough to slap a temporary ban. They want to hear arguments before anyone gets admitted or transferred, which, frankly, is a reasonable request when dealing with a disease that can wipe out communities. It's a stark reminder that even with foreign funding, due process and public safety should, ideally, come first.

This whole saga is a classic Nairobi realist's dream: government making big promises, foreign powers throwing cash, and then the legal system, with its own set of players and agendas, wading in. It's a dance we've seen before, from concerns about bio-mining operations potentially contaminating our land, to the shadowy dealings that make you wonder if that cheap miracle drug you're chasing is actually part of a larger cartel's scheme. The court's intervention is a temporary reprieve, but the real drama, and the eventual outcome, is still very much up in the air.
