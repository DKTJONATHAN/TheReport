Another day, another wave of austerity hitting us like a matatu driver who's had too much chai. Remember John Githongo's story about the Serena Hotel? Back in the day, students could actually afford a beer there. Then came the IMF and World Bank, and overnight, that place became a symbol of how quickly the "balance sheets" of faraway suits can turn our lives upside down. Now, it's happening all over again.

This whole "Fuliza" thing? It's become our national anthem. We're the country that pays Peter to pay Paul, and everyone from the boda boda guy to the mama mboga is on some kind of loan tab. It’s a normalized absurdity, a daily performance of our economic gymnastics. My old colleague from Mathare had it right: "nothing goes to waste. Everything-even shit-has a price." This garbage economy popping up everywhere is just another grim testament to how the underbelly of this capitalist society scrapes by. It makes you wonder if even the elite have a plan, or if they're just playing musical chairs while the country burns. It’s a far cry from any real prosperity, unlike what some might have hoped for after reading about [Wavinya’s Road To Riches]( /posts/wavinya-s-road-to-riches--how-to-triple-debt-and-ignore-potholes).

And the kids. Oh, the kids. We're back to the 1990s nightmare, with 1.3 million children out of school. The gains of the Kibaki era are just a memory, a cruel joke told by the ghosts of progress. Families are spending 60% of their budget on food, and the taxman is sniffing around every corner. So, what do we do? We dig up our rural networks, hoping to pull food back from the village. Meanwhile, our bridges are washed away and our schools are crumbling because debt servicing is more important than, you know, actual development.

Politics always gets better when the economy tanks, they say. Well, the politics are still a mess, but at least the young people are waking up. They see the reality. Back in the 90s, it was tent churches offering a "blessing" or a green card. Now, it's street preachers shouting about the end times, a fitting soundtrack to our impending economic doom. And then there's the twisted romance with "zungu zungu" - the white man as a savior, offering financial escape. It's a sad commentary on our situation, leading to things like that horrifying case of the European man and those thousand girls. It's reorienting everything.

The day starts with the weight of this city. You see the tea-bearer, his thermos carrying the warmth of a thousand conversations. Beside him, others shoulder burdens that would crush most people - sacks of grain, crates of produce. They are the engines of this city, not hustling to dream, but to avoid the nightmare that is our debt-driven austerity. The hustle has moved from boardrooms to pool tables and bars, a clear sign of collapse and the mediocrity of our elite, who seem to have no political program for genuine prosperity.
