So, President Ruto has unveiled his "Next Generation of Kenya Youth Employment Programme." One hundred thousand young Kenyans, they say, over three years, with the UN and the private sector holding hands. Structured internships, entrepreneurship training, financial literacy, life skills, workplace readiness - the whole nine yards. Sounds like a magic potion, doesn't it? Almost makes you forget the empty promises that came before.

Fifteen thousand graduates will get stipends for internships. The rest get "specialised training." And all this, for a cool KSh2 billion this year, bumped up to KSh5 billion next year. It’s not just money, it’s a "declaration of confidence." Confidence? Or just another way to throw money at a problem that’s been festering for decades? We’ve heard this song before, usually sung loudest when elections are on the horizon.

They're calling it an investment in "productivity, competitiveness and long-term economic growth." But let’s be real. How many of these internships actually turn into permanent jobs? How many of these "entrepreneurs" will actually survive the cutthroat Kenyan market, where even a matatu race seems like a more realistic path to fame and fortune than some government-backed program? Remember Tileh Pacbro challenging Ashton Hall to a matatu race? That felt more authentic.

And let's not forget the other "achievements" trotted out. 122,000 from NYOTA, 100,000 teachers hired, 642,000 jobs from housing, and 540,000 Kenyans abroad. Impressive numbers, if you squint hard enough and ignore the millions still struggling daily. The goal? To make Kenya a "hub for export of services." Right. Because sending our youth off to do other people's data entry is the pinnacle of national aspiration.

The President is asking the private sector to "open workplaces, share knowledge and mentor." Noble, but how much of this will be genuine mentorship and how much will be companies using cheap, government-subsidised labour? It’s a nice thought, building a "stronger bridge between education and employment," but bridges are only useful if they actually lead somewhere solid, not just to more waiting rooms.

Ultimately, this sounds like the same old script. A grand launch, a pile of cash, and a lot of hopeful faces. Let’s see how many of those 100,000 actually emerge with more than just a certificate and a polite "thank you" for their time. Because in Nairobi, promises are cheap, and reality bites hard.
