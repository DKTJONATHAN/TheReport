So, the Americans and their friends decided to poke the bear in Iran, and surprise, surprise, the bear roared. Now, with ballistic missiles flying and drones buzzing around the Gulf, Kenya, the perpetually optimistic land of "Hakuna Matata," is finding out that global drama has a direct debit attached. Our entire economy, built on importing fuel and hoping for the best, is suddenly staring down the barrel of a very expensive gun. Remember how we thought we were so clever with that G-to-G oil deal? Turns out, even 180 days of credit doesn't mean much when the actual oil can't get here, or when the price doubles overnight.

Forget the picturesque image of Mombasa port being a hub. While some diverted ships might stop by for a quick drink and a chat, the actual goods we need - the manufacturing inputs, the electronics, the food - are going to be late, fewer, and ridiculously overpriced. The Cape of Good Hope detour alone is costing millions in fuel for each trip. So yes, Nairobi's factories, markets, and farms will feel this. It’s not abstract economic theory; it's your landlord demanding rent and the supermarket shelves looking a little emptier.

And let's not even get started on our export earners. Our tea, coffee, and horticulture, the few things we actually manage to sell abroad, are now facing a double whammy. Iran, our biggest tea buyer, has gone silent, and our other export markets are getting choked by insane shipping costs and delays. It's a perfect recipe for squeezing the life out of businesses precisely when they need a breather. This is the kind of situation that makes you wonder if we're just destined to be the perpetual recipients of everyone else's bad decisions, all while hoping [The Great Decoupling: How the Weaponization of the Dollar Birthed a New Financial Underground](/posts/the-great-decoupling-how-the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-birthed-a-new-financial-underground) somehow shields us.

Our leaders, bless their cotton socks, are doing their usual diplomatic dance, condemning violence while simultaneously trying not to upset anyone too much. It's a tightrope walk, sure, but when the storm hits, that tightrope is just a thin thread over a very deep, very expensive abyss. The US partnership is all well and good, but when the oil prices go through the roof, even Uncle Sam can't magically make fuel cheaper for your matatu.

So, while Washington and Tel Aviv might be having their geopolitical showdown, Kenya is left to pick up the pieces, or rather, the inflated bills. This isn't a war fought on our soil, but it's certainly being paid for from our soil, or what's left of our disposable income after the fuel surcharge. Expect more grumbling, more empty pockets, and a whole lot more "what if" scenarios from the talking heads. This is the reality of being a net importer in a world that's decided to go to war over oil.
