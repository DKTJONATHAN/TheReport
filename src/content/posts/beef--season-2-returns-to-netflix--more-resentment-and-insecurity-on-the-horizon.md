Netflix is once again serving up a heaping plate of bottled-up frustration with the return of "Beef" for its second season. Fresh cast, fresh setting, same old song and dance of resentment, insecurity, and the hollow pursuit of validation. This time around, we're treated to the marital discord of Joshua and Lindsay Martín, played by Carey Mulligan and Oscar Isaac, who are apparently too busy being miserable to maintain a respectable country club.

The premise, as if we needed it reiterated, is a "big argument" that, with a little help from Hollywood's penchant for melodrama, conveniently escalates to involve their entire social and professional circle. Cue Cailee Spaeny and Charles Melton as a newly engaged couple, Ashley and Austin, who have the misfortune of witnessing their bosses' public meltdown and then find themselves entangled in the ensuing chaos. The entire charade is designed to culminate in a desperate scramble for the favor of the club's billionaire owner, played by Youn Yuh-jung - because nothing says relatable drama like the desperation of the lower classes groveling before the arbitrarily powerful.

Produced by the perpetually trendy A24 and soundtracked by Finneas, "Beef" Season 2 promises more "dark humor" and "high stakes." Translation: more characters making poor decisions driven by ego and envy, all wrapped up in a slick package designed to keep you passively consuming. The entire eight-episode season will be available on Netflix starting April 16th. Just remember, as you queue up the next episode, that Netflix has recently seen fit to increase its subscription prices, ensuring you're paying more for the privilege of watching fictional characters navigate their self-inflicted misery. Whether you're in the US, UK, or Australia, the cost of this particular brand of escapism continues to climb, a testament to the enduring profitability of manufactured conflict.
