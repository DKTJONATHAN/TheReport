Welcome to the era of selling the cow to buy a bell. The government has finally decided that the Kenya Pipeline Company is better off in private hands, all so they can pour 20 billion into the bottomless pit that is JKIA. They call it an IPO, but for those of us living in the real Nairobi, it feels like we are pawning the family silver to pay for a facelift on a house we might not even own in ten years.

President Ruto is calling this a "new model of financing," which is government-speak for "we ran out of other people-s money." They have already offloaded a 65 percent stake for over 100 billion, claiming this will fix our highways and ports. It is the same pattern of institutional decay we see in the [Nyayo House Special: Buy A Passport, Lose A Country](/posts/nyayo-house-special--buy-a-passport--lose-a-country) report, where the state is treated like a clearance sale. If you believe this cash will actually reach the tarmac without being diverted, you are clearly new to the city.

The logic is simple yet terrifying. You take a strategic, revenue-generating asset like the Pipeline and slice it up to fund the "modernization" of an airport that currently leaks every time a cloud looks at it funny. They say JKIA is operating beyond capacity, which is true, but throwing 20 billion at it under a "National Infrastructure Fund" sounds like a recipe for another decade of stalled projects and "technical glitches."

We are told this is "seed money" and that domestic institutional investors will jump in. That is just a fancy way of saying your pension fund is about to be used to gamble on a project managed by the same people who cannot keep the lights on during a storm. While they talk about equity participation, the average Kenyan is just wondering why we are selling the few things that actually work to fund the things that never have.

In the end, this is not about expansion or modernization; it is about liquidation. We are hollowing out the state piece by piece, hoping the private sector will save us from our own mismanagement. By the time they finish "optimizing" our assets, there will be nothing left but the debt and a shiny new terminal where the coffee costs more than a daily wage.
