So, a teacher couple, Patrick Karimi Kamara and Alice Wanjiru Kanyoro, thought they were slick. They decided to scam a Kenyan living the good life in Denmark out of Sh2.4 million for a piece of land in Ruiru. Classic Nairobi move, really. They were supposed to be selling a parcel in Mwihoko, but surprise, surprise, when it came time to actually transfer the title at Ardhi House, it turned out the land wasn't theirs to sell. Figures.

The DCI, bless their persistent hearts, sniffed this one out during the title transfer. Apparently, the land was registered to someone else entirely. This isn't exactly rocket science; it's the same old song and dance, preying on those who aren't here to see the smoke and mirrors themselves. This whole operation smacks of the kind of desperation we see everywhere, from the streets of Eastlands to the corridors of power, like those questionable Sh3.1 billion foreign trips our President seems so fond of taking, [/posts/ruto-s-sh3-1-billion-foreign-trips--from-nairobi-to-a-global-money-pit].

The Directorate of Criminal Investigations is now calling this a "broader pattern of fraudulent schemes," which is just a polite way of saying it's a daily occurrence. They're pushing for due diligence, as if people who are already hustling to make money aren't being fleeced by those who should be upholding the law. It’s a good thing the DCI is finally catching up to what we already know: Ardhi House is less about land ownership and more about who you know and how much they can get away with.

These two teachers are now cooling their heels in custody, awaiting charges of obtaining money by false pretences. It’s a small win for justice, I suppose, but it won't stop the next scam. The system is rigged, and these petty arrests are just noise to distract from the real rot. Kenya's land sector is a perpetual circus of deceit, and frankly, I’m not surprised anymore.
