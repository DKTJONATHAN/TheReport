Nairobi’s ego just took another hit. While we keep calling ourselves the "Gateway to East Africa," the actual money is moving elsewhere. According to the latest Knight Frank data for the second half of 2025, our prime office yields have slumped to a mediocre 8.5 percent. Meanwhile, Kampala and Dar es Salaam are sitting pretty at 9 percent. It turns out that being the loud-mouthed cousin in the region does not actually pay the bills when your neighbors are charging more and getting better returns.

The math is simple and depressing. If you own a prime office in Kampala, you are pocketing 16.50 dollars per square metre. In Nairobi? You are lucky to see 13 dollars. This two-year streak of "rental stability" that the suits are praising is just a polite way of saying the market is dead in the water. Investors are feeling the pinch as the [Central Bank Minutes Show Deep Divide Over Rate Decisions](/posts/central-bank-minutes-show-deep-divide-over-rate-decisions) continue to haunt the broader economy, leaving property owners to wonder why they bothered with concrete instead of just keeping their cash under a mattress.

We are told that occupancy is up to 80.3 percent, but do not let the PR spin fool you. This isn’t because of a booming economy. It is because developers finally realized that building more glass towers in a city that cannot even manage its own drainage was a bad idea. There were no significant new office completions in 2025 because everyone is terrified. The only reason the numbers look "strengthened" is because we have finally hit the ceiling of our own historic oversupply.

Now, we have to hear about "ESG credentials" and "sustainability." Apparently, having a LEED certification is the only way to get a multinational to look at your building. It is a tenant-favoured circus where landlords are basically doing backflips and offering free fit-outs just to keep the lights on. If you do not have a fancy green badge on your wall, you are basically trying to rent out a ghost town. The power has shifted, and the landlords are the ones begging for scraps.

Even the legendary Hilton Hotel has surrendered, partially transforming into something called "Tulivu Coworking." When iconic landmarks start getting chopped up into hot-desks for "digital nomads" and SMEs who cannot afford a real lease, you know the dream of the conventional corporate headquarters is gasping for air. The flight-to-quality is really just a flight-from-reality, as we pretend that flexible workspaces and eco-friendly labels can hide the fact that Nairobi is no longer the undisputed champion of the regional balance sheet.
