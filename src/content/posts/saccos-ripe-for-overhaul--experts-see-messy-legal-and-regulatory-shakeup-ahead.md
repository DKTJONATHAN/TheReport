So, 7.4 million Kenyans are apparently entrusting their hard-earned shillings to these things called Saccos, right? And these Saccos, mind you, are supposedly propping up agriculture, housing, and small businesses, contributing a hefty 30% to our GDP. Sounds like a good deal, doesn't it? Except, beneath the shiny surface, it’s a different story. We're talking about a financial sector held together by duct tape and wishful thinking, riddled with governance nightmares and regulatory loopholes big enough to drive a matatu through.

Now, some eggheads have churned out a report, and guess what? It’s all the usual suspects: fragmented rules, dodgy leadership, insider loans (because why not lend to yourself, eh?), and a general lack of modern infrastructure. It’s a recipe for disaster, and the KUSCCO scandal, where a cool Sh13.3 billion vanished into thin air, is just the latest episode in this ongoing Kenyan drama. It’s enough to make you wonder if we’re just repeating the same old mistakes, a bit like that cautionary tale in [The Mirage of Excellence in a Decaying Global Arena](/posts/the-mirage-of-excellence-in-a-decaying-global-arena).

The "experts" - and I use that term loosely - are now calling for a massive overhaul. They want to tidy up the laws, give the Sacco Societies Regulatory Authority (SASRA) more teeth, and basically bring some order to the chaos. Apparently, over 5,000 Saccos are operating in the wild, completely unregulated, posing a "systemic risk." That's a polite way of saying they could go belly up and take your savings with them. Only Saccos with a cool Sh100 million in deposits even get a glance from SASRA. The rest? Well, good luck.

They’re even suggesting things like a Deposit Guarantee Fund, a Central Liquidity Fund, and a Stabilisation Protection Scheme. Basically, they want to create a safety net because so many of these Saccos are on the brink of collapse, some even borrowing money just to pay out dividends. Unsustainable, much? And let's not forget the politically connected boards, the insider lending, and the general inefficiency. It’s a wonder anyone still trusts these outfits.

The report also points out that our Saccos are a global embarrassment, deviating wildly from international best practices. We’ve got this confusing mess of Deposit-Taking and Non-Withdrawable Deposit-Taking Saccos, unlike the clean, transparent credit unions you find elsewhere. And these insider loans? They’re not just frowned upon internationally; they’re a direct slap in the face to good governance. Global best practice demands strict rules and independent committees, not board members acting like they own the place.

And as for loan guarantees, instead of proper risk assessment, it’s a free-for-all with multiple members signing on the dotted line. This just dilutes accountability and spreads the risk thinner than my bank account after rent. Oh, and requiring huge deposits just to sit on a board? Brilliant way to keep power concentrated with the rich and exclude anyone with actual good ideas but shallow pockets. It’s a system designed for failure, and these reforms are long overdue. Let's just hope they actually implement them this time, instead of another committee sitting around drinking chai and filing reports.
