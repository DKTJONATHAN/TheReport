Ruto's "Stable" Economy: Inflation Down, Cartels Up?

The President assures us the economy is stable, but for how long? He warns oil cartels, promising to deal with them like the sugar and coffee ones. Let's see if that actually happens.

Belinda Achieng's take: Economy is stable, Ruto says, but cartels are watching.

Ruto says the economy is stable, inflation is down, and the shilling is holding steady. He sounded quite pleased with himself, spouting numbers like a good politician does. But let's be real, "stable" is relative. It's like saying your leaky roof is stable because it hasn't collapsed yet. Meanwhile, he's warning the oil cartels about the Middle East crisis, promising firm action. It’s the same old song and dance about eliminating cartels, a tune we’ve heard about sugar, coffee, and tea. We’ll believe it when we see it, which, knowing Nairobi, means never.

He claims these cartels will be "dealt with firmly, decisively, and conclusively." Sounds impressive, doesn't it? It’s the same promise he made about the sugar, coffee, and tea cartels, which, apparently, have been "eliminated." Funny how some of us still remember the price of a kilo of sugar a year ago. And let's not forget the fertilizer issue - those distributing sub-standard stuff have also been "punished." Punished how, exactly? A stern talking to? A slap on the wrist with a Sh500 note?

Then, the usual call for unity, rejecting divisive politics, hatred, and tribalism. Lovely sentiments, truly. It’s always easier to talk about unity when you’re riding high on promises of shared prosperity for all. He also bragged about education, 23,000 classrooms, 100,000 teachers, and better financing for higher education. And in Narok, two sugar companies are apparently beefing up farming. Good for them.

Prime Cabinet Secretary Musalia Mudavadi chimed in with the genius idea of adopting electric vehicles because of global oil challenges. Brilliant. Because every Kenyan struggling to afford bus fare can just magically switch to Teslas. He also dropped the bombshell that 47 of the Kenyans fighting in Ukraine have returned. Forty-seven. Makes you wonder what happened to the rest. Meanwhile, some local politician, Tonkei, is dismissing critics as "jealous" of the "transformation agenda." Jealous of what? The rising cost of kerosene?

This whole spiel is a classic Nairobi production: a healthy dose of economic jargon, a dash of tough talk against imaginary enemies, and a generous helping of platitudes about unity. We’ve seen this movie before, and the ending is usually the same. The cartels, whatever their flavour, always seem to find a way to keep their heads above water, while the rest of us are just trying to keep our heads above the rising cost of living. It makes you wonder if some of these cartels are as elusive as those we’ve discussed before, like the ones involved in the trade of dubious goods that have real-world consequences.
