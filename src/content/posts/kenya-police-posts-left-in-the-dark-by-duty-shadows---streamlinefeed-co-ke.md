The flicker of a paraffin candle, casting long, dancing shadows on ink-stained books and damp case files. This isn't some dusty museum exhibit; it's the nightly reality for our so called protectors in rural Kenya. While the rest of the world races ahead, these officers are stuck in the dark ages, with "Utumishi kwa wote" - service for all - ringing hollower than a politician's promise. Built with NG-CDF money and local pennies, these structures are monuments to brilliant ideas that never bothered with the follow through. A shell built for the future, now just another abandoned building waiting to crumble.

They drone on about digitizing everything, about fancy digital Occurrence Books, but out here, where the power grid is a myth and the night is a thick blanket of darkness, it’s all just hot air. No electricity means no charging radios, no computers, nothing. They’re not just hindered; they’re actively put in danger, expected to enforce the law while fumbling for a torch. This whole NG-CDF business, meant to bring services closer, has just created more problems. They’re great at laying bricks and cutting ribbons, but keeping the lights on? That’s a forgotten expense. It’s the classic "asset-only" development trap, where we build it up and then just… leave it.

And what about us, the citizens? When we’re in distress and crawl to a police post, only to find it literally dark, what are we supposed to think? The perception of state competence evaporates faster than yesterday's chapati. The psychological toll on these officers, expected to uphold order while struggling to see the very papers they’re handling, is immense. It kills morale and fuels the distrust we all feel. While Nairobi gets fancy forensic tech, these rural outposts are stuck in a technological vacuum. Evidence that should be uploaded in minutes gets delayed for days, forcing officers to travel miles just to get it into the system. It’s the difference between a solved case and another file gathering dust.

This isn't rocket science. We need a proper infrastructure policy, not these piecemeal, constituency-led vanity projects. Relying on NG-CDF for construction without a dedicated budget for operations is a recipe for "white elephant" projects all over the country. If the government wants a digital, agile police force, it needs to provide the most basic utility: electricity. Until then, expecting law and order from officers working by candlelight is just setting them up for failure. It’s administrative negligence, plain and simple. The flickering candle isn't a symbol of their dedication; it's a symbol of the state's utter failure to provide even the bare minimum. How long can this go on? It’s like asking how long the same old script plays out in this country, from mangoes to millions.
