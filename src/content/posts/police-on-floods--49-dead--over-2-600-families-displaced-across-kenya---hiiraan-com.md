Police on Floods: 49 Dead, Over 2,600 Families Displaced Across Kenya - Another Day, Another Disaster.

Nairobi is drowning, but the show must go on. Forty-nine dead, thousands homeless, and the police stations are flooded. Typical.

Nairobi's Drowning: 49 Dead, Over 2,600 Families Displaced Across Kenya

Just another Tuesday in this city. Rains come, people die, and the government scrambles to pretend they're in charge. Forty-nine souls gone, families scattered like garbage in the wind, all because our "several counties" decided to turn into swimming pools. And guess who's right in the thick of it? Nairobi, of course. The gift that keeps on giving.

The National Police Service, bless their soggy boots, are out there with their "multi-agency emergency teams." Search and rescue, evacuations from high-risk areas - you know, the usual song and dance. They're even trying to keep the police stations operational, because heaven forbid a criminal has to wait an extra hour for the law to catch up while the station is underwater. It’s almost as if they didn't see this coming, despite living in a country where the weather likes to throw tantrums.

Muchiri, the spokesman, is busy counting the dead - 49, he says, as if it's just a statistic and not a whole lot of lives extinguished. He offers condolences, which is nice, I suppose, but it won't bring back the drowned or rebuild the homes that have become water features. Meanwhile, "vulnerable areas" are being "assisted," which probably means a few lukewarm blankets and a promise of better drainage next time.

President Ruto has, predictably, ordered the "immediate deployment" of yet another team. More meetings, more coordination, more promises of relief food from "national strategic reserves." Let's hope those reserves aren't as waterlogged as everything else. At least the injured will have their hospital bills covered, a small mercy in this grand theatre of disaster. It’s a bit like watching the great Kenyan sell-off at Karen, only instead of land, they're selling off lives and livelihoods to the rain.

And don't forget the advice: exercise caution, follow weather updates, and call 999, 911, or 112. Because when your house is being washed away, the first thing you'll think of is which toll-free number to dial. Or maybe just try to swim for it. It’s the Kenyan way, isn't it? Adapt or drown.
