The year 2026 has barely kicked off and the headlines are already dripping with blood. We are told that 70 percent of homicide victims are men, as if that statistic makes the targeted slaughter of women any less of a national emergency. Between 2016 and 2024, over 930 women were erased by the very people who shared their beds. We are living in a country where "I love you" is increasingly becoming a death sentence, and the only thing the National Crime Research Centre can offer is a promise that they are still compiling the official 2025 data.

The police tell us they are working hard, but we know the drill. They are under-resourced, often unprofessional, and seemingly more interested in the optics of "intelligence-led policing" than actually stopping a domestic dispute before it turns into a funeral. While the media treats every new femicide like a fresh piece of entertainment to drive clicks, the protective mechanisms remain as fragile as a politician's promise. Reporting pathways are a joke, and enforcement is so inconsistent it might as well be non-existent.

It is funny how we have resources for everything except the things that matter. While the elite celebrate [Washington's $50 Million ‘Gift’ to African Film: More Gatekeeping or Just New Suits?](/posts/washington-s--50-million--gift--to-african-film--more-gatekeeping-or-just-new-suits), the youth in our informal settlements are drowning in unemployment and cheap liquor. This is not just a policing issue; it is a mirror reflecting a society that has decided life is cheap. From the remote rural villages to the heart of Nairobi, we are watching a slow-motion collapse of the Kenyan moral compass while the big shots talk about economic growth.

We even have "Star Persons of the Year" like Eliud Muriithi winning awards for mental health advocacy, but let's be real. Mental health services are still a luxury for the rich while the rest of the country is told to "stay strong" until they snap. The roots of this violence are buried deep in socio-economic inequality and untreated trauma that no amount of government PR can fix. You cannot solve a bloodbath with a few workshops and a fancy report that will eventually gather dust in some forgotten office in Upper Hill.

The most terrifying part of this whole mess is how normal it has all become. We read about a woman killed by her partner, sigh, and then scroll to the next post. We are growing comfortable with death. When a society loses its ability to be shocked by the sight of its own people being slaughtered, it is already dead inside. Kenya does not need more "honest dialogue" or "decisive leadership" - it needs a total system overhaul because the "business as usual" crowd has already sold us out to the highest bidder.
