The upcoming SpaceX IPO is the perfect specimen of this terminal hubris. When a company with "fantasy" projections and a valuation that doubles its actual worth hits the market, it is not a sign of innovation. It is a sign of a system that has forgotten how to say no. Investors are not buying into a space program. They are buying into the unspoken promise that if Elon Musk fails, the American taxpayer - and by extension, the global financial network - will be forced to hold the bag.

This moral hazard has gone global. From Australian superannuation funds to European pension schemes, the world is being force-fed a diet of overvalued tech stocks and debt-fueled semiconductor rallies. We are no longer investing in productivity or industrial output. We are betting on the ability of governments to keep the printers running forever. The "Greenspan put" has metastasized into a universal guarantee that the house will never let the players lose, even as the casino's foundation crumbles.

The danger of this "one-way bet" is that it relies on the infinite firepower of the state. But as bond yields creep toward the five percent alarm bell and the US Treasury struggles to find buyers for its mountain of debt, the illusion is thinning. When the state can no longer afford to underwrite the stupidity of the private sector, the ensuing crash will not be a typical recession. It will be a systemic reset that wipes out the savings of an entire generation that was told risk had been conquered.

We are witnessing the final stages of an economic experiment that treats gravity as a suggestion. The "bliss" we are experiencing is the weightlessness of a freefall before the impact. When the liquidity finally dries up and the state-sponsored safety nets tear, those who treated the market as a one-way bet will find that the only thing left to catch them is the hard reality of a bankrupt world.
