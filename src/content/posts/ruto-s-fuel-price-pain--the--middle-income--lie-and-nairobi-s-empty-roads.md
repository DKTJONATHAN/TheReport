So, President Ruto, bless his shiny shoes, decided to explain why Kenyans are coughing up an arm and a leg for petrol while our neighbours, seemingly less developed in his eyes, are paying less. In a church, of all places, he’s throwing around the "middle-income" label like it’s a magic wand to justify the pain at the pump. Apparently, we shouldn't compare ourselves to Uganda or Tanzania because they're "least developed." Right. And we're just "middle-income," which, conveniently, means our prices are just… normal. Never mind that the World Bank says we're *lower*-middle-income, a detail he seems to have conveniently forgotten, just like he forgets the empty promises of cheaper fuel.

He then tries to pull the wool over our eyes with roads. "We have 20,000 kilometres of tarmac to maintain," he chirped, conveniently linking it to the KSh25 levy on every litre. The truth? Our paved road network is closer to 25,000 or even 26,000 kilometres, not the measly 20,000 he’s peddling. And that figure of 6,000 kilometres under construction? Pure fiction. At best, it’s a multi-year target, at worst, it’s him dreaming out loud. It’s funny how he can spin a yarn about roads, but the reality of our wallets is a lot less glamorous. It reminds me of when the US was baffling Ruto with details about Operation Epic Fury; sometimes, it feels like our leaders are living in a different reality altogether, one where PR trumps actual progress.

Then there’s the grand boast about our tarmac roads dwarfing those of our neighbours. He claims Kenya’s 20,000 kilometres of paved roads are more than the combined total of Uganda, Tanzania, DRC, Rwanda, Burundi, and South Sudan. Except, as we've established, Kenya doesn't even have 20,000 kilometres. And when you actually look at the numbers - or what little reliable data is available, especially for the DRC and South Sudan - the combined total of our neighbours *easily* surpasses 20,000 kilometres. Tanzania alone has over 12,000 kilometres of paved roads. It’s a classic case of taking a slightly incorrect figure for Kenya and then wildly exaggerating the deficit in neighbouring countries.

And the 28,000 kilometres of tarmac he plans to lay in the next seven years? That timeline keeps shifting like sand in a dust storm. It was 10 years, then seven, then back to 10, and now, depending on who he's talking to, it's seven again. It’s the kind of inconsistency that makes you wonder if he even believes his own pronouncements. This isn't about building infrastructure; it's about making the numbers sound good for the next election cycle, while we're stuck paying through the nose for fuel and dealing with the potholes that *are* very real.

The bottom line is, Ruto’s claims are a classic Nairobi special: a generous helping of spin, a dash of convenient omission, and a whole lot of wishful thinking served with a side of public discontent. He’s trying to justify the unbearable cost of fuel by painting Kenya as a more developed nation than it is, while simultaneously exaggerating our infrastructure achievements. It’s a bold strategy, I’ll give him that, but for those of us actually living here, it’s just another Monday.
