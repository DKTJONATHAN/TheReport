At UN Security Council, Kenya Again Defends Its Police Deployed to Haiti, Amidst Allegations

WARNING: Kenya's claims of impeccable conduct by its police in Haiti are being challenged by a UN report alleging grave violations. As officials trumpet successes, the reality on the ground remains shrouded in controversy and doubt.

Kenya's representative at the UN is shouting from the rooftops that its officers in Haiti are angels, singing praises of professionalism and accountability. This is after a UN report dared to suggest that these very same officers might have, you know, violated civilians, including minors. Apparently, a board of inquiry found no such thing, which is convenient, isn't it? They're so confident, they're even offering to cooperate with further investigations, which, knowing how these things go, will probably just be another exercise in deflection.

The whole Haiti deployment has been a real mixed bag, hasn't it? Kenya sent in nearly a thousand troops to fight gangs, all under the guise of international law. Now, as they're packing up to be replaced, some Haitians are apparently protesting their departure, which the Kenyan officials are spinning as proof of their good work. It's almost as if the locals are desperate for any form of order, even from forces accused of serious misconduct. This whole charade reminds me of when our own social media stars thought they were going to war for clout in [OPERATION EPIC FAIL: WHY OUR SOCIAL MEDIA STARS THINK THEY ARE GOING TO WAR](/posts/operation-epic-fail--why-our-social-media-stars-think-they-are-going-to-war).

Kenya's government, including the Prime Cabinet Secretary himself, has been busy writing letters and telling envoys to "speak candidly." Candidly about what? About how well they performed while being accused of rape? They're already talking about building on this "strong foundation" for the next force, the Gang Suppression Force. It’s a classic move: deny, deflect, and then pivot to how great the next phase will be, provided, of course, it's adequately funded. Because that’s always the real issue, isn’t it? Not the alleged crimes, but the lack of cash.

The UN Security Council meeting was supposed to be about the humanitarian crisis in Haiti, but it turned into Kenya's PR session. While the Kenyan representative was busy defending his troops, the UN Secretary-General's report highlighted a grim reality: intensified enforcement and rising civilian risk. It's a tough balancing act, and judging by the accusations, Kenya's forces might have leaned a little too hard on the "enforcement" side, with some rather unpleasant consequences for the "civilians."

Meanwhile, the mission formally ended in March, with a new one, the GSF, kicking off. This new force will apparently receive pre-deployment training, which includes a civilian protection component. One can only hope that this training is more than just a box-ticking exercise, and that the officers involved actually absorb the lessons, unlike some we’ve heard about. The cycle of violence and alleged abuse in Haiti seems to be a perpetual one, with new forces coming in to replace the old, all while the same old problems fester.
