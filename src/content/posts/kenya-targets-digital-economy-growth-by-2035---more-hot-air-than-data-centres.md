Kenya Targets Digital Economy Growth By 2035 - Prepare For More Smoke And Mirrors

The government is at it again, polishing the same old promises with a fresh coat of digital paint. ICT PS John Tanui is out here, babbling about AI policies and data frameworks at the Kenya School of Government. Apparently, we're not just chasing a dream, we're "developing policies" to "guide the digital space." Sounds impressive, doesn't it? Except, we’ve heard this tune before, remember the Silicon Savannah hype? Turns out, 'Silicon Savannah or Digital Slaughterhouse?' was more prophetic than we realized.

Tanui’s grand vision hinges on three pillars: AI infrastructure, data, and skills. He’s talking about a "Data Governance Framework" to make sure AI can "take advantage of standardised data." This is the same old song and dance - create the infrastructure, then hope the data magically appears and does something useful. And let’s not forget the "value chain for our country." We're so busy building these shiny new chains, we forget to check if they're actually strong enough to hold anything. The demand for digitisation and storage is high, he says. Of course, it is. Everyone's trying to get a piece of the pie before it evaporates.

The ultimate goal? To grow the digital economy from a measly 7 billion US dollars to a staggering 100 billion US dollars by 2035. How? By building more data centres and cloud services. India and Malaysia did it, so Kenya can too. That’s the logic. Meanwhile, our truckers are eyeing a 14% hike as diesel prices reach new heights. Seems like some value chains are a bit more tangible than others. We’re ranked number two in the digital economy, apparently, with nearly 20 data centres. But the "potential is huge." Always is, when you're trying to sell a mirage.

Kenya is positioning itself as Africa's next big hub for data centres and AI. We've got submarine cables aplenty, a stable regulatory environment (ha!), and renewable energy. They say we're the digital gateway to East and Central Africa. And yes, we have the big names operating here: IXAfrica, Africa Data Centres, Safaricom, Liquid Intelligent Technologies, iColo, Dimension Data, and even the Konza National Data Center. Oracle is even setting up shop. All this sounds fantastic, but for whom? For the average Kenyan struggling with the cost of living? It feels more like Kenya's Digital Economy: Retention Is The New Growth Engine - Nairobi Pays The Price, a cycle we've seen before.

Tanui keeps talking about an "enabling environment" and "incentives" to boost investment. It’s a consultative meeting, after all. Data centre operators, cloud service providers, government officials - they're all there, patting each other on the back. They’ll discuss regulatory frameworks, policies, and interventions. Meanwhile, the real "investment" for most Kenyans is just trying to keep their heads above water. The promise of 15 to 20 percent of GDP from the digital economy sounds nice, but let's see how much of that actually trickles down beyond the privileged few.

The government continues to develop policies and incentives aimed at enhancing ease of investment in data centres, cloud infrastructure, AI ecosystems and digital manufacturing. It's all about making it easier for the big boys to set up shop. The consultative meeting discussed regulatory frameworks, policies and interventions required to promote investment and growth in the data and cloud services sector. Sounds like a lot of talk, and we all know how much talk translates into actual progress for the common mwananchi.
