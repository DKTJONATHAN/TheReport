So, the government has finally decided to grease the palms of the boys in blue. Starting this July, your local police constable will be pocketing a maximum of Sh57,700, a massive jump from the peanuts they were earning before. They call it a "structured transformation framework," but for anyone living in the real Nairobi, it sounds more like a bribe to keep the boots on our necks while the cost of living continues to suffocate us. Shifting a constable's pay by 48 percent is a nice headline, but it does not change the fact that the culture of the force is built on "kitu kidogo" rather than actual service.

The National Steering Committee is patting itself on the back, claiming this is the biggest pay rise since independence. They are talking about "professionalism" and "accountability" as if a bigger bank account suddenly installs a moral compass. We have seen this movie before. Every time the state feels the ground shaking from public anger, they make sure the people with the guns and the teargas are well-fed. While the rest of us are debating whether we can afford meat once a week, the prison warders and NYS personnel are getting their "welfare" improved to ensure they do not join the protests.

Let us be honest about what this "operational preparedness" actually looks like on the streets. More money for the police rarely translates to more safety for the mwananchi. In fact, it often just gives them more fuel for their patrol cars to set up illegal roadblocks. Even with these new salaries, the appetite for extra cash remains unsated, especially when you consider how [Kenya’s Road Carnage Industry](/posts/blood--billions--and-the-big-lie--kenya-s-road-carnage-industry) has become a secondary payroll for half the traffic department. A higher basic pay just means they might start their bribes at a higher floor.

The prisons service and the NYS are also getting their slice of the cake, with senior officers now earning enough to make a middle-class corporate worker weep. They claim that 50 percent of the planned reforms are already implemented. But reform in this country is usually just a fancy word for moving the furniture around while the house is still on fire. They talk about "human resource management" while the average Kenyan still views a police station as a place where you go to lose money, not to find justice.

By the time the final phase of these revised salaries hits the bank accounts in July, the inflation will have probably swallowed half of it anyway. We are stuck in a cycle where the state taxes us to death just to pay the people who enforce those very taxes. It is a cynical game of survival where the house always wins, and the house, in this case, is anyone wearing a uniform and carrying a government-issued baton. Do not expect the "momentum across the security sector" to do anything except make the handcuffs feel a little tighter.
