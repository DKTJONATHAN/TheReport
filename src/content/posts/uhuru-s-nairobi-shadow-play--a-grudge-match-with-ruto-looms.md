So, the man who supposedly hung up his political boots, Uhuru Kenyatta, is now a regular at funerals and weddings. Apparently, that's where retired presidents go to “stay above the fray.” What a joke. It’s just a convenient excuse to mingle with the right people, isn’t it? He’s out there, shaking hands, offering condolences, and pretending he’s just a bystander while whispers of him orchestrating a grand opposition plan for 2027 grow louder. Don't be fooled by the humble pronouncements; this is about power, pure and simple.

He’s the former president, the chairman of Azimio, and conveniently placed to rally the troops against Ruto. The recent endorsement of Kalonzo Musyoka as the opposition’s likely candidate? That’s not just happenstance; that’s a calculated move. And don't forget Fred Matiang’i, another contender from Kenyatta’s own party. It’s a carefully constructed chessboard, and Uhuru is the one moving the pieces, even if he pretends he’s just there to pay respects to Daniel Karaba. It’s all about setting up the next big fight, the one they're calling a “grudge match.”

The Kenyatta shadow even stretches to the current government. They brought ODM members into Ruto’s cabinet, propping up a “shaky administration” after those youth-led uprisings. And some ODM folks are pointing fingers at Uhuru, accusing him of stirring up internal party drama after Raila Odinga’s death. Why? To sabotage any chance of a pre-election coalition with Ruto’s UDA. It's a classic divide-and-conquer strategy, the kind of political maneuvering that makes you wonder if anyone in this country is truly dedicated to governance or just to their own survival. It’s almost as if the whole process is a monument to incompetence, much like that Dudley Library book’s journey.

Ruto, sensing his stronghold in Mt. Kenya slipping, is desperate to cling to ODM’s support base. And his allies are convinced Uhuru is running a proxy campaign to deny him a second term. It’s a fascinating twist, considering they were once political bedfellows, Kenyatta as candidate and Ruto as his running mate in 2013 and 2017. But that alliance fractured when Kenyatta decided he’d rather have Raila Odinga as his successor, a decision that ultimately led to Ruto’s victory in 2022. The bad blood between them has been simmering ever since, with Kenyatta’s allies even being linked to the cost of living protests that plagued the country.

Sure, things seemed to cool down briefly last year when some of Uhuru’s allies landed plum public appointments, including Cabinet posts. A fragile peace, no doubt. But this latest move, this carefully orchestrated dance at funerals and endorsement of opposition figures, shows that the real political games are far from over. Uhuru might claim he’s retired, but his influence is very much alive, casting a long shadow over Kenyan politics and setting the stage for a bitter showdown with his former running mate. It’s all about that 2027 election, and the stakes are higher than ever.
