Ruto Refinery Plans Hit Snags From Fuel Cartels, But He's Pushing Ahead Anyway

The President claims powerful interests are fighting his dream of a regional oil refinery, but he's determined to see it through, promising "long-term transformation" even if it means temporary pain.

President William Ruto has declared that Kenya will proceed with plans to build a regional oil refinery, despite what he calls "resistance" from existing fuel import cartels. Speaking at the National Prayer Breakfast, Ruto insisted that powerful players, who currently profit from importing fuel into the region, are actively trying to sabotage the project. He cited a conversation with Nigerian billionaire Aliko Dangote, the proposed project's champion, who apparently confirmed the opposition. Ruto's message? Tough decisions are needed for the nation's future.

According to the President, this refinery is not just about ego; it's a "long-term strategy" to fix the region's energy woes and reduce dependence on imported petroleum. He's even promised construction will begin this year. A technical team was apparently dispatched months ago, leading them to Dangote and his impressive Nigerian refinery. This outreach then involved reaching out to Ugandan President Museveni and other regional leaders, who have apparently "agreed." It’s a bold vision, assuming everyone actually plays ball.

Dangote himself has been quite vocal, offering to build a refinery capable of processing 650,000 barrels of oil daily, similar to his Lagos operation. He presented this offer at the Africa We Build Summit in Nairobi, directly to Ruto and Museveni. Dangote’s argument is that Africa has the resources to fund its own industrial projects, rather than exporting raw materials and importing finished goods at inflated prices. He calls it exporting jobs and importing poverty. It’s a sentiment many Kenyans can relate to, especially when trying to make ends meet, much like the ongoing struggle with volatile cryptocurrency prices, which sometimes feels like another puppet show for powerful global interests.

Ruto seems to have bought into Dangote’s vision, backing the proposal and highlighting the need to move away from the current system of exporting raw materials and importing finished products at higher costs. The proposed refinery is expected to spur further discussions among Kenya, Uganda, and other neighbours regarding energy infrastructure and long-term fuel stability. Whether this ambitious plan avoids the usual bureaucratic quagmires and actual "resistance" from the powers-that-be remains to be seen. We've heard big promises before, haven't we?
