So, 90 or so assorted "leaders", a mix of the pious, the ideologically rigid, and the predictably self serving, convened in a New Orleans Marriott. The premise: a secret meeting to address the existential threat of artificial intelligence. The outcome: the Pro-Human AI Declaration, a document so blandly benevolent it could have been drafted by committee on a Tuesday afternoon.

Let's be clear. This wasn't about saving humanity. It was about ensuring that whatever gravy train AI becomes, these particular individuals and their respective groups get their slice, or at least a veto on who else does. You have church leaders hobnobbing with conservative academics, labor unions schmoozing with MAGA types, and progressive power brokers sharing oxygen with the very forces they claim to oppose. The AI "thought leaders" who invited them? They likely saw this chaos not as a threat, but as an opportunity to legitimize their own burgeoning influence by demonstrating a supposed consensus.

The "declaration" itself, with its five vaguely worded guidelines, is a masterclass in appeasing everyone without committing to anything concrete. "Centered on humanity first," they cry. "Avoiding the concentration of power." Oh, the irony. The signatories are a who's who of groups and individuals whose entire existence is predicated on concentrating power and influence, whether it's the AFL-CIO Tech Institute, the American Federation of Teachers, Glenn Beck, or Steve Bannon. Even Sir Richard Branson is there, because of course he is.

This isn't resistance. It's a pre emptive land grab. Everyone wants to control the narrative, to ensure that their particular brand of ideology or economic interest is baked into the AI cake before it's even fully formed. Ralph Nader, a man who has spent his career railing against corporate power, now finds himself on a document alongside corporate titans. Meredith Whittaker, from Signal Foundation, a bastion of privacy advocacy, is now sharing a stage with figures who represent the antithesis of that ideal.

The Future of Life Institute, so authoritative they say, is merely providing the fig leaf of intellectual legitimacy for what is, at its core, a cynical political maneuver. They've gathered a motley crew, paraded them before the public as a unified front, and presented a document that offers platitudes instead of practical solutions. This is how power operates. It masquerades as altruism, finds common ground in vague pronouncements, and then proceeds to solidify its own position under the banner of collective good. Don't be fooled. This "resistance" is just the latest iteration of the same old power games, played on a new, digital battlefield.
