PARLIAMENT CLEARS BUMULA MP OF BRIBERY CLAIMS BUT HIS OUSTER FROM NCIC ROW STANDS
WARNING: Another day, another parliamentary circus where "justice" is whatever the powerful decide. Our MPs are masters of the selective acquittal, clearing bribery allegations while conveniently finding other excuses to sideline those who ask too many questions. Don't expect accountability for public funds, just more political theatre.
This is what happens when the circus runs the show.
Gossip
Parliament, Bribery, Politics
Parliament

So, the parliamentary committee, in their infinite wisdom, has decided Bumula MP Jack Wamboka is clean as a whistle when it comes to demanding bribes. Shocking, I know. Apparently, Rev Dr Samuel Kobia's complaint, accusing Wamboka of demanding cash for an audience, was all smoke and no fire. The committee, chaired by one Samuel Chepkonga, sniffed around and declared the bribery claims "NOT substantiated." Fancy that. They even threw out a Kemu Vice-Chancellor's testimony because it was "new material." Because, you know, fairness means only considering what's convenient.

But don't pop the champagne just yet. While Wamboka might be cleared of taking actual bribes, he's still guilty of being a nuisance. The committee *did* find him culpable for "harassing witnesses" and treating them with "hostility and in a demeaning manner." Because standing up to officials who are, according to Auditor General Nancy Gathungu, allegedly misusing public funds is apparently a punishable offense. It's almost as if these committees are designed to protect the very people they're supposed to scrutinize. You know, much like how Nairobi seems to be a crime scene and nobody is coming to save you.

This whole drama unfolded suspiciously fast, right after the House rubber-stamped reports on the audited accounts of various bodies, including the NCIC. Coincidence? In this country, especially when public funds are involved, there are no coincidences, only carefully orchestrated moves. Wamboka himself claims this whole charade is an attempt to shield former NCIC commissioners from an audit that would likely expose their financial shenanigans. He's right to suspect it; these guys are more interested in protecting their own than in genuine accountability.

The upshot? Wamboka is out as chair of the Public Investment Committee (PIC) on Education and Governance and has been de-whipped and reassigned to the less influential National Cohesion and Equal Opportunity Committee. The committee recommended he be barred from leading committees dealing with cohesion, governance, audit, and oversight of constitutional and statutory bodies for the rest of his parliamentary term. So, even though he didn't take bribes, he's effectively punished for daring to question the status quo and those who might be lining their pockets with taxpayers' money. Welcome to Kenyan politics, where the truth is whatever serves the agenda.
