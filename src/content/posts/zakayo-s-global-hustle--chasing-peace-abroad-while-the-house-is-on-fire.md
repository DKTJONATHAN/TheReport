Our "Global Chairperson" is at it again. President Ruto is tweeting about peace in the Middle East like his opinion carries the same weight as a UN Security Council veto. He is calling for multi-stakeholder engagement and de-escalation while the average Kenyan is just trying to engage with a plate of ugali that costs twice what it did last year. It is the classic Nairobi move: acting like a CEO when you are actually just the intern holding the coffee and hoping no one notices your shoes are worn out.

Iran’s Ambassador, Dr. Ali Gholampour, did not even try to hide the shade in his response. He basically told us that while we are busy singing songs of multilateralism, the real problem is that our trade volumes are in the basement because of Uncle Sam. He pointed out that trade has tanked because of US sanctions on their banking sector. It is just [Another Paper Shield for a Leaking Roof: The SACCO Reform Scam](/posts/another-paper-shield-for-a-leaking-roof--the-sacco-reform-scam) where the high-level rhetoric completely ignores the rotting foundation of our actual economic reality.

Ruto loves the spotlight of international peace and security because it is a great distraction. It is much easier to tell Iraq and Jordan to behave than it is to fix the mess at home. He talks about indispensable frameworks like he is reading from a textbook he found in a business class lounge. Meanwhile, the Middle East crisis is just another convenient excuse for the next fuel price hike that we all know is lurking around the corner like a mugger in a dark alley.

The irony here is thick enough to cut with a rusty panga. We are condemning strikes in the UAE and Qatar, hoping for a seat at the big boys' table, but the big boys only see us as a convenient voting block or a landing strip for their interests. Iran is calling for us to point the finger at the aggressors, but we know our government will never bite the hand that feeds it via IMF loans and bilateral deals that never seem to trickle down to the ground.

In the end, this is just more PR for a government that thrives on optics over substance. We are deeply concerned about Qatar, but we are not even concerned enough to fix our own internal disasters. It is all a gamble that never pays off for the taxpayer. It is just another day in the city under the sun, where talk is cheap, diplomacy is a hobby for the elite, and the rest of us are left to wonder when the next tax will land.
