The real story is not the slight yield adjustment of the past week, but the brutal reality of the 30 percent. In 2008, foreign accounts held 56 percent of the US Treasury market. Today, that share has cratered. The US Treasury likes to claim it relies less on foreign capital by choice, but the reality is far more cynical. The world is not "choosing" to step back; it is being forced to liquidate its American holdings to survive the very volatility that US policy often creates.

Geopolitical instability in the Middle East is acting as a catalyst for a divorce that was already in progress. As oil prices fluctuate, energy-importing nations like India, Turkey, and Thailand are not looking to the US dollar as a "safe haven." Instead, they are raiding their Treasury cupboards for cash to keep their own economies from imploding. The Fed holdings for foreign official accounts have dropped by roughly 66 billion dollars since the conflict began. This is not a "cautionary move" as some analysts suggest; it is a liquidation sale.

The American empire is increasingly forced to eat its own tail. As foreign demand evaporates, the domestic market is expected to shoulder the burden of a 31 trillion dollar debt pile. When institutional giants like BNY Mellon note that cross-border holdings have been falling "almost inexorably" since the US unveiled its aggressive tariff agendas, they are describing the slow-motion collapse of a global financial consensus. The US is no longer the undisputed anchor of the world economy; it is a source of inflationary noise and policy uncertainty that rational actors are looking to hedge against.

We are witnessing the beginning of a secular decline in the demand for US Treasuries. Investors are no longer just pricing in inflation; they are pricing in the risk of a superpower that can no longer guarantee the stability of its own currency or its political system. The "cleaner positioning" mentioned by JPMorgan analysts is merely a polite way of saying that the smart money has already left the room. What remains is a market propped up by volatility and the hope that the next war will not be quite as expensive as the last one.
