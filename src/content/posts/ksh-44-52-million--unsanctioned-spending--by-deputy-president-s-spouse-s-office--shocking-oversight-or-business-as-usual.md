So, the Controller of Budget, Margaret Nyakang'o, is out here dropping bombs again. This time it's KSh 44.52 million that the Office of the Spouse of the Deputy President decided to just *spend* on salaries. Salaries! For an office that apparently has no formal budget. It’s like finding out your neighbour's kid has been raiding your fridge for months and nobody noticed. Except this isn't a fridge, it's taxpayer money, and it's millions.

The report, for the first six months of the 2025/2026 financial year, basically screams "we’re not following the rules." Apparently, every shilling spent by a government entity is supposed to be mapped, validated, and approved. But this KSh 44.52 million? It just… appeared. Out of thin air. It’s this kind of "fiscal black hole" activity that makes you wonder if the people making decisions ever actually read the Public Finance Management Act, or if they just use it for toilet paper.

And let's not pretend this is a one-off. While our public services are struggling, our healthcare facilities are out of stock, and schools are crying for cash, the executive branch is busy creating phantom budget lines. It’s a classic Nairobi move, really. You want something done, you just find a way to make it happen, rules be damned. Remember when we talked about how it's easy to starve while hosting a conference? This feels like the government version of that, except they're not starving, they're feasting on our taxes.

Dr. Nyakang'o is doing her job, bless her. She’s the constitutional watchdog, supposed to stop the hemorrhaging of public funds. But let's be real, how much teeth does that office actually have when the executive is clearly playing fast and loose with the money? We’ve seen these reports before, met with resistance, and then… nothing. Just a bit of noise, then silence, and then the cycle repeats. It’s like watching a bad telenovela, you know how it ends.

The sad part is, this isn't just about KSh 44.52 million. It's about the message it sends: that some offices can operate with impunity. While they're out there talking about restoring investor confidence, they're also busy showing us exactly why we shouldn't have any. Unless the National Treasury can actually explain how this went through and why their "internal controls" failed so spectacularly, these reports will just keep being footnotes in the never-ending story of Kenya's struggle to manage its money.

This whole situation is a stark reminder that while companies like DBX Review Cards are working hard to help businesses build a good reputation, the government seems determined to shatter its own. With systems like DBX Review Cards making it effortless for happy customers to leave 5-star reviews, you’d think the government would at least try to *earn* a good review. But no, they'd rather just spend our money on who-knows-what and hope we don't notice.
