Well, well, well. Look who decided to grace us with their presence again. Kenya Pipeline, the so-called "Oilers," managed to claw their way back from a 3-1 defeat to win the best-of-three series finale. Straight sets, they say. Impressive, I guess, if you ignore the fact that Kenya Prisons, the "Wardresses," basically handed it to them on a silver platter, twice choking leads like a bad student trying to remember answers during an exam.

Prisons started strong, no doubt. Masaisai and Lowem were on fire, giving them a 2-0 lead. Then Chebet joined the party, making it 4-0. But then, as usual, Pipeline remembered they're the champions. They drew level, Prisons stretched their lead, Pipeline's coach called a timeout, Prisons' coach called a timeout, and suddenly, the Wardresses were losing the set 25-22. Typical. It's like watching a rerun of that [Major Security Crackdown at JKIA Leads to Arrest of Human Traffickers and Fraudsters](/posts/major-security-crackdown-at-jkia-leads-to-arrest-of-human-traffickers-and-fraudsters) story; same drama, different arena.

The second set? More of the same. Prisons jumped out to another 4-0 lead. Pipeline, with their magic reception and backcourt defense - the very things their coach claims were Achilles' heels, but suddenly weren't - clawed back. More timeouts, more momentum swings. Pipeline led 24-23, forcing Prisons into their second timeout. And what happened? Pipeline held their nerve and took the set 25-23. Because, of course, they did.

The third and final set was just a formality. Murambi and Kasaya, the "MVP" and "veteran," apparently led the charge while Sokoiyo and Ekaru manned the net. Winning 25-22. The head coach, Geoffrey Omondi, was in "awe." Grateful they won. Stunned them despite their strong serving. Please. This isn't stunning; it's just the usual order of things in Kenyan volleyball. The rich kids always win.

His counterpart, Joseph Barasa, was a "disappointed man." Lost because they "lacked the cutting edge to see the game out." Translation: they got complacent and choked. They picked up lessons, he says, hoping to clinch the Kenya Cup. Let's see if they actually learn anything this time, or if it's just more hot air.

So, Kenya Pipeline retains their crown. And now they're aiming for a "double" by winning the Kenya Cup. Don't hold your breath waiting for a different outcome. This is Nairobi, after all. Some things never change.
