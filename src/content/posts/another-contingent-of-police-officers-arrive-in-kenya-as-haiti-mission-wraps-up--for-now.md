Well, well, well. Look who's back at JKIA, all smiles and shiny uniforms. Another batch of our finest, fresh off the plane from Haiti. Apparently, they've been playing peacekeepers for over a year, battling gangs and all that jazz. Monica Juma and Douglas Kanja were there to greet them, probably to hand out some more platitudes about pride and professionalism. Because that's what you get when you come back from a war zone, right? A warm welcome and a pat on the back.

This whole Kenya-led mission to Haiti, approved by the UN in 2024, sounds all noble and stuff. We've got contingents from all over the place - Guatemala, El Salvador, Jamaica, the Bahamas, and Canada. All supposedly there to help the Haitian National Police fight gangs, restore order, and secure infrastructure. You know, the usual song and dance. They talk about securing airports, seaports, and roads. It's like they think they’re building paradise over there, not wading through a cesspool of gang violence.

And let's not forget the "institutional rebuilding efforts." They've been training police and re-establishing an armed forces college. All very important, I’m sure. The 208 officers who landed yesterday were showered with praise for their "valour, discipline, and dedication." Promises of welfare support, including mental health and counselling services, were also dished out. Because after a year in a place like Haiti, who wouldn't need a bit of therapy? It’s all very touching, really. Makes you wonder if they’ll get the same level of support back home, especially when you consider that [Your Payslip is a Crime Scene, and the Government is Holding the Knife](/posts/your-payslip-is-a-crime-scene--and-the-government-is-holding-the-knife).

This latest arrival is just a week after the second contingent made their grand entrance. It’s almost like a revolving door of our police force being shipped off to trouble spots. Let's hope they’ve learned something useful, something beyond how to dodge bullets and navigate chaos. Because frankly, all this international posturing doesn't exactly translate to any tangible improvement in our own daily struggles. They’re back now, showered with accolades. Let's just hope the real work, the kind that actually impacts lives here in Nairobi, isn't forgotten in the fanfare.
