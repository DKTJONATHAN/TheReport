So, the big man is in Busia, shaking hands and laying foundation stones for a stadium that will magically hold 10,000 people. According to the President, this is all part of a grand plan to unite Kenya. He's had enough of leaders who "thrive on tribalism, hate, and division," and apparently, they should just "disappear." It’s a nice thought, isn't it? Like wishing away the mosquitoes in August. He even asks the crowd in Busia, "Tuungane ama tutengane?" (Shall we unite or divide?). As if the answer isn't already written in the dust of this country.

He's also defending this "broad-based government arrangement" as some kind of national glue. And to his critics, he’s got a message: engage with "clarity and reason," but if they "descend into insults and distraction," well, they'll only get a "portion" of his time. Because apparently, he's too busy for petty squabbles, too busy launching projects that cost Sh900 million and are expected to be done by December. Just another stadium in a long line of promises, much like Nairobi’s office market is chasing shadows while Kampala cashes in.

Then there's the little spat with his former Deputy, Rigathi Gachagua. Ruto claims he was "first provoked," that he was just telling them to "reduce on food" and let others benefit. Ah, the classic "they started it" defense. It's always someone else's fault, isn't it? Especially when it comes to who gets to "feed" from where. He's touring the Western region, supposedly inspecting development, but let's be honest, it's all part of the same political theatre.

He's launched markets, roads, a gold refinery, affordable housing, a referral hospital, another stadium, and now a campus. All in a week. One wonders if these projects will actually transform lives or just serve as backdrops for photo ops. The president’s pronouncements against tribalism are as predictable as the Nairobi annual baptism - you either pay the price or learn to tread water. And right now, Kenya seems to be treading water, listening to the same old lines about unity while the same old divisions persist.
