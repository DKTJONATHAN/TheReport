Let us talk about the absolute mess that went down at the Milimani Law Courts this week. You all remember David Mokaya, the 24-year-old Moi University student who thought it was a "vibe" to post a fake funeral procession for President Ruto on X? Well, the plot twist just dropped, and honey, the prosecution’s case was as empty as a socialite’s bank account after a Dubai trip. Mokaya has been acquitted, leaving the police looking like they skipped every law class ever held. 

Throughout the trial, Mokaya was serving "distressed victim" realness, shedding tears and looking deeply troubled. His lawyer, the ever-present Danstan Omari, claims the boy is so traumatized he can't even speak. While we love a good courtroom performance, the real tea is how the police managed to frame him so poorly. Magistrate Caroyne Mugo basically told the cops to go back to school because they tried to play detective without a court order for his Safaricom data. It is the sheer incompetence for me.

The whole drama started because of an image showing a coffin draped in the flag with the President’s face on it. Talk about a risky post for a few likes! It is funny how the state moves with lightning speed when the big man’s name is mentioned, yet they cannot even find a forensic link to a Twitter account. It reminds me of the [high-flying rhetoric and empty pockets](/posts/high-flying-rhetoric-and-empty-pockets--the-addis-ababa-special) we usually see from the administration. If only they put this much effort into actually fixing the economy instead of chasing university kids in Eldoret.

The magistrate did not hold back, pointing out that there was zero evidence Mokaya actually pressed "post." The kid was managing three different business accounts and sharing them with friends. The police could not even prove he was in Nairobi during the alleged crime. He was actually in Eldoret! The investigating officer basically admitted in court that he had no idea where the post came from. At this point, I am convinced the police get their training from watching TikTok skits rather than actual law books.

Now, Omari and his team are planning to sue the State for malicious prosecution. Of course they are! Why miss a chance for more billable hours and headlines? This whole saga is just a reminder that in the world of Kenyan politics and social media, the truth is usually buried under layers of ego and bad digital footprints. Mokaya gets his cash bail back, the police get a lecture, and we are all left wondering when the next viral hoax will drop. Stay messy, Nairobi.
