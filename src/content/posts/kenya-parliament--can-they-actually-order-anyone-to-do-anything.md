So, you read the headlines, right? "Senate Orders Fish Ban at Lake Nakuru," "National Assembly Directs Crypto Bill Prep." Makes you think these guys are running the show, issuing commands like they’re ordering ugali at a Mama Oliech's. But let's be real, most of that is just media drama or, at best, a polite "please and thank you" from a parliamentary committee. The actual power to *order* anyone to do anything specific? It’s as rare as a politician keeping a promise.

The constitution, that grand document they all swear by, is pretty clear on law-making. Parliament can certainly *legislate*, tell us all what to do through new laws, as long as it doesn't clash with the big book itself. This involves both the National Assembly and, when it comes to county matters, the Senate. Though, knowing our Parliament, they often forget to involve the Senate, leading to petty lawsuits. It’s like a marriage where one spouse always forgets the other’s birthday.

Beyond making laws, Parliament's "power" often boils down to oversight and recommendations. They can demand reports, like the Auditor-General’s findings on parastatals, and then "reprimand" or "direct" someone. But these are usually just recommendations, the kind you get from a nagging aunt, not actual commands. Think of it like the PAC, the Parliamentary Accounts Committee, issuing a report. They’re basically saying, "Hey, you guys screwed up here, maybe fix it." It's all very polite, very… Kenyan.

Where they *do* have a bit more bite is in financial matters, though even that's messy. They approve budgets, but often it's "spend first, ask forgiveness later" with supplementary budgets. And while they can approve or reject things, the actual implementation is miles away. They can also get involved in removing officials, like CSs or governors, but again, it’s a whole process, not a snap judgment. It's like watching a slow-motion car crash where everyone is debating the best way to brake.

The most concrete "power" they have is compelling people to appear as witnesses. If you ignore a summons, they can actually act like a court, issue arrest warrants, and even fine you up to Sh500,000. This is where the "ordering" really kicks in, though even then, there’s been court cases questioning their absolute authority to order specific arrests. So, while they might look like they have all the power, it’s more about making a lot of noise and a few select commands. It’s a bit like the drama surrounding Raphael Tuju a while back - lots of speculation and urgent police action that sometimes leads nowhere concrete. [Raphael Tuju: Police Scramble After Foreign Minister Vanishes Amid Abduction Claims](/posts/raphael-tuju--police-scramble-after-foreign-minister-vanishes-amid-abduction-claims).

Ultimately, most of what Parliament does is deliberation, recommendation, and approval. They can't just snap their fingers and make things happen. The idea that they can "order" things into existence is a myth peddled by catchy headlines. They represent the people, sure, but mostly by talking about it and passing laws that might, just might, get implemented eventually. Don’t hold your breath waiting for them to suddenly solve all your problems with a stern directive.
