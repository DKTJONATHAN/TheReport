Valve has thrown down the gauntlet, publicly declaring its intention to vigorously contest the lawsuit filed by New York Attorney General Letitia James. The AG's office last month branded Valve's popular loot boxes as a vehicle for "illegal gambling," a claim the gaming company vehemently denies. In an email communication directed at players of its flagship titles - Counter-Strike 2, Dota 2, and Team Fortress 2 - within New York, Valve expressed its "disappointment" with the lawsuit's premise.

The company's defense hinges on the argument that these "mystery boxes" are ubiquitous, citing their presence not only across a multitude of other video games but also in the tangible world through collectibles like baseball cards, Pokémon cards, and even novelty items. Valve asserts it has been actively engaged with state Attorneys General since 2023, attempting to clarify the operational mechanics of its virtual items and mystery boxes. It further contends that the ability to participate in its games is not contingent upon opening these boxes, and that the majority of players do not do so, as the contents are primarily cosmetic. While committed to fighting the lawsuit in court, Valve acknowledges it would adhere to any regulations specifically enacted by the state legislature concerning loot boxes.

James, upon filing the lawsuit, issued a statement painting a grim picture: "Valve has made billions of dollars luring its users, many of whom are teenagers or younger, to engage in gambling in the hopes of winning expensive virtual items that they can cash in on." Her office highlighted in-game mechanics that bear a striking resemblance to slot machines, capable of dispensing items valued at up to $1 million.

However, Valve voices "serious concerns" regarding the potential repercussions of the lawsuit's demands, particularly measures that would render boxes and their digital contents non-transferable. The company argues that the transferability of digital assets is a benefit to consumers, allowing them to exchange or sell unwanted items, much like one would with physical collectibles. This stance underscores Valve's view that the lawsuit seeks to dismantle a consumer-friendly aspect of digital ownership.
