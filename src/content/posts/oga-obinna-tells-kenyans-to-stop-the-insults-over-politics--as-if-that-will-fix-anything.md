So, Oga Obinna has decided to grace us with his wisdom, telling Kenyans to stop the political bickering. Apparently, we're all supposed to hold hands and sing Kumbaya, even when the people we elected are busy lining their pockets. He's got friends on both sides, you see. How noble. He expects us to believe that disagreeing with a politician's ideas is fine, but personal attacks are where we draw the line. As if the current political climate hasn't already blurred that line into oblivion.

He claims he'll speak out when roads are bad or schools need fixing, but also when things are good. Fascinating. It's almost like he's discovered that politicians are human beings who sometimes do things right and sometimes mess up spectacularly. Who knew? And the idea that we should acknowledge good deeds is just… quaint. Like expecting a stray dog to bring you a bouquet of flowers. We're too busy trying to survive the latest tax hike to notice the "good deeds."

The man states that political decisions affect us all, which is about the only sensible thing he's said. He's a registered voter, he says, and every decision impacts him directly. Well, duh. It impacts all of us, Oga. That's why we're all on the brink of a collective breakdown, not because we "support different candidates," but because the whole system seems to be designed to keep us down. Remember the days when even a football match could end in a draw, like that thrilling Shabana vs. Kenya Police game? Politics is supposed to be less dramatic, but it's turned into a bloodbath for our sanity.

He goes on to say it's "immature" to insult someone over their political choice. And he'd never fight someone over it. He's even played golf and had coffee with politicians from opposing sides. Oh, how civilized! It's like he's forgotten that for most Kenyans, the "political choice" often translates to which brand of suffering they'll endure for the next five years. This isn't about who likes which politician; it's about whether our lives are improving or degrading.

The whole sentiment is nice and all, a pleasant little fantasy where everyone’s a reasonable adult. But let's be real, Oga. We’re not just disagreeing on policy; we're watching our futures get gambled away. When leaders fail, we all suffer the consequences, regardless of who we voted for. So, while you’re sipping coffee with your political chums, the rest of us are dealing with the fallout. Your "respect for each other" is a luxury most of us can't afford when our stomachs are empty.
