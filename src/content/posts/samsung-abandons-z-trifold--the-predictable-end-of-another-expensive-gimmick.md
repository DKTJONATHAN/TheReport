So, Samsung might be done selling the Galaxy Z TriFold. Shocking, I know. This isn't a testament to innovation; it's a stark illustration of corporate ambition bumping up against harsh economic realities. The device, with its pretensions of being a phone and a tablet simultaneously, was always a vanity project. Six point five inches on the outside, ten inches when you unfold it twice - sounds impressive, right? It sold out quickly, apparently. Which, for a limited, astronomically expensive gadget, means precisely nothing. It means the early adopters, the ones with more money than sense, bought it before the inevitable price drop or discontinuation.

Now, the report from Donga, dutifully amplified by SamMobile, tells us why. High manufacturing costs. Rising component costs. It’s the same old story. They poured resources into a complicated, niche product, hoping to dazzle the market, and now they're facing the consequences. The article conveniently mentions Samsung's mobile division is already in "difficult position." Of course it is. Pouring money into unproven, ridiculously complex foldable technology while your core business is under pressure is not a strategy; it's a desperation play.

They'll trot out the usual platitudes, of course. Samsung is "still committed to foldables." They'll tease new models, like some hypothetical "wide foldable." It's the corporate dance: acknowledge the current failure, but promise something shinier and more expensive in the future. Don't be fooled. This isn't about pushing boundaries; it's about managing losses and trying to recoup investment in a product that never had a viable market beyond a handful of tech enthusiasts eager to throw money at the next big thing. The Galaxy Z TriFold was an expensive experiment, and like most experiments that ignore basic economics, it failed. Expect more of the same.
