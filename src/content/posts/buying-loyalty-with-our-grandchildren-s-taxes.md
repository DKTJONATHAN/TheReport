Here we go again. President William Ruto stood at the KICC on Thursday, painting a picture of a Kenya that sounds more like Singapore than the reality we wake up to in Nairobi. He is talking about joining the "league of first world economies" within a generation, a classic line for someone who knows most of us will be too tired or too broke to remember the promise by then. The new hook is the National Infrastructure Fund, which is just fancy talk for another pot of money that will likely vanish into the same black hole as previous projects funded by our sweat.

To get this "take-off" moving, the President is doing what he does best: playing politics with the people who hold the keys to the grass-roots. He is backing the reclassification of MCAs from job group D4 to E2. It is a classic move to keep the foot soldiers happy while the rest of us wonder where our next meal is coming from. If you think this is about "service to the people," you probably also believe that [The Sh80 Billion Trojan Horse: How Nairobi Just Got Sold Back to the State](/posts/the-sh80-billion-trojan-horse--how-nairobi-just-got-sold-back-to-the-state) was actually a gift to the city residents. It is all about consolidating power, one "protected fund" at a time.

The President claims the economy has recovered because the Central Bank dropped the interest rate by a fraction of a percent. Someone should tell the people at the Treasury that low inflation on paper does not mean a lower price for unga at the kiosk. He talks about "fiscal space" like it is something we can breathe, but for the average Kenyan, the only space we have is the gap between our empty pockets and the rising cost of living. This "intentional leadership" sounds a lot like being intentionally tone-deaf to the cries of the middle class.

Then there is the talk about the Social Health Authority (SHA). Ruto wants MCAs to push people to register for "free" healthcare while hospitals are still turning away patients because the government owes them billions. Calling out "unscrupulous hospitals" is a neat trick to shift the blame from a failing system to individual doctors. In this country, "reforms" usually mean the same old exploitation with a new, shinier acronym and the same old bill at the end of the day.

In the end, the County Assemblies Forum was just another stage for the President to secure his base. By supporting the Ward Development Fund and the County Assemblies Pension Scheme, he is ensuring that even if the country does not reach the "first world," the MCAs certainly will. We are being told to ignore the "form" and look at the "substance," but when the substance is just more debt and higher taxes, the form starts looking like a threat to our very survival.
