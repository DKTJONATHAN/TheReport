So, Moi Forces Academy decided to throw a "Career Day." Big deal. They paraded doctors, engineers, and military types to tell impressionable kids about education, discipline, and personal development. Apparently, this is supposed to magically open doors in a country where even those with degrees are scrambling for scraps. Madam Jacqueline Okwaro, bless her heart, thanked everyone for making the day a success. Success, I suppose, is measured by how many kids walk away with a vague sense of hope before reality slaps them in the face.

They talk about choosing subjects early to influence future opportunities. Cute. As if subject choice is the biggest hurdle when the economy is as stable as a drunken matatu driver on a potholed road. Good discipline and strong character are essential, they preach. Sure, that’ll definitely land you that high-paying job while your connections are busy networking at exclusive clubs. It's all about holistic education, bridging the gap between classroom learning and real-world career expectations. Except the "real world" out there is a jungle, and this little career day is more like showing them a brochure for a safari they can't afford.

The list of associated institutions reads like a who's who of government and military entities - National Defence College, Kenya Space Agency, Ministry of Defence. It’s all very impressive on paper, a sprawling network of opportunities for the chosen few. For the rest, it's just a reminder of the established order. You see, while they're busy pushing STEM and discipline, it's worth remembering that sometimes, the biggest career move is knowing the right person, a concept often overlooked in these well-intentioned but ultimately naive sessions. It's a harsh truth, much like how Ruto's China Trade Deal is about duty-free access, but *for whose benefit* remains the burning question.

They boast about strengthening collaboration between the school and industry professionals. What they don't mention is how many of these "professionals" are just there for the free lunch and the chance to feel important. The students showed great enthusiasm, of course. They're kids, they're supposed to be enthusiastic. But enthusiasm doesn't pay rent or guarantee a secure future in a nation grappling with unemployment. It’s another exercise in managing expectations, dressing up the same old system with a fresh coat of paint.
