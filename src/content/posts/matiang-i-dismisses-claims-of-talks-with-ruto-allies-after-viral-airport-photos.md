Matiang'i Denies Ruto Ally Chats After Airport Snaps Go Viral - Just Another Kenyan Political Fable

Just when you thought you'd seen it all with our esteemed politicians, Fred Matiang'i pops up with another dazzling display of "innocence." Apparently, a casual airport run-in with Simba Arati and Junet Mohamed, folks known for sniffing around the current administration, is just a "chance encounter." The former Interior CS, who's supposedly still in the opposition trenches, is trying to convince us that photos of them all together at JKIA are just a coincidence, not some backroom deal being cooked up. Because, of course, politicians are always honest and transparent, right?

He claims they were all just headed to Kisumu, each on their own flight. A convenient narrative, wouldn't you say? Matiang'i spins a yarn about church services with Governor Orengo and Martha Karua, and then "political engagements" in the region. It’s the standard playbook: deny, deflect, and throw in a dash of "religious duty" to make it sound more believable. Meanwhile, while he was busy being "coincidental," other opposition bigwigs were gathering in Kisii for some "empowerment forum." Seems like everyone's getting their ducks in a row for 2027, with whispers of closer ties to the Ruto camp swirling around these very individuals.

The viral images, of course, sparked the usual online frenzy. People saw Matiang'i, Arati, and Junet - a trio whose recent activities have made them look like they're auditioning for roles in President Ruto's cast - and jumped to conclusions. But Matiang'i, ever the master of spin, insists it was nothing more than a non-event. No secret meetings, no negotiations, just three leaders happening to be in the same airport at the same time. It's the kind of story that makes you wonder if they think we're all still buying the snake oil. This whole charade feels as transparent as the "Second-Hand" Scam we've seen unfolding in Luthuli Avenue, where goods are passed off as something they're not.

One has to admire the audacity. In a political landscape where alliances shift faster than the Nairobi traffic on a Friday evening, claiming a chance encounter is a bold move. It’s the politician’s equivalent of a crocodile claiming it just happened to be swimming near the bank where the gazelles drink. The truth, as always, is probably somewhere in the dusty, uncomfortable middle, or perhaps entirely somewhere else, being carefully concealed.
