While the world was distracted by Powell’s power grab, the Fed quietly moved its goalposts. Raising the long-run growth forecast from 1.8 percent to 2.0 percent is a calculated maneuver. It is not an admission of newfound economic vigor, but a desperate attempt to normalize a high-inflation, high-debt environment. By raising the target, they are signaling that they will tolerate more heat in the economy before they are forced to admit failure. It is a loosening of policy dressed up as optimism, a classic trick to keep the global debt engine from stalling while the average worker’s purchasing power continues to erode.

The energy market is currently delivering a brutal reality check that no Fed spreadsheet can ignore. Oil at $110 a barrel is a tax on existence. The climb from $60 to triple digits is the sound of the global middle class being hollowed out. We are well past the three-year high, a threshold that historically screams "recession" to anyone not blinded by official talking points. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively a geopolitical trigger, the hope for a "soft landing" is a fantasy sold to the gullible. There is no policy fix for a world where the physical supply of energy is a hostage to war and geography.

Artificial intelligence is being marketed as the next great labor disruptor, but the reality is much darker. The comparison to Eli Whitney’s cotton gin is telling. History shows that labor-saving technology rarely liberates the worker; it simply makes the exploitation of that labor more profitable. If AI makes a worker twice as productive, the system will not give that worker half the day off. It will demand twice the output for the same price. AI is not coming for your job; it is coming to ensure you never stop working in a world where the cost of living, driven by $110 oil, makes retirement an obsolete concept.

The rise and fall of the Dutch East India Company serves as the ultimate memento mori for our current era of economic nationalism and corporate empires. They had the armies, the coins, and the monopolies, yet they were eventually liquidated by the same corruption and debt that haunt modern institutions. Your Payslip is a Crime Scene, and the Government is Holding the Knife: /posts/your-payslip-is-a-crime-scene--and-the-government-is-holding-the-knife. Just like the VOC, today’s global entities believe they are too big to fail right up until the moment they are sold for scrap.

We are witnessing a convergence of institutional decay, energy shocks, and technological enslavement. Powell can claim he is the chairman forever, and the Fed can pretend 2.0 percent growth is a victory, but the reality is a global system straining under its own contradictions. The dividends might still be paying out for now, but as any cynical realist knows, the insolvency is already baked into the charter. When the cost of filling a tank exceeds the value of a day's labor, no amount of AI-generated productivity or central bank posturing will save the empire from its inevitable correction.
