Ruto's Mau Mau Road: 60% Done, Ksh 2 Billion More for Another Two Years

WARNING: More road promises from the President. While he talks big about the Mau Mau Road project, expect more delays and more billions disappearing. Just another day in Kenyan infrastructure development.

President Ruto, in his usual style, was out in Murang'a dishing out promises at a church function. Apparently, the Mau Mau Road project, connecting Kiambu, Murang’a, Nyeri, and Nyandarua, is now a whopping 60 percent complete. That's a lot of concrete and a lot of time, considering it's been two years since it kicked off. And what’s 60 percent without a little more cash? Cue the Ksh 2 billion allocation to finish off the Murang’a section, with the promise of it being done in "the next two years." Sounds familiar, doesn’t it?

This "historical" road, supposedly tracing the steps of freedom fighters, is a gargantuan Ksh 30 billion affair stretching 540 kilometers. The idea is to boost agriculture, cut travel time, and apparently, build museums. Because what Kenya needs more than functioning infrastructure is more relics of the past while the present chugs along at a snail's pace. We’ve seen this song and dance before, haven't we? Remember when we talked about [Ruto promises nuclear power plant for Siaya in 2027 - Hope or More Hot Air](/posts/ruto-promises-nuclear-power-plant-for-siaya-in-2027---hope-or-more-hot-air)? More grand visions, same old reality.

The President's vision extends beyond this one road, of course. He’s got this "Singapore Dream" where 28,000 kilometers of roads will magically transform Kenya from third-world to first-world in seven years. Meanwhile, the current project, meant to ease the transport of tea and coffee, seems to be more about easing money out of the national treasury. The Ksh 2 billion for Murang’a alone, covering just 48 kilometers, means each kilometer is costing us a cool Ksh 41 million. That's enough to buy a small mansion, or perhaps, a few more stadiums, which he also promised for Murang’a.

Speaking of goodies for Murang’a, Ruto didn't just stop at roads. We’re talking two modern stadiums at Ksh 1.8 billion, upgrades for 27 markets, and thousands of affordable housing units. It's a buffet of promises, all served with a side of lofty economic transformation. One wonders if these projects are designed to truly benefit the people or just to tick boxes on a politician's checklist. It’s a classic case of keeping the masses happy with shiny new things while the deeper economic issues remain stubbornly unresolved.

This Mau Mau Road, with its historical significance and economic aspirations, is just another brick in the wall of Kenyan infrastructure projects. We're told it will ease transport and boost the economy, but the reality on the ground often tells a different story. With Ksh 30 billion on the table and a completion timeline that keeps shifting like sand, it’s hard not to be a little cynical. After all, roads need cash, and where that cash ultimately ends up is always the million-dollar question. We've seen leaders tell the opposition to [stop blaming Ruto for everything, because roads need cash](/posts/ledama-tells-opposition-to--stop-blaming-ruto-for-everything--amid-fuel-price-debate---because-roads-need-cash), but perhaps it's time to look at *how* that cash is being used.
