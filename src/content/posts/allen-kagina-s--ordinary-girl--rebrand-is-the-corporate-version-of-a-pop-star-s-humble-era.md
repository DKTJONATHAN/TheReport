Allen Kagina is currently on a media tour trying to sell us the most tired trope in the PR handbook: the "I’m just an ordinary girl" narrative. During a recent radio appearance, the former queen of Uganda’s tax and roads departments stripped away the mythology of her career to claim she was not born a leader. Please. We have heard this before from every celebrity trying to pivot from a scandalous past to a "humble" present. It is the corporate equivalent of a pop star releasing an acoustic album after a public meltdown, hoping we forget the pyrotechnics and the ego.

She claims her journey from the hills of Kanungu to the top of the Uganda National Roads Authority (UNRA) was just a series of opportunities she was "ready" for. Let us be real- being "ready" usually means having the right connections in the President’s Office, where she conveniently spent some of her early career. She reminisced about her days as a teaching assistant at Makerere University, where she had to "establish authority" over peers her own age. It sounds less like a lesson in leadership and more like a classic power trip for someone who knew they were destined for the high table while the rest of us were still figuring out our morning coffee.

Then there is the whole Katosi road scandal chapter. Kagina admitted she did not know much about road construction when she took the Executive Director job at UNRA. Imagine a CEO of a major record label saying they do not really "get" music. She framed the subsequent restructuring and mass job losses as "necessary risks" to rebuild credibility. It is the same kind of corporate doublespeak we see when [Kim Kardashian’s Latest 'No Bull' Campaign is Actually Piling It Higher Than Ever](/posts/kim-kardashian-s-latest--no-bull--campaign-is-actually-piling-it-higher-than-ever) while the actual substance is buried under layers of expensive marketing fluff.

Now that UNRA has been mainstreamed back into the Ministry of Works, Kagina has moved on to her next project: the TVET Council. She is now the champion of "skilling," trying to tell the youth that being a tailor or a construction technician is the path to national glory. It is incredibly easy to preach about the "dignity" of vocational work when you have spent decades in air-conditioned offices with a government motorcade. The stigma around technical education exists for a reason, and no amount of "restoring structure" is going to change the fact that the pay gap in this country is wider than the potholes she was supposed to fix.

To top off the "perfect life" aesthetic, she is bragging about her 33-year marriage to her husband, Paul. She calls it a "covenant for life" anchored in respect. While the rest of the entertainment world is busy filing for divorce after six months of "irreconcilable differences," Kagina is playing the long game of moral superiority. It is a clean, disciplined story designed to make her look like the ultimate role model, but we know that behind every "steady growth" story is a carefully curated script and a very busy PR team making sure the "ordinary girl" stays on brand.
