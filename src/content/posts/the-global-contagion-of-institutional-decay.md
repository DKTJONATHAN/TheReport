This is the era of the lawsuit as a primary weapon. As recently noted before Congress, the collegiate rulebook has been replaced by the litigation brief. This mirrors the same institutional rot we see in international bodies where rules are only applicable to those too weak to hire a high-priced legal team or lobby a friendly politician. The integrity of the game is a secondary concern to the preservation of political optics.

Look at the parallels in our previous reporting on Kyiv-s Revolving Door of Graft: The Energy Minister Who Tried to Run. Whether it is energy contracts in Eastern Europe or recruitment scandals in West Texas, the mechanism remains the same. Authority is no longer derived from rules or merit, but from the ability to paralyze the system with bureaucratic or legal theater.

The Big 12 stands at a crossroads that many global organizations have already crossed. By allowing state officials to dictate internal sanctions, they effectively dissolve their own sovereignty. If the integrity of a game cannot be maintained without a court injunction, then the game itself has ceased to exist as a fair competition. It becomes just another theater for the exercise of raw power.

We are watching the commodification of rules. In a world where every regulation is just a starting point for a lawsuit, the entities with the deepest pockets and the most aggressive lawyers win by default. This is not just a threat to college football. It is the blueprint for the collapse of global institutional trust, proving once again that rules are for the small players while the big players simply sue the rules out of existence.
