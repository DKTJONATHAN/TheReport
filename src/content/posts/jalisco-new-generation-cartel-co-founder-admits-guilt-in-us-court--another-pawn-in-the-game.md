The United States Department of Justice (DOJ) trumpets the guilty plea of Érick Valencia Salazar, also known as "El 85" and a co-founder of the Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG), as a significant win. Valencia Salazar, captured by the Mexican army in 2022 and subsequently extradited to the US in February 2025 along with 28 other alleged kingpins, has admitted to one count of conspiracy to distribute over 5 kilograms of cocaine. The mandatory minimum sentence for this offense is a mere 10 years, a paltry sum considering the cartel's destructive reach.

The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) paints a grim picture, describing CJNG as a "ruthless organization that uses violence as a business model" responsible for "murdering for control in Mexico while flooding the United States with poison." This rhetoric, while accurate, conveniently sidesteps the uncomfortable truth that the demand for these "poisons" from within the US fuels the cartel's very existence.

Valencia Salazar's plea change from not guilty to guilty is not a revelation of newfound remorse, but a pragmatic calculation in the face of overwhelming evidence and the likelihood of a lesser sentence through cooperation. This is not the dismantling of a cartel, but the processing of one cog in a much larger, perpetually churning machine.

The CJNG, one of Mexico's most formidable criminal enterprises, has demonstrated its resilience and power repeatedly. The recent wave of violence across 20 Mexican states, triggered by unconfirmed reports of its leader "El Mencho's" death, serves as a stark reminder of the organization's ability to project power and sow chaos. The death or capture of one leader or co-founder simply creates a vacuum, quickly filled by ambitious subordinates eager to climb the bloody hierarchy.

This guilty plea is an isolated event, a single data point in a conflict that has spanned decades and cost countless lives. The US justice system may secure a conviction, but the underlying forces that empower organizations like the CJNG - corruption, socioeconomic disparity, and insatiable demand - remain largely unaddressed. Until these root causes are confronted, the cycle of drug trafficking, violence, and hollow victories will undoubtedly continue.
