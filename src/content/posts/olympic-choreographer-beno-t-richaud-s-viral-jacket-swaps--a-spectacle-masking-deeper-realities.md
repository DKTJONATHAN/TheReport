The 2026 Winter Olympics are awash in spectacle, and amidst the manufactured drama, Benoît Richaud has emerged as an unlikely star. His claim to fame? An incessant ritual of jacket switching, a sartorial chameleon act performed from the edge of the ice. Richaud, a choreographer for a frankly absurd number of skaters - sixteen, hailing from thirteen different nations - has become a viral sensation. Viewers, starved for any narrative hook beyond the predictable athletic contests, latched onto his stoic expression and his uncanny ability to be present for nearly every performance, cloaked in the colors of whatever nation was currently competing.

Let's be clear: this isn't about Richaud's dedication. It's about the desperate need for narrative in an event increasingly driven by broadcast rights and sponsorship deals. The "kiss and cry" zone, already a stage for manufactured emotion, has found its unlikely puppet master. Richaud's "omnipresence" is a carefully curated illusion, a visual anchor in the choppy waters of international competition. He tells WIRED Italia that he coaches "a lot more" than the sixteen who qualified, a testament to his ability to commodify his coaching services across a global market.

His work, he explains, is a matter of "planning." The post-World Championships window from April to July is his "big window" for choreography. Then it's a whirlwind of travel, or increasingly, the sterile efficiency of internet-based updates. "New technologies" allow him to "do much more and do it much faster." This isn't about artistic connection; it's about optimizing output. He receives "almost all of my skaters’ programs every day" to ensure "effectiveness." This is not coaching, it's quality control on a global scale.

The challenges he cites are not about the emotional toll of artistic creation or the personal growth of his athletes. They are about the logistical nightmare of "remembering the choreography and details of every skater’s program." The "emotional roller coaster" he describes is less about the intrinsic beauty of sport and more about the financial and professional implications of a skater's score. A third-place finish plummeting off the podium, a fifth-place finish agonizingly close to a medal - these are not moments of profound human experience, but data points in a complex economic equation.

Richaud claims he has no favorites, that the 100 percent he gives to each skater "balances your emotions." This is a convenient platitude. In a system that rewards individual achievement, the notion of completely unbiased emotional investment is a fairy tale. He confesses to "times when I cry and times when I have so much joy inside that I have a hard time even controlling myself." These are not expressions of genuine empathy; they are the visceral reactions of a man whose livelihood is tied to the success and failure of others. He experiences "emotional peaks," yes, but they are the peaks of a gambler who has placed bets on numerous horses.

The public's newfound fascination with Richaud is telling. His fame, he claims, is "unexpected," yet he's "grateful for the attention it brings to the sport." This sport, he laments, "isn’t quite as popular on social media as it could be." His viral fame, he hopes, will boost figure skating's meager presence online. He calls it "one of the most beautiful in the world," a description that rings hollow when contrasted with the transactional nature of his profession.

His amazement that "jackets have made him a social media sensation" is disingenuous. He sees the videos, he acknowledges the attention, and he enjoys the "good and fun feeling." This isn't about the art; it's about the algorithm. He is a product of a system that prioritizes virality over substance, a fleeting distraction in the endless scroll of digital content. The true story here isn't about a choreographer's dedication; it's about the desperate measures taken by a sport, and its practitioners, to remain relevant in an attention-starved world, all while draped in the superficial veneer of Olympic spectacle.
