So, the bigwigs have gathered for the 19th Ambassadors and High Commissioners Conference. The same old song and dance: "Leverage trade and investment," they shout from their ivory towers. Apparently, foreign investors still think Kenya is a shiny new toy, but as Dr. James Mwangi, Equity Group's big boss, pointed out, the long-term returns? They conveniently vanish abroad, bleeding our precious foreign exchange. This is supposed to make us feel good, I suppose.

Mwangi also had the audacity to mention that infrastructure alone is not the magic bullet. Shocking, I know. He’s urging Kenyans to save. Save what, exactly? The coins rattling in our pockets after paying for everything else? He’s touting partnerships with Trade Connect and AfCFTA - more acronyms, more promises of facilitation. It’s all very grand, but I've seen this kind of talk before, particularly when we’re discussing digital talkfests. [High-Level Hallucinations: Nairobi Prepares for Another Digital Talkfest](/posts/high-level-hallucinations--nairobi-prepares-for-another-digital-talkfest).

President Ruto, bless his optimistic heart, wants our diplomats to position Kenya as a "credible investment destination." Credible? We’re still trying to figure out where the AGOA money went, and now we’re supposed to attract more? He wants them to "unlock new markets." I’m sure they’re busy unlocking champagne bottles and private jets, while our local enterprises are still struggling to pay for electricity.

And then there are the digital platforms - BiasharaLink and Deal House. Launched in Addis Ababa, no less. These are meant to transform embassies into "active hubs for cross-border commerce." Because what we really need is more online forms and fewer actual deals. They aim to help SMEs, especially women-led businesses. Bless their hearts, they're probably hoping for some petty cash to trickle down from the grand pronouncements.

The gist of it all is this: more talk, more meetings, more digital interfaces. The foreign investors will keep coming because they know where the money is. We, on the other hand, are still urged to "increase our share of investment locally." Which, in layman's terms, means we're expected to somehow conjure money out of thin air to invest in our own country, while the profits are siphoned off elsewhere. Just another Tuesday in Nairobi.
