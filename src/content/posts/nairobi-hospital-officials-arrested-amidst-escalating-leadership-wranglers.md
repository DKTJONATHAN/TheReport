NAIROBI HOSPITAL LEADERSHIP WRANGLES GO CRIMINAL AS OFFICIALS ARRESTED

Another day, another Kenyan institution proving it's more about the drama than the actual service. The Nairobi Hospital, that fancy place where the well-heeled go to get fixed, is now a police station waiting room. Three top board officials, including the chairman and vice chairman, got themselves arrested over the weekend. Apparently, playing by the rules is too much to ask when there are multi-billion shillings at stake.

The charges? Falsifying the members' register and conflict of interest. Classic Nairobi. Dr. Job Obwaka, Samson Kinyanjui, and Dr. Chris Bichange are cooling their heels in police cells, with Bichange apparently having a particularly exciting Friday night abduction. Lawyer Cliff Ombeta is out here crying foul about anticipatory bail, but let's be real, when powerful interests want something, due process often takes a backseat. This isn't just about governance; it's about who gets to siphon off the millions.

This whole mess is just another episode in the never-ending saga of who runs The Nairobi Hospital, a place that's been around longer than some of us have been paying taxes. It’s a free-for-all with rival board factions, government busybodies, and the medical professionals themselves all scrambling for a piece of the pie. And surprise, surprise, their annual general meeting is on hold because nobody can agree on whose name is even supposed to be on the damn register. It's like a boardroom soap opera, but with real money and possibly real jail time.

The Law Society of Kenya vice president, Mwaura Kabata, is spinning the usual line about police trying to force resignations so "powerful interests" can seize control. Who are these "powerful interests"? The same ones who always are, I guess. Meanwhile, the Kenya Medical Practitioners, Pharmacists and Dentists Union is shocked, shocked, that an 83-year-old obstetrician got nabbed, even if he "wasn't present" for the alleged crimes. It's a well-worn script: claim innocence, blame the system, and hope the public forgets by next week. It’s a familiar tune, much like the warnings issued by Kenya Prisons over [fake accounts impersonating their officials](/posts/kenya-prisons-issues-warning-over-fake-accounts-impersonating-commissioner-general).

This whole charade is unfolding right after former Cabinet Secretary Justin Muturi called out government-linked goons trying to stage a hostile takeover. He's accusing them of using investigators and raids to install their own cronies. Intimidation and abuse of power, he says. Of course, he does. It's all about control and the spoils. The courts have been drowning in lawsuits over this hospital's leadership for years, but now, with actual criminal charges, it’s gotten messy. This isn't just about management anymore; it's a full-blown heist, and the public is just expected to watch.

This isn't the first time we've seen institutions become battlegrounds for power and money. Whether it's the [narcissism of post-victory hijacks](/posts/the-narcissism-of-the-post-victory-hijack) or desperate attempts to salvage a failing narrative, the core issue remains the same: people looking out for themselves, not the people they're supposed to serve. The Nairobi Hospital drama is just the latest, most public display of this ingrained Kenyan reality.







Nairobi Hospital officials arrested as leadership wrangles escalate

The long-running leadership dispute at The Nairobi Hospital took a dramatic turn over the weekend after police arrested three senior board officials, pushing the governance battle into the criminal justice system.

Board chairman, Dr Job Obwaka, vice chairman and lawyer Samson Kinyanjui and former chairman, Dr Chris Bichange, were taken into custody on Saturday over allegations of falsifying the hospital’s members’ register and conflict of interest. The officials were detained at Muthaiga and Pangani police stations and are expected to be arraigned in court on Monday. Dr Bichage, who was reportedly abducted by security agents on Friday, was found later in Pangani, according to lawyer Cliff Ombeta. Mr Ombeta alleged on X that Dr Bichage was taken despite an existing anticipatory bail barring his arrest, in connection with a legal dispute over the hospital’s management.

The arrests mark the latest twist in a bitter struggle that has engulfed the prestigious private hospital, owned by the Kenya Hospital Association, which has operated for nearly 70 years. Rival board factions, government interests and medical professionals have been embroiled in disputes over leadership positions, financial management, and the legitimacy of the members’ register. The crisis follows court orders halting the hospital’s 2025 annual general meeting after rival factions challenged both the register and the meeting’s convening process. Allegations linked to a multi-billion-shilling financial scandal have further intensified the conflict.

Law Society of Kenya vice president Mwaura Kabata, representing the three officials, said the arrests came despite anticipatory bail orders from the High Court in Kibera. He accused police of attempting to force the officials to resign to enable unnamed parties to seize control of the hospital. “The intention is to coerce them to resign so that powerful interests can take control of the hospital and its properties,” Mr Kabata said. The Kenya Medical Practitioners, Pharmacists and Dentists Union (KMPDU) condemned the detention of Dr Obwaka, describing it as shocking and troubling. The union noted that the 83-year-old obstetrician and gynaecologist was arrested at his clinic’s parking bay outside the NSSF Building, despite reportedly not being present during the alleged irregularities. The union also expressed concern that he was denied cash bail.

The arrests came two days after former Cabinet Secretary and Democratic Party leader Justin Muturi alleged that government-linked actors were attempting a forceful takeover of the hospital. Mr Muturi claimed that investigators had summoned directors and conducted raids at their homes as part of a coordinated effort to install proxies of the State on the board. “This is intimidation and abuse of power against a private institution,” he said. Court records show that The Nairobi Hospital’s governance struggles have triggered multiple lawsuits over the board authority and representation, with cases repeatedly reaching the High Court and Court of Appeal. With senior officials now facing criminal charges, the fight for control of the iconic hospital has entered its most explosive phase yet.
