Ruto Demands Banks Lend More to MSMEs, Unveils Another Policy That'll Probably Go Nowhere
WARNING: President Ruto's call for more MSME lending is a tired refrain, and another policy likely won't change the reality for struggling entrepreneurs.
President William Ruto speaking at KICC on Saturday, June 27, 2026.
Politics
Economy, Business, Government
Speech
The President, William Ruto, stood at the KICC the other day, looking all concerned about micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs). He's apparently "challenged" the banks - as if they haven't heard this song and dance a million times - to lend more. He spouted statistics about MSMEs contributing 40% of the economy, yet conveniently glossed over why these businesses, the supposed "backbone," are still starving for capital. Ksh.1 trillion lent over three years? That's pocket change compared to the Ksh.3 trillion gap, but hey, at least he acknowledged it.

He posed rhetorical questions about growing the economy while "locking most of our people off the balance sheet." Good questions, Mr. President. Perhaps you could start by looking at your own government's policies. Meanwhile, we're treated to the usual government spin: a dedicated State Department and the mythical Hustler Fund, which has apparently disbursed Ksh.90 billion to 27 million Kenyans. Let's be real, that's not exactly rocket fuel for businesses, and many are still struggling to even *get* access, let alone rebuild credit.

Then comes the Grameen Bank comparison. Bless his heart. Comparing our Kenyan reality to a Nobel Peace Prize winning model from Bangladesh is like comparing a boda boda to a SpaceX rocket. The "unbanked are a market waiting to be served"? Tell that to the loan officers who still demand collateral as if it's going out of fashion. It feels more like a convenient excuse to avoid dealing with the actual structural issues that keep people locked out of formal finance. It's the same old story, whether it's the stock market or small businesses, if you don't have the right connections, you're invisible.

And let's not forget the "Revised MSMEs Policy 2026." Another policy. Another document to gather dust on some official's shelf. We’ve seen these before. They promise to eliminate barriers, but the barriers are usually made of red tape, corruption, and a general lack of political will to truly empower the little guy. Nairobi Governor Sakaja chimes in about his Unified Business Permit, which is great for streamlining things in the capital, but what about the countless businesses operating outside Nairobi, or those just trying to get registered in the first place?

The United Nations Resident Coordinator, Garry Conille, offered some praise, calling it "a nation deciding that no Kenyan with ambition should be invisible." A noble sentiment, but invisibility seems to be the default setting for many entrepreneurs in this country. They hear promises, they see grand pronouncements, and then they go back to their hustle, facing the same old hurdles. It's not charity they need; it's actual support, not just more pronouncements.

So, here we are again. Another World MSME Day, another speech from the President, another policy unveiled. The banks are urged, the government claims reforms, and the usual cast of characters are paraded for the cameras. But for the millions of Kenyans running small businesses, the fundamental problem remains: access to affordable credit is still a pipe dream for most. We'll see if this time is any different. My money's on it not being so.
