So, the President has finally signed this "Division of Revenue" bill. Apparently, it's Sh428 billion for the counties. After seven - yes, *seven* - mediation sessions between the National Assembly and the Senate. You know what that means, right? It means a whole lot of hot air, backroom deals, and probably just enough to keep the lights on in some offices while the real issues fester.

This Sh428 billion is supposed to cover everything from your healthcare to the dusty roads you drive on. It's the "equitable share," they call it, a fancy term for the pot of money counties get from whatever the national government manages to scrape together. And this year, they're estimating a cool Sh2.9 trillion in "shareable revenue." Which, let's be honest, is probably optimistic. Remember that chaos with school placements last year? This feels like the same song, different verse, just with more zeros. [Kenyan School Placement Chaos 2025: The KJSEA Grade 10 Disaster, Kisumu-to-Mandera Glitch, and What Parents Must Do Now](/posts/kjsea-grade10-placement-chaos-2025).

They even managed to bring back Clause 5, which is supposed to protect county cash from national revenue shortfalls. Noble, I'm sure. But let's not forget the constant political wrangling that precedes every single one of these "milestones." Politicians patting themselves on the back, calling it a victory for devolution. It’s a victory for them, more like, as they get to divvy up the spoils and pretend they’re delivering.

And while we're talking about money and public funds, it's always worth remembering how easily it can disappear. We've seen scandals before, haven't we? From ticket scams to… well, let's just say other less visible hustles. [Exposed: CHAN 2024 Ticketing Scam; How Fake Tickets and Political Hoarding Locked Out Kenyan Fans at Kasarani](/posts/CHAN-tickets-Scam). This Sh428 billion is just another target.

Sure, the Senators and MPs are chirping about progress and pushing the country forward. But the real test isn't the signing; it's what happens *after*. Will this money actually reach the people who need it? Will it be spent wisely on healthcare, water, and education, or will it find its way into inflated tenders and comfortable salaries? We’ve heard promises before, and we’ve seen the results. Let's just hope this isn't another Sh428 billion lost in translation.

The law is signed, the constitutional hurdle cleared, and now the real games begin as they figure out how to divide this Sh428 billion amongst the 47 counties. Expect more noise, more lobbying, and probably more frustration when your local government still can't fix that pothole you’ve been complaining about for months.
