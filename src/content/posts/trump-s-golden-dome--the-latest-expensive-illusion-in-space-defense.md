The Pentagon is, predictably, pouring money into Donald Trump's "Golden Dome" vanity project, a supposed missile defense system that sounds more like science fiction than sound national security policy. Satellite startup Impulse Space and defense tech company Anduril have been tapped to develop prototypes for the core technology. This, we're told, will involve space-based missile tracking and targeting - a concept that has been perpetually on the horizon and perpetually failing to materialize since the Reagan era's "Star Wars" fantasies.

Let's be clear: a significant military attack on American soil hasn't occurred since World War II. The "looming, urgent threat" Trump's administration claims to be addressing is, to put it mildly, a stretch. Yet, the White House is moving full steam ahead with a plan that CBO reports could take two decades to build, while simultaneously touting near-term testing before the 2028 election. This temporal whiplash is typical of ambitious, poorly conceived government initiatives.

The heart of this endeavor, space-based interceptors, doesn't even exist in a practical sense. Experts deem it theoretically ineffective and impractical, a fact that hasn't deterred the Space Force from engaging in market research for capabilities that are, at best, speculative. Impulse Space, led by a former SpaceX executive, is now set to subcontract for Anduril, a company whose track record is more about feeding the defense industrial complex than delivering foolproof solutions. This is a dance we've seen before, with defense giants like Northrop Grumman and Lockheed Martin also involved in the initial stages, all vying for a piece of a potentially astronomical pie.

Estimates for this "Golden Dome" already run as high as $3.6 trillion through 2045. For context, that's exponentially more than the Manhattan Project. The Brookings Institution, not exactly a radical fringe group, has rightly pointed out that this plan is not only costly but actively destabilizing, potentially provoking Russia and China into a new arms race that will ultimately make everyone less secure.

In short, "Golden Dome" is the latest iteration of a long-standing American obsession with an impenetrable missile shield - an obsession that has consistently yielded expensive failures and geopolitical risks. This isn't about genuine security; it's about technological hubris, political theater, and the endless enrichment of defense contractors. The shiny dome may be in space, but its foundations are firmly rooted in wishful thinking and taxpayer dollars.
