Constitution Sets Us Free? More Like It Chains Us Back Together

A grim reminder that Kenya's 2010 Constitution, hailed as a beacon of freedom, seems to have only reinforced the chains of ancient, authoritarian traditions. We've traded the Tsar for a President, and elected representatives for self-appointed tribal elders.

The ink is barely dry on the 2010 Constitution, proclaiming that all power rests with the people. Yet, here we are, watching President Ruto dole out development like pocket money, rewarding communities he fancies with roads and hospitals. And the sycophants, our so-called regional leaders, are down on their knees, begging for scraps. It's a slap in the face of every citizen's right to development, reducing it to a presidential whim.

Then there's the northern Kenya charade, where "negotiated democracy" means old men in barazas decide who gets elected, conveniently excluding young people and women. And don't even get me started on the "tribal kingpins," often egged on by educated folks who should know better, dictating electoral outcomes. It's a regression to a time when leaders were demi-gods, capable of plunging communities into poverty or lifting them out. We are, it seems, addicted to the old authoritarian models, even when the constitution sets us free.

This obsession with old loyalties is precisely why the Gen-Z movement, with their cry of "We are leaderless and tribeless!", resonated. They understood the psychological bondage we’re trapped in, the craving for chains even after emancipation. Their allegiance was to Kenya and the constitution, not to some self-proclaimed chief. Their demand for a national renaissance was a cry against the political class’s determination to keep us shackled by outdated notions. It's almost as bad as the KPSEA "clout-chasing" we've seen, where fraudulence is disguised as progress.

The path to rebuilding Kenya requires shattering these psychological and political chains. We need to stop relinquishing our constitutional power to councils of elders, barazas, and tribal kingpins. We are capable of thinking for ourselves, but we choose to appoint others to do it for us. Like serfs after emancipation, we crave the familiar comfort of our chains.

The author, Tee Ngugi, a Nairobi-based political commentator, highlights a fundamental flaw: we are given the power to choose, but we willingly hand it back to outdated structures. The constitution offers freedom, yet we remain beholden to ancient, authoritarian cultures, a cycle that needs to be broken for any real national renaissance to occur.
