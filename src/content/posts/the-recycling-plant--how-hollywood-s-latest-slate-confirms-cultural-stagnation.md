Hollywood is no longer a creative hub; it is a recycling plant where nostalgia goes to be processed into flavorless, high-fructose content for the masses. The recent flurry of announcements-from the desperate revival of "Blake's 7" to the inevitable "Incredibles 3"-serves as a bleak reminder that the culture industry has officially run out of oxygen. The strategy is clear: minimize financial risk by maximizing familiarity. It is a cycle of creative cannibalism where the only goal is to sustain subscription numbers for platforms that are already struggling to justify their existence.

The announcement of a "Blake's 7" reboot is particularly telling. It has a director and a producer but no network. In the current economic climate, this is less of a "development" and more of a "plea for attention." It is the television equivalent of a developer announcing a luxury condo before they have the permits, the land, or the funding. It exists as a headline to pump up brand value, not as a project with a reason to exist. Similarly, Holly Hunter’s admission that she begins recording "The Incredibles 3" without even knowing the story highlights the assembly-line nature of modern animation. When the actors are secondary to the brand name, the narrative becomes an afterthought to the merchandising schedule.

The trend extends into the "eat the rich" horror genre, which has now become a parody of itself. Projects like "Exquisite Corpse" promise a deadly game played by the wealthy, a concept so overused it has become the very thing it seeks to satirize: a commodity sold to the masses by the corporate elite. Even the casting of Jason Momoa as Lobo in the DCU feels like a desperate pivot for a franchise that has spent the last decade tripping over its own feet. This is not artistic expression; it is asset management.

While consumers are distracted by these shiny, recycled objects, the reality of their daily lives continues to degrade under the weight of corporate overreach. While you wait for a 2027 film release that will likely be delayed or canceled, the actual world is busy implementing [Legislation Introduced to Ban Surveillance and Surge Pricing in Grocery Stores](/posts/legislation-introduced-to-ban-surveillance-and-surge-pricing-in-grocery-stores), a sign that the "dystopia" Hollywood loves to sell is already being codified in your local supermarket. The industry offers you "Supergirl" while the real world offers you surge-priced milk. It is a trade-off only a fool would find entertaining.
