This is the reality of the international sporting industrial complex. Organizations would rather see high-quality food rot in a landfill than risk a fractional percentage of their profit margins to a liability claim. In Houston, a city where thousands live on the streets, the "official philanthropic projects" mentioned by the team are merely line items for tax breaks and brand polishing. The actual act of feeding someone under a bridge is viewed as a systemic threat because it cannot be quantified, photographed for a brochure, or controlled by a legal department.

The juxtaposition of the team’s history-specifically players like JR Richard who experienced homelessness-against their current corporate rigidity is telling. It highlights a global trend where human stories are only valuable when they can be packaged into a "comeback" narrative. When the suffering is happening in real-time, it is a nuisance to be managed. Baker, at 80 years old, is finally discarding the "politically correct" mask that the league demands, exposing the friction between individual morality and corporate survival.

Furthermore, the organization’s attempt to link its legacy to Jackie Robinson through Baker’s book is the height of cynical marketing. They celebrate the "desegregation" and "diversity" of the past because those battles are won and profitable to commemorate. Meanwhile, they actively suppress grassroots efforts to address the modern economic segregation of the unhoused. It is a grim reminder that in the eyes of the brass, the only "community" that matters is the one with a ticket in hand.

As the Astros chase a postseason spot in 2026, the optics will be carefully curated to ensure the "sign-stealing" era remains a distant memory. But the stench of discarded food and discarded people remains. This isn't just a Houston problem; it is the blueprint for how global entities navigate the human condition.
