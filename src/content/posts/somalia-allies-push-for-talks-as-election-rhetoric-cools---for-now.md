So, Somalia’s leaders are suddenly talking again. Fancy that. After months of shouting matches over how to run their next election - or the one after that, who’s counting - the big boys in Nairobi and Addis Ababa have apparently given President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud a stern talking-to. They’re worried the chaos could mess up their own security plans. Because God forbid Somalia’s mess spills over and inconveniences them.

President Ruto, always keen on looking like he’s sorted something, met Mohamud and promised to “strengthen bilateral relations.” Which probably means more handshakes and less actual progress on that border that’s been shut for a decade. The real prize for Nairobi, of course, is focusing on Al-Shabaab. Because fighting terrorists is much easier when your neighbour isn't busy imploding. A joint statement later confirmed that “security cooperation is central to their stability.” Translation: they want Somalia to sort itself out so Kenya can sleep at night.

Down in Addis, Abiy Ahmed echoed the same sentiment. “Seek consensus,” they probably said, “and for crying out loud, stop the inflammatory nonsense.” This week, the first crumb of actual dialogue appeared at the US Embassy, of all places. Mohamud, the man who’s been pushing for one-person-one-vote like it’s the cure for everything, actually met with opposition leader Sharif Sheikh Ahmed. Months of public spats, and now this? It’s almost enough to make you believe in miracles, or at least, in the power of international pressure.

The opposition, the Somali Future Council (SFC), has tossed out their own proposal. It’s a bit of a compromise: MPs get elected, then they elect the president. It’s a step back from the chaos of one-person-one-vote, but still a move towards direct elections. They’re even talking about clans and 4.5 power-sharing, because some things never change in Somalia. And paper ballots, not the fancy electronic stuff the government likes. Transparency, they call it. We call it another way to argue.

The government, however, is still clinging to its “one person, one vote” ideal, even though some federal states are threatening to go rogue. Jubbaland and Puntland, among others, are saying the conditions just aren't right for nationwide direct elections. It’s a familiar tune. Remember when Kenya’s passport system seemed to vet people better than our actual intelligence services? [Kenya’s Passport-Gate: When Your Bank Account Vets You Better Than the NIS](/posts/kenya-s-passport-gate--when-your-bank-account-vets-you-better-than-the-nis). This is Somalia, where the "right conditions" are as elusive as a guaranteed peace.

Now, all eyes are on the diplomats from the UN, EU, US, UK, and Türkiye. They're the ones who’ll be consulted next. Will they push for a real solution, or just another temporary fix? Given Somalia’s history, it’s more likely to be the latter. Don’t expect any sudden shifts. This is just the lull before the next storm.
