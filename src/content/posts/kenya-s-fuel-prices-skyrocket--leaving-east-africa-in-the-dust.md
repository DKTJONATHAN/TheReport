So, the May 2026 numbers are out, and surprise, surprise, Kenya is once again proving to be the region's premier destination for getting fleeced at the petrol station. While other countries are managing to keep a lid on energy costs, or at least make it look like they are, we’re here setting new records for how much we can charge for a litre of petrol. Rwanda has officially snatched the "most expensive petrol" crown, but don't get too comfortable, because we’re a very close second, and our prices are still climbing faster than a politician’s promises.

And it gets worse. For diesel, the fuel that actually keeps this whole country moving - the trucks, the matatus, the factories - Kenya is now officially the undisputed champion of pain. Prices have jumped like a panicked gazelle, leaving businesses struggling and the cost of everything else set to follow. This isn't just about filling up your car; this is about the price of ugali, the cost of getting goods to market, the overall sanity of our already stressed economy. It’s a vicious cycle, and guess who’s at the top of the food chain? Us.

The only "good" news, if you can even call it that, is our kerosene situation. While Tanzania is burning a hole in its citizens' pockets with sky-high kerosene prices, ours have remained stubbornly the same, and relatively low. This might suggest that a good chunk of us are still stuck using kerosene for cooking and lighting. Fancy that. Meanwhile, Ethiopia, bless their government-controlled hearts, is still managing to keep prices significantly lower across the board, proving that maybe, just maybe, there’s another way to do this.

But let's be real. These price hikes are a classic case of import dependence, currency fluctuations, and a taxation structure that seems designed to drain us dry. It's the same old story, just with a different flavour of misery each month. While we're out here grappling with these rising costs, one wonders if any of our leaders are even paying attention, or if they're too busy trying to figure out how to get their own Tamagotchi pets working. It’s a tough grind, and fuel is just another reminder of how much it costs to simply exist in this country.
