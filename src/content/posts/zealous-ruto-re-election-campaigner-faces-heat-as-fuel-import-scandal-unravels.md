Ruto's New Campaigner Gets Fried: Fuel Scandal Burns His Energy Dreams

Belinda Achieng's take on the unfolding drama where James Wandayi, the zealous Ruto re-election campaigner and Energy CS, finds himself caught in the crossfire of a messy fuel import scandal.

When your "Damascus moment" lands you in a hot seat, it's not divine intervention, it's just Nairobi politics. James Wandayi, fresh off the ODM boat and into President Ruto's Cabinet, was singing hosannas about a broad-based government and, of course, defending the Head of State with an intensity that would make a cult leader blush. His new gig as the Energy CS looked like a cushy landing, especially after all that fiery criticism of Kenya Kwanza. But as they say in this city, easy money rarely lasts.

Wandayi’s grand pronouncements about Kenya swimming in fuel, even as the Middle East war sent shockwaves globally, now sound like a bad joke. He kept assuring us, with a confidence usually reserved for con artists, that there was no crisis, no need for panic buying. He was the man with the comprehensive strategies, the contingency plans, the unwavering optimism. Meanwhile, the rest of the world was scrambling, rationing, and cutting taxes, but our man Wandayi? He had it all under control. Just like that circus we saw with the MozzartBet Cup, another spectacle for the masses.

But reality, as it always does, has a nasty habit of biting back. Suddenly, the burden of reassurance shifted from the enthusiastic Wandayi to his more grounded Treasury counterpart, John Mbadi. Mbadi, the realist, dropped the bombshell: Kenya has a mere 16 days of petrol. And then, just as quickly as Wandayi’s optimism evaporated, so did his senior officials. A late-night DCI sting operation nabbed Petroleum Principal Secretary Mohamed Liban, Kenya Pipeline MD Joe Sang, and EPRA DG Daniel Kiptoo, all linked to shady fuel imports and quality concerns. Talk about "comprehensive strategies."

Now, with Liban, Sang, and Kiptoo already gone - one way or another - the calls for Wandayi to take responsibility are getting louder. This isn't just about a few arrests; it's about a gaping hole in the government's fuel import scheme, a scheme that's currently more scandal than substance. The delicate political dance between Ruto and ODM, thanks to Wandayi’s defection, now risks a serious misstep. It’s a classic Nairobi play: rise fast, shine bright, and then get burnt when the actual work starts.

Wandayi, the career politician who has no qualms about campaigning for Ruto’s re-election while in office, seems to have confused explaining government policy with campaigning. He's also shown a rather shaky grasp of his portfolio, fumbling to explain the basics of oil production. It’s clear his primary talent lies not in managing complex energy issues, but in political maneuvering and singing the President's praises. Now, with his docket in chaos and his subordinates arrested, his fervent loyalty might be his undoing, leaving Kenyans to pay the price for his "Damascus moment."
