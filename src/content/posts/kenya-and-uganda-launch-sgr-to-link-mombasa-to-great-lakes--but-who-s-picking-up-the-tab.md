Kenya and Uganda Launch SGR to Link Mombasa to Great Lakes, But Who's Really Paying?

Another mega-project, more grand pronouncements about economic transformation, and the same old questions about where the money is coming from. Ruto and Museveni are cutting ribbons for a railway line that sounds like it's straight out of a development textbook, promising to turn landlocked countries into trade powerhouses. But let's be real, this SGR dream comes with a price tag that's already making our debt look like pocket change.

So, the Naivasha-Malaba-Kampala Standard Gauge Railway is a thing. Big deal. President Ruto, with his usual flair for the dramatic, broke ground, babbling about boosting trade and regional integration. It’s a $5 billion project, mind you, and the only thing clearer than the Kenyan sky after a downpour is the murkiness surrounding its financing. Museveni is expected to grace us with his presence later, probably to also talk about how this iron snake will make us all rich.

This SGR extension is supposedly part of some grand East African Railway Master Plan, aiming to replace our tired old meter-gauge lines with shiny new ones stretching all the way to the DRC. They say it’ll breathe life into the economy. Yeah, right. More likely, it'll breathe more debt into our already struggling coffers. Ruto is calling it a "game-changer," and perhaps it is, but for whom? Unlocking Kenya's potential as a commercial hub? Or just another avenue for Chinese loans and inflated contracts?

The contractor, China Communications Construction Company, the same folks who built the Nairobi-Naivasha stretch - a project that already had its fair share of financial wobbles - will be doing this part too. Ruto claims this will transform the SGR into a "two-way economic system," moving imports inland and exports outward. He’s also crowing about how it’ll reduce the wear and tear on our roads from those pesky trucks. Funny, considering how much those trucks are already keeping the garages in business. And let's not forget the nearly twice the international average transporters are paying per kilometre. It’s a shocker, but hardly surprising in this part of the world.

Uganda is busy building its own electric SGR, and the DRC has also pledged to get on board. Tanzania's already upgrading its Central Corridor with its own SGR. Everyone’s in a mad rush, trying to catch up. Meanwhile, the official numbers show cargo is the main moneymaker for the existing SGR, with freight volumes ticking up. Uganda remains the leading transit destination, gobbling up most of the throughput. But hold on, imports dominate transit traffic at a whopping 92.6 percent. So much for exporting our own goods.

Now for the juicy part: the money. Ruto conveniently fell short of disclosing how they plan to bankroll this colossal project. He mumbled something about "significant resources" and "billions of shillings" while "managing our debt prudently." Prudent debt management? In Kenya? That’s a good one. We heard whispers of a Ksh390 billion ($3 billion) bond backed by the Railway Development Levy Fund, and talk of tolling the new line to repay debt. But guess what? No bond has been issued, and the Chinese Exim Bank, which financed the first phase, apparently pulled out due to "uncertainties over the commercial viability" and Kenya's "growing indebtedness." Sounds about right.

Then there's the whole saga of the financing with China. Initially, they were going to fund 90 percent, but now it's down to 30 percent, with Kenya chipping in another 30 percent and the rest from "private investors." Kenya apparently had trouble raising its share, leading to talk of a Panda bond - which, surprise, surprise, hasn't materialized. This whole SGR saga feels like a never-ending telenovela, much like the endless drama surrounding political maneuvering, [Death, Choppers, and the High Cost of Political Saint-making](/posts/death--choppers--and-the-high-cost-of-political-saint-making).

Uganda, bless their hearts, has managed to secure $800 million from Saudi Arabia's Islamic Development Bank for their Kampala-Malaba stretch. They're also looking at syndicated loans from various international lenders. But here's the kicker: Uganda wants a European standard SGR, which means their electric trains won't play nice with Kenya's diesel-powered ones. They're talking about harmonizing systems, but let's be honest, this is just another bureaucratic hurdle. And to top it all off, Uganda now wants to link its SGR to Tanzania's network too. It's a race to the bottom, or perhaps a race to the debt trap.
