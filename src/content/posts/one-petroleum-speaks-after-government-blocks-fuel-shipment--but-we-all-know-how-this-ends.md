Company Speaks After Government Blocks Fuel Shipment, But We All Know How This Ends

This is just another Tuesday in Nairobi. One Petroleum Limited, a company whose name sounds like it’s trying too hard to be important, has finally decided to grace us with their statement after the government, in its infinite wisdom, decided their "emergency" fuel shipment wasn't quite emergency enough for our pockets. Apparently, they were one of four "successful bidders" for an emergency request. Emergency? Since when is fuel prices going up an emergency for anyone *but* us, the ones who have to pay for it?

They claim they’ve already “acted on government instructions” and the disputed cargo, which arrived on March 27, 2026, via some ship called MT Paloma, will not enter the Kenyan market. Oh, how noble. Meanwhile, Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi is out here spilling the beans, telling us this petrol was bought at a cool Ksh198,000 per metric tonne. That’s Ksh58,000 more than the government-to-government deal, which, let’s be honest, probably still costs an arm and a leg. This translates to a sweet Ksh14 hike per litre. Thanks for that, One Petroleum.

Wandayi, bless his heart, has directed One Petroleum to pull all their invoices and issue credit notes. He's also told Oil Marketing Companies not to pay or even touch this overpriced fuel. They need to "exit its product from Kenya as soon as possible." This whole charade sounds like a classic Kenyan drama where everyone plays their part to look good, while we're left footing the bill, one way or another. It’s no surprise we’ve seen similar “fuel scandals” before, like the one involving Liban, Sang, and Bargoria, who faced jail time. Another day, another corrupt Kenyan official.

The CS assures us the government will be "vigilant" and "safeguard stability, transparency, and accountability." We've heard it all before. It’s the same old song and dance. They’ll promise to protect us from "artificial shortages" and "unjustified price increases," but somehow, the prices always go up, and the shortages… well, those are just a matter of perspective, aren't they? This is Kenya, after all. We just have to hope this doesn't turn into another one of those situations where the "soft life" influencers are peddling their goods while the rest of us are stuck in the real world.
