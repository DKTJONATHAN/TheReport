SABOTAGE IN THE WEST: Salasya Says Sifuna’s Power Grab Could Shatter Opposition

The whispers from Mumias East MP Peter Salasya are less a warning and more a blunt assessment of the usual political theatre. He's pointing out the obvious: some folks are more interested in their own shine than in actually uniting the opposition for 2027. According to Salasya, this whole song and dance about Senator Edwin Sifuna dominating Western Kenya politics is a recipe for disaster, just like the time many in the region supported Raila but then Mudavadi split the vote. This isn't about strengthening the opposition; it's about some individuals playing their own game.

Salasya isn't just blowing hot air. He’s reminding us of the messy internal squabbles that have plagued Western Kenya before. Remember the drama between DAP-K's Jack Wamboka and Eugene Wamalwa? Apparently, Wamboka thought he’d be the party’s secretary-general, but Wamalwa wasn’t having it. The justification? Wamboka's role as chairman of the Public Investments Committee was seen as cozying up to the government, while DAP-K was supposed to be the opposition. This isn't unity; it's just ego clashes disguised as political strategy.

And it's not just them. Salasya throws in Governor George Natembeya and the ever-shifting loyalties of Boni Khalwale and Cleophas Malala. This is the same old story, folks. When leaders back different horses, the whole region ends up divided. We saw it in 2013 with Raila and Mudavadi, and again in 2022. The opposition can’t afford to trip over its own feet like this, especially when they’re supposed to be offering a credible alternative to the current government. It's like watching a bunch of street kids arguing over a dropped chapati while the real meal is on the table.

The MP throws a bone to Eugene Wamalwa, calling him an experienced leader who could actually be useful. But the real message is clear: stop the petty rivalries and focus on the bigger picture. This isn't about individual ambitions; it's about gaining public trust and presenting a united front. If these leaders keep prioritizing their personal agendas, they’ll just erode whatever little confidence the public has in them, and by extension, the entire region's political leadership. It's a classic case of the internal competition that can lead to a lot of wasted energy, much like the confusion around refurbished electronics being sold as new in places like Luthuli Avenue.

Ultimately, Salasya’s point is that the opposition’s success hinges on unity, discipline, and choosing leaders based on consensus, not ego. They need to stop acting like spoiled children fighting over toys and start acting like serious contenders. If they keep this up, 2027 will be another year of watching from the sidelines while the real players take the stage.
