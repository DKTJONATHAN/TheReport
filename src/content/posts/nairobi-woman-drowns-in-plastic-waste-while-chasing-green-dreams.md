So, Nzambi Matee, hotshot from the National Oil Corporation, decides her "heart wasn't in it." Right. Trading a secure, well-paying gig for a pile of plastic in her mother's backyard. Her family thought she'd lost it, and frankly, who can blame them? Apparently, the blueprint for sanity in Nairobi is now building paver blocks from discarded rubbish. It's a real "genius" move in a city where you're lucky if your rent doesn't double overnight.

She talks about leaving a mark, a legacy like Wangari Maathai. Please. Maathai dealt with trees, something tangible. This is physics applied to garbage. She thinks turning plastic into construction material is a way to escape the daily grind, to "do what she truly wanted." Most of us just want to get to Friday without someone breathing down our necks. She’s out here trying to reinvent the wheel, or in her case, the brick, from things that are literally trash.

Then comes the classic sob story. Banks, of course, told her to take a hike. "Banks are not in the business of funding innovation," she muses, "They are in the business of selling money." Brilliant observation. Who knew? She finally gets a leg up from an incubation program. Imagine needing a crash course in social entrepreneurship just to get your hands on a few coins. And don't even get me started on her nine-month "pregnancy" analogy for developing her paver blocks. Some of us are still waiting for our real pregnancies to deliver, let alone for a pile of plastic to mature.

Now she’s looking for "patient capital," like it's hiding under a matatu seat. She's complaining the government isn't stepping in, and impact investors are too busy counting their money to fund actual machinery. It’s a circular economy, alright - a circle of unmet expectations. She's talking about hundreds of millions for mass production, but right now she's employing 30 people full-time and 250 on temporary contracts. That’s the Kenyan dream, isn't it? A whole lot of hustle for a little bit of progress. This whole affair sounds like the kind of predictable, endless dread you'd expect from [‘Backrooms’ Trailer Drains Hope With Predictable Endless Dread](/posts/backrooms--trailer-drains-hope-with-predictable-endless-dread).

She wants investment in R&D grants, calling it the "seed that shifts us from being a consumer market to a productive one." Again, who knew? Turns out, innovation costs money. But hey, at least she's still at it, expanding "at her own pace using her own resources." Which, translated from Nairobi realism, means she'll keep chipping away until she either makes it or drowns in her own recycled mud. Let's just hope she doesn't expect a Nobel Prize for it.
