So, the Confederation of African Football (Caf) has dropped a report, and guess what? Our grand plans for Afcon 2027 are, as usual, a complete shambles. February 2026, and not a single one of our so-called top-tier stadiums meets the basic Category 4 requirements. It's a joke, really. We're talking about hosting a continental tournament, and our infrastructure is still in the "let's see how it goes" phase.

They mention Moi International Sports Centre, Kasarani, and Nyayo National Stadium - names that sound impressive but are apparently begging for a serious facelift. Kasarani needs a complete overhaul, from the pitch to the floodlights. Nyayo? Well, Caf’s basically told us to use it as a glorified training ground because it's old and has no real plan for renovation. Typical Kenyan efficiency, right?

Then there's Talanta. The new darling, the "flagship" stadium. Supposed to be built from scratch and align with CAF standards. Except, surprise, surprise, it's nowhere near finished. Deadlines are being pushed back faster than you can say "corruption." It’s supposed to be our big symbol, but it’s currently just a symbol of how we love to start things and then get stuck in the mud, much like some of the "investors" we've seen land in the cells recently. [The Terminal Trap: Another 'Investor' Lands in the Cells](/posts/the-terminal-trap--another--investor--lands-in-the-cells).

Caf is pointing out all the obvious flaws: spectator circulation, segregation, venue operations, team zones, media integration, lighting, backup power, safety. Basically, everything that makes a stadium functional and safe. They say upgrade works have started, but the "extensive scope" requires "sustained progress." Sustained progress. In Kenya. I'll believe it when I see it, and even then, I'll probably be skeptical.

Meanwhile, our Sports Cabinet Secretary is out there reassuring everyone that things are "on track" and they’ll meet financial obligations, like paying that Ksh 3.9 billion hosting fee. Sure, Salim, just like the roads were "on track" before the rains turned them into rivers. And to top it all off, Uganda is in the same boat. Apparently, none of their proposed stadiums are ready either. It’s almost comforting to know we’re not alone in our spectacular failure to get our act together.

So, while the government is busy talking tough and probably lining pockets, the reality is that our stadiums are a mess. We're chasing deadlines with a project that's far from complete, relying on flimsy promises and hoping for a miracle. It’s the same old story, just with a bigger price tag and more international embarrassment waiting to happen.
