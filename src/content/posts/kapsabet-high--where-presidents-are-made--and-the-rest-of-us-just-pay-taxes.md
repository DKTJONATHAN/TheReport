So, Kapsabet High School. Apparently, it's the place to be if you want to run Kenya. Forget merit, forget experience, just get yourself through those gates between 1925 and now, and boom, you're practically guaranteed a seat at the big table. It's like a factory for the country's movers and shakers, pumping out presidents, ministers, and whoever else decides they fancy a bit of power.

And who are these esteemed alumni? Well, none other than Daniel arap Moi, who graced its halls when it was just "Government African School, Kapsabet." He did his prefect duties, learned to be a teacher, and then spent the next few decades running the show. Then there's William Ruto, our current "Hustler-in-Chief," who popped in for his A-Levels. Seems a good education, or perhaps just the right connections, is all it takes to go from MP to Deputy President to the top job. It's almost like the system is designed for them.

We've also got the late Nicholas Biwott, the "Total Man," who, surprise, surprise, was a powerful minister under Moi. And Kipruto arap Kirwa, who went from agricultural engineering to Minister for Agriculture. And Stephen Sang, who went from Kapsabet to law school and then straight into being a Senator and Governor. It’s a well-trodden path, isn’t it? This whole "Kapsabet pedigree" thing seems to be the express lane to power, while the rest of us are left dealing with the consequences.

Even the younger crop are lining up. Samson Cherargei, who chaired an SDA group - very spiritual - is now a Senator. And Julius Yego, the "YouTube Man," who apparently learned javelin from online videos. At least one of them is an athlete, which is a refreshing change. But still, the same school, the same ambitions. It’s a cycle that repeats itself, and frankly, it’s exhausting.

Then there are the public servants like Dr. Belio Kipsang, who’s been busy with education reforms, including that whole CBC saga, and Dr. Chris Kiptoo, who’s currently at the National Treasury. So, it’s not just politicians; it’s the technocrats too. All educated, all part of the system, all seemingly coming from this one magical school. It makes you wonder if all those promises about opportunities for everyone are just hot air, especially when you consider stories like [Ruto Cedes Kenya Pipeline Control to Uganda's Museveni? Standardmedia.co.ke Reports Shocking Deal].

Look, it’s great that some people get a good education and achieve success. But when an entire generation of leaders, and the people shaping our country’s future, all seem to emerge from the same elite institution, it raises a few uncomfortable questions. Is this meritocracy, or is it just a very well-oiled machine for perpetuating the same old guard? Don't hold your breath for an honest answer.
