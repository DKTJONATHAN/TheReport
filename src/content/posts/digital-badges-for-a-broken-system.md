So, the DCI has finally discovered the internet. They have launched the INTERPOL Policing Capabilities Foundation Course, a fancy name for teaching our boys in blue how to use a database without accidentally deleting the whole thing. Supported by the UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office and something called Project Soteria, it is the latest attempt to make our law enforcement look like a scene from CSI-Nairobi. They say it is about strengthening capacity to fight transnational crime, but most of us would just be happy if they could find a stolen phone without asking for fuel money first.

The plan is to give officers access to global policing tools and secure communication platforms. It sounds impressive on a press release, but let us be real. We are talking about an agency where files still mysteriously go missing when a big fish is involved. They want to track suspects across borders while some suspects are busy walking into local chemists and ordering customers out like they own the place. The disconnect between these high-level investigative systems and the chaos on the streets of Nairobi is wide enough to fit a cargo ship through.

This whole obsession with global standards and foreign-led training fits perfectly into the narrative of [The Hegemon’s Shadow: Decoding the Rise of the American World Police and Africa’s Path to Sovereignty](/posts/the-hegemon-s-shadow-decoding-the-rise-of-the-american-world-police-and-africa-s-path-to-sovereignty) where we let outsiders define how we keep our own peace. We are importing foreign projects to handle sexual and gender-based violence, as if the problem is a lack of international databases and not a lack of basic witness protection and a functional justice system that does not take ten years to move a single file.

While the DCI is busy modernizing and thanking Inspector General Douglas Kanja for this milestone, the rest of the country is drowning in the usual noise. We have flu cases rising, the Ministry of Health is issuing warnings that nobody heeds, and CS Duale is talking about billions in teacher medical claims. It is all one big circus. They tell us that criminals will not be able to evade justice by crossing borders, but in this city, you only need to cross the right palm with enough cash to make justice look the other way.

In the end, it is just more capacity building while the foundation is crumbling. We can have all the INTERPOL tools in the world, but as long as meritocracy is a foreign concept, these courses are just expensive tea parties for the top brass. We will believe the modernization when the suspects who cause chaos in our neighborhoods actually see the inside of a cell without a technical hitch occurring in the prosecution's laptop. Stay cynical, Nairobi; it is the only way to survive the headlines.
