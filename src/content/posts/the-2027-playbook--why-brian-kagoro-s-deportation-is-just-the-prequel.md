Nairobi is currently undergoing its usual pre-re-election makeover, and by makeover, I mean the state is busy scrubbing the streets of anyone who asks too many questions. The latest victim of this administrative temper tantrum is Brian Kagoro, the Open Society Foundations Director who was tossed out of the country like yesterday's garbage. While the Law Society of Kenya and twenty other human rights groups are busy writing long letters about constitutionalism, the rest of us know exactly what time it is. This is not about immigration laws; it is about clearing the pitch before the 2027 General Elections.

The government's favorite trick is pretending that every local grievance is actually a foreign conspiracy. If you are hungry, it is foreign interference. If you want better roads, it is a plot. By deporting Kagoro, they are trying to scare off the people who fund the watchdogs. It is a classic move to frame legitimate advocacy as a threat to national security, which is funny considering the real threat to national security is usually the people in the high-end offices. This level of institutional gaslighting is nothing new; we have seen this [Government Ghosting: Why the Cabinet’s Femicide Memo is Giving Major Toxic Ex Vibes](/posts/government-ghosting--why-the-cabinet-s-femicide-memo-is-giving-major-toxic-ex-vibes) where the state makes big promises in public while stabbing its citizens in the back the moment the cameras stop flashing.

Our Constitution, specifically Articles 36 and 27, supposedly protects the right to seek and use resources for human rights work. But in this city, a piece of paper is only as strong as the person holding the baton. The Police Reforms Working Group is rightfully alarmed by the lack of judicial oversight, but let's be real, judicial oversight is just an inconvenient speed bump for an executive branch that wants a quick exit. Summary deportation is the ultimate delete button for people who make the state uncomfortable, and they are pressing it with zero shame and even less evidence.

Kenya used to brag about being the regional hub for civic engagement, but now we are just a hub for surveillance and paranoia. The authorities are terrified of youth-led organizing and peaceful protests, so they target the facilitators and the money. By painting cross-border solidarity as a crime, they hope to isolate us. If nobody can help us, nobody can hear us scream when the 2027 campaign trail starts getting messy. It is a calculated, cold-blooded strategy to ensure that the only narrative that survives is the one written by the state.

At the end of the day, the President can go to as many international forums as he wants and talk about accountability until his voice goes hoarse. Back home, the reality is a boot on the neck of anyone who tries to hold him to those words. We are entering a season of heightened hostility where being a human rights defender is treated like a felony. If you thought the last few years were rough, hold onto your hats. The deportation of Kagoro is just the opening act for a much darker show scheduled for 2027.
