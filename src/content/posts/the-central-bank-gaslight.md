The debate over single versus dual mandates is nothing more than a linguistic distraction for the masses. Whether a central bank claims to prioritize employment or price stability is irrelevant when the divine coincidence of economic theory fails. In practice, the result is always a tightening of the screws on the working class to preserve the sanctity of the currency. The poor are told that their suffering is a necessary sacrifice to prevent the perils of the 1970s, as if the current erosion of institutional trust is somehow less dangerous than a decade of stagflation.

This is the same hallucination engine we see in Silicon Valley, where the death of truth is packaged as progress. Central banks are now pivoting to artificial intelligence as the ultimate magic wand to solve the demographic collapse they cannot fix with interest rates. They hope that productivity gains from algorithms will mask the structural rot of aging populations and restricted migration. It is a gamble on a technology that is yet to prove it can do anything other than generate convincing lies and consume massive amounts of energy.

Ultimately, the modern consensus mentioned by these technocrats is a house of cards built on a flat Phillips curve that they themselves admit they misunderstood. They entered the pandemic with policy settings that were historically accommodative, fueling the very fire they now claim to be extinguishing. The international impact is clear: a global south crushed by the weight of a soaring dollar and a global north where the vulnerable are perpetually one supply-side shock away from ruin.

The scars of the last three years are not just visible; they are infected. As fiscal space narrows and political frustration boils over, the central bank promise of stability sounds increasingly like a threat. They are no longer the guardians of the economy; they are the janitors of a failing system, desperately trying to sweep the debris of a broken social contract under a rug of expert analyses and forward-looking assessments.
