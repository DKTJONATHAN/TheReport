In Nairobi, if you aren't shouting, you aren't winning. Edwin Sifuna figured this out early. He traded the quiet dignity of a Kakamega upbringing for the loud, dusty arenas of Orange Democratic Movement politics. He calls it public service, but we know a good career move when we see one. A University of Nairobi degree and a few years in private practice were just the preamble for his real job: being the loudest voice in a room full of people trying to out-shout each other.

His 2017 loss to Johnson Sakaja should have been a warning, but in the world of Kenyan opposition, failing is just a step toward a better appointment. Becoming the Secretary-General of ODM at 36 wasn't about youth empowerment- it was about finding an attack dog who could cite the constitution while baring his teeth. He turned his legal training into a weapon, framing every partisan squabble as a battle for institutional accountability while the rest of us just wondered when the rent would finally go down.

The recent drama in Mombasa, where they tried to kick him out for indiscipline, is just another chapter in the long history of political backstabbing. They say he defied the party's move toward a broad-based government, which is just code for he did not get the memo on whose turn it is to eat. It feels like another version of [The Boardroom Coup: How You Got Scammed by a Political Prenup](/posts/the-boardroom-coup--how-you-got-scammed-by-a-political-prenup) where the only thing that matters is who keeps the keys to the office. Now he is running to the Political Parties Disputes Tribunal, proving that once a lawyer, always a person who knows how to stay relevant through paperwork.

Even his personal life is packaged for the public. He talks about his marriage and his more-educated wife like a man who knows that a family man image sells better than a combative politician one. It is a calculated move to show he is grounded, but in this city, being grounded usually just means you have a solid place to hide when the teargas starts flying. His wife might laugh off the social media trolls, but the rest of us are watching the 2027 horizon with the same old weary eyes.

At 43, Sifuna is at a crossroads. He can either become a statesman or remain a party tactician who specializes in unconstitutional protests and court injunctions. Whether he actually believes in the youth participation he preaches or is just using it as a ladder is a question for the voters. For now, he is just another visible figure in a system that loves a good show, and Edwin Sifuna is, if nothing else, a very talented performer.
