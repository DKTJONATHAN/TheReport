MIDDLE EAST SHENANIGANS DEEPEN EAST AFRICAN HUNGER

This Middle East drama, which has nothing to do with us, is now conveniently serving up more food shortages for East Africa. Thanks to disrupted fertilizer, fuel, and shipping schedules, especially through that vital Strait of Hormuz, our already shaky agricultural future is looking even grimmer.

The Iran-Israel-US war, barely six weeks old, has already got farmers here rethinking their planting. Charity group Mercy Corps is out here telling us that over 30 percent of global urea fertilizer exports pass through that volatile region. Now, with shortages hitting and prices skyrocketing by a ridiculous 68 percent, our crop yields for 2026-2027 are effectively doomed. Reduced fertilizer use, insane input costs, and shipping delays are already strangling agricultural production in places like Somalia and Ethiopia. Melaku Yirga from Mercy Corps Africa basically said it: "The food security consequences of this war are already written into harvests that have not yet been planted." No kidding. These countries are nowhere near the conflict, but we’re getting hit hard through the markets, not borders. It’s going to be rough.

The impact is arriving through markets, not borders, and the consequences will be severe. Across Somalia, Sudan, Pakistan, Ethiopia, and Myanmar, over 60 million people are already desperate for aid, and response plans are chronically underfunded. The UN's FAO chimed in, warning that disruptions lasting more than 40 days mess with farmer behavior, and guess what? That threshold was crossed in early April. So, even if peace breaks out tomorrow, the damage to global food supply is pretty much locked in. Despite some flimsy ceasefire announcements, trade isn't exactly flowing normally. The US blockade on Iranian ports has just added more uncertainty for commercial traffic in the Strait of Hormuz, forcing humanitarian shipments to detour around Africa, adding weeks to delivery times. It’s a masterclass in making things worse.

Here in Kenya, our fuel prices just took a record jump. Diesel is up, even with a fuel tax cut, because of this Middle East mess pushing up global oil prices. Other countries like South Africa, Zambia, Namibia, and Ghana are fiddling with fuel levies, while South Sudan is rationing electricity and Ethiopia is playing favorites with sectors. In Somalia, fuel spikes have doubled the cost of water in drought-stricken areas. The World Food Programme is predicting an extra 45 million people could be pushed into acute hunger worldwide. Mercy Corps pointed out that global fertilizer prices have shot up during critical planting periods, and fuel prices have gone through the roof, driving up transport and water costs. It’s a vicious cycle, and we’re at the bottom of it.

The energy regulator here slapped a 40 shilling increase on diesel and a 28 shilling rise on petrol, bringing them to a cool 206 shillings a litre. This is all because of higher global oil and shipping costs, even though the government supposedly cut VAT. These prices are fixed until May 14th, but who are we kidding? They're not dropping anytime soon. The increased fuel prices mean higher transport costs across Kenya, which then spirals into education, health, and of course, food security. Commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is down by over 90 percent, completely messing with our agricultural supply chains. It’s a mess, and we’re the ones paying for it.
