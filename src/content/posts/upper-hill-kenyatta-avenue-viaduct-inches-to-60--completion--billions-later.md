The Office of the Government Spokesman is busy patting itself on the back, announcing Nairobi's Sh3 billion Upper Hill-Kenyatta Avenue Viaduct has finally limped to 60 percent completion. A "milestone," they call it. For who, exactly? For the rest of us stuck in traffic for years, it's just another reminder of how slowly things move when there are so many pockets to fill. This "flagship" project near All Saints Cathedral is "steadily taking shape," they claim, as if we haven't been watching the snail's pace since September 2020.

They're promising this elevated concrete monster will "significantly improve access to Nairobi's CBD and help ease traffic congestion." We've heard that one before, haven't we? Every new road project comes with the same tired script, always followed by new bottlenecks popping up elsewhere. It's like a politician promising to 'serve' while eyeing a fat salary. This grand design, connecting the CBD to Upper Hill, is supposed to let us "bypass some of the city's busiest intersections." More likely, it'll just funnel us into a new, equally frustrating jam, just at a higher elevation.

Let's not forget the "setbacks." That's the polite government speak for "works stalled for years due to payment disputes involving the contractor," China Road and Bridge Corporation (CRBC). While they haggled over who gets what cut of the Sh3 billion, Nairobians were left to choke on dust and endure endless, prolonged traffic. The irony isn't lost that this whole stretch includes the area around Integrity Centre, yet integrity seems to have been the first casualty.

Suddenly, in July 2025, money was "secured" under President William Ruto's administration, and construction magically resumed. KURA, the Kenya Urban Roads Authority, blames the years of delay on "technical complexity" and the "precision engineering" required for those massive support columns. Funny how technical complexity only becomes an issue after funds dry up and public outcry reaches a fever pitch. Where was this foresight and precision engineering in 2020?

So, they're "targeting" completion before the end of 2026. Another target date for us to mark on our calendars, right next to all the others that have come and gone. This viaduct is just one piece of a "broader strategy to modernise Nairobi's transport network," they say. But for the cynical realist, it's simply another cycle of grand pronouncements, inflated budgets, endless delays, and promises that only half-materialize, leaving us with slightly different traffic problems and a much lighter national wallet.
