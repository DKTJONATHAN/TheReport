The long-anticipated arrival of Virtual Boy titles on the Nintendo Switch Online (NSO) Expansion Pack tier has sparked renewed interest in Nintendo's distinctive, if historically challenging, console. While official accessories, including a dedicated $100 viewer or a $25 cardboard alternative, are available, many subscribers are seeking clarity on alternative methods to experience these classic games, particularly given the free download of the application for Expansion Pack members.

Initial experiences with the Virtual Boy NSO application reveal several key insights for players without the official peripherals. This report offers a preliminary overview, addressing common inquiries about accessibility and playability ahead of a full review and comparison with the original hardware.

## Labo VR Goggles: An Unofficial Solution

Contrary to Nintendo's official silence on the matter, existing Nintendo Labo VR goggles offer a functional, albeit unofficial, avenue for experiencing Virtual Boy games. A standard Switch 1 or Switch OLED Model console fits comfortably within the Labo peripheral, allowing for the intended 3D visuals. However, the larger Switch 2 console proves too cumbersome for the cardboard viewer without significant modification.

While Nintendo has reportedly walked back previous employee confirmations regarding official Labo support-likely due to the peripheral's limited availability and lack of updated disclaimers-the physical compatibility and software display remain as expected. Extended play sessions with the Labo goggles, much like with the original Virtual Boy, may not offer peak comfort, but the functionality is present.

## Comfort and Session Length Considerations

The comfort level for Virtual Boy games, even with peripherals, remains a point of discussion. The experience is generally not ideal for prolonged sessions, mirroring the original hardware's limitations when staring at a monochrome red screen through goggles. For the brief, novelty-driven play sessions most users will undertake, the setup is deemed acceptable. Creative solutions, such as detaching Joy-Cons and propping the Switch for comfort, could enhance the experience.

Adapting existing Labo VR goggles for a Switch 2 is technically feasible with modifications, such as cutting the cardboard. It is important to note that Labo VR was originally designed for the smaller screen of the non-OLED Switch 1, so Nintendo would likely not officially endorse its use with the OLED or Switch 2. Such adaptations are considered suboptimal but are possible for those willing to undertake the effort.

## Docked Mode and Screen Size Limitations

A significant limitation of the Virtual Boy NSO application is the complete absence of a docked mode. The Switch console will display a message indicating that the game is exclusively for handheld play, preventing any output to a television. This restriction, while intended to preserve the 3D experience, is notable given the option of displaying the twin images on a larger screen even without a 3D effect.

Furthermore, playing without any goggles highlights the minuscule screen size. On a Switch 1, the image cannot exceed 1.0x its default size, while a Switch 2 can expand it to 1.3x. Even at this enlarged setting, the individual images are approximately 28 x 45mm (1.1 x 1.8 inches), akin to postage stamps. Perceiving the intended 3D effect or comfortable play is challenging at these dimensions, rendering goggle-free play largely impractical for extended periods.

## In-Game Adjustments and Future Updates

The Virtual Boy app includes several adjustable settings to enhance the player experience. Users can modify the interpupillary distance (IPD), which adjusts the spacing between the two displayed images, across a range from -20 to +20. Image size can also be altered, though anything below 1.0x on Switch 1 significantly degrades resolution, and only Switch 2 can increase the size beyond 1.0x. These adjustments are accessible at any time during gameplay by holding down the right stick.

Nintendo has also announced a future update, slated for "later this year," that will introduce the ability to change the hue of the rendered visuals, moving beyond the default red to other color options. This flexibility may offer a new dimension to the classic monochrome experience.

## Launch Titles and Early Recommendations

As of February 17, 2026, seven Virtual Boy titles are playable on the NSO Expansion Pack. The current lineup includes classic games that capture the essence of the original console. Among the initial offerings, *Wario Land* stands out as a top recommendation for its engaging gameplay. The Virtual Boy, a device renowned for its unique, if polarizing, monochrome red visuals and head-mounted display, presents a fascinating case study in retro gaming preservation. Its re-release on Switch Online, complete with optional, dedicated accessories, prompts questions about how companies navigate the reintroduction of historically challenging products, sometimes akin to efforts to reshape narratives around [Old Radios, New Lies: KBC’s AI PR Stunt Won’t Save a Sinking Ship](/posts/old-radios--new-lies--kbc-s-ai-pr-stunt-won-t-save-a-sinking-ship) in the digital age.

The full roster of available titles is part of the broader NSO retro games guide, encompassing NES, SNES, GB(C), N64, GBA, Genesis/MD, Virtual Boy, and GC 'Nintendo Classics'. Further analysis and community feedback on the launch batch, as well as a hardware review of the new Switch-friendly facsimile, are anticipated.

Players are encouraged to share their experiences with non-official peripherals and workarounds as the Virtual Boy NSO app continues to evolve.
