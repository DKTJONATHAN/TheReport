President William Ruto has finally put his pen to paper, signing the Finance Bill 2026 into law. And just like clockwork, he’s trotted out the usual script: 'no new burdens on ordinary Kenyans,' 'enhanced fairness,' 'strengthening compliance.' It's a familiar tune, isn't it? One we've heard countless times as our pockets mysteriously shrink.

He spoke of 'extensive public engagement' and 'digital platforms.' As if typing a comment into a government portal truly translates to our concerns being heard, let alone acted upon. The narrative always circles back to 'closing loopholes' and 'ensuring everyone pays what's due.' Funny how those loopholes seem to exist for the well-connected until it's time to squeeze the rest of us.

Now, with this 'legal framework and resources,' we're promised jobs, livelihoods, and investment under the grand Bottom-Up Economic Transformation Agenda. We've been promised 'millions' and 'development' for decades, yet the script remains the same, just with different actors. Speaking of scripts, Ruto also vehemently denied proposed taxes on freehold land, mitumba, and bottled water, labelling it 'propaganda.' One has to wonder, then, why the public was so fiercely debating these very things if they were never even 'proposed'? It feels like another round of [Mangoes, Millions, and the Same Old Script](/posts/mangoes--millions--and-the-same-old-script), doesn't it?

The proof, as always, is in the pudding - or in this case, the parliamentary vote. 122 votes in favour against 40 nays. The government's 'numerical strength' carried the day, as expected. Even the drama of impeached Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua directing his allies to reject the Bill felt less like a principled stand and more like political theatre. When has 'opposition' truly stopped anything major here?

So, the Finance Bill 2026 is officially law, set to fund a Sh4.8 trillion budget. They say it’s about 'equity' and 'compliance,' not 'new burdens.' But for those of us on the ground, the only thing that’s truly equitable is how evenly the burden of these 'non-taxes' will be spread among the struggling. The ink is dry, and soon enough, so will be our wallets.
