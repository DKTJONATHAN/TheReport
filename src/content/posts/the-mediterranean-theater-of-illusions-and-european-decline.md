Behind the handshakes and the exchange of bicycles and athletic shoes, the numbers tell a story of systemic failure. While Greek officials wait for polls to see if PR stunts can recover lost political capital, the broader European project is rotting from within. Since 2008, the US economy has grown by 87 percent while Europe has limped along at 13.5 percent. This is not a temporary slump; it is a structural death spiral. Capital is fleeing to the US because Europe offers nothing but bureaucracy and high energy costs. The Banking Union remains a dream in a drawer while 300 billion euros leave the continent every year.

The energy sector provides the most damning evidence of this decline. PPC's massive capital increase is being hailed as a triumph of institutional confidence, yet it occurs in a vacuum where European electricity costs are 2.5 times higher than those in China or the US. Industry leaders are finally admitting what realists have known for years: Europe lost the battery race to China and is now losing the industrial race to its own regulations. Buying on the lowest price while demanding high standards has decimated the domestic manufacturing base. When it is more profitable to produce outside Europe and sell within it, the game is already over.

Even the crown jewels of Greek tourism, Mykonos and Santorini, are being sacrificed at the altar of short-term liquidity. The new Tourism Spatial Plan aims to unlock 2 billion euros in investment for areas that are already choking on their own success. It is a desperate grab for cash in a country that is moving away from the periphery but has yet to find a solid center. As the elite dine in the Presidential Mansion and exchange Gallic courtesies, the underlying structural issues-defense dependency, energy insolvency, and capital flight-remain unaddressed. Scrutiny is high, but as seen in previous cases, the fall from grace for those at the top is often long and painful.
