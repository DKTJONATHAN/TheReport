So, summer's once again being branded as "sci-fi season." How original. Andrew Webster, the entertainment editor who's apparently written more stories than most people have watched television, churns out the same predictable guide year after year. The supposed reason for this is a mystery, but let's be clear: it's not about a burgeoning cultural fascination. It's about filling the summer void with something that *sounds* exciting, a Pavlovian bell for consumers conditioned to expect something new every few months.

This year, the schedule is supposedly "nearly as full in the spring," which translates to "more stuff coming out earlier so we can get you hooked before you realize how little of it matters." We're told shows like _For All Mankind_ and _From_ are already halfway through their runs. Riveting. And of course, Apple TV is "heavily represented." Because why wouldn't the company with more money than sense push its own predictable fare?

Let's glance at the supposed highlights. _The Boroughs_ on Netflix, from the Duffer brothers who apparently can't escape their _Stranger Things_ shadow. They're serving up a retirement community with an extraterrestrial mystery. Sounds like a desperate attempt to recapture lightning in a bottle, relying on a cast of venerable actors to lend some gravitas to what will likely be another formulaic genre exercise. The premise is "great," apparently. It's a retirement home. The real mystery is why anyone still falls for this predictable narrative bait.

Then there's _Star City_ on Apple TV, a spinoff of _For All Mankind_. We're told _For All Mankind_ will "wrap up soon," which is code for "we're milking this franchise until it's drier than a Martian desert." The spinoff promises to "take things back to the beginning." So, more rehashing of familiar territory, packaged as something fresh.

This isn't a cultural phenomenon; it's a meticulously crafted illusion. Streaming services, desperate to justify their ever-increasing subscription costs and attract new eyeballs in an oversaturated market, churn out content with the predictable rhythm of a factory. "Sci-fi season" is just another marketing campaign, a convenient label to bundle a wave of often derivative, ultimately forgettable programming. Don't be fooled by the hype. Most of this will be lost in the digital ether before the leaves even change.
