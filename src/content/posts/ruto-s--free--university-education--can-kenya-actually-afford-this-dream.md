So, our President, the ever-optimistic William Ruto, has dropped a bombshell: the government will now apparently cover *all* higher education costs for students placed by KUCCPS. He’s waxing lyrical about how "it will not matter the background of any child in Kenya, it will matter how good they are." Sounds lovely, doesn't it? Like a fairy tale where everyone gets a pony and a degree without breaking a sweat. But let's be real, Nairobi is not a fairy tale, it's a place where promises often cost more than we can afford.

The problem, as always, is the money. Billions, the article says, billions of shillings needed for this grand plan. And where does that money come from? Hint: it’s not from thin air, and it’s certainly not from the pockets of those who actually benefit most. This is happening at a time when Kenya is already doing the tightrope walk with fiscal constraints. Remember when the National Treasury CS himself said they can only "heavily subsidise"? Funny how policies can shift faster than a matatu driver trying to beat traffic.

This whole "universal funding" thing needs parliamentary approval, which means more drama, more committees, and more hot air. But let’s look at the numbers they themselves provided. For the 2026-27 academic year, fully funding the university students could clock in at around Sh30.6 billion annually. Add the TVET students, and you're looking at another Sh6.2 billion. That’s a cool Sh36.8 billion, folks. That's over 22% of the *current* higher education budget, which, by the way, is already facing "persistent funding gaps." So, either they magically find this extra cash, or other crucial sectors like health and infrastructure are going to take a serious hit. It's the classic government shell game.

And what happens when you flood the system with "free" money? Quality. Or rather, a lack of it. Without a corresponding investment in infrastructure, lecture halls will become sardine cans, labs will be overwhelmed, and the "quality of education" will go the way of most Kenyan road projects - a distant memory. Plus, removing household contributions means the long-term burden shifts entirely onto us, the taxpayers, year after year. It’s like trying to fix a leaking roof by replacing a few tiles while ignoring the gaping hole above. We saw a bit of this before with Safaricom tying up internet for "affordable" houses, but even that came with its own set of bills, didn't it? (/posts/safaricom-ties-up-internet-for--affordable--houses---hope-you-can-afford-the-bills-though)

Now, the universities and TVETs might get some predictable funding, ending the perennial strikes and pending bills. That's the supposed silver lining. But let's not get too excited. This sounds like a band-aid on a bullet wound. The government has a habit of making grand pronouncements that sound great on paper but crumble under the weight of reality. So, while you enjoy the sweet sound of "free" education, remember who's ultimately picking up the tab. It’s never truly free, is it?
