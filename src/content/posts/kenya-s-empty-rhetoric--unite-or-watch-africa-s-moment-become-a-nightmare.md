So, I dragged myself to Kwale for this "Mashariki Cooperation Conference 2026." Theme? "Emerging Geopolitical Dynamics and Africa’s Security Architecture." Sounds grand, doesn't it? My father’s advice, “know what matters,” usually means avoiding these fluffy gatherings. But I figured, maybe, just maybe, some actual signals would emerge from the usual noise. Spoiler alert: it was mostly noise, laced with the predictable self-congratulation of those who thrive on talking.

The headline screams, "Kenya must end the bickering, unite with purpose, and secure Africa's moment before it turns into risk." Oh, really? Groundbreaking. It’s the same tired song and dance. We hear about unity and purpose when it's convenient, usually right after a few of them have shared their expensive lunches and swapped business cards. Meanwhile, the actual challenges, the ones that make people’s lives miserable, are left to fester. It reminds me a bit of how we’re bombarded with AI-generated nonsense online, ignoring the actual consequences, like the [dark underbelly of viral AI fruit videos](/posts/the-dark-underbelly-of-viral-ai-fruit-videos--predictable-misogyny-and-mindless-consumption).

These conferences are where power players convene to discuss how to *manage* Africa’s “moment,” not necessarily how to *seize* it for the ordinary man and woman. They talk about security architectures, but whose security? And at what cost? It’s easy to pontificate about geopolitical shifts when you’re not the one dodging the fallout of instability or wondering where your next meal is coming from. The focus is always on grand strategies, on appearing relevant on the global stage, not on the tangible needs of the people.

And the call for unity? It’s rich, coming from a political class that’s perpetually divided by petty squabbles and personal ambitions. They preach unity while actively fostering division, all for their own gain. They want Africa’s moment, but only if it’s a moment that benefits their wallets and their power. The constant bickering isn't just a nuisance; it's a deliberate strategy that paralyzes progress.

So, here we are, another weekend gone, another conference concluded. We’re told to subscribe to newsletters, pick our favourite topics for tailor-made homepages, and access journalism with "depth, courage, and independent stories." But what we really need is action, not just words. We need leaders who understand that "Africa's moment" is made of the everyday struggles and aspirations of its people, not just the geopolitical chess games played in air-conditioned rooms. Until then, it’s just more bickering, more noise, and a lot of wasted opportunity.
