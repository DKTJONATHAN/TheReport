Welcome to the 254, where the only thing cheaper than the lives of citizens is the price of a diplomatic favor. We are out here struggling to renew our national IDs and waiting months for a simple booklet, but if you are Algoney Hamdan Daglo, a sanctioned war-financier for Sudan’s RSF, the government rolls out the red carpet and a fresh passport. It turns out that while we were being told to "Buy Kenya, Build Kenya," someone at the top was busy selling the country's reputation to the highest bidder in Khartoum.

This is not just a little mistake at the immigration desk. The US Office of Foreign Assets Control literally read out the passport number AK1586127 like a receipt for a bad deal. This man is linked to ethnic killings and torture in El-Fasher, but here in Nairobi, we seemingly see him as a VIP. It is the classic Kenyan move: talk about regional peace in the morning and sign off on a warlord's travel documents by lunch. We are officially a haven for anyone with enough cash to bypass a background check.

While the government is busy performing for the masses with distractions like the [MozzartBet Cup: Another Circus for the Hungry to Watch the Broke](/posts/mozzartbet-cup--another-circus-for-the-hungry-to-watch-the-broke), the real damage is happening in the backrooms. We are supposedly fighting to get off the FATF "grey list" by May, but giving passports to genocidal financiers is like trying to put out a fire with petrol. The international financial watchdogs are not blind; they see a country that is acting more like a laundromat for dirty money than a serious regional hub.

President Ruto's "controversial alliance" with the RSF is finally catching up with us. The Human Rights Foundation is shouting from the rooftops, and Western governments are sharpening their sanction pens. We like to pretend we are the mediators of the continent - the neutral ground where everyone meets for tea and expensive photo-ops. In reality, we are just a convenient pit stop for people who need to hide their blood-soaked billions from international scrutiny.

The cost of this greed will not be paid by the people in high offices who signed those papers. It will be paid by the ordinary Kenyan who will find their bank transfers blocked and their own passports treated like trash at every international border. We are a nation that has traded its soul for a few pieces of silver, and now the world is coming to collect. If you thought the economy was a mess now, wait until the international community decides we are officially a rogue state.
