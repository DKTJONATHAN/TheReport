So, Financial Fortune, bless their optimistic hearts, are telling us it's time to make "climate risk insurance" part of Kenya's economic infrastructure. This comes after our capital city decided to reenact the Red Sea parting with its own drainage system. Rivers overflowed, cars became submarines, and nearly 70 people got a permanent ticket out of here. They call it "climate volatility" and "urbanisation." I call it another Tuesday in Nairobi, amplified.

They lament the economic cost, the billions of shillings lost, the fact that most of us are uninsured and left to dry - or drown, as it were. The writer, a Mr. Minet Risk Solutions associate, wants us to see insurance not as a niche product, but as "economic infrastructure." You know, like roads that mysteriously get potholes faster than they get paved, or electricity that flickers more than a cheap candle. This is the same old song and dance: problems arise, blame the weather, then pitch a "solution" that usually benefits someone else more than us. It reminds me of how [The Nairobi Hustle is Just a Digital Graveyard](/posts/the-nairobi-hustle-is-just-a-digital-graveyard), full of promises that never quite deliver.

They're touting "parametric insurance," which sounds fancy enough to be expensive and complicated. It triggers payouts based on things like rainfall levels. Apparently, this speed can be the difference between a small business reopening or staying shuttered for months. For households, it's a lifeline. Except, who's actually going to pay out? And when? Given our track record with getting things done efficiently, I'm not holding my breath. The mention of M-Pesa is supposed to be reassuring, but we all know how that goes when the powers that be decide to dip their hands in.

The article harps on "policy alignment," "regulatory frameworks," and "county governments" playing a "catalytic role." It's a whole lot of jargon designed to sound proactive. Meanwhile, the private sector is told to see climate exposure as a "balance sheet issue." Translation: they need to start caring about more than just their profit margins when the sky decides to open up. This is all well and good in theory, but the reality on the ground is that most Kenyans are still figuring out how to afford dinner, let alone an insurance policy that might, *might*, pay out someday.

They talk about "closing this gap" requiring more than "product innovation" but "building trust." Ah, trust. That elusive commodity in this country, particularly when it comes to money and official promises. They say "visible, reliable payouts when crises occur" build confidence. You know what else builds confidence? Not having your house washed away in the first place because the drainage system was designed for a tea party, not a monsoon.

Ultimately, the message is that Kenya has a choice: recurring economic setbacks or a system to absorb shocks. It’s a nice, neat little binary. But the real choice is whether we keep falling for these slick presentations that promise resilience while the same old problems fester, or if we demand actual, tangible solutions that don't involve a thousand intermediaries and a prayer. Making climate risk insurance "part of the country’s economic infrastructure" sounds like a good idea, until you remember who controls that infrastructure.
