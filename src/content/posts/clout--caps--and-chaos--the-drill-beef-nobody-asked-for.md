Oh, look at us. Another day, another group of boys in balaclavas pretending they are the toughest thing since overcooked githeri. The Buruklyn Boyz and Toxic Lyrikali are currently locked in what they call a "lyrical contest," but let-s be real- it-s a desperate grab for streaming numbers. This Buru Buru versus Kayole rivalry has moved from mediocre diss tracks to actual street confrontations, because apparently, we have not learned that chasing clout can lead to actual hospital bills.

The industry is, as usual, being completely hypocritical about it. On one hand, you have the "peace and unity" choir begging everyone to stop the violence, and on the other, you have artists telling their peers to postpone releases because the "mood" is all about the beef. It-s hilarious how some musicians are so insecure about their own talent that they are terrified of being overshadowed by two teenagers arguing over who is more "hood." It reminds me of how quickly people lose their minds over anything unconventional in this city, much like the moral panic surrounding the [No Panty Movement](/posts/is-going-commando-un-african-the-cultural-clash-over-kenyan-womens-modern-choices) where pastors and critics alike found something new to be offended by.

Social media is the real circus here. Fans on X are dissecting lyrics like they are Shakespearean sonnets, while the rest of us just hear a lot of bass and aggressive breathing. These "stans" claim this hostility is "sharpening creativity," but it looks more like a race to the bottom of the dignity barrel. If the creativity was that sharp to begin with, maybe they would not need to threaten each other in the streets just to stay relevant on a playlist. It is all a spectacle for a generation that values noise over actual substance.

While the drill scene claims to be the voice of the youth, it-s starting to feel like a scripted reality show with worse lighting. We love to talk about the "evolving urban soundscape," but if the evolution involves actual violence and goons chasing each other for TikTok views, then maybe we have just circled back to being primitive. Everyone is worried about the "wrong message" being sent to young fans, but let-s be honest- the fans are only here for the mess, and the artists are more than happy to provide it for a few extra shillings and a trending hashtag.
