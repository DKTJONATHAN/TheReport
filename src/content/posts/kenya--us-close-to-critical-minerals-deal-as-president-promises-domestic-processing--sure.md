KENYA, US CLOSE TO CRITICAL MINERALS DEAL AS PRESIDENT PROMISES DOMESTIC PROCESSING (SURE!)

President Ruto claims a deal is imminent with the US for processing Kenya's minerals at home, a supposed win for African nations tired of raw material export. Meanwhile, G7 leaders are busy trying to ditch China's grip on minerals for their own transition to fancy electric cars. It’s all about keeping value on the continent, they say.

President William Ruto, hobnobbing with G7 leaders in France, announced Kenya is on the verge of a significant minerals deal with the United States. This agreement, he boasts, will ensure that the country's valuable resources - think rare earths, niobium, lithium, graphite, copper, and nickel - are processed right here in Kenya. After decades of exporting raw materials and getting peanuts in return, this sounds like a dream come true. Just don't expect the change to be immediate or easy, this is Kenya after all.

Ruto emphasized a shift away from the old model of exporting raw materials, a tune sung by many African leaders. He insists that these natural resources "can no longer be exported and processed elsewhere." This is the same script we’ve heard before, usually followed by the same old story of corruption and missed opportunities. Remember how City Hall finally coughed up Sh1.55bn to Kenya Power [City Hall Finally Bows Down, Will Cough Up Sh1.55bn to Kenya Power (After Much Theatre)]? That was a saga of promises and delays. Let’s see if this minerals deal avoids a similar fate.

The G7, meanwhile, is actively trying to reduce their dependence on China for these critical minerals. They’ve agreed to coordinate their efforts, which basically means they’ll try not to outbid each other too spectacularly. Ruto, for his part, is playing the shrewd diplomat, stating that Africa won't choose between Washington and Beijing but will pursue multiple partnerships aligned with its own economic priorities. He wants investment, not aid, and relationships built on shared returns, not just resource extraction.

He also lectured the G7 on the global financial system, claiming Africa isn't short of capital but rather needs a framework to access the trillions already sitting in African pension funds and insurance assets. He's calling for guarantees and risk-sharing from the G7 to unlock this money and lower borrowing costs. The G7 leaders have supposedly signalled support in principle for strengthening African financial institutions, including ATIDI.

Ultimately, Ruto declares, "Africa is not a problem to be solved. It is an opportunity that can be harnessed for global progress." While that sounds nice, the reality on the ground often tells a different story. Let’s hope this minerals deal actually translates into jobs and development for Kenyans, and not just another headline.
