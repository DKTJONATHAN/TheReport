So, the big political truce between President William Ruto and Gideon Moi, the man who owns The Standard newspaper - yes, *that* The Standard - has gone up in smoke. Less than a year before we all have to line up and pretend to care about elections again, the ink on their little agreement has barely dried before it’s been smeared with mud. Typical.

Of course, The Standard's headlines, owned by a political player, are now being blamed for this spectacular unravelling. Who would have thought a newspaper with a vested interest would act in its own interest? Shocking. It’s almost as if they see their editorial pages as a battleground, not a source of independent news. But then again, who’s really surprised when we’ve seen how easily [clout-chasers get a legal pass](posts/the--fake-news--trap-is-dead--our-favorite-clout-chasers-just-got-a-legal-pass) these days?

Now we’re all supposed to be outraged that access to news might come with strings attached. Pay a measly 1€ to subscribe, or else you have to let them track your every digital move with cookies. They want your data for “content personalization” and “audience measurement.” In plain English, they want to know what makes you tick so they can sell you more and show you more ads, all under the guise of "supporting editorial work." What a noble sacrifice.

And if you’re foolish enough to refuse those cookies, oh dear, your access will be "restricted." How very democratic. They offer you the "free" option, which basically means selling your soul - or at least your browsing history - for a few articles. Or you can cough up the subscription fee for "full access with no advertising cookies." Because god forbid they interrupt your carefully curated news diet with something as crass as an advertisement.

So here we are, a country heading into another election cycle, with its political leaders back to their old tricks and its media serving up the drama. Whether it’s "trusted, independent news" or just thinly veiled political maneuvering, we'll be here, watching it all unfold, probably after being served a dozen targeted ads. Just another day in Nairobi.
