David Maraga is back at it, acting like he still lives in a country where the law actually matters. The former Chief Justice turned presidential hopeful is making noise about some Sudanese businessman, Algoney Hamdan Dagalo Musa, allegedly scoring a Kenyan passport. Maraga calls it a "profound constitutional crisis," but for anyone who has ever tried to get a basic permit at Nyayo House without a "facilitator," this is just another day in the office. In Nairobi, our sovereignty is a retail product, and apparently, the RSF has the right currency.

It is almost funny how Maraga expects the EACC and DCI to conduct a "transparent investigation" into this mess. He is talking about the same systems that have turned state security into a profit center. We have seen this movie before, where the people meant to guard our borders are the same ones opening the door for anyone with enough dollars to bypass the queue. It is the same logic behind the [Billion-Shilling Badges: The High Cost of Police 'Prosperity'](/posts/billion-shilling-badges--the-high-cost-of-police--prosperity) where the uniform is just a license to hunt for bribes while the country burns.

The hypocrisy is the loudest part of this whole saga. While President Ruto is busy flying across the globe playing the "regional mediator" and peacemaker for Sudan, his administration is allegedly hosting the very people accused of turning El Fasher into a graveyard. You cannot sit at the peace table with a Bible in one hand while the other hand is under the table handing out Kenyan travel documents to sanctioned warlords. It makes our "neutrality" look like a poorly scripted play where the lead actor forgot his lines.

Maraga says that Kenyan citizenship is not a gift to be extended at the convenience of power. That is a sweet sentiment for a law school textbook, but on the streets of Nairobi, power is the only thing that buys convenience. If you are the brother of a commander linked to genocide, you get the red carpet and a fresh AK-series passport. If you are a regular Kenyan, you get a three-month delay and a "system is down" message from a clerk who is waiting for you to drop a few thousand shillings.

At the end of the day, this investigation will go the way of all Kenyan investigations: nowhere. The government will stay silent, the files will go missing, and we will continue to pretend that our borders mean something. Maraga can shout about "diplomatic integrity" all he wants, but as long as our institutions are for sale, the only thing a Kenyan passport proves is that you had enough money to buy one. We are not a country; we are a high-end concierge service for the world's most questionable characters.
