Ruto Tells Diplomats: Sell Kenya as a Global Playground, Good Luck With That

President Ruto wants our ambassadors to hawk Kenya as the next big investment spot and a partner for the future. Fancy talk, isn't it? He was at some fancy conference, telling these folks to "project a credible national image" and "secure new markets." Basically, go out there and convince people that despite the chaos at home, Kenya is the place to be. Easy peasy, right? Especially when the global order is a mess of rising unilateralism and weakening partnerships.

The President is talking about how the world is a hot mess with conflicts in the Middle East and all sorts of geopolitical drama. But he expects our diplomats to just swan around, adapt, and be clear-eyed about it all. He's even outlined four strategic priorities - talk about buzzwords: economic diplomacy, global partnerships, promoting interests. It all sounds like a lot of hot air to me, and frankly, a bit rich considering the state of things back home. Are they going to talk about the "Math and the Great Kenyan Private Property Scam" while they're at it?

Then there's the whole song and dance about Kenya being a "convener of consequential international dialogue." We're hosting a couple of big conferences, apparently. The Africa-France Summit and the Our Ocean Conference. They say it’s a chance to reframe partnerships and advance marine conservation. Sounds grand, but let's be real, these are just more photo ops and talking shops. Will they actually bring any real change, or is it just more posturing? We’ve seen how easily these pronouncements can just be hot air, especially when you look at the kind of “unite or state power and ‘fire’ go” situations happening locally.

Ruto is telling these diplomats to amplify Kenya's voice and build momentum for these events. It’s all about presenting a polished image, a stark contrast to the often gritty reality on the ground. They need to tell our story with conviction, he says. Well, conviction is easy when you’re not the one dealing with the daily grind, the corruption, and the broken promises. Let's see if these ambassadors can actually "convert that promise into partnerships that deliver real results," or if it's just another round of diplomatic theatre.







President William Ruto has sent his diplomats out to sell Kenya as the next big investment destination and a trusted partner for the future. Speaking at the 19th Ambassadors and High Commissioners Conference in Nairobi, he stressed the need for a "credible national image" and securing new markets. It’s a grand ambition, especially when the global landscape is as choppy as it is, with rising unilateralism and geopolitical tensions creating worldwide economic instability.

The diplomats are gathered to discuss "Advancing Kenya’s Interests in a Rapidly Evolving Geopolitical Landscape." Ruto expects them to articulate Kenya’s potential, tell our story with conviction, and turn that promise into tangible results. This is meant to happen while reforms are happening back home, a rather optimistic outlook given the usual pace of change here. He’s talking about global conflicts affecting trade and investment, and urging diplomats to be adaptable. One wonders if they’ll be discussing the intricacies of "Mwangi-s Math and the Great Kenyan Private Property Scam" as part of their market analysis.

Kenya is set to host two major international conferences: the Africa-France Summit and the 11th Our Ocean Conference. Ruto sees these as opportunities to underscore our role as a global convener. The Africa-France Summit is meant to reframe partnerships on parity, and the ocean conference aims to boost marine conservation and the blue economy. He wants ambassadors to amplify Kenya’s voice for these "historic engagements." It’s all about building momentum and showcasing Kenya’s growing influence on the world stage.

Ultimately, Ruto wants a "principled and pragmatic foreign policy," engaging partners on mutual respect and shared benefit, all while advancing our sovereignty and national interests. It’s a neat little package of diplomatic goals. The real question, however, is whether these pronouncements and grand plans will translate into anything more substantial than glossy brochures and polite handshakes, especially when the local political climate often feels like a constant struggle, reminding us of the need to "unite or state power and 'fire' go."
