Kenya, France Ink Youth Innovation Deal: More Hot Air for the Unemployed?

Another summit, another photo op. Mudavadi is out here singing about deepening bilateral cooperation with France, focusing on youth innovation, education, and entrepreneurship. Sounds fancy, right? It’s the same old song and dance, really. They talk about investing in human capital and technology, but for most of Kenya's 80% under-35 population, it just means more empty promises while the elite sip champagne.

Mudavadi waxed lyrical about placing young people at the "centre of bilateral cooperation." Seriously? The centre of what, exactly? More jargon to mask the fact that concrete opportunities are scarce. They’re "equipping young people with the requisite knowledge and skills" - skills for what? To compete with even cheaper labour elsewhere? This feels a lot like the "Value Addition Fairy Tale" we’ve heard before, where conferences are held but real change remains elusive.

The French are apparently helping with Kiswahili AI research, which is almost comical. While they’re busy trying to digitise our languages, our youth are struggling to find basic jobs. And then there’s the Engineering and Science Complex at the University of Nairobi. Funded by France and both governments. Let's see how long it takes for that to become another white elephant, or a place for well-connected kids to get their degrees, while the rest of us are left to fend for ourselves.

Hosting this "Youth Innovation Day" at UoN is just optics. Mudavadi encourages young innovators to think beyond Kenya. Great advice when the domestic market is saturated with graduates and the opportunities are either non-existent or require connections you probably don't have. It’s a nice sentiment, but it doesn't put food on the table or provide a stable future.

So, Kenya and France are deepening their partnership. Wonderful. More committees, more meetings, more pronouncements. Let's hope this "strengthening long-term development partnerships" actually translates into something tangible for the average Kenyan youth, and not just another avenue for politicians to make speeches. We've seen how "stability" can be a mirage when economic realities bite, and this partnership might just be another one.

Mudavadi even thanked the French Ambassador. Of course. Someone has to play the diplomat. But behind the pleasantries, it's just business as usual. New collaborations are announced, new complexes are planned, but the fundamental issue of widespread youth unemployment and underemployment in Kenya remains a stubborn, inconvenient truth.







Kenya and France are once again touting their commitment to deepening bilateral cooperation through youth innovation, education, scientific research, and entrepreneurship. Prime Cabinet Secretary Musalia Mudavadi, ever the optimist for the cameras, spoke of moving beyond traditional diplomacy to investing in human capital, technology, and knowledge. It’s the same old script, really. They promise to empower the youth, but for most of Kenya's young population, it's just more talk while real opportunities remain as scarce as a stable shilling.

Mudavadi’s assertion that this partnership places young people at the "centre" sounds good on paper. But what does that "centre" actually entail? More training programs that lead nowhere? More grants that only benefit the connected few? It’s reminiscent of the endless talk about value addition, where conferences are lavishly hosted, yet the promised benefits often evaporate into thin air. The focus on "equipping young people with the requisite knowledge and skills" is a hollow echo when the job market is already saturated.

The supposed collaboration in advancing African languages through AI research is almost comical. While the French are helping us digitise our linguistic heritage, our youth are struggling to secure basic employment. And the shiny new Engineering and Science Complex at the University of Nairobi, funded by a mix of French and Kenyan money? Let's wait and see if it actually produces groundbreaking innovators or just becomes another brick in the wall of expensive infrastructure that serves a select few.

Hosting the Kenya-France Youth Innovation Day at UoN is a convenient stage for pronouncements. Mudavadi’s encouragement for young innovators to think beyond Kenya is a fine sentiment, but it rings hollow when the domestic landscape offers so little. How can someone build a global enterprise when their own backyard is barren? This feels like another chapter in a long story of dashed hopes, much like the fragility we've seen when economic stability falters.

So, yes, Kenya and France are "deepening their partnership." Expect more handshakes, more summits, and more carefully worded press releases. The question remains: will this translate into actual, tangible opportunities for the vast majority of Kenyan youth struggling to find their footing, or will it simply be another round of diplomatic theatre, leaving everyone else to continue the struggle?

Mudavadi’s thanks to the French Ambassador are expected. It’s all part of the diplomatic dance. But behind the smiles and the polished rhetoric, the reality for many young Kenyans is a grim search for meaningful employment and a stable future. This latest partnership might be designed to impress, but the real test lies in its ability to deliver concrete results, not just promises.
