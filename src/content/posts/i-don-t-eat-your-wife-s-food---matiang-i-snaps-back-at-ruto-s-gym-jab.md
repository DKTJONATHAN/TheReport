So, President Ruto, the man supposedly steering the nation, thinks the most pressing issue is Fred Matiang'i's lunch habits. Apparently, our esteemed Commander-in-Chief spends his valuable time worrying about whether opposition leaders are hitting the gym or just the buffet. Honestly, it's a masterclass in distraction, a classic move when you've got nothing substantial to offer. Matiang'i’s response, while a bit crude, is exactly what this circus needs: a brutal dose of reality. "I don't eat your wife's food" - touché. At least someone is calling out this pathetic descent into insults.

Matiang'i’s plea for respect and maturity is as likely to be heard as a sensible economic policy from this administration. He correctly points out that the office of the President should be a symbol of unity, not a platform for petty jabs about body weight. But when has logic ever won an election in this country? We're supposed to believe that this kind of talk elevates leadership? It's a race to the bottom, and both sides seem determined to dig deep. It just reminds me of how often these politicians ditch policy for personal attacks, a predictable pattern in the 2027 Playbook.

The implication that Ruto is "cornered and desperate" if he's stooping this low is perhaps the most accurate observation. When the policy papers are empty and the promises are wearing thin, what's left? Resorting to playground taunts. Matiang'i is right; this is not the kind of example we should be setting for the youth. They're watching, and they're learning that a leader's worth is measured by their ability to sling mud, not to build a nation.

Matiang'i’s call for religious leaders and government officials to "tell him to calm down" is quaint. As if those in power listen to anything other than their own egos. The reality is, this is the new normal. The urgency of funding for the health sector, the intricacies of local languages in legislative debates, even the onset of long rains - these are real issues. But why bother with those when you can focus on who’s got a bigger belly? It’s the Kenyan political theatre at its finest, and frankly, it’s exhausting.
