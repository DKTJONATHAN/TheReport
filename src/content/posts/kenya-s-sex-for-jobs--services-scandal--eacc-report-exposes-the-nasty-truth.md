NAIROBI'S SEX FOR JOBS SCANDAL: EACC REPORT UNCOVERS THE NAKED TRUTH

This EACC report is no surprise. It’s the same old story, just with more official paper. Women, especially young ones desperate for a sniff of a decent job or even just basic service, are being forced to offer up their bodies. The country’s so-called leaders, the ones meant to serve us, are preying on the vulnerable. It’s a shame, a disgrace, and frankly, just business as usual in this country.

Take Mary, for example. Three years after getting her degree, still scraping by with peanuts as a correspondent. She sees an advert for a Hansard reporter, thinks she’s got a shot. But no, the clerk, the esteemed public servant, wants a "club date" and makes it clear what’s expected. She walks away, smart girl. Many aren't so lucky, or so strong. This isn't just corruption; it's a systematic exploitation of desperation, a quiet war waged on women's dignity.

The EACC, bless their bureaucratic hearts, are calling for laws. Laws. As if a piece of paper will stop these predators who are emboldened by years of impunity. They claim it's "sextortion," a new buzzword for an age-old practice of power and perversion. Eighty-two percent of women asked for sexual favors at least once in the last year? Eighty-four percent twice? It’s a pandemic dressed up as a statistic. And the men demanding these favors? Eighty percent of them are men, of course. Because who else would be in a position of power to demand such things? It’s the same old boys' club, just with a sleazier entrance fee.

They’re talking about loopholes in the Sexual Offences Act, about how "quid pro quo" outside of employment is hard to prosecute. As if "sex for fish" or "sex for water" or "sex for a market stall" isn't a clear enough transaction. This is why we keep seeing the same patterns, like the endless cycle of corruption we’ve discussed in other contexts; [The Global Monopoly of Recycled Mediocrity](/posts/the-global-monopoly-of-recycled-mediocrity). It’s all about who has the leverage and who’s desperate enough to give it up.

Even Parliament, in its infinite wisdom, rejected a petition to explicitly criminalize sextortion, citing "duplication." Duplication? Tell that to the women who are still being asked to trade their bodies for basic services. They’re saying existing laws cover it, but the reality on the ground is that these laws are failing. They’re just paper tigers, roaring at the wind while the real predators continue their hunt.

So, what’s the solution? More laws that will be ignored? More reports that will gather dust? The EACC wants stronger legislation, but the real issue is enforcement and a fundamental shift in the culture that allows this to fester. Until then, women in Kenya will continue to navigate a landscape where their bodies are currency and their ambitions are for sale, if they're lucky enough to be offered a "deal."
