Ruto's "Future Fund": Another Slot Machine for Kenya's Minerals?

A grand pronouncement from State House. President Ruto has signed into law the Sovereign Wealth Fund Bill, 2026, a shiny new piggy bank for mineral and petroleum riches. He’s calling it the “most consequential piece of legislation,” the kind of talk that usually precedes a massive rip-off, if you ask me. Thirty percent of the loot goes into a "Future Generations Fund," untouchable, they say. The rest? Shared between a "Stabilisation Fund" and a "Strategic Infrastructure Investment Fund." Sounds like a well-rehearsed play to me.

The President waxed lyrical about this fund preserving wealth, not burning it. He claims Kenya has significant mineral deposits, enough to "transform the economy." We’ve heard this song before, haven't we? Every time there’s a new discovery or a new government initiative, it’s always the magic bullet that will change everything. Yet, somehow, the pockets of the ordinary Kenyan remain stubbornly empty, while the coffers of the connected swell. It’s the same old story, just with a different fancy name. Remember how the SGR was supposed to link us to the Great Lakes and transform trade? [Kenya and Uganda Launch SGR to Link Mombasa to Great Lakes, But Who's Picking Up the Tab?](/posts/kenya-and-uganda-launch-sgr-to-link-mombasa-to-great-lakes--but-who-s-picking-up-the-tab) - a lot of fanfare, and we’re still waiting for the promised prosperity.

Ruto’s trying to tie this new fund to the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF), claiming the NIF builds the present while the SWF saves the future. He even trotted out the Sh150 billion JKIA upgrade as an example, a project too "heavy a cost" for the budget, but apparently fine for the NIF to attract private sector cash. It’s a classic move: dangle a big project, promise future returns, and hope everyone forgets to ask where the money *really* comes from and who *really* benefits when the dust settles. This is just another way to shuffle money around, making it look like prudent management when it’s more like a slick rebranding of old habits.

The Treasury Cabinet Secretary, John Mbadi, is also singing praises for the 13th Parliament, listing a string of "economic reform laws." And then there's ODM leader Oburu Oginga, who dropped a bombshell about needing "a little bit of benevolent dictatorship" to get things done. Talk about revealing the true intentions behind the shiny legislation. It seems the real goal isn't just saving for the future, but consolidating power and pushing through agendas with less public scrutiny. When you hear words like "benevolent dictatorship" in the same breath as economic reforms, you know it's time to hold onto your wallet.

Even the Speaker of the National Assembly, Moses Wetang'ula, is calling it "landmark legislation," conveniently forgetting the many failed attempts before this one. And let's not forget the parade of industry bigwigs and student presidents at the signing. It’s all part of the theatre, designed to convince us that this is a genuinely progressive move for Kenya. But when you’ve seen enough of these grand pronouncements and shiny new funds, you start to smell a rat. This isn't about building a better future; it's about managing the present and ensuring the right people are in charge of the next generation's inheritance. It’s a way to make a quick buck today by promising tomorrow, a promise that often evaporates like mist in the Nairobi sun.
