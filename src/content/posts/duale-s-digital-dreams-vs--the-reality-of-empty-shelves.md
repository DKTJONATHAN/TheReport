Aden Duale is back on X, formerly Twitter, selling us the same old medicine in a new bottle. He calls it a "landmark Contract Addendum," but to anyone who has actually stepped into a Level 6 hospital lately, it just sounds like more English. They sat in a boardroom, probably with expensive tea and mandazis, to tell us that SHA, KEMSA, and the Digital Health Agency are now best friends. We have seen this movie before, and usually, the sequel is even more expensive than the original.

The big "innovation" here is the Split Payment Mechanism. Duale wants us to believe that SHA paying KEMSA directly will magically stop the stock-outs. In reality, it is just a faster way to move taxpayers' money into the black hole that is our medical supply chain. They talk about "financial discipline" as if KEMSA hasn't been the headquarters of scandals for the last decade. Giving them a direct line to the cash is like giving a termite a direct line to your wooden roof and calling it "structural support."

Then there is the Centralised Digital Platform. Because apparently, the reason we do not have basic antibiotics is that we lack a dashboard. The Digital Health Agency is the new toy in town, promising "real-time visibility" of ordering and supply. I can give them real-time visibility for free: go to any ward and see the empty cabinets. We are becoming a country that prioritizes apps over actual healthcare. It is the same systemic rot we see everywhere, much like the [South C Building Collapse: Cost of Corruption and Incompetency in Kenya Exposed](/posts/south-c-building-collapse-corruption-incompetency-kenya) where the paperwork was perfect but the actual structure was a death trap.

Duale says these reforms will strengthen Bima Bora and Afya Nyumbani. These names sound very sweet, but they do not treat malaria or fix broken bones. While the CEOs sign papers and post photos on social media, the ground remains the same. Universal Health Coverage in Kenya has become a buzzword used to justify more bureaucracy and more agencies that do nothing but eat our taxes while the sick are left to fend for themselves.

At the end of the day, a "reliable and transparent supply chain" does not exist in a vacuum of integrity. You can automate the check-off system and build the fanciest digital platform in East Africa, but if the people running it are the same ones who have presided over the collapse of the health sector, nothing changes. We are tired of being told to wait for the system to boot up while our relatives are being told to go buy their own syringes at the chemist across the road. High-level meetings do not save lives-actual medicine does.
