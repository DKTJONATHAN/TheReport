Benoît Richaud has turned the Olympic "kiss and cry" area into a personal runway, using the flags of thirteen different nations as disposable accessories for his own brand expansion. While the internet coos over his wardrobe changes, the reality is far grimmer. This is the birth of the coaching monopoly, where a single individual controls the aesthetic and technical output of an entire sport, rendering the concept of national competition entirely moot.

Richaud claims to coach sixteen Olympians simultaneously, but this is less about mentorship and more about market saturation. When one man dictates the choreography for nearly twenty percent of the field, the sport ceases to be a diverse clash of cultures and becomes a monotonous reflection of a single man’s ego. The viral nature of his jacket-switching is not an accident. It is a strategic maneuver to ensure that no matter who wins the medal, the man in the background remains the primary subject of social media discourse.

This brand-first approach mirrors the recent trend of executive fluidity seen in other sectors, such as [Corporate Musical Chairs: Risper Ohaga Swaps Beer Bottles for Insurance Policies](/posts/corporate-musical-chairs--risper-ohaga-swaps-beer-bottles-for-insurance-policies). In both cases, the specific mission of the institution is secondary to the career trajectory of the individual. For Richaud, the jacket is not a uniform. It is a temporary billboard.

The logistical defense that technology allows him to coach via phone reveals a chilling detachment. Skating used to be about the grueling, physical presence of a mentor. Now, it is a remote-controlled business model. Richaud’s admission that he experiences emotional peaks in rapid succession suggests a superficiality that should concern anyone who values the mental health of athletes. It is mathematically impossible to give 100 percent of your emotional energy to sixteen different rivals. You cannot truly grieve with a loser when your next client is waiting in the wings wearing a different color.

By his own admission, Richaud is using these Games to increase the popularity of the sport on social media. In the cynical logic of the 21st century, popularity is the only metric that matters, even if it comes at the cost of the Olympic Games’ founding principles. The beauty he claims to promote is increasingly just a backdrop for his own slender, bald, and very well-marketed silhouette. The athletes are merely the vehicles carrying his choreography to the podium, while he remains the only one allowed to win every single time.
