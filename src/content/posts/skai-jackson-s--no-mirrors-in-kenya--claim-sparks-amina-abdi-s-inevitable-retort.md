Well, here we are again, staring down another celebrity-fueled storm in a teacup. This time it's American actress Skai Jackson, who, after a trip to Kenya, apparently came away with the profound insight that mirrors are a luxury here, and our children are wandering around like vampires, never having seen their own reflections until her blessed phone came along. Naturally, media personality Amina Abdi has stepped up to deliver the predictable, yet necessary, dressing-down.

Skai, bless her well-traveled heart, recounted her revelation on social media, claiming mirrors are "very expensive" and that "a lot of people don’t know what they look like." She then shared the heartwarming tale of children seeing themselves for the first time on her smartphone - a moment that, if true, paints a rather dismal, isolated picture of our nation. It’s the kind of story that gets clicks, fuels pity, and completely misses the mark on a country of 50-plus million people.

Amina Abdi's response was a mix of incredulity and outright frustration. She rightly pointed out the absurdity of such a blanket statement from someone supposedly well-exposed, asking if Skai truly believed Kenya, a country with modern cities and world-class destinations, lacks basic mirrors. It's the same worn-out script: a foreign visitor parachutes into a specific, often disadvantaged, area, then uses that narrow lens to define an entire, diverse nation. Why do they only see the worst, then paint it as the whole picture? It's not unlike how we keep facing the same old problems, year in-year out, with the same old promises and no real change, like how [Nairobi braces for another deluge, its infrastructure failing yet again](/posts/nairobi-braces-for-deluge--infrastructure-fails-again-as-officials-dither) while officials dither.

This isn't about whether mirrors are expensive in *some* remote or impoverished areas - of course, they might be. It’s about the audacity to extrapolate that singular experience to a whole country, dismissing its complexities, its successes, and its everyday realities. Amina's anger at "poverty tourism" is justified. These visitors come looking for a specific narrative, confirm their biases, and then broadcast it globally, creating a misleading image for anyone too lazy to Google.

So, the debate rages online. Some agree with Amina, others argue Skai was just sharing her "personal experience." But what good does it do? We'll have another celebrity next year, making another equally baffling claim, and another Kenyan voice will rise to correct them. It's a cyclical dance of misrepresentation and exasperation, and frankly, I'm tired of watching it.
