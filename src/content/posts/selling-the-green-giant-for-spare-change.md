Only in a world run by bureaucrats could someone look at a company controlling 70 percent of the mobile market and say it is not dominant. The COMESA Competition and Consumer Commission has finally rubber-stamped the sale of the Kenyan government's 15 percent stake in Safaricom to Vodacom. They are calling it a strategic move to ensure the market remains competitive, which is just Nairobi-speak for letting a monopoly get even bigger while we keep paying M-Pesa fees that could fund a small country.

The government is desperate for Sh204.3 billion to plug a hole in a budget that looks like a sieve. We are watching the family silver being auctioned off to pay for Eurobond debts, a cycle that feels as futile as [Polishing the Brass on a Sinking Continent: The UN's Nigerian Delusion](/posts/polishing-the-brass-on-a-sinking-continent--the-un-s-nigerian-delusion). While the elite talk about leveraging shareholding, the reality is that the State is offloading six billion shares at Sh34 each just to keep the creditors from the door for another week.

Once this deal is done, Vodacom Group will own 55 percent of Safaricom. The pride of Kenya is now officially a South African subsidiary. It is funny how we celebrate being a tech hub when we cannot even hold onto our most profitable asset. We are just the workers in the field, clicking away on our smartphones while the real profits head south to Johannesburg.

Look at the math if you want to lose sleep. The government expects to spend Sh4.7 trillion this year but only has Sh3.5 trillion in the bank. Selling 15 percent of Safaricom is like selling your car to pay for one month of rent when you still do not have a job. It is a temporary fix for a permanent disaster, yet we are supposed to applaud the regulatory approval like it is a victory for the common man.

Even the local investors are being told to pipe down. While groups like GEMA want retail investors and local pension funds to have a seat at the table, the big boys have already decided who gets the meat. We are left with the bones and a higher interim dividend that will probably be swallowed by inflation before it even hits our bank accounts. This is not a merger; it is a surrender.
