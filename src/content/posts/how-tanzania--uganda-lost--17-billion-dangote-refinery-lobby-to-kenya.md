Tanzania, Uganda Lost $17 Billion Dangote Refinery Lobby to Kenya: A Tale of East African Scramble

This exposé reveals how Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania tripped over themselves, showcasing their clunky competition for a massive $17 billion Dangote refinery, ultimately landing it in Lamu. It's a classic East African drama of promises, jostling, and ultimately, the inevitable compromise.

The whole song and dance around Dangote's $17 billion refinery, meant to be the region's biggest industrial jewel, has finally ended with Kenya snatching it. Apparently, the months of back-and-forth, with each country practically groveling, exposed the raw, unvarnished truth of how these neighbors "compete" - which is to say, how they bungle opportunities. Lamu it is, because apparently, having a clean title deed is more important than actual convenience.

Uganda, the oil-rich darling, seemed like the obvious choice. Imagine, crude right there! But no, their "regulatory and land environment" was apparently a nightmare, making it harder to navigate than a Nairobi matatu rush hour. Plus, being landlocked is a curse no amount of oil can fix if you’re trying to export. And then there’s the awkwardness of their own struggling refinery - a Dangote-sized behemoth would have simply swallowed it whole, a bit like how some of our politicians swallow public funds.

Kenya’s win wasn't exactly a masterclass either. Mombasa was the initial pick, but land disputes and access issues - classic Kenyan gridlock - scared them off. So, Lamu it is, a trade-off of logistical headaches for a less complicated land situation. It’s a bit like choosing a broken-down but legally owned car over a sleek stolen one. And let's not forget Kenya’s banking sector, apparently deep enough to swallow a piece of this mega-project, a stark contrast to the often-struggling financial scenes elsewhere. It’s a reminder that in this region, infrastructure dreams often collide with harsh realities, much like the perpetual struggles of our own [Nairobi Hustle Economy: The Unvarnished Cost of Hope](/posts/nairobi-hustle-economy--the-unvarnished-cost-of-hope).

Tanzania, bless their hearts, entered the race late, distracted by their own internal political dramas. By the time they got their act together, the deals were already cooking in Kenya’s favor. It’s a pity, really, that they couldn't get their ducks in a row sooner. Now they’ll just be a regional partner, which is fine, I guess, if you enjoy watching from the sidelines. Dangote, ever the pragmatist, is still inviting them to the party, because why not?

Ultimately, this is less about strategic brilliance and more about who could navigate the messiest of bureaucratic landscapes with the least amount of immediate drama. East Africa's scramble for investment is a recurring theme, and this refinery saga is just the latest chapter. Whether Lamu becomes a success story or another cautionary tale of inflated costs and delayed timelines, only time, and a lot more money, will tell.
