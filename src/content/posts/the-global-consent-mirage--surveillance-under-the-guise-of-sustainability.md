This same logic is currently being weaponized against African maritime sectors. Just as functional cookies are sold as a way to "enhance experience," green shipping mandates are marketed as a way to save the planet. In reality, they function as a barrier to entry for developing nations that lack the capital to retrofit entire fleets. It is a form of regulatory colonialism where the Global North sets the pace and the Global South pays the bill to stay in the game. The "Necessary" label is applied to policies that ensure the existing power structures remain unchallenged.

Analytical and performance cookies serve as the digital equivalent of maritime surveillance. They are tools used to understand metrics, traffic sources, and behavior, allowing corporations to map out human desire and exploit it. When global bodies pressure African nations to support emissions rules, they are seeking that same level of data-driven control over trade routes. By forcing compliance, they ensure that every movement of goods is tracked, taxed, and throttled according to the whims of international financiers who prioritize their own ESG scores over local economic survival.

The final layer of this trap is the advertisement cookie, which uses the past to dictate the future. This is the ultimate cynical loop. We are tracked so we can be sold a version of the world that keeps us consuming. On the global stage, this manifests as a cycle of debt and "assistance" where African nations are forced to buy expensive green technology from the very countries that polluted the world to achieve their own dominance. It is a closed system where "consent" is merely a button you press before the machine continues its work of harvesting your data and your autonomy.
