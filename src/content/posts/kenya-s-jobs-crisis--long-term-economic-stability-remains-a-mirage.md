The recent uproar over fuel prices wasn't just about a few shillings at the pump, was it? No, that was just the smoke from a fire that's been smoldering for years. Geopolitics might light the match, but our internal rot provides the fuel. We're bleeding from debt, a shilling barely clinging on, and a cost of living that laughs in the face of our stagnant wages. The pump price is a symptom, not the disease. The real sickness? Corruption. It's a cancer that eats away at everything - classrooms, medicine, roads, and most importantly, trust. Every shilling stolen is a job denied, a future dimmed. We can't budget our way out of outright theft.

And it's not just the thieving. We're crippled by trade rules that favour imports, credit markets that treat African risk like a cardinal sin, and local monopolies that crush anyone trying to build something honest. Then there's tribalism, turning every opportunity into a clan inheritance. We're living on a powder keg, constantly exploding politically because the economy keeps failing to deliver. As 2027 looms, don't fool yourself; ballots alone won't save us. Only proper jobs will.

They talk about "six pillars" and re-anchoring the economy on productivity. Sounds grand, doesn't it? Irrigate agriculture, add value, provide credit. Great on paper. But when a tomato farmer has to pay five bribes just to get her produce to Nairobi, and loses half of it on broken roads, what's the point? Manufacturing is supposed to move from aspiration to floor space, but try getting a VAT refund in 30 days or registering a firm without a bribe. Industrial parks are just billboards for the connected, and the promises of a [National Infrastructure Fund Council](/posts/ruto-names-national-infrastructure-fund-council-members--who-gets-the-pie) often seem to benefit the same old faces, not the common mwananchi.

Then there's the talk of financing tools, not consumption. A Jobs and Enterprise Fund for sewing machines and tuktuks? Sounds lovely. But if the government itself can't pay its suppliers on time - killing more businesses than actual competition - how can we expect banks to lend or jobs to multiply? And "killing the middlemen"? That's a classic. Farm gate to market is a cartel corridor, always has been. Digitising county levies is a good idea, but without real enforcement and a political will to break cartels in energy, costs will always ripple through every price.

They want to align skills with market reality and dignify the informal economy. Rebuilding TVETs into "factories, not classrooms" is what we've been asking for, but will graduates leave with a contract and a toolbox, or just another certificate to frame? Formalising "jua kali" is crucial, but it needs real workspace, legal protection, and market links, not just another government slogan. And as for restoring the social contract and starving corruption of oxygen - digitising services and transparent procurement are essential, but if the oversight isn't empowered and the sacred cows aren't publicly prosecuted, leakage will remain the norm, not the exception.

Global storms will keep hitting us. Oil will spike, ships will get blocked, droughts will return. A country where most people have decent, dignified work can take a punch and keep standing. A country where most people are idle, disillusioned, and struggling, will fall from a mere shove. Build the roads, jail the thieves, pay people on time, and let them compete fairly. Do that, and maybe, just maybe, discontent will cool into purpose. Fail, and 2027 won't be an election; it will be an audit. And this time, the people will be the auditors. The post-election period won't be easy, as expectations for change will be sky-high. It's time to get serious, or prepare for the worst.
