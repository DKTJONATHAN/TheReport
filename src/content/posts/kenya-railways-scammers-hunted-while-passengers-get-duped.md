Kenya Railways is out here "launching a hunt" for fraudsters. How noble. Meanwhile, unsuspecting passengers are losing their hard-earned cash to fake websites and social media pages, all because they just want to hop on the Madaraka Express. It's a tale as old as time in this city, really. People want a service, someone promises it, and then poof! Your money is gone, and you're left staring at a blank screen.

The latest official notice, dated July 10, 2026, dropped on Saturday the 11th. They're finally admitting these "impersonators" are directing people to "unauthorised payment channels." Surprise, surprise. So, while Kenya Railways is busy writing sternly worded notices and presumably sipping chai, people are out here counting their financial losses. It's enough to make you wonder if they’re truly committed to solving this, or just going through the motions. This is Nairobi, after all; sometimes a good old-fashioned investigation sounds more like theatre than actual justice.

They've even provided phone numbers and an email for victims. "Report the incident immediately," they urge. As if those who've been duped aren't already feeling foolish enough. The corporation is "working closely with relevant security agencies." Let's hope those agencies aren't as busy as the fraudsters. They're mum on the number of victims or how much money has vanished, but they *do* want you to know the scammers are using "fake digital platforms." Groundbreaking. It’s like they’re discovering fire all over again. This whole situation feels like one of those convoluted boardroom coups we've heard about, where everyone’s playing dumb while the money walks.

To "protect travellers," they're reminding everyone that tickets are *only* available through official channels: the website, the USSD code *639#, and physical stations. Groundbreaking advice, truly. They're also specifically calling out TikTok, where fake accounts are apparently offering booking services. So, in case you hadn't figured it out, Kenya Railways doesn't do TikTok bookings. Who would have thought?

This isn't new, of course. Kenya Railways has been issuing these "warnings" for ages. It's a cyclical thing, especially during holidays and weekends when demand is high. They claim the fake platforms not only steal your cash but can also compromise your personal information. You know, the usual digital mugging. It’s a shame people still fall for it, but then again, desperation and a good sob story have always been a potent combination. Just remember, if it sounds too good to be true, it probably is, especially when it comes to booking a train ticket in this country.
