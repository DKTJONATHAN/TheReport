So, Nancy Gathungu and the NCIC have finally confirmed what every Kenyan with a brain already knew. Our centers of higher learning are just glorified village meetings with better stationery. Nineteen public universities have been caught red-handed, behaving like the ethnic enclaves they were designed to be. If you thought your degree was about merit, you clearly haven't checked the payroll. It is not about what you know, it is about whose cousin is signing the vouchers in the finance department.

We tribalise everything in this country. We wake up, breathe, and immediately start wondering if the person next to us speaks the correct mother tongue. The reports reveal entrenched patterns of ethnic dominance that make the Constitution look like a poorly written suggestion. We are building institutions where the gatekeeper, the lecturer, and the Vice-Chancellor probably share the same family tree. It is a miracle they even bother teaching English when the real language of instruction is nepotism.

While the intellectuals are busy counting heads by tribe, the rest of the system is leaking like a broken tap. The total number of police officers has dropped by nearly four thousand in a single year, leaving the gates wide open. Meanwhile, the internet is becoming a digital battlefield where ethnic rhetoric is expected to jump by 50 percent. We are arming ourselves with keyboards while the actual security forces are vanishing. It is the classic Kenyan recipe for a disaster we will all pretend to be shocked by when it finally boils over.

This is the same systemic rot that makes every government interaction feel like a shakedown. Whether you are trying to get an education or just trying to navigate the bureaucracy of existence, the vibe is the same. You only have to look at the [Nyayo House Special: Buy A Passport, Lose A Country](/posts/nyayo-house-special--buy-a-passport--lose-a-country) to see how deep the decay goes. We are selling off pieces of our national soul for a seat at the tribal table, and the price is only going up while the service quality hits the floor.

They say that losing many things in life is part of the experience, but to lose a mother is simply careless. In this country, the Motherland is being neglected by her own children in favor of narrow, ethnic interests. We are too busy fighting over who gets the biggest piece of the carcass to notice that the house is on fire. Welcome to Kenya, where your surname is your resume and the truth is just an expensive rumor that nobody can afford to believe.
