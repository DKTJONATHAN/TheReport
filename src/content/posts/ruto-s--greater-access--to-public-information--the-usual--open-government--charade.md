President William Ruto recently stood at State House, promising Kenyans "greater access to public information" and an "open government" push. He spoke grandly of building public trust, combating misinformation, and launching a "State of Openness Report" - the first of its kind globally, apparently. It all sounds rather noble, doesn't it? Like a fresh coat of paint on a very old, very leaky roof. But anyone who’s lived in Nairobi long enough knows that in this town, promises of transparency often precede the very opposite. "A confident Government does not fear scrutiny," he declared, which is rich coming from a system that historically excels at deflecting it.

He's pledged KSh100 million to the Commission on Administrative Justice to facilitate this report, a document meant to be an "honest assessment" of the country's progress. Honest assessment, or another carefully curated PR piece? Pouring a hundred million into a report is one thing; actually acting on its findings, especially if they reveal inconvenient truths, is quite another. We've seen enough reports gather dust to fill a small library; what makes this one different beyond the fancy global "first"?

Ruto also highlighted reforms over the past decade: digitizing 26,000 government services, a unified digital payment platform to "eliminate revenue leakages," and an electronic procurement system to boost "transparency." While convenience is welcome, the idea that going digital magically erases corruption is naive at best. Crooks adapt. They just find new digital ways to siphon funds. One wonders if this push for digital platforms is truly for transparency or just a more sophisticated way to manage a [digital puppet show](/posts/the-digital-puppet-show--how-human-vanity-ruined-the-first-ai-social-network), where the strings of accountability remain firmly in unseen hands.

Then there's the Government-Owned Enterprises Act, touted for "strengthening merit-based recruitment" and "limiting political influence" by replacing nearly 250 board members. Let's be serious. In Kenya, "merit-based" often means "politically connected but with a shiny CV." Replacing old faces with new ones doesn't guarantee a clean slate; it often just shuffles the deck chairs on the same Titanic. The Conflict of Interest Act and National Infrastructure Fund Act are also on the list of good intentions. Good intentions, like promises of transparency, pave the road to… well, you know the saying.

So, while British and German ambassadors clapped politely and praised Kenya's "commitment," calling transparency an "essential pillar," the cynicism on the ground remains. These are the diplomatic pleasantries, the kind words exchanged at formal events. For the ordinary mwananchi struggling with daily realities, seeing real, tangible change in how government operates - not just how it talks about operating - is what matters. Until then, these grand pronouncements of "open government" remain just that: pronouncements.
