So, Demarre Johnson, one of the so called "finest boys in finance," has been unceremoniously ejected from PwC. Just weeks after gracing the pages of Interview magazine, looking like he stepped off a runway rather than out of a cubicle, Johnson is no longer with the firm. PwC confirmed his exit, which they claim occurred in mid February, and that it has "no relation" to his viral photo shoot.

Of course, the narrative that his departure is purely coincidental is about as believable as a junior banker actually understanding subprime mortgages. Johnson himself, in his brief moment of fame, acknowledged the "controversy" his appearance would stir. He even admitted he anticipated being "clowned" for thinking they were "pretty." Well, the clown car has officially left the lot, and it appears he was a passenger.

The whole affair reeks of a cautionary tale for the attention starved digital generation. Wall Street, for all its supposed modernization, still operates on unspoken rules. Apparently, not outshining your superiors with designer threads and a magazine spread is one of them. While Johnson's departure wasn't officially linked to the photoshoot, one has to wonder if the brouhaha over potential unapproved media appearances and the sheer audacity of his public vanity didn't simply accelerate the inevitable.

Goldman Sachs, another firm represented in the spread, was quick to distance itself, stating media relations did not approve their bankers' participation. PwC, predictably, offered a terse confirmation of the departure. Johnson, who was actively reposting social media buzz about his newfound virality, has now gone quiet. It’s a stark reminder that in the high stakes world of finance, a fleeting moment of glamour can quickly turn into a swift exit. The "finest boys" often find their shine dims considerably when it clashes with the established power structures.
