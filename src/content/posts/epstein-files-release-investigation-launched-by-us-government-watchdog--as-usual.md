So, the US government watchdog is going to "investigate" the release of Epstein files. Color me shocked. This is the same system that let Epstein operate for decades, and now we're supposed to believe a new investigation will magically uncover anything significant.

Let's talk about the players. Pam Bondi, who was apparently "fired" from overseeing the justice department over her handling of these files, is out. Replaced by Todd Blanche, Trump's former lawyer. And what does Mr. Blanche think? That the Epstein files "should not be a part of anything going forward." Translation: Let's just sweep this under the rug, shall we? It's almost as if there's a vested interest in keeping certain names out of the spotlight.

The criticism of Trump for not doing "more" to uncover Epstein's associates is rich. From this vantage point, it appears the administration's priority is less about justice and more about damage control and protecting its own. The call for accountability from survivors like Annie Farmer is a stark reminder of the real human cost of this systemic failure. Her statement about a potential pardon for Ghislaine Maxwell being "insulting" and a "deep betrayal" is precisely the kind of raw emotion that the system seems designed to ignore.

She wants the Department of Justice to pursue a criminal investigation into the enablers. A noble, yet likely futile, request. The DoJ's silence speaks volumes. It's not a sign of diligent deliberation; it's the sound of an institution accustomed to avoiding uncomfortable truths. The "investigation" will likely be a carefully managed process, designed to produce a report that looks busy but ultimately changes very little. The real criminals, the ones who profited and facilitated, will probably continue to enjoy their ill-gotten gains, shielded by a system that prioritizes its own survival over genuine justice. The Epstein files release is just another chapter in the long, sordid history of powerful people protecting each other.
