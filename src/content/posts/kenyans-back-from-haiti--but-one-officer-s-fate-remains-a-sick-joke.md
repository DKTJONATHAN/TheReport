So, the last batch of our boys is back from Haiti. 150 officers, home safe and sound, according to the papers. They say it’s a "difficult chapter" closed. Easy for them to say, they weren't the ones dodging bullets or, worse, disappearing into thin air. This whole Multinational Security Support mission, sounds fancy, doesn't it? Like some grand gesture. More like a grand mess, if you ask me.

And speaking of messes, let’s talk about Benedict Kabiru. His family is still waiting, months later, for a straight answer about what happened to him after he vanished on March 25, 2025. The "Haitian statement" - whatever that is - has apparently "killed the hope" for his family. Hope? In this arena? That's a naive gamble. When you’re dealing with places like Haiti, and when our own government can’t even give a clear update, hope is just a polite word for delusion.

His family is not taking this lying down, though. They’re demanding to know why the officials are being so cagey. And if they don't get their answers, they’re threatening to sue the State. Good for them. Someone needs to push back when the system decides it’s too inconvenient to tell people the truth about their loved ones. This is the kind of political theatre that makes you wonder if we’re living in a gladiatorial state, where lives are just pawns in larger games. [The Gladiatorial State and the Death of Diplomacy](/posts/the-gladiatorial-state-and-the-death-of-diplomacy) anyone?

The fact that an officer can go missing and the official line is so murky is frankly, insulting. It’s not just about Benedict; it’s about what this says about our commitment to our people, both at home and abroad. When you send officers into dangerous situations, you owe them, and their families, more than vague pronouncements and dead ends.

This whole situation reeks of the same old story. We get involved in grand international schemes, and when things go south, it’s the little guy, and their families, who are left to pick up the pieces. The politicians fly in, shake hands, pose for photos, and then fly out, leaving the consequences behind. It's a cycle, and it's a depressing one.

So yes, the officers are back. But the question remains: what really happened to Benedict Kabiru? And why does it feel like the truth is as buried as his hope? Until we get those answers, this "closed chapter" is just another open wound for one Kenyan family.
