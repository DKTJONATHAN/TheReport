US Throws Ksh.1.75B at Kenya's Ebola Fears While Ruto Shakes Rubio's Hand

Warning: The US is "preparing" Kenya for Ebola with a cash injection, but the real question is who benefits when they start eyeing our soil for their own sick citizens.

President William Ruto with U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio after a past press address. PHOTO | COURTESY

So, the Americans, bless their cotton socks, have decided to throw Ksh.1.75 billion at Kenya’s "Ebola preparedness." Apparently, Marco Rubio and our very own President Ruto had a little chat about it. It’s all about "coordination" and "strengthening our capacity." Right. Because what Kenya really needs is to become the region’s designated Ebola dumping ground, just in case the virus decides to take a vacation to the US.

Never mind the fact that this whole song and dance comes on the heels of whispers about Americans exposed to Ebola potentially being shipped over here for "monitoring and treatment." Health Cabinet Secretary Aden Duale assures us that any international health cooperation will be guided by Kenyan laws and our "obligation to protect its citizens." That’s reassuring, coming from a government that seems to have a peculiar talent for prioritising foreign interests over national welfare. We’ve seen similar "strengthening" efforts before, haven't we? Remember when we were supposedly beefing up our health system, only for the Senate Health Committee flags congestion and staffing gaps in Rift Valley counties? The pattern is as predictable as a rainy season in Nairobi.

Duale also insists Kenya is "well prepared," with "intensified screening" and "strengthened surveillance." More than 55,000 travellers screened, all ten suspected cases negative. Fantastic. But let’s be honest, when the US government’s "highest priority remains protecting the health and security of the American people," it’s hardly a leap to assume Kenya is being positioned as a convenient, cheap solution to their problem. They want to prevent it reaching their shores, and conveniently, we have an Air Base.

This whole charade has the Katiba Institute scrambling to the courts, filing an urgent petition to block any foreign-funded Ebola facilities on Kenyan soil. And then there’s the Kenya Medical Practitioners, Pharmacists and Dentists Union (KMPDU), who are not mincing words, calling out "backdoor negotiations" and threatening nationwide industrial action. They’re disgusted by the government’s "apparent willingness to trade national biosecurity and the lives of its citizens for foreign aid." Frankly, I can't blame them. It smells like the same old song and dance we saw with [The Digital Puppet Show: How Human Vanity Ruined the First AI Social Network](posts/the-digital-puppet-show--how-human-vanity-ruined-the-first-ai-social-network), where good intentions paved the road to somewhere less than ideal.

Meanwhile, Medical Services Principal Secretary Dr. Ouma Oluga is busy defending Kenya’s "capacity" and "longstanding role in supporting global disease response efforts." He chirps about strengthened infectious disease management, especially after COVID-19 investments. Sure, the investments might be there, but the execution and the underlying priorities seem to be consistently skewed. It’s a classic case of "the left hand doesn't know what the right hand is doing," or worse, "the right hand is making a deal the left hand will have to clean up."
