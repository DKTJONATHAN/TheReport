So, President Ruto is at it again, jetting off to Mandera to talk about how his government is fixing "60 years of neglect" in northern Kenya. According to him, the problems in that region are so deep, they predate his birth, let alone his presidency. He’s been trotting out this line about giving the north "equal access" to development, as if it's some forgotten corner of the universe that just needs a sprinkle of government magic.

And naturally, his loyalists are out in full force defending him. Health CS Aden Duale claims the President wasn't being disrespectful, just "candid." Candid? Please. It’s a classic deflection, blaming everyone else for the mess while conveniently sidestepping any real accountability for his own administration's pace. Mandera Governor Adan Khalif, predictably, is singing Ruto's praises, boasting about the President's multiple visits. Apparently, three visits in eight months are a record, unlike those ancient times when presidents barely showed up. It's all about the optics, isn't it?

The opposition, bless their hearts, are still questioning the government's priorities. They've been scrutinizing the administration's performance, which, let's be honest, hasn't exactly been setting the world on fire. But Ruto and his crew are too busy trying to convince us that this is all about correcting historical injustices. It’s a convenient narrative, especially when you're facing criticism. It’s almost as if they’re trying to turn Mar-a-Lago into a battlefield chic set, but for national development promises.

What's missing from all this grandstanding is any concrete evidence of transformative change. We're supposed to believe that a few speeches and a handful of infrastructure projects will magically erase decades of what they call neglect. It sounds more like the same old song and dance, with different players. This is Kenya, after all. We've seen these promises before, and we know how they usually end. Just remember the unexploded bombs and lions faced by Kenyan workers, and you'll see the real picture of our "development."

The governor’s insistence that this endorsement reflects the "views of the broader North Eastern community" is also a bit rich. Social media critics are questioning local leaders' support, and for good reason. It’s easy to rally behind a visiting president when you’re on the receiving end of his attention, but will the actual transformation materialize beyond the photo ops?

Ultimately, it’s the same old story dressed up in new rhetoric. "60 years of neglect" is a convenient bogeyman for Ruto to chase, deflecting from the present-day realities and the slow, often non-existent, progress. We’ll see if this time it's different, but my money's on more of the same.
