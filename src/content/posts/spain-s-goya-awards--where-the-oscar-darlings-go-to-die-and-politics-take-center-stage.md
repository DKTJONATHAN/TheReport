Oh, look. Another awards ceremony where the industry elites gather to pat themselves on the back while pretending they aren't obsessed with Hollywood's validation. This year's Goya Awards in Barcelona was the usual mix of high-fashion and low-key resentment. While everyone expected Oliver Laxe's "Sirāt" to sweep everything because it's Spain's official Oscar submission, the local academy decided to play favorites. They handed the top prizes-Best Picture, Director, and Screenplay-to Alauda Ruiz de Azúa’s "Sundays." It’s a Basque family drama, because apparently, nothing screams "prestige" like watching relatives argue in a picturesque setting.

Poor Oliver Laxe. "Sirāt" might have a Cannes Jury Prize and an Oscar nod for Best Sound, but at home, it was relegated to the technical categories. It picked up six trophies for things like editing and cinematography-basically the film industry’s way of saying, "Your movie looked and sounded great, but we didn't actually want to sit through the plot again." It’s the ultimate snub when the "consensus choice" is a safe family drama over a techno-tracked dystopian masterpiece. It’s almost as messy as the [African Giant Or Corporate Puppet? The Messy War For Burna Boy's Soul](/posts/african-giant-or-corporate-puppet--the-messy-war-for-burna-boy-s-soul) situation where artistic integrity is constantly being traded for whatever the board members find most palatable.

And of course, it wouldn't be a European awards show without a heavy dose of performative politics. Co-host Luis Tosar and a bunch of other winners spent the night sporting pins and making sure everyone knew exactly where they stood on global conflicts. Don't get me wrong, having a conscience is great, but there's something so "celebrity" about condemning a genocide while holding a gold-plated statue in a room full of people wearing five-figure outfits. The irony was thicker than the makeup on the red carpet, with every "Stop Genocide" badge carefully positioned for the cameras.

Then we had Susan Sarandon, the queen of the lifetime achievement honor, praising the Prime Minister and local artists for their "moral lucidity." It’s funny how these stars only feel "less alone" when they’re being given a trophy in a room full of people who are contractually obligated to clap for them. She’s found a "larger community" in Spain, which is convenient when you’re being treated like royalty for just showing up and existing. In a world marked by cruelty, it must be nice to find comfort in a standing ovation.

At the end of the day, the Goyas are just another reminder that film awards are rarely about the best film. They’re about who played the game best and which narrative the academy wants to push. Whether it’s Eva Libertad’s "Deaf" cleaning up the "New Director" categories or Joachim Trier picking up a "Best European Film" trophy for "Sentimental Value," the whole thing feels like a carefully choreographed dance. We’ll see if "Sirāt" can actually win that Oscar, or if it’s destined to be the bridesmaid both at home and abroad. Yawn.
