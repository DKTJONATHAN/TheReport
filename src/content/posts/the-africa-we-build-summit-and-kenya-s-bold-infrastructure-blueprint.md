THE AFRICA WE BUILD SUMMIT: KENYA'S "BOLD" INFRASTRUCTURE BLUEPRINT OR JUST MORE HOT AIR?

This grand summit at the JW Marriott is for the bigwigs - heads of state, investors, all the folks who love to talk about "moving infrastructure from priority to implementation." Meanwhile, back on the ground, we're still waiting for roads that don't resemble an obstacle course. They promise trillions, but let's see if it translates to anything more than fancy dinners and photo ops.

President Ruto is being lauded as a man of action because he's flagging off projects. Sure, he’s talking a big game about a modern, well-connected Kenya and the AfCFTA dream. But honestly, the real test isn't the grand pronouncements or the fancy new National Infrastructure Fund aiming to "mobilise nearly Ksh 5 trillion." The real test is whether this money actually gets used for things that benefit ordinary Kenyans, or if it just disappears into the pockets of well-connected individuals. We've heard promises before, haven't we? It reminds me of when I read about how [Europe profits while the dollar burns](/posts/the-architecture-of-collapse--europe-profits-while-the-dollar-burns) - a lot of complex financial talk, but for most of us, not much changes.

This National Infrastructure Fund, supposedly shifting from debt to investment, sounds like another attempt to dress up old problems in new clothes. They're bringing in the big players like Aliko Dangote and a whole council of esteemed figures to steer the ship. Let's hope this "Limited Liability Company" with its "commercial independence" is actually insulated from the usual political interference that plagues these initiatives. Otherwise, it's just another layer of bureaucracy to navigate.

The talk about attracting private sector investment and making infrastructure a "bankable asset class" is all well and good for the suits in the room. But what about the everyday impact? Will these highways and railways actually improve commutes, lower transport costs, or bring reliable electricity to rural areas? The document boasts about channeling Ksh 400 billion annually into transport, energy, water, and agribusiness because they "drive productivity" and "enhance food security." Fine words, but we've heard them all before.

The real issue, as always, is execution and accountability. Africa has the potential, but it’s bogged down by bureaucracy, political instability, and a lack of genuine commitment to transparency. While they're busy "syndicating institutional investors" and talking about "aggregation platforms," we're still stuck in traffic, hoping for a better future that always seems just out of reach. It's a nice idea on paper, this NIF, but let's wait and see if it actually delivers anything tangible, or if it's just another shiny object to distract us.
