Paapa Essiedu, a name soon to be synonymous with a certain boy wizard on HBO, is now wading into the murky waters of televised emotional trauma with his new series, "Babies." His stated ambition? To "break pregnancy loss silence." A noble goal, perhaps, but one that feels increasingly like a well-trodden path for actors seeking to lend their star power to "important" causes. The series, we are told, chronicles the journey of a couple, Steven and Lisa, through the emotional minefield of repeated miscarriages. Essiedu himself admits he's never experienced such a thing, relying on "specialists and midwives" and conversations with women in his life. It’s commendable he did his homework, but it’s still an actor performing empathy, not experiencing it.

Siobhán Cullen, his co-star, echoes the sentiment, crediting midwives on set and "generous" women who shared their stories. The implication is that this drama will somehow conjure a magical "space to share." This is, of course, wishful thinking. The silence around pregnancy loss isn't due to a lack of potential stories, but rather a deeply ingrained societal discomfort with grief, a topic Essiedu correctly identifies as being met with "sensitivity" and an unwillingness to ask the "difficult questions." This leads to the very "stigma and shame" he claims the show will combat. Yet, the formula remains the same: dramatize a pain, expect it to resonate, and hope for a ripple effect. It's a predictable cycle.

Writer and director Stefan Golaszewski, whose previous credits include the Bafta-winning "Mum" and "Him & Her," claims to have drawn on similar experiences, yet insists the show is "more objectively dramatic." This is a convenient way to distance personal pain while still claiming authenticity. He hopes the show will "create a space where people can feel less alone." This is the universal promise of such dramas: to alleviate isolation. However, the reality is that once the credits roll, and the next fast-paced drama takes over, the "space" created often dissipates, leaving individuals to navigate their grief largely as they did before. The "banality" of grief, as Golaszewski puts it-the need to still eat dinner and go to work-is precisely what makes it so isolating, and television, with its inherent need for narrative arcs and resolutions, struggles to truly capture that mundane, persistent ache.

The show’s attempt to highlight the shared emotional impact on relationships, with Lisa reminding Steven, "they are your babies too," is a valid point. Miscarriage is often viewed as a woman's physical burden, overlooking the profound psychological toll on partners. Essiedu’s observation that this shared experience isn't given the same weight because it’s "more difficult to see" is astute. However, the actors’ pronouncements of shifted perspectives and becoming "more compassionate" feel like standard press junket fare. While they may have gained a deeper understanding, the average viewer is unlikely to experience such a profound transformation from a single viewing.

The notion that opening up is a "leap of faith" is true, but the show’s protagonists, much like Essiedu himself, use coping mechanisms. Steven’s humor, Essiedu’s limited engagement with social media and public opinion-these are all strategies for self-preservation in a world that often feels hostile. Essiedu's mention of death threats for his casting as Snape in the Harry Potter series underscores this tension. While he claims a "flood of positivity" accompanies the negativity, it’s a stark reminder that vulnerability is often met with aggression, and the hope of pleasing everyone is a fool’s errand. "Babies" may offer a temporary balm, a shared sigh of recognition, but it’s unlikely to fundamentally alter the landscape of grief or conversation. It's a well-intentioned, but ultimately predictable, attempt to tackle an enduring human struggle through the predictable medium of television.
