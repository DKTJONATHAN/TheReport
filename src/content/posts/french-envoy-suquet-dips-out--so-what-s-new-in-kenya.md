So, the French ambassador, Arnaud Suquet, is packing his bags. Four years, they say. Learnt a bit of Swahili, even tried to wrestle with Sheng. Good for him, I suppose. Meanwhile, the rest of us are still navigating the Nairobi traffic and wondering when things will actually change. He was here to promote France's agenda, build bridges, all that jazz. Did it really matter to the average Kenyan struggling to make ends meet? Probably not.

He apparently loved Kenya, called Nairobi "home." Bless his heart. We're used to these farewell pronouncements, the flowery speeches about "urafiki" and "cherishing memories." It's part of the job description, isn't it? You charm them for a bit, pretend to understand their struggles, then jet off to the next posting. The real test is whether any of that diplomacy actually translates into tangible improvements on the ground. My money says it doesn't.

At least he tried the local lingo. "Akili ni nywele, kila mtu ana zake!" A wise proverb, indeed. But knowing proverbs and understanding the systemic issues that plague this country are two very different things. He was also involved with Swahilipot Hub in Mombasa, empowering youth. That’s nice. Let’s hope the young people there actually get somewhere, unlike some who get caught up in bigger games, like the ones where you [hunt the whistleblower while the thief escapes](/posts/nairobi-s-favorite-game--hunt-the-whistleblower-while-the-thief-escapes).

He held diplomatic roles in Sudan and Ethiopia before this, and was a political adviser for the Horn of Africa. So, he’s seen a bit of the continent. But Africa, and Kenya in particular, is a complex beast. It’s easy to be charmed by the sunsets and the wildlife, but the real picture is far more intricate. These ambassadors come and go, and while they might leave with fond memories and a few Swahili phrases, Kenya’s fundamental challenges often remain.

His tenure here was meant to strengthen bilateral relations, in politics, economics, culture, science, and security. We’ll see what that amounted to in the long run. It’s the usual cycle: promises, partnerships, and then, inevitably, departures. We’re left to pick up the pieces and hope the next envoy has a more lasting impact than just learning a few words of Sheng.

Ultimately, Suquet’s departure is just another chapter in the long, drawn-out story of international diplomacy in Nairobi. He played his part, a spectacle for some, a representative for others. Now he's gone, and the wheels of government and everyday life keep grinding on, often in the same old ways.
