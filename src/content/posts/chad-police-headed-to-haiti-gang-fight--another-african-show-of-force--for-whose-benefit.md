So, Chad wants to send 800 of its finest police and gendarmes to Haiti to help fight gangs. A senior official let this juicy bit of news slip to Reuters, because, you know, transparency is just *so* last year. Apparently, these brave souls are slated to arrive by June, fresh off training from "European and American partners." Which partners, exactly? The US State Department is already playing coy, denying they’re doing any training while the Dominican Republic’s foreign minister seems to think otherwise. Classic international politics, where everyone points fingers and nobody takes direct responsibility. It’s like watching our own politicians play games, just on a much bigger, more dangerous stage.

This whole song and dance is meant to bolster the UN-backed Gang Suppression Force, which is supposed to hit 5,500 by October. Funny, because the Kenyan-led Multinational Security Support mission, the predecessor to this grand plan, hasn't exactly set the world alight since it was approved last September. Mostly Kenyan police are on the ground, with a smattering from Central America and the Caribbean. Sounds like a real crack team, doesn't it? Just enough to make a statement, not quite enough to actually *solve* anything. Reminds me of those grand promises we hear back home, like that Sh44 Billion Mirage that promised prosperity but left us with empty pockets.

The Dominicans, meanwhile, are already suggesting the Kenyans should pack their bags. Because why keep a failing model when you can shuffle in new faces and hope for a different outcome? It’s a strategy that’s worked wonders everywhere else, I’m sure. Chad themselves offered troops back in October 2023, without the nitty-gritty details. Benin and Bangladesh were also on the list of potential contributors. It’s a global potluck of promises, hoping someone brings the actual food.

And this is where it gets interesting. The US denies training the Chadian forces, even as the Dominican Republic says they are. So, who's telling the truth? Does it even matter when the end result is likely to be more confusion and less actual progress? It’s a familiar tune, isn’t it? Big pronouncements, elaborate plans, and then… well, we all know how these things often end up. Like a faded promise, or another failed rally where everyone talks a good game but nothing fundamentally changes. It's not about saving Haiti; it's about looking like you're doing something, somewhere, for someone.
