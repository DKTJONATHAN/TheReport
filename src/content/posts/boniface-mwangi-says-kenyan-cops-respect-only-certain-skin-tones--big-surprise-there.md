ORIGINAL HEADLINE: "Police only respect a particular skin colour: Boniface Mwangi criticises conduct of Kenyan cops"








So, Boniface Mwangi, bless his idealistic heart, is up in arms again. This time it’s about the arrest of retired Chief Justice David Maraga and other activists during a protest on June 8, 2026. According to his Instagram ramblings on June 10, 2026, the police have a favourite colour, and it’s not black. Apparently, they’re more likely to rough up or arrest a black Kenyan, even a former CJ, than a white or Indian person. Who knew? It’s almost like the system is rigged, but don’t quote me on that.

Mwangi’s little protest had a bit of everyone - white, Indian, black. But when the police decided to play rough, they knew exactly who to target. The black folks got the full treatment - the beating, the shooting, the arresting. The others? They just went home. Makes you wonder if the police training manual has a special chapter on “how to handle citizens who aren’t melanin-rich.” It’s the same old song and dance, and frankly, it’s getting tired. We’ve seen this play out before, and it’s no different from the issues plaguing things like the [Ruto's New Land Commission: Six Years of the Same Old Story](/posts/ruto-s-new-land-commission--six-years-of-the-same-old-story).

But wait, there’s more! While the police are busy enforcing their racial hierarchy, they’re also allowing chunks of Nairobi National Park to be carved up. Seventy-six acres, to be exact. Mwangi’s baffled as to why there’s no public outcry against this land grab. He’s got people out there, including his Indian mentee, trying to rally support, but it seems the public prefers to keep their heads down and their wallets open for safaris. It’s a sad state of affairs when our wildlife heritage is treated like an inconvenience to developers.

Mwangi’s got a point about the environmental pressure too. Infrastructure projects, urban sprawl - it’s all pushing in on the park, gobbling up wildlife corridors. And now, the bright idea is to build a state-of-the-art orphanage *inside* the park. Because what the park needs is more buildings and enclosures to turn wild animals into tourist attractions. It’s not about rehabilitation; it’s about making a buck. They want to take pristine, wild land and turn it into some zoo for gaping tourists. It’s just another example of how money talks and conservation walks, or rather, gets trampled.

Kenya’s got its parks and conservancies, sure, about 11% to 16% of the land. But that’s still not enough to protect it from the relentless march of “development.” The conservationists are fighting, the government’s got its own agenda, and the rest of us are just left to watch as our natural heritage gets eroded, piece by piece, while the police focus on enforcing their own warped sense of order. It’s a mess, a predictable, disheartening mess.
