Ruto's "Cushioning" Act: Fuel VAT Cut Is Just Another Band-Aid For Broken Wallets

A temporary fuel VAT reduction is the government's latest attempt to placate Kenyans while global chaos makes everything more expensive. Don't expect miracles.

President Ruto, in his infinite wisdom, has decreed a temporary holiday from half the VAT on fuel. Apparently, the war in the Middle East is making our petrol prices jump like a flea on a hot tin roof. So, to "shield" us, they've slashed the tax from 16% to a whopping 8% for 90 days. Just enough time for them to figure out their next move, or for the prices to climb even higher.

Ruto claims this urgent and necessary step will have a "ripple effect" on consumer goods. Right. Like a pebble tossed into the ocean of our national debt. He’s hoping this little tax break will ease the pressure on households and businesses. Bless his heart. It's a good move, sure, but it feels more like a bandage on a gaping wound. We've seen this play before, haven't we? Remember when they promised to fix things and we ended up just paying more? It’s like trying to buy peace with a few coins, as Rihanna famously demonstrated that even billions can't buy real peace.

The Parliament, in its infinite haste, apparently passed this bill in a single day. Swift intervention, they call it. More like a knee-jerk reaction to a public screaming about empty pockets. Fuel prices are the engine of inflation, driving up the cost of everything from your matatu ride to that loaf of bread. So yes, a fuel price drop, however temporary, is a drop. But will it actually make a dent in the relentless march of rising living costs? I'm not holding my breath.

This 90-day window is supposed to give policymakers time to "assess global oil trends." What they’ll likely assess is how much more they can squeeze out of us once this little reprieve is over. The government is committed to "stabilizing the economy," they say. Usually, that means more taxes or more borrowing. They’re talking about a mix of tax adjustments, subsidies, and "procurement reforms." Sounds like a lot of fancy words for the same old song and dance.

The transport and logistics sector might welcome this, thinking it’ll reduce their costs. Maybe. But let's be real. If the global price of oil goes up again, this tiny VAT cut will be swallowed whole, and then some. It's a temporary fix for a much bigger problem, and frankly, after years of this cycle, I'm just tired.
