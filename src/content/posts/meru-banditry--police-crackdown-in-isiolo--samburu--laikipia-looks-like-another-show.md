So, the National Police Service has decided to grace Meru and its neighbours with their presence. A "multi-agency security team," they call it, ready to "recover stolen livestock and mop up illegal firearms." Sounds impressive, right? Especially with "robust air support" for a few targeted forests and areas. It's the same old script they trot out every time the situation gets too noisy, promising to "restore normalcy." Just like they promised in Turkana, remember? [The day elite paramilitary unit stormed Turkana to flush out illegal gun owners](/posts/the-day-elite-paramilitary-unit-stormed-turkana-to-flush-out-illegal-gun-owners) was supposed to be the end of it, but here we are.

And the numbers? Oh, the numbers are always good for a headline. Over 11,000 cattle stolen in four years, more than 110 people killed. And since January alone, 11 dead and 600 cattle gone. The bandits are so bold they're taunting the Deputy Inspector General himself, striking hours after he holds a "security meeting." It’s almost as if they know these meetings are just photo ops and the real action happens when everyone packs up and goes home.

The politicians, of course, are out in full force, yapping about "unacceptable" losses and demanding "immediate and decisive action." Governor Mutuma and Senator Murungi are threatening to withdraw support, as if that will magically make the guns disappear. Senator Murungi even wants to take 10 days off from the Senate to "camp in Meru" and make sure the police are "active on the ground." Bless his heart, he’ll probably be back in Nairobi by next week complaining about the heat.

Then there are the Meru MPs, led by Mugambi Rindikiri, demanding stolen animals be returned and the government cough up cash for funerals and lost property. They also want buffer zones and better roads. As if bandits care about roads. And Mpuru Aburi, bless his panicked soul, is worried about facing the electorate because of the government's "failure." It’s a performance, all of it.

And to top it all off, some chap named Mithika Linturi is starting a signature drive for a court petition. Compel the government to provide security and compensate victims. Because that’s worked so well in the past, hasn't it? This whole circus is just a temporary distraction, a way for the authorities to look like they're doing something before the next wave of attacks. Don't hold your breath for any real change.

These so-called "operations" are just a temporary pause in the chaos, a few heads might get rolled, a few arrests made to appease the media, but the core problem remains. The firearms are still flowing, the financiers are still hiding in plain sight, and the people who actually need protection are still caught in the crossfire. Just another day in Nairobi, really.
