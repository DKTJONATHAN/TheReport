Another day, another metal bird falling out of the sky because maintenance is apparently a suggestion rather than a rule in this country. Emurua Dikiir MP Johana Ng-eno is gone, and the usual requiem mass theater is in full swing. Six men are dead, and suddenly everyone has forgotten the venom that usually spills from these political podiums. It is the classic Kenyan way: live as a polarizing figure who makes half the country look over their shoulders, but die as a national hero.

Rigathi Gachagua is leading the choir, calling Ng-eno a "patriot" and an "agitator for wellbeing." Let-s translate that from politician-speak to Nairobi reality: he was a tribal kingpin who made folks in Mt Kenya lose sleep over their land in the Rift Valley. When a man spends his career stirring the ethnic pot, calling him a patriot is like calling a pickpocket a wealth redistributor. The hypocrisy is thick enough to choke on, but here we are, pretending the "mixed reviews" from his life were just minor misunderstandings.

We are told the chopper burst into flames seconds after take-off. Now we have the inevitable "thorough investigation" demand from the leadership. We all know how these scripts end. They bury the report deeper than the wreckage itself, while the public moves on to the next scandal. It-s almost as messy as when [ODM Leadership Disowns Activist Oketch Salah Amid Growing Internal Friction](/posts/odm-leadership-disowns-activist-oketch-salah-amid-growing-internal-friction), showing that in this country, you are only useful to the powers that be until you become a liability or a corpse.

Even the "State agents" are part of this morbid circus. They were busy recording private conversations between the legislator and the then-DP, because why focus on actual crime when you can play Big Brother? In this city, your secrets are just currency for the next political blackmail session. Even in death, the skeletons of these recorded deals are the only things that stay dry in the rain of crocodile tears falling at the funeral service.

Now the villagers remember Captain Were for his kindness, which is likely the only genuine thing in this entire mess. The rest of it is just a PR exercise for the living politicians who need to look sad for the cameras. We will see the big cars, the expensive suits, and the fake grief tomorrow at the burial. Then by Monday, the same leaders will be back to the same tribal rhetoric that Ng-eno was famous for, waiting for the next tragedy to reset the clock.
