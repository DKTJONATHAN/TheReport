Monica Juma is packing her bags for Vienna, leaving behind the chaos of Nairobi for the sanitized hallways of the United Nations. After years of "advising" on national security while the country dodged one crisis after another, she has been rewarded with the top job at the UN Office on Drugs and Crime. It is the ultimate Kenyan dream: serving the public until you can finally escape the public you serve for a tax-free salary in Euros.

Her CV is a dizzying list of every powerful ministry we have. From Energy to Defence to Foreign Affairs, Juma has been the ultimate insider. In Nairobi, we call this the "recycling program." You do not have to be effective; you just have to be present. While the youth are drowning in [the 'soft life' debt trap](/posts/the-soft-life-debt-trap-the-rising-rate-of-loan-app-suicides-among-young-professionals) trying to survive a crumbling economy, the people at the top are busy polishing their LinkedIn profiles for the next global appointment.

The irony of her heading an office dedicated to crime and drugs is not lost on anyone who has tried to walk through downtown Nairobi after sunset. We are told this is a "significant milestone" for Kenya, but in reality, it is just another example of [high-flying rhetoric and empty pockets](/posts/high-flying-rhetoric-and-empty-pockets--the-addis-ababa-special) that defines our diplomatic presence. We export our bureaucrats to solve the world's problems while our own local issues are left to "acting" directors and committee meetings that lead nowhere.

Secretary-General Guterres is "confident" in her leadership. Why wouldn't he be? She has the Oxford PhD and the right connections. But for those of us on the ground, a Kenyan official leading the UNODC feels like a dark joke. It is like hiring a fire chief from a city that is currently on fire. Vienna might get a strategic leader, but Nairobi just gets another empty office in the National Security Council and a "thank you for your service" that sounds more like a sigh of relief.

So, Juma joins the ranks of the global elite, trading Kileleshwa traffic for Austrian winters. She will talk about transnational crime and international cooperation in rooms that smell like expensive coffee and old money. Meanwhile, the streets she left behind will stay exactly the same - messy, dangerous, and completely ignored by the very people who claim to represent us on the world stage. Reality remains here; she is just moving to a better zip code.
