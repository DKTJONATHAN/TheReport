Ruto, Macron Rally Africa for Tech Sovereignty - More Like Tech Dependency Ploy

This isn't a call for independence, it's another round of the same old song and dance. Macron, with his usual flourish, is telling Africa to build its own AI, while conveniently forgetting that France itself is still trying to shake off the US tech grip. Expect more promises than actual infrastructure.

Macron, trying to spin his way out of France's historical baggage, landed in Nairobi preaching about "strategic autonomy" for Europe and Africa. He's lecturing us on not being consumers of US and Chinese tech, as if France has suddenly become a beacon of innovation instead of a follower. It’s all about Paris wanting a bigger slice of the pie, not about genuine empowerment for the continent.

The French president jabbed at the US and China, a classic move to position himself as the benevolent outsider offering a "pan-African" alternative. He’s talking about nuclear facilities for electrification, a grand vision that conveniently glosses over the immediate realities of who controls the power grids, and who gets charged for it. Meanwhile, Kenyan President Ruto is playing the neutral card, saying Kenya faces "forward," which in local parlance means "wherever the best deal is," not necessarily aligning with France. This whole summit feels like a rehash of old patron-client dynamics, just with a shiny new tech veneer.

Macron's grand pronouncements about 50 digital centers sound good on paper, but let's be real. We’ve seen these initiatives before, with outcomes that are often more about data collection for foreign interests than true African development. This is just another strategic move to cement French influence, using the youth and innovation buzzwords to mask the underlying economic and political agenda. It’s a familiar script, and frankly, it’s getting tiresome. This feels like a continuation of the same old power plays we've seen discussed when analyzing how [KDF Plants Trees, But Who's Really Sowing Service?](/posts/kdf-plants-trees--but-who-s-really-sowing-service).

Ruto, standing beside Macron, tried to play it cool, stating Kenya faces "forward, across the globe." Translation: we're not picking sides, we're looking for opportunities. It’s a smart move to avoid being pigeonholed, but the underlying message is clear: Africa is tired of being a pawn in global power games. The summit aims to showcase Africa's innovative capacity, but the real question is whether these partnerships will translate into tangible benefits for the continent or just more opportunities for French companies to exploit the narrative of mutual benefit.

Macron’s talk of expanding partnerships between universities and accelerating investment is all well and good, but it’s the same old tune. France wants to maintain its relevance in Africa by offering what it can, rather than truly investing in homegrown solutions. It’s a clever bit of diplomacy, repackaging old ambitions with a modern twist, but the underlying power imbalance remains.
