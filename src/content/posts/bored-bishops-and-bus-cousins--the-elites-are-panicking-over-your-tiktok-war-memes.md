Here we go again. Just when you thought the pulpit was strictly for prayers and judging your Sunday outfit, Bishop Francis Kamau of Christian Outreach has decided to pivot into economic forecasting. Apparently, the ongoing conflict in the Arab world is keeping him up at night - not because of the human cost, mind you, but because Kenya relies on that region for oil and "key commodities." It is giving very much "my V8 needs fuel and I do not want to pay extra at the pump" energy. The hypocrisy of preaching about spiritual wealth while sweating over the price of crude oil is the kind of comedy you just cannot buy.

The Bishop is also very concerned about your "misuse" of social media. He wants us to promote "peace and unity" online, which is a cute way of saying "stop making memes that make the elders uncomfortable." It is almost hilarious that Kamau is begging us to use digital platforms for peace when we all know that [OPERATION EPIC FAIL: WHY OUR SOCIAL MEDIA STARS THINK THEY ARE GOING TO WAR](/posts/operation-epic-fail--why-our-social-media-stars-think-they-are-going-to-war) is the only reason half these influencers have a following. The church is finally realizing they cannot control the narrative once it hits the TikTok streets, and they are absolutely terrified of it.

Then we have Andrew Mwangi from Narokline Services Limited joining the choir. He is worried about his Dubai imports - vehicles, fuel, and textiles. Let us be real: he is worried about his profit margins. He warns that instability will lead to higher transport fares for the "ordinary citizen," which is the classic corporate move of using the poor as a shield to protect their own bank accounts. If the price of your designer "Dubai" fits goes up, do not blame the war; blame the businessmen who refuse to take a pay cut to keep their buses moving.

The most delusional part of this entire presser? Mwangi is out here appealing to the G8 countries to help reconcile Donald Trump and Iran. I am sorry, but since when did a bus company director from Narok become a geopolitical mediator? The ego required to think that the G8 is listening to a press conference in Kenya about a beef between two nuclear-capable powers is the kind of main character energy we usually reserve for C-list socialites and failed rappers. It is messy, it is desperate, and it is entirely too much.

To top it all off, these leaders are wagging their fingers at the youth for "politicizing" the conflict on social media. They call it "unintended consequences," but we all know they just want the Gen Z kids to stop being so loud and disruptive. While the elites are busy playing "Global Peace Envoy" to save their textile imports, the rest of us are just watching the mask slip. If you want to see real drama, do not look at the Middle East; look at these men scrambling to keep their luxury lifestyles intact while pretending to care about your Twitter fingers.
