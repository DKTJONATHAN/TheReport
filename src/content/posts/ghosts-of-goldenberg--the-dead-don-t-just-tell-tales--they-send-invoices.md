Welcome to Kenya, where a company can be dead for a decade and still find its way into the Court of Appeal. Westmont Holdings, a Malaysian firm that was wound up when most of us were still in primary school, is back from the afterlife. They want Sh3.5 billion for a deposit paid back in 1997 for the Grand Regency. In this city, even the ghosts have better legal representation than you do.

This whole mess smells like the Goldenberg scandal because it is. We are talking about Kamlesh Pattni and the Central Bank of Kenya dancing a tango that started thirty years ago. It is the kind of legacy that keeps on haunting our coffers while the rest of us struggle to breathe. You would think thirty years is enough to bury a corpse, but in our courts, a well-connected ghost is as good as a living billionaire.

The timing is always perfect, isn't it? Just as we are distracted by the shifting political landscape, the vultures start circling. It reminds me of how [BABA IS GONE AND THE VULTURES ARE HAVING A FIELD DAY](/posts/baba-is-gone-and-the-vultures-are-having-a-field-day) as the old guard looks for new ways to feast on what remains of the public purse. There is no rest for the wicked, and certainly no rest for the Kenyan taxpayer who has to foot the bill for these ancient disputes.

If this case goes the wrong way, we are looking at a Sh3.5 billion hole in our national budget. That is money that could have fixed roads or hospitals, but instead, it might go to a firm that technically does not even exist. The judiciary is treating this like a serious legal inquiry instead of the daylight robbery it clearly resembles. They call it justice; I call it a well-orchestrated heist with a very long fuse.

So, prepare your wallets, Nairobi. The ghosts are hungry and the lawyers are ready to serve them a three-course meal paid for by your sweat. Don't act surprised when the news drops that the money is gone. This is Kenya, where the only thing more certain than death and taxes is that dead men will always find a way to tax the living.
