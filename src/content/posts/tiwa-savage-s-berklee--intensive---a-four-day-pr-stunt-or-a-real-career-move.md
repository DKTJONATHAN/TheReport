Oh, darling, pull up a chair because the "Queen of Afrobeats" is back in the headlines, and no, it is not for another fashion week slay or a cryptic "Who Is He?" post. Tiwa Savage has decided to play the role of the benevolent godmother by bringing Berklee College of Music to Lagos in April 2026. They are calling it a "four-day intensive" program. Four days? Please. You can barely learn how to properly mix a demo in four days, let alone master the "business of music" in a city as chaotic as Lagos. It is giving high-society summer camp vibes, but with more expensive lace and better lighting.

The press release is dripping with the usual corporate word salad about "investing in Africa’s next generation" and "shaping global charts." We all know the real tea, though. Berklee is an Ivy League-level expensive institution that has finally realized Afrobeats is the golden goose. They are sending their suits to Nigeria to scout the next big thing before Sony or Universal can get their claws in deeper. It is a strategic move wrapped in the shiny foil of "educational outreach." Tiwa, an alum herself, is the perfect face for this because it keeps her name relevant as the "Artistic Advisor" while the younger generation of Tems and Ayra Starr fans start to forget who pioneered the sound.

And let us talk about the "free" price tag. They claim there is no cost for accepted participants to enroll, which sounds lovely on a glossy flyer. However, the fine print says participants are responsible for their own travel and lodging in Lagos. Have you seen the price of a decent hotel in Victoria Island lately? It is giving the same energy as those [First World Problems, Third World Solutions: Germany Discovers the Thika Tunnel](/posts/first-world-problems--third-world-solutions--germany-discovers-the-thika-tunnel) narratives where the "solution" still requires you to have a massive bank account just to show up. By the time these "emerging artists" pay for flights and a week of Lagos traffic, they might as well have just paid for a semester online.

Tiwa’s quote about being "humbled" is the cherry on top. We love Mama Jam Jam, but "humble" has never been in her vocabulary, especially not when she is securing bridges to her own legacy. This program is essentially a four-day networking mixer where the elite get to rub shoulders with Berklee deans while the actual "struggling" artists are likely filtered out by a "selective admissions process" that favors those with the right look and the right followers. It is a classic industry gatekeeping move dressed up as a scholarship opportunity.

The program ends with a student performance blending West African traditions with global styles. Translation: a talent show for the Berklee executives to see who is worth a "partial scholarship" that will still leave the student in debt for the next decade. If these kids really want to thrive in the music field, they should spend those four days figuring out how to get their masters back from the labels, not learning how to harmonize in a classroom. But hey, at least the closing ceremony will have great photo ops. Stay cynical, sweetie; the industry never does anything for free without a camera rolling.
