TATUU SINGER ANGELA MWANDANDA BACK ON STAGE FOR MENTAL HEALTH PLAY, EXPECT NO MIRACLES

The Nairobi entertainment scene, a place where dreams go to get chewed up and spat out, is seeing another blast from the past. Angela Mwandanda, one half of the once ubiquitous Tatuu duo, is dusting off her acting shoes for a play about mental health. Yes, the same Angela who belted out hits like "Teso" and "Ulopa" back when Ogopa Deejays was the only sound in town, is now tackling the delicate subject of the mind. Apparently, the creative process starts upstairs, who knew?

After a stint with Tatuu and various other gigs in music, film, and theatre, Mwandanda is now the National Coordinator for the Recording Industry of Kenya. But this Friday, she's back on stage for "In the Seashell Hum." She claims to strongly advocate for mental wellness, especially as an artist, because, you know, the mind is where the magic supposedly happens. Let’s hope this play offers more than just platitudes, unlike some of the "divorce traps" people fall into, a topic that seems to be as common as bad music these days.

Her recent Kalasha Awards nomination for Best Lead Actress is a "welcome surprise." She loves to perform, bless her heart. It's a chance to finally be recognized for acting, not just for the pop tunes she churned out years ago. Apparently, the whole Tatuu thing started because they all loved pretending on stage at Phoenix Players. Good to know some things haven't changed - the struggle for recognition continues.

Mwandanda talks about her career evolution, moving from being typecast to taking on more "challenging and thought-provoking roles." She's also working behind the scenes with RIKE, which she admits is "unglamorous" but supposedly leads to "meaningful change." The "Sonic Nation" documentary she's producing aims to preserve Kenyan music history, because apparently, much of it only exists in the memories of people who are already dropping off the radar. A noble effort, I suppose, if you're into that sort of nostalgia.

When it comes to women in the arts, she’s brutally honest: "We still remain underrepresented on many fronts." Despite the progress, she hopes women will continue to "take up space." Finally, on the mental health play, she’s drawn to the writer and the challenge of tackling a "sensitive subject." She stresses the need for "safe spaces and safe people" because, let's face it, the creative world can be a brutal place, and mental health is often trivialized.

Her advice to emerging artists? Diversify. Don't just stick to one thing. If you can sing, can you write for others? If you can present, can you strategize? It’s a volatile industry, after all. So, while Mwandanda is returning to the spotlight, let’s see if this play can actually bring some meaningful dialogue, or if it's just another fleeting moment in the endless cycle of Nairobi's entertainment scene.
