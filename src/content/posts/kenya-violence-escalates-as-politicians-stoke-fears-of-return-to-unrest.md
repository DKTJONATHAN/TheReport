So, another politician, Senator Godfrey Osotsi, got a rude welcome to the streets of Kisumu. Coffee break interrupted by hooded youths, a quick beating, phones and valuables gone. All caught on CCTV, of course, because in Kenya, we broadcast our shame. The police are "investigating," which is code for "we'll eventually file a report and forget about it." The senator claims it wasn't about robbery, but about him not supporting the president's re-election bid. Because why would it be about something as mundane as money when we can have good old-fashioned political thuggery?

This whole song and dance is as predictable as the sunrise. We've been here before, haven't we? Since the 90s, politicians have been turning unemployed youth into their personal attack dogs. Remember 2007? About 1,500 dead because some leaders couldn't handle losing. Now, with Rigathi Gachagua eyeing the presidency after his impeachment - talk about holding a grudge - and infighting in the major parties, the temperature is rising faster than a matatu on a Friday evening. Interior Minister Kipchumba Murkomen admits the government is "struggling" to contain gangs that have become "sophisticated and decentralised." Sophisticated? They’re just better at hiding their masters, that's all.

Murkomen, bless his cotton socks, acknowledges that these gangs are "owned by political leaders." Chaos, indeed. And an irresponsible leader is a threat to national security? Tell us something we don't know. They've arrested 300 suspects, found weapons and gadgets, but not a single politician. Shocking. It's like trying to catch a cockroach in a dark room with a sieve. These gangs mutate, rebrand, and pop up with new names every election cycle. They’re not just street thugs anymore; they’re "entrenched institutions." We've even seen reports of how [Russian State Hackers Exploit Microsoft Office Vulnerability]( /posts/russian-state-hackers-exploit-microsoft-office-vulnerability), showing how easily systems can be compromised, and it’s no different with our political "security."

The former Deputy President, Gachagua, has been on the receiving end of this "state-sponsored criminal network" business more than two dozen times, apparently. He blames them for trying to derail his presidential ambitions. Meanwhile, opposition leaders accuse the police of either being in cahovery or just looking the other way while these goons, armed with crude weapons, descend on rallies. And heaven forbid anyone dares to question President Ruto. A 28-year-old man got shot dead during a clash with anti-Ruto supporters. The government spokesperson denies any state sponsorship, promising accountability. We’ll see.

It’s not just the opposition getting roughed up, either. A senatorial aspirant from the ruling alliance was kicked out of a church service in Kakamega. By-elections in November were a mess of attacked polling agents and clashing armed groups. Security analysts call it "organised street violence executed by hired gangs operating with precision and impunity." Professor Makau Mutua, an adviser to Ruto, calls it a "Kenyan culture, an epidemic." He's not wrong. Hundreds of gangs, over 120 linked to politicians, according to a state report. The Inspector General of Police promises it will "stop soon." We've heard that before.

The Interior Minister blames "operational challenges," including corruption and leaks. Critics say the government's response is all talk and no action. The real question, as a security analyst put it, isn't who the goons are, but "who sends them, funds them and protects them?" The architects remain in the shadows, while ordinary Kenyans are left hoping the authorities can do more than just issue stern warnings before the next election cycle descends into more chaos.
