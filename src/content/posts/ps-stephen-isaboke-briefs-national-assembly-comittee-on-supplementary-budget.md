PS Stephen Isaboke Disses National Assembly With Supplementary Budget
WARNING: Politicians are lining their pockets again while promising to "strengthen" your digital life. Expect more talk, less action.
Stephen Isaboke, the Principal Secretary for the State Department of... well, something important involving communications, apparently, decided to grace the National Assembly's Departmental Committee on Communication, Information and Innovation with his presence. He was there to discuss the Supplementary I Budget for FY 2025/2026. Because that's what they do in government, right? They talk about budgets, usually after they've already spent the money.

The main agenda? Strengthening the broadcasting, telecommunications, and digital ecosystem. Sounds fancy, doesn't it? Translation: more buzzwords, more promises about faster internet, and probably more fees for us. PS Isaboke apparently "welcomed the insightful engagement and constructive feedback," which is code for "they told us what we wanted to hear, and we'll nod along while continuing to do whatever we want."

He was flanked by "senior officials and CEOs of parastatals." Translation: a whole entourage of people to look important and probably to help divvy up the loot. They're all keen on "enhancing service delivery for all Kenyans." Yeah, right. We've heard that one before. It's usually followed by more taxes or even worse service. Remember when we thought the "10k Circus" was bad? This is just a bigger, more expensive circus. Let's be real. When politicians talk about strengthening digital ecosystems, what they really mean is more control and more opportunities for kickbacks. It’s a classic Nairobi move. They'll promise you the moon, and deliver a handful of dust. Maybe this time they'll actually do something, but I wouldn't hold my breath. This is just another day in the Kenyan political theatre.

They'll probably make a big show of "deliberations," but at the end of the day, it's just a way to justify spending more of our money. And we, the common mwananchi, are left to wonder where it all goes. Probably into someone's offshore account, while our data gets harvested and our internet speeds remain a joke. It's a digital jungle out there, and these guys are just looking for their next meal.
