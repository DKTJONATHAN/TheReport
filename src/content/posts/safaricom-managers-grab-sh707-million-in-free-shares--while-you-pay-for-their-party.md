So, Safaricom's bigwigs are at it again, scooping up a cool Sh707.5 million worth of shares, absolutely free, in the year ending March 2026. Apparently, this is their reward for "past performance." Funny how their performance seems to consistently involve lining their own pockets while the rest of us are still figuring out how to afford airtime. Last year, they nabbed 15.4 million shares. This year, it’s 20.1 million. The only thing growing faster than their share count is the gap between them and us regular folks.

Peter Ndegwa, the CEO, now sits on shares worth a staggering Sh425.8 million, a significant jump from his previous holdings. Dilip Pal, the CFO, also saw his stake increase, adding a tidy sum to his personal fortune. This whole "stock compensation scheme" they keep pouring billions into is just a fancy way of saying they're giving themselves a bonus, funded by the company’s success - a success that, let’s be honest, is built on the backs of millions of Kenyans topping up their phones daily.

And the best part? Safaricom buys these shares on the open market and hands them over to employees, who can then cash out after three years. It’s a win-win for them, a drain on the company’s resources that could be used for more practical things, like network upgrades that actually reach the remote areas. While other NSE-listed firms are figuring out how to genuinely motivate staff, even contemplating alternative remuneration schemes like Car and General, Safaricom just keeps handing out free money to its top performers. It’s almost like they’re not even trying to hide it anymore.

This isn’t like those other Esops where new stock is issued, diluting existing shareholders. Oh no, Safaricom is too clever for that. They buy back their own shares, ensuring the market price stays propped up while their chosen few reap the rewards. Meanwhile, the share price has nearly tripled since October 2023, thanks to profit growth and dividend payouts. So, the rally that makes these free shares so valuable is also directly benefiting the company and, by extension, its shareholders. But it’s the managers who get the direct, no-effort payday. It makes you wonder if they even remember how the rest of Kenya's stock market is going mobile, with your M-Pesa potentially acting as your stockbroker.

While the company claims these schemes align employee and shareholder interests, giving shares for free or at a negligible cost weakens that alignment. Most of these shares end up with top executives, the "critical talent" driving strategy. Never mind that most NSE-listed firms pay their employees, including senior managers, in actual cash. Safaricom's approach is clearly a more "generous" way to reward its chosen few, a system that benefits the already privileged. It’s a good old-fashioned Nairobi story: the powerful get richer, and the rest of us get to pay for it.
