Billboard really thought they could slide into our Sunday morning with a "25 Biggest One-Hit Wonders of the 21st Century" list and not get dragged for filth. They ranked Nigeria’s golden boy, Rema, at number six for the "Calm Down" remix, reducing a global anthem to a series of "lo-lo-lo" onomatopoeias. It is the kind of high-brow condescension we have come to expect from Western gatekeepers who think African music is just a collection of funny noises until a white pop star sprinkles some holy water on it. The writer actually suggested Rema "took a swing" and Selena Gomez "shockingly" agreed to help him out. Please.

The sheer audacity to frame Selena Gomez as some benevolent saint of the slums is hilarious when you look at the facts. Selena herself posted that Rema "changed her life forever" and thanked him for choosing her. Yet, Billboard wants us to believe she was doing missionary work in the studio. This is the classic Western playbook: diminish the African creator, center the Western collaborator, and pretend the record was not already a monster hit before she hopped on. It is giving "I discovered Great Britain" energy, and frankly, we are tired of the historical revisionism.

Labeling Rema a one-hit wonder because he is not camping out in the top ten of the US Hot 100 every week is peak delusion. The man is selling out arenas across Europe and Africa while his peers are struggling to keep their lights on. But of course, if it does not happen in America, it does not count, right? This obsession with foreign validation is a disease. Much like how [Kenya Is Rotten To The Core](/posts/new-post) due to systemic failure, the global music industry is built on a hierarchy that refuses to see African success as legitimate unless it is measured by a Billboard chart. Rema just debuted at 29 on the Hot 100 with a feature, but apparently, that is just "beginner’s luck" to these people.

We have to talk about the "global ambition tax." Everyone wants that US radio play and those sweet, sweet Western streaming dollars, but it comes with the cost of being shoved into a box. The Grammys did the same thing with their "Best African Music Performance" category. It is a "separate but equal" playground designed to keep us from competing for the big trophies. They want our rhythm, they want our "vibes," but they do not want to admit that the center of the musical universe has shifted to Lagos and Accra.

The real problem is that we still wait for Pitchfork or Rolling Stone to tell us if our own music is good. We have platforms like TurnTable Charts and Native Mag doing the actual work of documenting our culture with nuance, yet the industry treats a Billboard mention like a divine revelation. It is time to stop being supplicants at the altar of Western media. If we do not respect our own institutions and our own metrics, we cannot act surprised when some writer in New York reduces a multi-billion stream career to a "lo-lo-lo" joke. Respect yourself so they have no choice but to follow suit.
