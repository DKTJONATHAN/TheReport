Welcome to the annual Nairobi swimming gala, where the entry fee is your dignity and the prize is a stalled engine. Every year the clouds gather, and every year our glorious leaders act like gravity and water are new inventions. The "drainage systems" in this city are little more than glorified trash bins that overflow the moment a cloud looks at them funny. Thousands of commuters are currently stuck for hours, watching their cars drown because someone at City Hall likely ate the budget for infrastructure repairs years ago.

Out in Kisumu, the Sondu-Miriu River decided it didn’t like its banks anymore, leaving over 380 households in Wang-chieng Ward living in a swamp. The Kenya Red Cross is out there giving "early warnings" like that actually helps a man whose farm is already under six feet of water. It is the same old story of [profit over pulse](/posts/profit-over-pulse--the-dystopian-reality-behind-the-pitt-s-medical-gadgetry), where human lives are just statistics to be managed by NGOs while the people in power look for the next photo opportunity. Warnings are cheap when you are not the one sleeping on a wet mattress.

Senator Sifuna says there are "no excuses" and calls the situation an indictment. Well, thank you, Captain Obvious. We know it is an indictment. It is an indictment of decades of eating money meant for the common man. While the elite are busy getting appointed to high-flying UN jobs, the average Kenyan is busy negotiating with a boda-boda rider who just doubled his fare because he bought a fifty-shilling umbrella. It is the Nairobi hustle at its most desperate and pathetic level, where everyone is trying to squeeze a coin out of a disaster.

Even the courts are joining the conversation, talking about "structural inequality" and migrant workers. They should take a walk down Mombasa Road during a downpour if they want to see real structural inequality in action. Justice is a luxury when you are wading through raw sewage just to get to a job that barely pays for the salt in your food. The Met Department warned us the rain would peak, but a warning to a person in a flood-prone slum is like a weather report to a fish. They have nowhere else to go.

We are told millions are being allocated to "beneficiary households" for 2026, but that does nothing for the woman whose house is floating away today. In this city, you either have a 4x4 or you have a prayer. Most of us are just praying the rain stops before the authorities find a way to tax the puddle in your living room. It is just another day in a country where the only thing that works efficiently is the tax collector's calculator and the PR machine of the ruling class.
