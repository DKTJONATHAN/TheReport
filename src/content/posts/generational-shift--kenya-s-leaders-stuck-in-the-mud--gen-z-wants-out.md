So, the esteemed President Ruto and his former deputy Gachagua have decided to give us all a free show. Forget about the crushing poverty, the failing infrastructure, the millions starving - that's too much work. No, their priorities are far more pressing: dissecting each other's physical ailments and slinging mud like toddlers in a playground. One minute it's about ears sticking out, the next it's accusations of thievery and, well, things you wouldn't repeat in polite company. Honestly, you'd think with the state of this country, they'd have better things to do.

At first, it was almost funny, wasn't it? A bit of slapstick from the pampered political elite, just what we needed to distract us from the fact that 125 Kenyans own more wealth than 43 million of us combined. But the amusement quickly curdled into something uglier: shame. How can we, a nation of 50 million, parade these two buffoons as our finest? It’s a global embarrassment. It’s like showing the world our most broken toys.

Then came the anger, sharp and bitter. These jet-setters, living in their mansions and flitting about on helicopters, seem to have forgotten we're not some first-world utopia. Kenya is a Third World country, choking on underdevelopment. While they're busy debating who looks thinner, Oxfam is telling us 20 million Kenyans are "seriously hungry." Our roads are flooded, the north is parched, and people die because they can't afford a doctor. Meanwhile, every week, the Auditor-General uncovers billions stolen, and our State House has a bigger budget than the White House. It's enough to make you spit.

This is the system these politicians are psychologically, ideologically, and financially addicted to. They're stuck in the past, still playing the tribal card, still peddling handouts like it's the only currency they know. They hold "handout rallies," for crying out loud. It’s a joke that’s gone on for too long. We've seen this script before. Remember when we discussed how Ruto’s tax breaks were supposed to magically fix everything? [Ruto's Tax Breaks: Kenya Targets Deals, But Don't Hold Your Breath](/posts/ruto-s-tax-breaks--kenya-targets-deals--but-don-t-hold-your-breath). It’s the same old song, different verse.

The real problem isn't just their petty squabbles; it's their fundamental inability to reform a system that benefits them. They can't, and won't, fix a machine they're all plugged into. But there’s a glimmer of hope, a different vision emerging. It's the Gen Z generation, seeing Kenya not as a collection of squabbling tribes, but as a nation desperately in need of a renaissance. They’re tired of the mudslinging and the empty promises. They want a real future, not just another round of political theatre.
