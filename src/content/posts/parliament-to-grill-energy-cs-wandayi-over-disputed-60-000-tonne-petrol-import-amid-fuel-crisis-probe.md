So, Parliament's Energy Committee has finally woken up and decided to summon CS Opiyo Wandayi. Apparently, 60,000 tonnes of petrol just waltzed into the country without anyone official knowing or approving it. They're calling it a "disputed" import, which in Kenyan terms means someone probably pocketed a hefty sum while we all get to struggle with fuel shortages and, surprise, potentially substandard fuel. Wandayi missed the first summons, naturally, probably busy with "official duty abroad" while our economy takes a beating. It's just another chapter in the ongoing story of how things work here - or rather, how they *don't* work when there's a quick buck to be made.

The committee is apparently loaded with questions, 16 to be exact, for the CS. They want to know who gave the green light for this shady deal, why it bypassed the usual government-to-government arrangements, and what exactly went wrong with oversight. It's rich, isn't it? They're asking for documents, verbal versus written approvals, and any ministry or agency records. As if those records are going to magically appear and reveal the truth. We've already seen the former PS, EPRA DG, and KPC MD shown the door, which is usually code for "scapegoats needed." It's all part of the theatre, designed to make us *think* something is being done.

The entire circus is happening while many parts of the country are still grappling with fuel shortages. So, not only are we potentially getting watered-down fuel, but we can't even get enough of the good stuff. The committee is demanding to know where the quality assurance chain failed, from pre-shipment inspections to final compliance. They want to know if test results were fiddled with, waived, or conditionally approved. It’s like asking a fox to guard the henhouse, but then again, what else do you expect from a system that seems to thrive on incompetence and graft? This is classic Kenya, reminiscent of how corruption and incompetence seem to be the constant architects of disaster, like the South C Building Collapse that exposed similar systemic rot.

They're asking about disciplinary action, investigations, and what measures are being taken to trace or recall the dodgy fuel. They also want to know how safeguards failed and what immediate corrective actions are being implemented. It's a lot of "ifs" and "whens," and frankly, I'm not holding my breath. The calls for legislative changes and using technology to reduce human discretion are also standard fare. It's the same song and dance every time one of these scandals blows up. We'll get promises of reform, new systems, and then, before you know it, another 60,000 tonnes of questionable petrol will be on its way.

Committee chair David Gikaria is all huffy about the CS missing the meeting, calling it "unfortunate" and saying "people don’t want to be accountable." Obadiah Barongo, another MP, calls the absence "suspect." They're demanding answers and responsibility, and warning that "Parliament will not be taken for granted." Good for them. But let's be real, this is just another Tuesday in Nairobi. The economy is at stake, they say. Of course it is. It’s always at stake when these deals go down. We’ll see if Wandayi shows up, and more importantly, we’ll see if any real accountability actually materializes, or if it’s just another parliamentary performance.
