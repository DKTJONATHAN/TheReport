Justin Young, fresh off his "big project" adapting Jane Austen’s unfinished *Sanditon*, is now tackling *Crookhaven* for the screen. His pronouncements to the BBC reveal a weary pragmatism about the endeavor. "If you want to meet people who are protective, it's Jane Austen fans," he quips, a thinly veiled jab at the inherent difficulties of satisfying purists. This sentiment underscores a broader, cynical understanding of how fundamentally different media operate.

The core of Young's complaint, dressed up as an observation, is the insatiable hunger of television for constant narrative propulsion. "When I first started writing for television, the thing that was so shocking to me was how much story you need for television. It's unbelievable, it's like a machine," he states. This "machine" requires a relentless barrage of "big surprises" every five to ten minutes, a pace that often sacrifices depth for cheap thrills. The adaptation of *Crookhaven* will undoubtedly be shaped by this imperative, demanding a simplified, frenetic approach that may bear little resemblance to its source material.

Despite claims that the show is "aimed at all ages" and that the young cast is "sophisticated," the underlying aim is clear: to create a product palatable to the broadest possible demographic. Young insists he "never... thought of writing for kids," but the phrase "family friendly TV drama" speaks volumes about the intended lowest common denominator. Producer Laniado echoes this sentiment, hoping families can "sit down together" and that the show "engages everyone." This isn't about genuine artistic merit or universal resonance; it's about maximizing eyeballs and minimizing the risk of alienating any significant segment of the potential audience. The promise that "anyone will be able to see themselves in that world" is a standard marketing platitude, a hollow reassurance that the creative team has little faith in the actual substance of their work to achieve genuine connection. The real hope, buried beneath the PR speak, is simply to avoid being a colossal failure in a crowded, indifferent market.
