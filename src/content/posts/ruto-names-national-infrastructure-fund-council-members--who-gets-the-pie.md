Ruto Names National Infrastructure Fund Council Members: More Jobs For The Boys?

The President has 'operationalized' a new fund, which sounds grand. But let's be real, it's just another way to shuffle the deck and put more of the usual suspects in charge, promising progress while lining pockets.

President William Ruto has apparently decided to get serious about infrastructure by assembling a seven-member governing council for the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF). Leading the pack is National Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi, who I'm sure will bring his vast experience in… well, being a CS. Rounding out the official crew are the Governor of the Central Bank, Dr. Kamau Thugge, and Attorney General Dorcas Oduor, appointed as statutory members. Because nothing says "infrastructure development" like the people who already control the money and the laws. This whole setup smells like the same old musical chairs we've seen before, reminiscent of [Oburu Takes ODM Helm, Osotsi Axed: More Musical Chairs in the Political Circus](/posts/oburu-takes-odm-helm--osotsi-axed--more-musical-chairs-in-the-political-circus).

Then we have the "other members," a motley crew of former presidents and CEOs. We've got Prof. Benedict Oramah, former president and chairperson of Afreximbank, and Faith Boinett from Kenya Pipeline Company. And to top it off, KCB Group CEO Paul Russo and Liberty Holdings chairperson Richard Etemesi. It seems like if you've held a significant position in Kenya, you're automatically qualified to sit on a council that's supposed to oversee a Ksh 106 billion fund. Don't expect any radical new ideas from this bunch; they're more likely to maintain the status quo.

The NIF Act, now apparently capitalized with KSh 106 billion from the KPC IPO, states this council is responsible for recruiting the fund's board and overseeing investment policy. Felix Koskei, Head of Public Service, assures us their immediate priority is recruiting the Fund’s Board of Directors, who will then recruit a CEO. This is the classic Kenyan strategy: create a council to hire people to hire more people to maybe, possibly, eventually get something done. It's a bureaucratic labyrinth designed to look like progress.

The appointees are set to serve for three years, renewable once. That’s ample time to ensure their connections are solidified and their terms are extended. This whole exercise feels less about building roads and more about building careers for the well-connected. We’ve seen this play out before, where promises of development fade into the background, much like the hope for [Kenya's 2027 Race Reopens Old Fault Lines: Another Round of Empty Promises](/posts/kenya-s-2027-race-reopens-old-fault-lines--another-round-of-empty-promises). Let’s just hope this fund doesn’t become another example of [From Courts to Courts: Why a Lawyer Running the Sports Fund is the Ultimate Kenyan Joke](/posts/from-courts-to-courts--why-a-lawyer-running-the-sports-fund-is-the-ultimate-kenyan-joke).
