IRAN WAR HITS KENYA FUEL RETAILERS RUNNING SHORT OF SUPPLIES

Just when you thought Nairobi couldn't get any more chaotic, here we are, struggling to keep our wheels turning. The esteemed head of Kenya’s independent fuel retailers, Martin Chomba, has let the cat out of the bag: hundreds of dealers are gasping for air, running drier than a politician's promise. Apparently, this whole Iran war drama is causing ripples, or more like tidal waves, through our supply chain. About 20% of the outlets are already feeling the pinch. And why? Because the brilliant minds at the regulator, EPRA, decided to freeze pump prices. Yes, you read that right. Global oil costs are doing a jig upwards, but our fuel prices are stuck in neutral. Classic Kenya.

Chomba, bless his realist heart, is predicting dealers will start hoarding fuel faster than influencers stockpile sponsored content, all in anticipation of the inevitable price hikes next month. It’s a vicious cycle, and guess who gets squeezed? Us, the common mwananchi, trying to get to work or, you know, escape the city's perpetual madness. African countries, like ours, are apparently prime targets for this kind of disruption. We get all our fuel from the Middle East through these sweet government-to-government deals. Sounds secure, right? Except when there's a war messing with the Strait of Hormuz, which, by the way, is a pretty crucial choke point for about a fifth of the world's oil.

"We have constrained supply," Chomba states, and his association represents a whopping 68% of the market. So, this isn't just a few dodgy petrol stations. He reckons that in two weeks, it'll be a full-blown crisis with most outlets running on fumes, assuming the Middle East continues its penchant for drama. This comes hot on the heels of EPRA deciding on March 14th to keep prices stubbornly fixed, despite the international market screaming otherwise. It's a textbook example of how our grand infrastructure projects, like the SGR to Kisumu, seem to bring more debt than actual progress, and this fuel situation feels like another chapter in that same old story.

While EPRA's Director General Daniel Kiptoo Bargoria is spouting lines about "sufficient stocks," a ruling party lawmaker, Nelson Koech, is more candid, pointing fingers at "speculation, panic buying, and hoarding... particularly hoarding by oil marketers." It seems everyone's playing the same game, anticipating the next price surge. POAK has been begging the authorities to ditch these government-to-government deals and let marketers buy from private suppliers. It's a contingency measure, a lifeline, but so far, it seems our pleas are falling on deaf ears. Just like that Talanta Stadium dream, this could easily turn into another Talanta Scam if not handled with more than just hot air. The real shock is indeed on its way, and it smells suspiciously like empty fuel tanks.
