So, the "global refined petroleum products supply" is catching up with us. Fancy talk for "we're screwed again." Apparently, the Middle East is throwing a tantrum around the Strait of Hormuz, making it harder to get the fuel we need. And as if that wasn't enough, some Somali pirates decided to play dress-up with an oil tanker, scaring the shipping lines into hiding their vessels. This whole "abundance of crude oil" narrative seems to have conveniently forgotten about the actual *usable* fuel part.

Here in Nairobi, it's the usual story. Motorists are doing the rounds, from one empty pump to another, while taxi drivers and boda boda riders are already hiking prices because, well, they have to hustle. Apparently, the "whole of CBD, petrol stations are empty." It’s a beautiful chaos, isn't it? Independent dealers, who are supposed to serve the rest of the country, are being squeezed dry by the big Oil Marketing Companies (OMCs) who are either capping sales or just locking them out completely. These OMCs, who corner a huge chunk of the market, are likely playing the same old game of hoarding in anticipation of more price hikes, just like they did last month. It's a masterclass in how the little guy always gets trampled.

The government, bless their hearts, insists "supply remains stable." Stable for who? For the fat cats at the OMCs while the rest of us are left scrambling? The Petroleum Outlets Association of Kenya chairman himself admits it's global supply constraints, vessel delays, and reduced volumes. Meanwhile, the only major fuel deliveries scheduled are a "one petrol delivery" and a "diesel consignment" due later this month. Goldman Sachs is already wailing about depleting fuel buffers, warning of risks to aviation and industry. But hey, as long as our leaders are jetting off on their Sh3.1 Billion foreign trips, who cares about the actual fuel in our tanks, right?

This whole mess, from the Hormuz crisis to pirates playing games, is pushing freight costs higher and threatening to add more fuel to the already raging inflationary fire that's burning through the Kenyan economy. Close to 800 vessels, including over 450 oil tankers, are stranded globally. Shipping schedules are in the bin, and insurers are suddenly feeling brave enough to avoid anything remotely risky. It’s a perfect storm, and guess who’s standing in the rain? Us. This is just another reminder that when global markets get choppy, Kenya is usually the first to feel the cold splash.
