WORLD BANK SLAPS 2 KENYAN FIRMS WITH 21-MONTH BAN OVER KSH149.8 BILLION SCHEME

The World Bank has finally admitted what many of us have known for ages: consultants and these big international projects are often just a fancy way to siphon money. Two Kenyan firms, along with a Rwandan one, are now cooling their heels for 21 months after getting caught red-handed in a Ksh149.8 billion electricity scam. Fancy that.

PricewaterhouseCoopers Associates Africa Ltd., PricewaterhouseCoopers Limited Kenya, and PricewaterhouseCoopers Rwanda Limited - try saying that five times fast while lining your pockets - are the unlucky ones this time. They were caught using confidential information to try and snag contracts for Ethiopia's power projects. It seems even the supposedly clean international money has a sticky residue. This isn't exactly surprising; corruption has a way of clinging to these massive schemes, much like the incompetence exposed in the [South C Building Collapse](posts/south-c-building-collapse-corruption-incompetency-kenya).

The World Bank, in its infinite wisdom, announced this ban on March 18, 2026. Apparently, these "consultants" decided to play dirty in 2019, getting their hands on procurement secrets to influence who got the gig for Ethiopia's financial reporting standards. Winning that would have given them serious sway in one of East Africa's biggest power players. They didn't stop there, eyeing another juicy deal for fixed assets.

Things apparently got even murkier during the execution of one of these contracts. PwC Associates started fudging details about their "key experts" and conveniently forgot to mention all the subcontractors they roped in. It’s the same old story: when large sums of money are involved, transparency goes out the window faster than a politician’s promise. The firms, to their credit, admitted guilt - probably to avoid an even longer timeout.

So, while the World Bank pats itself on the back for catching these few, it’s a stark reminder that the system is rigged. These firms might be out for a while, but you can bet your bottom shilling that as soon as the dust settles, they, or others just like them, will be back, looking for the next Ksh149.8 billion opportunity. It’s a cycle as predictable as the debt traps countries like Bangladesh find themselves in.

The World Bank is banning PwC Associates Africa, PwC Kenya, and PwC Rwanda for fraud and collusion in a massive Ethiopian electricity project.
