Darling, we all knew that "content is king," but apparently, in Kenya, that king is wearing a hood and carrying a tire. CS Murkomen just dropped a bombshell report that confirms what any sane person with a Twitter-now-X feed already suspected: our obsession with viral "instant justice" videos is turning the country into a real-life horror movie. It turns out that while you are scrolling for the latest celebrity break-up, your neighbors in Kiambu and Nairobi are busy filming lynchings for the "gram." It is the ultimate dark side of pop culture that nobody wants to tag.

The Ministry of Interior is pointing the finger squarely at social media for the surge in mob violence, claiming these graphic videos are triggering copycat behavior. It is the ultimate toxic trend. People see a thief being "handled" on their timeline and suddenly feel like they have the moral authority of a High Court judge. Kiambu is currently leading the charts with 90 cases, while Nairobi is right behind with 85. It is almost like a competition for who can be the most barbaric while someone else holds the camera steady for that perfect 4K shot. Who needs Netflix when you have a neighborhood WhatsApp group, right?

Of course, the government is shocked-shocked, I tell you! Murkomen admits that people have zero faith in the courts because they are too slow, too biased, and simply ineffective. But while the state talks about "digital transformation" and rolling out fiber optics to every village, the reality is much grimmer. It feels like just another chapter in [the 2026 ICT budget: another digital mirage for a hungry nation](/posts/the-2026-ict-budget--another-digital-mirage-for-a-hungry-nation) where the technology we are promised only serves to broadcast our collective descent into chaos. We are literally paying for the data to watch our own society crumble.

The report also highlights the messy reality of "misidentification." Imagine being an innocent bystander and suddenly becoming the star of a "thief caught" video because some bored youth with a smartphone and a drug habit decided you looked suspicious. It is the ultimate cancel culture, except instead of losing your brand deals, you lose your life. This is what happens when poverty, unemployment, and a thirst for "clout" collide in the most violent way possible. It is a tragedy dressed up as "justice" for the sake of a few thousand views.

And what is the government’s grand plan to stop this? Public education campaigns and toll-free hotlines. Because clearly, a mob in the middle of a frenzy is going to stop and call a 0-800 number to check the legal procedure for citizen arrests. It is giving "thoughts and prayers" energy while the streets are literally on fire. Stay safe out there, darlings, and maybe try watching a makeup tutorial instead of a stoning for once. The hypocrisy of demanding justice through lawless violence is a look that suits nobody.
