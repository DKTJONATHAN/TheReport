If you thought being a former MP and a big man in Eldoret bought you immunity from the repo man, Swarup Mishra is here to prove you wrong. Mediheal Hospital, once the crown jewel of his private medical empire, is currently on the life support of a failing legal battle. The High Court just handed Bank of Baroda the green light to auction the Eldoret property over a Sh173 million debt. It turns out that in the real world, prestige does not count as legal tender when the interest rates start biting and the bank loses its patience.

The hospital management's defense was as predictable as a Nairobi traffic jam on a rainy Friday evening. They tried to claim they never received the statutory notices, a classic move from the Kenyan playbook of "I was not aware." It is the same tired strategy we see everywhere, from the streets of Pangani to the boardrooms of Upper Hill. Unfortunately for them, the bank actually kept its receipts. The court saw the proof of postage and basically told the hospital to stop wasting everyone's time. You cannot ignore a debt for a year and then act surprised when the auctioneer shows up at your gate.

This is not just a one-off bad month for the facility; it is a systemic collapse. Just last year, Bank of India got the nod to sell another facility linked to Mishra for a staggering Sh701 million. We are watching a slow-motion car crash of a legacy built on credit and prayer. It reminds me of the hollow promises we see in national projects, much like the [Tarmac You Can’t Eat: The KSh 100 Billion Mirage in the North](/posts/tarmac-you-can-t-eat--the-ksh-100-billion-mirage-in-the-north) where the shiny exterior hides the fact that the foundation is completely rotting. In this case, the patients might have beds, but the owners cannot even pay for the land they sit on.

Ms. Maryline Chepkosgei Langat tried to pull at the court's heartstrings, arguing that the hospital is a well-known medical facility that should be spared. Since when did being famous exempt anyone from the Banking Act? She blamed challenging business conditions for the delay, which is Nairobi-speak for "we spent money we did not have and now the bill is due." The arrogance of thinking a permanent injunction would just appear because you provide a public service is exactly why these elite institutions eventually crumble under their own weight.

So, mark your calendars for May 29, 2025. That is when the hammer is scheduled to finally drop on the Eldoret property. The court was very clear: if you do not rebut the bank's evidence with facts, you lose. Mediheal is now just another cautionary tale for the Kenyan political class who think they are too big to fail. In this city, the only thing more expensive than a hospital bill is the cost of pretending you are still solvent when the bank accounts are bone dry.
