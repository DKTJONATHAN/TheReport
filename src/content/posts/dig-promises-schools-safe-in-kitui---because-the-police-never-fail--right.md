So, the Deputy Inspector General of Kenya Police, Eliud Lagat, has been out in Tseikuru, Kitui County, doing his rounds. He's assured parents that their precious offspring will be safe as schools creak back open. This follows a bit of a "series of retaliatory attacks," which is a polite way of saying people have been slaughtering each other. Sunday, April 26, 2026, he tells us, was the day for reassurances and a good old-fashioned security assessment. He wants those kids back in school, no sweat.

Lagat, apparently quite keen to show his face, was accompanied by General Service Unit Commandant Ranson Lolmodooni. They got the lowdown at Kwa Kamari Police Station from Sub-County Commander Joakim Mecha. After the briefing, they hobnobbed with the residents, probably dropping platitudes like confetti. He even managed to pass on condolences from the Inspector General himself, calling the killings "heinous, cowardly, and barbaric." Fancy words for what's likely just another Tuesday in some parts of this country.

The DIG is urging calm, telling everyone to avoid taking the law into their own hands. Because, of course, the legal channels are *so* efficient when your neighbor has just decided to settle a score with a machete. He wants intelligence sharing, as if people who are too scared to send their kids to school are suddenly going to be spilling state secrets to the very police force that can’t seem to keep them safe in the first place. It's almost as if they think we haven't seen this movie before, like when Kenya Police FC somehow managed to nick three points off KCB FC - pure predictability.

He wants collaboration, more eyes and ears to help catch the culprits. It’s the same old song and dance. Promises of investigations, assurance of facing the full force of the law, and a call for unity. Meanwhile, the families who lost loved ones are still mourning, and the rest are just trying to figure out if sending their kids back to school is a gamble worth taking. Let’s just hope this time the "enhanced security" isn’t just a fancy coat of paint on an empty building.
