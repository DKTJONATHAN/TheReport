Kenya Eyes China Partnership For Nairobi Thika Overpass To Ease Congestion - But Don't Hold Your Breath







So, the deputy president, Kithure Kindiki, is all smiles and handshakes, telling China Daily how great it is that we're teaming up for another overpass on the Nairobi-Thika road. Because clearly, what Nairobi needs is *more* concrete and *more* cars funneled onto an already choked artery. They call it easing congestion, I call it paving the way for more chaos. Remember the Thika superhighway? Built with Chinese help, opened in 2012. Still a parking lot most days.

This new "expressway" they're talking about, stretching over 50 kilometers, is supposed to make Kenya a "gateway for trade and investment." Funny, that's what they said about the SGR, the Expressway, and every other shiny project that costs us billions and still leaves us stuck in traffic. They're quick to mention how this improves our role as a hub, but never mind the fact that the actual people using these roads are stuck bumper to bumper, breathing exhaust fumes.

President Ruto apparently announced this project will start in September. Let's hope he's got a better plan than just building more road. Kindiki goes on to say this makes Kenya attractive for manufacturing and distribution, especially for Chinese investors. He even throws in the EAC, COMESA, and AfCFTA figures - 1.4 billion people, $2.83 trillion GDP. Sounds impressive, but can we even move goods efficiently within Nairobi, let alone across the continent? It’s a bit like boasting about a huge mansion when your toilet is broken.

Honestly, it all feels like a repeat performance. We've seen this movie before. More infrastructure, more debt, and the same old traffic jams. It's a cycle that benefits the few, while the rest of us are left to navigate the mess. It makes you wonder if they're building roads for progress or just to impress international partners, while ignoring the real issues. We need solutions that actually work, not just more talk and more concrete. Perhaps if we addressed the root causes of this traffic, like poor public transport planning, we wouldn't be relying on endless road expansions, which frankly, feels a bit like First World Problems, Third World Solutions sometimes.
