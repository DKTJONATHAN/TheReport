Oh, please. Spare me the "balanced music landscape" PR talk. While the industry heads want you to believe that the doors of Bongo-Flava have swung open out of the goodness of men's hearts, we all know the truth. It is less about "empowerment" and more about which female star can generate the most clicks by attachment to a powerful man. Take Zuhura Othman, our dear Zuchu. She has been a "hit-making machine" since 2020, sure, but let is not pretend that being signed to WCB Wasafi and having a "high-profile partnership" with Diamond Platnumz did not do ninety percent of the heavy lifting. When your manager is also your "husband," the line between talent and a well-funded marketing campaign gets very blurry, very fast.

Then we have Nandy, the self-proclaimed "African Princess." She has mastered the art of the "soothing voice" and the even more soothing PR strategy. She is currently balancing a career, motherhood, and personal ambitions, which is basically code for "I am trying to stay relevant while the younger girls nipping at my heels." Her transition from a talent show runner-up to a household name is impressive, but it feels more like a corporate takeover than a musical journey. It is all very polished, very professional, and very, very boring. The industry is just as messy as the [BROKEN BROTHERS AND BOLLYWOOD BEEFS: THE WEEK THE GLOSS STRIPPED AWAY](/posts/broken-brothers-and-bollywood-beefs--the-week-the-gloss-stripped-away) we saw recently, where the shine is just a thin coat of paint over deep-seated drama.

The newcomers are even more calculated. Abigail Chams is out here collecting titles like they are Infinity Stones. Sony Music at eighteen? UNICEF Youth Advocate? BET nomination? It is almost too perfect, darling. She is "genre-defying" and "globally recognised," but one has to wonder if she has ever had a single unscripted thought that was not approved by a board of directors. Then there is Hevi, the latest "soulful" addition who made it onto a Rolling Stone list. It is the same old story: a smooth, accessible vibe that is designed specifically to play in the background of a coffee shop in Nairobi or a lounge in Johannesburg without offending anyone.

And do not get me started on the TikTok-to-stage pipeline. Yammi is the poster child for this "strategy over substance" era. She covered Indian songs on TikTok, caught Nandy’s eye, and suddenly she has an EP. It is efficient, I will give her that. But when "dedication and strategy" are used to describe a music career instead of "soul and grit," you know the art form is in trouble. Phina is another one, winning awards for "Best Female Performer" before she even had a chance to develop a personality that was not curated by a Bongo Star Search judging panel.

Even the "tough" girls like Rosa Ree are just playing a part. She is the "leading female symbol" in hip-hop, a genre that is supposedly too tough for women, yet she is winning regional awards and gaining "acceptance." It is all very sanitized. Maua Sama is the only one who seems to be holding onto some shred of authenticity with her "emotionally charged" songs, but even she was "discovered" by a politician-rapper. In this new Bongo-Flava era, the women are definitely visible, but they are all standing in the exact same spotlight, wearing the exact same designer labels, and singing the exact same notes their labels told them to. Balanced? Maybe. Interesting? Not even close.
