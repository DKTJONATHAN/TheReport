Here we go again. Just when you thought the well of public coffers couldn't be drained any faster, the Daily Nation has pulled back the curtain on Finsprint, a "private company" now caught with its hand deep in the SHA cookie jar. This grand Digital Health Agency (DHA) system, promised as a "one-stop shop" for our health, seems to be a one-stop shop for a select few to line their pockets, all while ordinary Kenyans cough up more of their hard-earned cash.

The report paints a familiar picture of a layered network designed more for obfuscation than efficiency. We're talking about a Sh104 billion consortium involving big names like Safaricom, UAE's Apeiro Limited, and Konvergenz Network Solutions. Apeiro alone got a Sh5 billion contract just for "e-claims processing." It's a classic setup: huge contracts, big promises, and a suspiciously complex chain of command, all under the guise of "improving healthcare."

But the real kicker is Finsprint. This company, conveniently subcontracted by Konvergenz, is the alleged final stop before hospitals get their dues. And what do they do? They help themselves to a 2% cut, claiming it's for "logistical costs." Let's be real, in Nairobi, "logistical costs" is just a fancy term for what looks like [secret profits](/posts/ant-and-dec-court-order-reveals-intermediary-s-suspected--secret-profits--in-banksy-deals). If the estimates are right, Finsprint has already pocketed a cool Sh2.9 billion since SHA started. And that's not even counting the Sh2 from every Sh100 Kenyans contribute, or the Sh5 from every Sh100 hospitals claim. Your contributions are literally their personal ATM.

And who are these masterminds behind Finsprint? Incorporated with a measly Sh100,000 share capital in 2020, its directors are Issa Sheikh Mohamed and Abdulhakim Ibrahim Sheikh. The plot thickens when you discover that 575 shares are held by Impactsoft Technologies Group Limited - a "Kenyan entity" with no known address. Convenient, isn't it? Abdulhakim also owns 425 shares and is part-owner of Konvergenz, the very company that subcontracted Finsprint. It's a closed-door club, designed to keep the money circulating amongst themselves.

This isn't just about numbers; it's about the lives of Kenyans. We're being forced to endure higher SHIF deductions, 2.5% of our salaries, only to see patients turned away from hospitals and critical care denied because these "systems" are more interested in deductions than deliveries. The SHA was meant to save us, but it's quickly becoming another example of how the elite fleece the masses, leaving us sicker and poorer while they count their billions. Same story, different day, same old Nairobi.
