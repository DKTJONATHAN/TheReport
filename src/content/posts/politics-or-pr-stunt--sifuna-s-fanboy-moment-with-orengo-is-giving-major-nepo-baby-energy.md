If you thought the Grammys had too much brown-nosing, you clearly have not seen Edwin Sifuna’s social media lately. On Sunday, the ODM Secretary General decided to drop a tribute to Siaya Governor James Orengo that was so sweet it gave half of X cavities. Sifuna is out here talking about "learning at the feet" of the veteran politician like he is some sort of disciple in a blockbuster epic. It is giving major fanboy energy, and frankly, the "inexplicable twist of history" he mentions is just a fancy way of saying he has been hanging around the right VIP lounges for too long.

Of course, it would not be a Kenyan political post without a little "do you know who my family is?" flex. Sifuna just had to remind everyone that his late uncle, Lawrence Sifuna, was part of the "seven bearded sisters" club with Orengo back in the day. We get it, Edwin. Your family tree is basically a blueprint for the opposition. This whole attempt to link his current career to the second liberation struggle is a classic move from the [Mangoes, Mandates, and Premium Tears: The Kenyan Cycle of Theft](/posts/mangoes--mandates--and-premium-tears--the-kenyan-cycle-of-theft) playbook where the names change but the dynasties stay exactly the same.

Then we have the "Linda Mwananchi" brigade, which sounds less like a political movement and more like a rejected boy band lineup. You have got Sifuna, Orengo, Babu Owino, and Caleb Amisi all playing the role of "rebels" because apparently being in the opposition for decades is the new punk rock. They spent their Saturday in Kakamega at the Amalemba grounds, shouting into microphones about defending the common man while probably checking their watches for the after-party. It is the same tired script: scream at the government, claim you are the only one who cares, and then go back to your gated community.

The height of the comedy, however, is the announcement that the "revolution" is taking a one-month coffee break. Apparently, the fight against economic hardship and government policy is on hold because of Ramadan. While everyone respects the holy month, it is hilarious to see a political movement that has "scheduled" its outrage. They will be back to "safeguard democracy" in Mombasa once the break is over. It is nice to know that the struggle for a better Kenya has office hours and a very generous vacation policy for its lead actors.

At the end of the day, Orengo is the seasoned veteran who knows exactly how to keep these younger leaders in line. Sifuna says "history will be kind" to Orengo, but let us be real: history is usually written by the people who managed to stay in power the longest. Whether this Linda Mwananchi group is actually about the people or just a launchpad for the next election cycle is not even a question anymore. It is just another season of Kenya’s favorite soap opera, and we are all just forced to watch the reruns of the same families playing musical chairs.
