Ruto's Land Commission Shuffle: Same Old Song, Different Dance

Here we go again. Another reshuffle at the National Land Commission, and you'd think they're about to solve Kenya's land woes. President Ruto has gazetted his new team, headed by Abdillahi Saggaf Alawy, for a six-year stint. Six years. Enough time to get cozy, forget the promises, and maybe, just maybe, line a few pockets.

The National Assembly, bless their efficient hearts, rubber-stamped these appointments faster than you can say "land grabbing." After a nod from some special committee, it was all done and dusted. This follows a court ruling, because apparently, the wheels of justice turn ever so slowly, and only when pushed. So, we have Alawy and his six companions, ready to tackle land administration, dispute resolution, and the ever elusive "transparency." Sounds familiar, doesn't it? It’s the same script they’ve been reading for years, and the only thing that seems to change is the cast.

Land is the elephant in the room, the poisoned chalice of Kenyan politics. It’s the source of so much conflict, so much heartache, and yet, here we are, with a new commission that's supposed to magically fix it. They're meant to steer the agenda "in line with constitutional requirements." Let's see how long that commitment lasts when the real pressures kick in. Remember how land issues have fuelled plenty of drama, like the whispers around [The Mandera Mirage: Trading Blood for Miraa?](/posts/the-mandera-mirage--trading-blood-for-miraa)? This is just another chapter.

And the timing? Just after Parliament cleared some Safaricom deal with a few conditions. It’s almost as if the big news is being buried under a pile of other political maneuvers. We've seen how these things play out before, the promises made and then conveniently forgotten. Whether this new NLC will be any different is a question only time, and perhaps a healthy dose of skepticism, will answer. Expect more of the same, with a fresh coat of paint.

Ruto Appoints New NLC Leadership

Another round of musical chairs at the land commission, don't get too excited.

President William Ruto has formally unveiled his new lineup for the National Land Commission (NLC), appointing Abdillahi Saggaf Alawy as Chairperson for a six-year term. This move, gazetted on March 12, 2026, sees a fresh team tasked with overseeing land matters, resolving disputes, and championing transparency. One can only hope this isn't just a new coat of paint on an old, rotten structure.

The National Assembly apparently saw fit to approve these nominations with remarkable speed, confirming Alawy and six other members - Susan Khaemba Oyatsi, Daniel Murithi Muriungi, Kigen Vincent Cheruiyot, Julie Ouma Oseko, Mohamed Abdi Haji Mohamed, and Mary Jiane Seneta - for similar six-year tenures. This swift approval, following a court ruling that nudged the process along, suggests a desire for efficiency, but the real test will be in their actions, not their speed of appointment.

Land remains one of Kenya's most volatile and consequential issues, a perennial source of tension and controversy. With this new leadership in place, the expectations are high, but history has a nasty habit of repeating itself. We've seen countless commissions promise change, only to fall prey to the very forces they were meant to combat. It makes you wonder if any of this will truly alter the landscape, or just provide another six years of political theatre, much like the maneuvers seen in deals like [PARLIAMENT CLEARS SAFARICOM STAKE SALE WITH SIX CONDITIONS](/posts/parliament-clears-safaricom-stake-sale-with-six-conditions).

The appointments come after a presidential nomination on February 17, a process now cemented in a special gazette notice. The outgoing commission's tenure has been marked by its own set of challenges, and the hope is that this new team will learn from past mistakes. However, given the deeply entrenched nature of land governance issues in Kenya, the cynical observer would rightly question the likelihood of any substantial, positive shift. We’ll be watching, with a healthy dose of doubt.
