THE UN GIVES A NOD TO KENYA'S FALLEN PEACEKEEPERS, WHILE THE WORLD KEEPS SENDING THEM TO DIE

The United Nations, bless its bureaucratic heart, is going to honor two Kenyans this Friday. Major Paul Ndungu Njoroge and Ms. Alice Wanjiru Chege. Sounds nice, doesn't it? Another wreath, another medal, another speech about the "cause of peace." Meanwhile, nearly 4,500 souls have been sacrificed since 1948, and 59 just last year. Forty-five hundred. That's more than some small towns. And these two Kenyans are just the latest additions to that grim tally.

Kenya, of course, is a "valued contributor," sending 440 of its finest to various dustbowls and war zones. They're the 27th largest provider of uniformed personnel, apparently. Numbers. That's what matters to these international bodies. Not the broken families left behind, not the endless cycle of conflict that seems to just get worse. We're talking about places where "conflicts are increasingly fragmented" and shaped by "emerging threats." Sounds like a Tuesday in Nairobi these days, doesn't it? Maybe they should send some of these peacekeepers to sort out [Kenya's Expanding Economy of Violence: A Goons' Paradise Fueled by Power and Impunity](/posts/kenya-s-expanding-economy-of-violence--a-goons--paradise-fueled-by-power-and-impunity). At least that's a problem we understand here.

The theme this year is "Invest in Peace." Hilarious. As if pouring more money into a system that's clearly struggling is the answer. The Secretary-General drones on about "steady political backing" and "reliable financial support." What they really mean is more taxpayer money for more missions that often just prop up failing regimes or get caught in the crossfire. They talk about peacekeepers protecting civilians and preventing escalation, but let's be honest, they're often just the first casualties when things go south.

And then there's the Captain Mbaye Diagne Medal for Exceptional Courage, awarded to a corporal from Uruguay and a contractor from Ukraine. Plus, a Military Gender Advocate and a Woman Police Officer of the Year. All very noble. Meanwhile, back in Kenya, the real "exceptional courage" is just trying to survive the everyday chaos. We're good at sending people to die for someone else's idea of peace, but when it comes to fixing our own messes, suddenly resources are "shrinking."

The UN Headquarters will be marking this day on June 5th. So, the world can pause for a moment, pat themselves on the back, and then go back to business as usual. Because as Jean-Pierre Lacroix, Under-Secretary-General for Peace Operations, so eloquently puts it, "Investing in peacekeeping means investing in stability, prevention and the possibility of peace itself." Except, it often feels more like investing in prolonged, expensive, and ultimately futile endeavors that just add to the body count.

Maybe one day, instead of honoring the fallen, they'll actually figure out how to prevent the need for such honors in the first place. But I wouldn't hold your breath. This is the UN, after all. And we're still talking about the same old problems, just with shinier new medals.
