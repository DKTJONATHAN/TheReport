The Nigerian music industry is basically a hall of mirrors where "singers" get the fame while the actual musicians do the heavy lifting in the basement. Enter Nsikak Okon David. He’s being hailed as this "visionary" guitarist, but let’s call it what it is: the industry’s favorite security blanket. Whenever a star needs to sound "deep" or "soulful," they drag this man into the studio to weave some "tapestries of sound." It is a classic case of the talent staying in the shadows while the "faces" of the brand collect the checks and the accolades.

He’s out here talking about "weaving tapestries" and "soul-stirring" vibes, but I see a man who has been playing backup for a decade. He’s worked with everyone from Burna Boy to Davido, which basically means he’s seen enough ego to fill a stadium. The industry observers call him a "musician’s musician," which is just code for "the guy we do not pay enough but rely on to save the track." It is hilarious how these superstars claim to be "innovators" when they probably cannot even find a C-major chord without calling Nsikak to fix their messy arrangements.

Let’s talk about that Grammy "recognition" for Tems’ Love Me Jeje. It is lovely that he gets a shout-out, but we all know how this works. The singer gets the trophy, the red carpet, and the global brand deals, while the instrumentalist gets a "surreal" feeling and a LinkedIn update. It reminds me of how massive corporations operate when things go south; it is always about the face of the company while the real foundation is ignored. This kind of industry imbalance is just as messy as [The Substack Breach: Another Corporate Failure Masked as a Security Incident](/posts/the-substack-breach--another-corporate-failure-masked-as-a-security-incident) because it is always the people at the top stealing the narrative from the ones doing the actual work.

Nsikak recently dropped an instrumental EP called Strings of Solace. He says it is about "seeking peace" and "introspection." In gossip terms, that means he’s finally tired of being the background noise for people who cannot sing in key. He wants us to believe he’s "communicating without words," which is actually a genius strategy when you realize most of the lyrics in Afrobeats these days are just repetitive nonsense anyway. Why bother with words when a guitar solo can hide the fact that the "superstar" has absolutely nothing to say?

He claims his creative process is all about "emotion" and "the way light hits a room." Give me a break. It is about surviving the chaotic energy of stadium tours and "intimate" studio sessions with artists who think they are gods. He talks about "flexibility" and "listening," which is just a polite way of saying he knows how to pivot when a diva throws a tantrum in the booth. At the end of the day, Nsikak is the real MVP, but in this industry, the MVP usually ends up as a tiny footnote in someone else’s glossy biography.

So, will Nsikak ever truly step out of the shadows, or is he destined to be the "ghost" in the machine forever? He’s trying to sell us on this "reflective journey," but the fans are still busy screaming for the next brainless club banger. He might have the technical brilliance, but in a world obsessed with hype and TikTok dances, a soulful guitar riff is just a nice-to-have. Stay tuned to see if he actually gets the flowers he’s earned or if he just keeps making the talentless look like legends for another ten years.
