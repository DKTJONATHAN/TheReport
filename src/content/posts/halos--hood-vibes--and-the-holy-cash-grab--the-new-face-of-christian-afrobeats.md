It is no longer about choir robes and organ music, darlings. Now, if you want to evangelize, you apparently need a trap beat and a designer tracksuit. The industry is panicking because the youth find traditional worship "recycled" and boring. Enter the "cool" Christians like Lecrae and Limoblaze, who are desperately trying to prove that the Bible isn't just a collection of bedtime stories. They want the mainstream's respect, but they want it while claiming they aren't "preachy." Pick a side, babes. You cannot be "vibe-driven" and "intellectual" at the same time when your main goal is just to stay relevant on TikTok.


Limoblaze is the poster boy for this "Afrogospel" movement, and honestly, the hustle is impressive. He sat down with Spotify and Amazon like he was pitching a tech startup, and suddenly we have "Afrogospel" playlists everywhere. It is all very strategic. While these artists claim it is a "massive evangelistic tool," it looks a lot like a rebranding exercise for a demographic that is tired of being called corny. They want to be in the club without the "profane" lyrics, but let-s be real- the beat is still designed to make you move in ways the elders would definitely call "demonic."


Then we have Jackie Hill Perry, who claims she-s giving the kids sounds that are "ghetto and cool" but not profane. Talk about a walking contradiction. It is funny how they preach humility while obsessing over "visibility" and industry buy-in. Much like the [Digital Judas and the Sh10,000 Justice Fee](/posts/digital-judas-and-the-sh10-000-justice-fee) situation, the music industry is a den of wolves where loyalty to the "message" often takes a backseat to the pursuit of clout and coins. If the music is so "soul-feeding," why are we so worried about whether the Recording Academy has a specific "Rhythm and Praise" category yet?


Childlike CiCi is another one to watch, trading trap houses for the sanctuary. She says the Bible is not "Kidz Bop," which is a fair point, but using drill music- a genre literally birthed from neighborhood warfare- to spread the "good news" is the kind of irony you can only find in 2024. It is "vibe-driven" faith for a generation that can-t sit still through a twenty-minute sermon but will loop a three-minute Afrobeats track about "blessings" all day long. It is not about the gospel; it is about the aesthetic.


The real tea is the gatekeeping. The Christian music industry has been white and "homogenous" for too long, and now that Black artists are actually making numbers, the big labels are suddenly "catching on." It is not about the soul, it is about the stream. They-ll give you a playlist, but they won-t give you the budget. These artists are out here trying to compete with secular giants while the industry keeps them in a "faith-based" box that pays pennies compared to the mainstream. They want the visibility but they are still fighting for crumbs at the Grammy table.


At the end of the day, whether it is Ryan Ofei-s R-and-B fusion or CeJae-s songs about "heartbreak," it is all just another product in the spiritual marketplace. They want to be "humble," but they also want the "momentum" and the "visibility." Honey, you cannot have your communion wine and drink it too. Either stay underground and "holy," or admit you are just another artist chasing the same Grammy-shaped golden calf as everyone else in this messy business.
