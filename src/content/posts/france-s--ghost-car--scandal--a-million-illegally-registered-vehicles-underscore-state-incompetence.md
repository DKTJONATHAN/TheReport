France, in its infinite wisdom, has managed to unleash a million "ghost cars" onto its roads, a testament to the sheer ineptitude that can arise when the state attempts to embrace modernity while clinging to a misplaced sense of optimism. The French vehicle licensing agency, the SIV, once a bastion of bureaucratic slowness, has become a playground for criminals and tax dodgers thanks to a 2017 decision to "part-privatize" the system. The idea was to speed things up by allowing car dealers direct access to issue registration papers. Naturally, what this actually achieved was to open the floodgates for unscrupulous operators.

According to the state auditor, the Cour des Comptes, the new system relied "excessively and mistakenly on good faith." This is a polite way of saying the government was utterly naive. Hundreds of "fictitious companies" have popped up, operating entirely outside of state control, fiddling with the registry for a fee. These phantom dealerships issue seemingly legitimate papers, rendering the vehicles and their owners untraceable. The financial damage is staggering, with an estimated €550 million lost in just two years from uncollected registration fees and fines.

The fraud isn't limited to petty tax evasion. The report details 30 different types of scams, including dodging environmental taxes on polluting vehicles, faking roadworthiness tests, and obscuring the identities of previous owners. Luxury car importers are registering Rolls Royces and Mercedes as specially adapted vehicles for the disabled to avoid import duties. Stolen vehicles are being laundered, and drug gangs are using these untraceable cars for high speed deliveries. The surge in "very fast" speeding offenses, up 160% between 2016 and 2022, was an early indicator of this widespread malfeasance, a clue the authorities apparently took their sweet time to decipher.

The Cour des Comptes rightly points out that the state was "lax in not checking the credentials of the 30,000 dealers" granted access. All it took was setting up a shell company and applying. As one insider bemoaned, "Before, people had to queue for two hours for their papers. Everyone complained, but at least everything was properly checked." This sentiment encapsulates the eternal struggle between efficiency and security, a struggle France has clearly lost in this instance.

The interior ministry, in a predictable display of damage control, claims it's addressing the problem. An action plan has led to an "increase in fraud detections," and the number of access authorisations has been "sharply reduced." One can only wonder how many more "ghost cars" will be unleashed before any meaningful change actually takes hold. This entire episode is a perfect microcosm of governmental overreach and subsequent failure, a story as old as time, just with more modern technological trappings.
