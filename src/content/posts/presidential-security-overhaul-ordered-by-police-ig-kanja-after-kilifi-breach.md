POLICE IG KANJA ORDERS PRESIDENTIAL SECURITY OVERHAUL AFTER KILIFI FUMBLES

The President's security is apparently a joke, and it took a near-miss in Kilifi for the IG to wake up and smell the coffee. Another day, another security lapse, proving that while the speeches might be grand, the execution is pure Nairobi chaos.

The IG's tough talk about national security and overhauling the presidential detail sounds good on paper, but let's be real, we've heard it all before. Promises of change are cheap when the real problem is a system so riddled with holes, even a determined citizen can waltz right up to the Head of State.

This latest incident, where some ambitious young man decided to have a direct word with the President during a speech, is just the cherry on top of a growing security mess. It's not just a lapse; it's a pattern. From individuals getting too close to being intercepted, to visible confusion among the elite protection unit, it’s clear the President's security isn't just paramount, it's practically a lottery. Remember those grand pronouncements after other "gladiatorial state" events? They seem to have evaporated like mist. [The Gladiatorial State and the Death of Diplomacy](/posts/the-gladiatorial-state-and-the-death-of-diplomacy).

President Ruto's casual dismissal of the Kilifi intruder, calling him a "good young man," is exactly the kind of detached perspective that fuels these desperate gambits. When citizens feel they have no other recourse but to risk their lives for a moment with the President, it speaks volumes about their "desperate situations." But hey, as long as the photographers get their shots, right?

The IG's "massive changes" and "special investigations team" are just the usual theatre. We'll see if this leads to actual competence or just a reshuffling of the same old faces who can barely coordinate a decent handshake, let alone protect a president. The balance between accessibility and safety is a tightrope, and it seems this government is more interested in falling off than walking it.

The question remains: when will we see actual security, not just more headlines about near misses and investigations that go nowhere? Until then, Kenyans will keep looking for their own ways to be heard, and the presidential security detail will keep scrambling to pretend they’re in control.
