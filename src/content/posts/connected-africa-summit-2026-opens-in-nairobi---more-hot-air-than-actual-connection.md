So, Nairobi is hosting the Connected Africa Summit 2026, and the buzzwords are flying thicker than the dust on our unpaved roads. Government leaders, policymakers, investors - all gathered to "chart the next phase of the continent’s digital transformation." Apparently, last year's "Vision to Reality" and the year before's "Unlocking Growth Beyond Connectivity" weren't enough to get us there. Now, it's all about "measurable delivery, implementation, and sustained action." We’ve heard this song before, haven't we? It’s the same old tune played with slightly different instruments.

Eng. John Tanui from the State Department for ICT and the Digital Economy is apparently all fired up, telling us Africa is at a "critical inflection point." He’s quoting McKinsey, saying generative AI and cloud could unlock billions. Billions for whom, exactly? For the same people who are already connected and benefiting? He also mentions submarine cables and fiber optics - great, those are nice infrastructure projects, but have they reached Mama Njeri in Kibera yet? Or the folks out in Turkana who still rely on a single bar of network if they’re lucky? Because as Tanui himself admits, there are still communities where fiber hasn't reached, and people who are digitally excluded. It’s always the same story - progress for some, promises for the rest. We’ve seen promises broken before; remember when [Ruto was ready to account for all commitments to Kenyans, apparently](/posts/ruto-ready-to-account-for-all-commitments-to-kenyans--apparently)? Let's see if this is any different.

Jessy Maruti Kiveu from the ICT Authority is pushing for "interoperability, harmonising policy and regulatory frameworks, and enabling seamless cross-border digital services." Sounds fancy. He also wants to create an environment for "Africa-led innovation" and ensure "inclusive participation." Again, the words are good, the intentions might even be there, but the execution is where it all falls apart. Building secure and resilient digital systems is crucial, he says. Without interoperability, we risk fragmentation. Without trust, transformation cannot endure. These are the very things that seem to be missing when it comes to tangible benefits for the average Kenyan.

The agenda is packed with discussions on cybersecurity, AI, digital identity, fintech, and connectivity. They're aiming to move "beyond dialogue and into action." That's the part I'm waiting to see. Will this summit result in actual, on-the-ground changes for ordinary citizens, or will it be another round of expensive conferences, handshakes, and glossy reports that gather dust? We've seen plenty of ministers from various countries attend. Will they be signing actual deals that improve lives, or just posing for photos?

The theme this year is "Uniting Africa’s Innovation for an Inclusive Digital Market." The irony is not lost on me. We talk about inclusivity while the digital divide widens. We talk about innovation while basic connectivity remains a luxury for many. It’s a lot of talk, a lot of ambition, and a lot of potential. Let’s just hope that this time, the "measurable delivery" actually includes the people at the bottom of the pyramid, not just the usual suspects. Because at the end of the day, digital transformation means little if it doesn't transform the lives of ordinary people.
