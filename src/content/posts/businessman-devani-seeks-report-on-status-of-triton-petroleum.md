Devani Wants His Toys Back After 18 Years In The Receiver's Sandbox

WARNING: Another tycoon thinks the law is for other people. This time, it's Yagnesh Devani, who's crying foul after 18 years of receivership for his company, Triton Petroleum. Apparently, he wants a status report, as if he wasn't the one who vanished for 16 years. The audacity.

This is just another chapter in the Nairobi saga of the rich and the slippery.





Yagnesh Devani, the man who apparently thought he could outrun justice for over a decade and a half, is now trotting back to court demanding to know what happened to his company, Triton Petroleum. Placed under receivership in 2008, nearly two decades ago, Devani claims the receivers have been dodgy, failing to update the board - a board he was probably too busy being "on the run" to pay attention to. The nerve of some people.

His urgent petition, filed after his grand return from exile last year (following a criminal case that conveniently collapsed because witnesses apparently developed sudden amnesia), is basically him saying, "Where's my stuff?" He’s suing the receivers, Abdul Zahir Sheikh and Peter Kahii, appointed by KCB Bank Kenya Ltd, claiming they've failed their "statutory obligations." Oh, the irony.

Devani, the "principal shareholder," wants a full accounting of everything - warehouses, vehicles, stocks, you name it. He argues that the receivers have been holding onto trust assets and deliberately withholding information, which he conveniently forgot about while he was apparently living it up elsewhere. It’s like a thief complaining the police took his stolen goods. This whole situation reeks of the kind of backroom deals and alleged corruption that makes you wonder if [More Coins for the Boys in Blue: A Price Tag on Our Silence](/posts/more-coins-for-the-boys-in-blue--a-price-tag-on-our-silence) isn't just a catchy title, but a business model.

He’s demanding a forensic accounting of 17 years of operations, disposals, recoveries, and expenses. Basically, he wants the court to force the receivers to show him the money, or lack thereof, that happened while he was away. The High Court will mention the case on April 29th, no doubt to a chorus of eye-rolls from anyone who’s seen this movie before in Nairobi.

Devani fled Kenya in 2008, accused of oil theft. He was eventually extradited from London after a lengthy legal battle. A PwC audit at the time highlighted irregularities by Kenya Pipeline Company. Now, with his criminal case conveniently falling apart and him back in town, he’s conveniently remembering his "company's affairs." It’s a classic play from the playbook of the entitled elite who expect the world to wait for them and then hand them back the keys.
