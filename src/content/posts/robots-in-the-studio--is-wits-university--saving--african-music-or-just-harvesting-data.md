Wits University is at it again, trying to convince us that the future of African music lies in a computer chip rather than actual human talent. They have teamed up with Charles Goldstuck, a US-based music executive who apparently has enough spare change and PhD-candidate free time to fund a six-month "pilot programme." It is all very shiny and "innovative" on paper, but let us be real - when a billionaire starts talking about "leveraging AI" in our culture, it usually means they are looking for a new way to own the masters without having to deal with the messy reality of human artists.

They claim over 150 artists from across the continent applied, but only five teams actually made the cut. The selection process was apparently so rigorous it makes [Kenya’s Passport-Gate: When Your Bank Account Vets You Better Than the NIS](/posts/kenya-s-passport-gate--when-your-bank-account-vets-you-better-than-the-nis) look like a simple "yes or no" question. While our governments are busy vetting our bank accounts just to let us travel, Wits and Goldstuck are putting these creators through "multi-stage assessments" just to see if they can play nice with a machine. It is the same old story: gatekeeping creativity under the guise of "technical feasibility" and regional representation.

Each lucky winner has been paired with an AI engineer, which is basically corporate-speak for a babysitter. Because heaven forbid an African creator just makes music without a tech bro from the Wits MIND Institute hovering over their shoulder to ensure everything is "culturally grounded" and "technically robust." They are calling it "complementary expertise," but it smells more like a way to make sure the "African sonic heritage" is neatly packaged for a Western server. They want to "safeguard" traditions by digitizing them? That is like saying you are protecting a lion by putting it in a high-definition zoo.

Of course, Billboard US and Billboard Africa are already circling like vultures, ready to "document" the process with editorial features. We already know how this script ends. There will be a glittering showcase at the Chris Seabrooke Music Hall in April 2026, a lot of self-congratulatory speeches about "global shifts," and plenty of champagne for the academics. Meanwhile, the actual artists will likely see more "mentorship" and "collaborative opportunities" than actual cold, hard cash while the tech giants at MIT and Wits pat themselves on the back for their "strategic commitment to AI leadership."

At the end of the day, this is just another way to make African art palatable for a global tech audience that prefers algorithms to real soul. They talk about "ethics" and "community-led design," but the power remains firmly in the hands of the institutions and the philanthropists. If this project actually cared about the "breadth and originality" of African innovation, they would be funding the bedroom producers in Nairobi or the street kids in Lagos who are already making global hits on cracked software, not holding fancy workshops in Johannesburg for a hand-picked elite.
