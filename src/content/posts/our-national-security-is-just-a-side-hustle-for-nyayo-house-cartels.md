In this town, if you do not have a "mheshimiwa" on speed dial, you are basically a ghost in the system. While you are sweating in a queue at Nyayo House for six months just to get a passport to go look for greener pastures, the government is busy handing out our green books like candy to foreigners with questionable CVs. We are talking about Sudanese RSF types and Zimbabwean businessmen who probably could not pass a basic background check in a village kiosk. It is the classic Kenyan story: the law is a suggestion for the rich and a cage for the poor.

Advocate Peter Wahinya is trying to play hero by demanding answers, but we all know how this script ends. He wants the Director General of Immigration to explain how 28 individuals, including the infamous Wicknell Chivayo, bypassed the National Intelligence Service vetting. It is almost as messy as [the orange divorce](/posts/the-orange-divorce--inside-the-civil-war-threatening-to-sink-odm) currently tearing through the political landscape. When the system is this broken, every institution starts looking like a private members club for the highest bidder.

The Ministry of Interior under Kipchumba Murkomen promised us transparency, but right now, the silence is deafening. They have 21 days to respond to Wahinya’s demand letter, but do not hold your breath. Usually, a foreigner needs years of residency and a clean record to even dream of citizenship. Here, it seems all you need is the right connection and a thick envelope to skip the line while the rest of us are told the printing machines are broken again.

Let us be real: the security of this country is being sold at a discount. If the people supposed to guard our borders are the ones opening the back door for anyone with a "controversial history," then the rest of us are just sitting ducks. The layers of oversight mentioned in the law books are just fancy words to decorate the desks of people who are likely looking the other way while our sovereignty is auctioned off.

We will wait for the audit reports and the internal investigations, but in Nairobi, an "investigation" is often just a way to let public anger cool down before the next scandal hits. By the time they give us a response, these "VIPs" will have probably used those Kenyan passports to fly to their next shady deal. Welcome to the 254, where our identity has a price tag and the receipt is hidden in a government drawer.
