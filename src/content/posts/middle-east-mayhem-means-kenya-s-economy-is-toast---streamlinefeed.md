So, the geniuses in the Middle East are at it again, and guess who's footing the bill? Us. The price of diesel at a Nairobi filling station is now directly tied to whatever drama the US, Israel, and Iran are cooking up. Forget local politics; your fuel gauge is now a geopolitical barometer, and it’s pointing straight to trouble, threatening our already shaky currency.

The government, bless their hearts, is trying to keep pump prices stable, but it's a losing battle. They're basically putting a band-aid on a gunshot wound with this “price stabilisation mechanism.” The average cost of imported diesel has shot up, and if this Middle East mess doesn't sort itself out, that stabilisation fund will go belly up, and we’ll all be paying through the nose. It’s a temporary fix, and we all know how well those work in Kenya.

And then there’s trade. The Red Sea and Strait of Hormuz are basically no-go zones now. Ships are taking the long way around, adding weeks to deliveries. That means your imported goods, from car parts to fertilizer, will cost more. Farmers in the Rift Valley are already sweating over fertilizer prices, which could mean less food on our tables later. It’s a domino effect, and we’re at the mercy of shipping giants and some very unstable regions. It reminds me of the time we were dealing with all the drama around [The EAC Leadership Musical Chairs: Same Circus, New Ringmaster](/posts/the-eac-leadership-musical-chairs--same-circus--new-ringmaster), where everyone was making noise but nothing was really changing for the better.

Businesses in Industrial Area are hoarding what they can, planning for delays. Manufacturers are no longer just planning for production; they’re planning for geopolitical chaos. Every shipment is now a gamble. The Central Bank is doing its usual tightrope walk, trying to keep inflation down without completely strangling our struggling industries. It’s a mess, and while the smoke clears over the Strait of Hormuz, we’ll be feeling the heat right here.
