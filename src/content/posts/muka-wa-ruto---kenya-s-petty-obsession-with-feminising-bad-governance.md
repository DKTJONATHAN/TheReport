So, the big brains in Nairobi are still at it, blaming women for everything that goes wrong in this country. It’s a classic move, really. When things are dire, and the system is clearly broken, who do they point the finger at? Not the guys making the decisions, oh no. It’s the women, or anyone they can slap with a feminine label, to show how weak and incompetent leadership supposedly is. They conveniently forget that these same systems were built by men, for men, and women have been locked out of shaping them from the start. Calling failure "umama" or "muka wa Ruto" is just a lazy, patriarchal way to avoid the real issues and keep power in the hands of the usual suspects.

This whole clown show gets worse with every election cycle, especially now that AI can churn out fake images and memes faster than you can say "corruption." Suddenly, President Ruto is in a bra, or Deputy President Kindiki is being called "Sophia the First." It’s not about policy or actual governance; it's about reinforcing this tired idea that anything less than aggressive, dominant masculinity is inherently weak. Loyalty to the President? That's "feminised obedience," not political strategy. Dissent? That's "masculine courage." It's a rigid, pathetic binary that harms everyone, including the men who feel pressured to act like aggressive thugs to be taken seriously.

And let's not even start on the "flower girls" nonsense. For years, women in politics have been dismissed as ornamental, useless. Now, even men like Kindiki and Mudavadi are being called "flower girls" by their peers, and the media is running with it. Why? Because the underlying insult is that femininity itself is inherently less valuable. It’s a deep-seated hypocrisy: femininity is despised as weakness, yet it’s the go-to explanation for failure. It’s the same logic that led to Martha Karua being called "the only man in Cabinet" when she dared to uphold the law, or Winnie Odinga being labelled "the son he never had." Competence, it seems, is only allowed when it’s masculinised.

The Constitution promised gender inclusion, but look around. We're still stuck with a pathetic 23% of women in the National Assembly. Major parties are still male-dominated at the top, yet when governance fails, guess who symbolically carries the can? The women. It's an institutionalised insult, amplified by social media where misogyny spreads like wildfire. Even when women push back, insisting that bad governance by men is just bad governance by men, the narrative persists. It's a hostile environment, and frankly, it’s exhausting.

This isn't just a Kenyan problem, by the way. Look at Tanzania’s President Samia Suluhu Hassan, facing the same gendered attacks, some amplified right here in Kenya. She's called "Idi Amin Mama," her personal life is dissected, and AI-generated cartoons show her undressed. Men who rule harshly are strong; women who do the same are monstrous. It’s a pattern of reducing complex leaders and debates to crude, gendered insults. This is where AI becomes a dangerous tool, trained on all our existing biases and spitting them back at us, at scale.

Even the lauded satirists aren't always clean. While Gado portrays "Wanjiku" as a strong, politically aware Kenyan, some of his own work has reinforced the feminisation of failure. Visualising corruption as a cow with exaggerated udders, for instance, subtly teaches people to associate misrule with femininity. It's a complex space, satire, but the line between holding power accountable and reinforcing harmful stereotypes is one we all need to watch. Especially when we're talking about a country that has never even had a woman leader.
