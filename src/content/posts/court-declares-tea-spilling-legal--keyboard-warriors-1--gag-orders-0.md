Finally, the "thought police" have been sent packing and we can all go back to being our most unhinged selves online without looking over our shoulders. The Court of Appeal decided that Sections 22 and 23 of the Cybercrimes Act were basically just fancy ways for politicians to throw people in jail for hurting their feelings. We were facing 10 years in the slammer or a cool 5 million fine just for "misleading information." Please, in this town, the only thing "misleading" is a socialite's Instagram filter, but the government wanted to treat a spicy tweet like a felony.

Let’s be real, this wasn’t about "protecting the public from panic." It was about protecting fragile egos from the consequences of their own actions. Since 2020, anyone with a smartphone and an opinion was a target. The judges finally admitted that these laws were so vague they could criminalize satire or a simple journalistic mistake. Imagine getting arrested for a meme? It is peak comedy, honestly. While the powers that be play their games, like how [THE DP SEAT IS JUST A MUSICAL CHAIR FOR THE ELITE](/posts/the-dp-seat-is-just-a-musical-chair-for-the-elite), they wanted us to sit quietly and watch the circus without pointing out the clowns.

The ruling is a huge win for the Bloggers Association of Kenya (BAKE). They have been fighting this since 2018 while the rest of us were busy tracking who unfollowed who on the Gram. The court pointed out that terms like "false" and "misleading" are so broad they could mean whatever a bored cop wants them to mean. We have seen the receipts- people were getting arrested just for criticizing the tax hikes during the protests. Now, the court says those "obnoxious characters" are a small price to pay for actual freedom of speech.

But do not get it twisted- you cannot just go around being a total menace. The court kept the parts of the law that actually matter, like the ones about cyber harassment and distributing intimate images. You still get a fine for being a creep or a hacker, which is the bare minimum for a civilized society. They just stopped the government from acting like the ultimate "cancel culture" authority. If you want to call out a politician for their blatant lies, the law is no longer their personal shield.

It is bittersweet, though, because it took real tragedy to get here. We should not forget that it took lives, like Albert Ojwang who died in police custody, for the system to realize how dangerous these gag orders were. The state’s desperation to control the digital narrative has been nothing short of embarrassing. Now that the muzzle is off, I expect the tea to be piping hot and the critiques to be even sharper. The internet never forgets, and now, it does not have to be afraid of the messy truth.
