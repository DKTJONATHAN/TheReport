The digital realm is being poisoned by a new breed of manufactured reality, with fake AI videos of UK urban decline flooding social media. These aren't just harmless exaggerations; they're sophisticated digital deceptions, a phenomenon the BBC appropriately labels "decline porn." At the heart of this digital rot is a creator like "RadialB," a faceless twenty-something from the north-west of England who admits to never having set foot in Croydon, the supposed setting for his viral fakes. His motivation? Pure, unadulterated engagement.

RadialB's modus operandi is simple: exploit the "selling point of generative AI," which is its uncanny ability to mimic reality. He churns out absurd, often offensive, scenarios - taxpayer-funded water parks filled with litter and "roadmen," or arcade machines stocked with knives. The inherent absurdity is precisely what grabs attention, but he readily admits he wants people to believe these fabricated scenes are real, at least initially, to hook them. This isn't about artistic expression; it's about gaming the system for views and clicks, a digital arms race where truth is the first casualty.

The ease with which these deepfakes can be created is alarming. RadialB points to the "huge jump" in AI quality and accessibility, effectively lowering the barrier to entry for anyone with a malicious or profit-driven agenda. This has spawned a wave of copycat accounts, international sharers from Israel to Brazil, and even fake British news accounts, all capitalizing on the trend. The content, often featuring "roadmen" as cultural archetypes, is frequently laced with racist undertones, which RadialB readily acknowledges but dismisses as a consequence of platform filters and the inherent nature of online discourse. He claims his prompts for "roadmen wearing puffer jackets, track suits, and balaclavas" are for comedic effect, not racial targeting, a convenient deflection that ignores the predictable racist backlash his work elicits.

This trend doesn't exist in a vacuum. It taps into and amplifies existing narratives of Western urban decay, fueled by fears of immigration and crime. YouTubers like Kurt Caz present exaggerated, sometimes doctored, depictions of cities as dangerous and dilapidated, omitting context and resorting to manipulative thumbnails. Even figures like Elon Musk amplify these anxieties, leveraging their massive platforms to push narratives of uncontrolled migration leading to Britain's destruction. While legitimate debates about societal issues exist, this AI driven content often distorts reality, as evidenced by YouGov polling showing a significant disconnect between public perception of London's safety and the lived experience of its residents.

RadialB, despite his protestations of purely artistic intent and a desire to "make people laugh," is undeniably a purveyor of digital division. His content, banned from one platform for its graphic nature, has simply migrated to another, a testament to the ephemeral nature of platform moderation against the relentless tide of AI-generated deception. The shame associated with posting fakes has seemingly evaporated, replaced by a cavalier attitude towards the real-world consequences of spreading manufactured fear and prejudice. In this new landscape, the creators of digital lies are not just entertainers; they are architects of social division, wielding AI as their weapon of choice.
