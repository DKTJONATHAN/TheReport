So, Bien-Aimé Baraza, the one with the voice from Sauti Sol, has decided to grace us with his "brilliant" defense after hobnobbing with French President Emmanuel Macron. According to him, sitting at a table with these global leaders doesn't magically erase his "Africanness" or his "beliefs." He claims he can be fully African, fully critical, and fully himself. "Presence is not submission," he chirps. Right. Because being present at the table where decisions are made, without any actual leverage or tangible benefit for the people he supposedly represents, is just a casual Tuesday. It's not like these meetings are designed to subtly influence narratives or secure future economic favors, is it?

His partner in this diplomatic dance, Chiki, chimes in with the usual platitudes about taking Africa to the world and not criticizing artists for entering "influential international spaces." Apparently, these creatives are the bearers of our stories, ensuring we remain visible. Visible for what? To be told how to speak, how to clap, or how to behave when the "world" decides to grace us with its presence? It's a convenient argument that conveniently sidesteps the power dynamics at play. It reminds me of the old adage, you’re either at the table or on the menu, and I’m not convinced our guys are the ones setting the menu.

The whole charade got even more interesting when Dr. Ofweneke, a comedian who fancies himself some sort of cultural commentator, decided to weigh in. He felt Macron’s tone during the summit was "a little bit weird," implying we’re not fighting for independence anymore. And honestly, who can blame him? Seeing the French President interrupt a cultural presentation to scold attendees for making noise felt less like a summit and more like a condescending lecture. It’s that patronizing air, the one that suggests he’s here to enlighten us rather than engage as an equal. It’s a familiar tune, really.

Of course, some Kenyans, bless their optimistic hearts, defended Macron, arguing attendees should have respected the proceedings. Others, with a bit more realism, agreed with Ofweneke that the tone was off. It’s a classic Nairobi response: some will cheer for any perceived validation from abroad, while others, the ones who actually have to live with the consequences of these global interactions, see the cracks. This whole spectacle is less about "carrying African stories" and more about who gets to control the narrative, and who gets a fleeting moment in the spotlight. It’s a good thing we have stories to tell, because actual progress seems to be as elusive as [The Illusion of Recovery in a Choked Global Market](/posts/the-illusion-of-recovery-in-a-choked-global-market).

So, Bien-Aimé can claim he’s still fully African, fully critical, and fully himself. And Chiki can argue for the importance of creative diplomacy. But the reality on the ground, in this bustling, often chaotic city, is that such meetings often leave us more visible, but not necessarily more empowered. It’s a delicate dance, and one that too often ends with us feeling like we’ve been lectured rather than listened to. Just another day in our globalized reality.
