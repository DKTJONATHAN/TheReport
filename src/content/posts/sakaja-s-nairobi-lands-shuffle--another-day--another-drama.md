So, Governor Sakaja thinks he can just swap people around in the Lands department like they're playing musical chairs? Nairobi South MCA Waithera Chege has given him a 24-hour deadline to undo this mess. Apparently, moving Cecilia Koigu from Lands to ICT and Infrastructure, bringing in Dr. Machel Waikenda from somewhere else, is a "suspicious" and "ill-timed" move. Because, you know, land in Nairobi is as stable as a wobbly stool in a hurricane.

Chege is spitting fire, calling the Lands docket the "backbone" of Nairobi's planning and development. She's worried about disrupting reforms and weakening oversight, which, let's be honest, is already a miracle if it exists at all. This is the department that decides where your house goes, where the next skyscraper pops up, and whether that "new development" is actually legal or just someone's uncle building on public space. It's not a playground, it's where the real money and the real trouble happens.

The South MCA is basically saying this reeks of "undisclosed interests." In Nairobi, when something smells fishy, it's usually a rotting fish. We've all heard the whispers, the allegations of irregular allocations and land grabbing. So, when the Governor makes a sudden move in a department notorious for its shadiness, people are going to ask questions. And Chege is demanding answers and the return of Koigu, because continuity is apparently a foreign concept here.

If Sakaja doesn't back down in 24 hours, the County Assembly is ready to unleash "firm oversight action." Sounds like a fancy way of saying they're going to make his life miserable. This is classic Nairobi politics: a lot of noise, a lot of threats, and by the time anyone remembers what it was about, they’ll be fighting over something else. It’s a never-ending cycle, much like the issues we see in other sectors, such as when we discussed [Police Killings 2025: Kenya's Gender Breakdown is a Man's Problem](/posts/police-killings-2025--kenya-s-gender-breakdown-is-a-man-s-problem).

The whole thing is a charade. The residents deserve clarity, but they'll probably get more political wrangling and backroom deals. This reshuffle isn't about better governance; it's about who controls the levers of power and the land that lines pockets. So, buckle up, Nairobi. Another day, another drama, and the only certainty is that nothing will truly change for the better.
