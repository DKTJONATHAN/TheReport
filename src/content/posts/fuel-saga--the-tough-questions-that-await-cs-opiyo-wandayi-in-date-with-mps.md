Fuel Saga: Wandayi's Parliament Date With MPs Awaits - And We All Know How This Ends

The Energy CS is finally facing the music, or at least a parliamentary committee, over a dodgy fuel import. Expect more smoke than fire, as usual.

CS Wandayi's summoned to explain a fuel import scandal. Motorists queueing, prices soaring - but don't expect any miracles.

Fuel

Another Tuesday, another parliamentary circus. Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi is finally scheduled to appear before the National Assembly Energy Committee. This time, it’s not about a polite chat; it’s about a 60,000-metric-tonne cargo of super petrol that somehow materialized outside the government's supposed "sanctioned framework." Apparently, our esteemed lawmakers have a few "tough questions" for him. Given how these things usually play out, I’d prepare for a lot of finger pointing, vague denials, and a whole lot of hot air.

It’s a classic Nairobi drama. While you’re stuck in endless queues, praying your car doesn’t cough its last breath, the bigwigs are busy orchestrating these "fuel scandals." MPs are chirping about a "man-made" crisis, which is rich coming from the same lot who probably benefited from the whole mess. And after failing to show up last week, Wandayi’s been issued fresh summons, with the usual parliamentary threats of "consequences." You know, the kind that never actually materialize.

The crux of the matter seems to be whether some obscure Security Council directive was violated, or if a certain Kiptoo was appointed on a whim. Frankly, it’s all a bit of a blur of acronyms and backroom deals. What’s clear is that fuel costs are set to climb even higher for the May-August consignments. So, while Wandayi parries with MPs, remember that the real cost is coming out of your pocket, just like with so many other issues that seem to follow the same old patterns, reminiscent of the convoluted dramas we’ve seen before.

At the end of the day, this is just another episode in the grand theatre of Kenyan politics. They’ll have their hearings, issue their statements, and likely nothing will fundamentally change. The fuel will eventually trickle back, at a higher price, of course, and we’ll all be left to pick up the pieces. It’s the way of things here, a constant cycle of scandal and perceived accountability that never quite delivers real justice. Just another Tuesday.
