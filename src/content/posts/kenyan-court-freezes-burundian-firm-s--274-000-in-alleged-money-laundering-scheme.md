KENYAN COURT FREEZES BURUNDIAN FIRM’S $274,000 IN SO CALLED MONEY LAUNDERING CASE

The Assets Recovery Agency is flexing its muscles again, freezing a cool quarter-million dollars from a Burundian engineering firm. They claim it's dirty money, but we’ve heard that story before. The Court of Appeal, with its usual flair for the dramatic, has decided the cash should stay locked up while they ‘appeal’ the High Court's decision to release it. Sixty days. That’s how long we wait to see if logic or bureaucracy wins this round.

Apparently, this EIS Afrika Group Ltd received a hefty sum from abroad and then did some transactions that sniffed of suspicion to the ARA. They’re calling it a money laundering scheme, a classic move to disguise “illicit funds.” It's amazing how often the ARA suddenly smells something fishy right after money lands in a Kenyan bank account. It makes you wonder if they have a special nose for funds that could actually do some good elsewhere, like maybe in the [2026 ICT Budget](posts/the-2026-ict-budget--another-digital-mirage-for-a-hungry-nation), which seems to be another digital mirage.

The company, however, is playing the World Bank card, saying the money is for a legitimate project building a border post. They claim banking issues in Burundi forced them to use Kenya, and they even provided documents. But here’s the kicker: the High Court initially dismissed the ARA's case, saying they hadn't proven the money was from crime and hadn't independently verified the company's paperwork. Sounds like common sense, doesn't it? Too bad common sense rarely wins in these legal dramas.

Undeterred, the ARA appealed, grumbling about inadmissible evidence and an ‘unduly high evidentiary threshold’. They’re worried the money will vanish if not frozen. It's a common tactic - if you can't win on merit, tie up the assets until the other side gives up or the legal fees bankrupt them. This whole saga hinges on a technicality about preservation orders, a dance of legal jargon that probably means very little to the average Kenyan trying to make ends meet.

The Court of Appeal, siding with the ARA, decided that these preservation orders are meant to stay in force during appeals. Their reasoning? To stop assets from being moved or disappearing. It's a convenient interpretation that keeps the money locked away, regardless of the initial ruling. So, while the company might have a valid project, and the High Court might have seen through the ARA's smoke and mirrors, the appeal court has decided to keep the money in limbo. Just another day in the Kenyan justice system.
