So, Linda Mwananchi, the latest noise from the political trenches, is calling the government's 10-point agenda a "fraud" and a "betrayal" of the late Prime Minister Raila Odinga's legacy. Their report, ominously titled "The Ten-Point Lies," dissects each point, claiming nothing's been implemented. Honestly, who's surprised? It's the same old song and dance, just with a different rhythm section. They even have MPs planning to skip events, probably to avoid looking complicit.

Godfrey Osotsi, Caleb Amisi, Antony Kibagendi, and Wilberforce Oundo are listed as the "brave" ones who'll be absent. Meanwhile, Orengo is crying foul about his bodyguard and staff being arrested before Ruto's visit to Siaya. Because clearly, when political leaders get agitated, the first thing to do is lock up the people around them. It’s a classic move, straight out of the playbook of those who'd rather not have any inconvenient witnesses around. It reminds me of how, after Justice Ibrahim is dead, the hyenas are mourning, always looking for their next meal.

And get this, there’s an eight-member joint technical committee supposedly set up to spearhead the 2027 coalition. Because what Kenya needs more of is committees, especially when the existing promises are apparently gathering dust. Sifuna and Orengo, leading this Linda Mwananchi outfit, are calling the March 7 anniversary a "day of political betrayal." It’s a powerful accusation, but in this country, betrayal is practically a national sport.

The whole exercise feels like a desperate attempt to reignite a dying flame. They’re pointing fingers and yelling about broken promises, but the truth is, the political landscape is a battlefield, and everyone’s just trying to gain an advantage. Whether it's the ruling party or the opposition, the ordinary Kenyan is usually left holding the short end of the stick, wondering when their turn for a decent life will actually arrive.
