The Motion Picture Association (MPA) has taken a firm stance against the platform. MPA Chief Executive Charles Rivkin stated that Seedance 2.0 has engaged in the unauthorized use of U.S. copyrighted works on a massive scale. Rivkin argued that ByteDance is operating without meaningful safeguards, thereby disregarding established laws that protect millions of jobs in the American creative sector. In response, a ByteDance spokesperson indicated that the company is taking steps to strengthen safeguards to prevent the unauthorized use of intellectual property and likenesses.

Seedance 2.0 enters a market already disrupted by OpenAI's Sora, which debuted in early 2024. However, the ByteDance model is noted for its improved aesthetic quality, making its output look significantly less artificial than its predecessors. In China, reports from the Global Times describe the tool being used to create complex action sequences featuring digital versions of young Jackie Chan and Jet Li. This level of realism has raised alarms regarding the potential for deepfakes and the erosion of personality rights.

The legal landscape remains fragmented as there are currently no clear global or national guidelines governing the AI-copyright debate. This regulatory vacuum has led to a flurry of litigation. The New York Times is currently pursuing a case against OpenAI and Microsoft, alleging that these companies "free-ride" on journalistic investment to create competing products. Similar legal challenges have emerged in India, where the Digital News Publishers Association (DNPA) filed a case in the Delhi High Court against major AI developers.

Precedents set in recent U.S. court cases offer a mixed outlook for creators. In rulings involving Anthropic and Meta, judges found in favor of the tech companies, suggesting that AI training is akin to a human reader learning from existing works rather than committing plagiarism. Conversely, some judges have noted that the immense profits earned by AI companies necessitate a re-evaluation of how copyright holders are compensated.

Within Hollywood, reactions range from existential dread to strategic adaptation. Producer Rhett Reese expressed concern that AI is encroaching on creative careers, while actor Matthew McConaughey has urged performers to assert their personality rights to ensure they retain control over their voices and likenesses. This tension is further complicated by major studios beginning to form alliances with AI firms. In late 2025, The Walt Disney Company signed a 1 billion dollar licensing agreement with OpenAI, allowing the use of its vast library of characters for social video generation on Sora.

While such corporate deals provide a legal framework for large-scale IP usage, they leave independent professionals and smaller entities in a precarious position. As the technology continues to evolve faster than the law, the industry faces a fundamental shift in how content is produced and who owns the resulting value. This trend mirrors broader movements in the tech sector, such as those seen in the music industry with startups like Mozart AI, which recently raised 6 million dollars to bring generative music creation to the masses (/posts/mozart-ai-raises--6m-to-bring-generative-ai-music-creation-to-the-masses).
