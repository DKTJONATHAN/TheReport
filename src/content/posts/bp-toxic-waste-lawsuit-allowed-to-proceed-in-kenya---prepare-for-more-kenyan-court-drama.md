Right, so the Environment and Land Court in Isiolo has given the green light for a class action against BP. Apparently, they’re blaming the oil giant for toxic waste from the 80s that’s supposedly killed over 500 people and a heap of livestock in northern Kenya. The story goes that Amoco, which BP swallowed up, was dumping drilling waste with all sorts of nasty stuff like radium, arsenic, and lead into unlined pits or just leaving it out in the open. Classic.

The lawsuit, brought by 299 folks from Kargi and Kalacha, lists BP and a whole parade of Kenyan government bodies as respondents. We’re talking the Environment Ministry, Water, Health, Mining, the Water Resources Authority, Marsabit County, the Attorney General, NEMA, the Nuclear outfit, and even the medical research types. Everyone’s getting dragged into this. BP, predictably, has "no comment." Shocking, I know.

This ruling doesn't mean BP is guilty, mind you. It just means these Kenyans can actually have their day in court. PARAAN, a network of civil society groups, is backing these petitioners, calling it a "long-standing historical injustice." They’re even calling this case a "pilot" for similar allegations popping up in Garissa, Wajir, and Isiolo. Seems like northern Kenya is one big toxic dump site. It makes you wonder if this is just another example of how external players often treat developing nations, a bit like how we've seen the [The Caracas-Tehran Blueprint: Why Africa is Abandoning the Washington Consensus](/posts/the-caracas-tehran-blueprint-why-africa-is-abandoning-the-washington-consensus) playing out in different spheres.

The 299 petitioners are likely just the tip of the iceberg. PARAAN’s coordinator, Liban Golicha, hopes this will be an "eye-opener." An eye-opener for whom? For the Kenyans who've been sick and dying, or for the fat cats who’ll probably lawyer up and drag this out for years, if not decades? History isn't exactly on the side of the little guy in these sorts of battles. It’s a long shot, a real long shot.

And let’s be honest, even if they win, what does "justice" really look like here? A cheque that barely covers a fraction of what’s been lost? Or maybe some PR statements about corporate responsibility that change nothing? This is Kenya, after all. We’ve seen plenty of grand pronouncements and then… crickets. This is just another chapter in the ongoing saga of who gets screwed and who gets away with it.

The fact that so many government bodies are also named suggests that this isn't just a BP problem, but a systemic one. Perhaps the Kenyan authorities were too busy with their own pipe dreams to notice the toxic waste. It’s a nice thought, isn’t it? The court is allowing it to proceed, so let’s wait and see if anything actually comes of it, besides more paper for the lawyers. Don't expect miracles.
