Meru Leaders Bank on Police Training Camps to Curb Banditry - Or So They Say

This is the latest grand plan from Meru County, where leaders are now convinced that building police and military training facilities on the border with Isiolo will magically stop cattle rustling. Governor Isaac Mutuma has apparently found 600 acres for a police training school and is even chatting up the Kenya Wildlife Service for another one. Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen was there to officially bless the opening of a simulation course for 2,000 AP recruits. Because clearly, the thousands of cattle lost over the last three years to bandits is just a minor inconvenience that a few training camps will fix.

The National Police College at Kiganjo is also setting up a "field school," and let's not forget the KDF is already squatting on vast tracts of land. This whole initiative is being sold as a "security buffer" and a way to "harden recruits." It’s funny, isn’t it, how the proposed solution to years of banditry and the loss of over 11,000 head of cattle is to create more bases for the very people who are supposed to be protecting us? It's almost as if they're just creating more jobs and land deals.

Governor Mutuma is ecstatic, talking about how this will "trigger development" and boost land values along the Isiolo - Modogashe road. He also conveniently mentions opportunities for locals to "do business by supplying food and other items to the recruits." So, the real beneficiaries are the ones getting fat off the government payroll, not the herders who have lost everything. This sounds suspiciously like the same old song and dance, similar to how [tea, tweets, and tomahawks](http://localhost:1313/posts/tea--tweets--and-tomahawks--nairobi-s-geopolitical-juggling-act-falls-flat) always ends up benefiting the connected few.

CS Murkomen, bless his heart, is thanking the Meru County Government for their "generous land donation," calling it a product of "public proposals" from "Jukwaa la Usalama forums." He even dismisses "petty politicians" who claim they want to close Kiganjo. It's all about "experiential learning" and giving recruits "real life experience." What a noble cause, especially when the "real life experience" involves a region consistently declared dangerous.

Meanwhile, the Nyambene Conservancy, a "source of hardship for herders due to rampant banditry," is where all this magic is supposed to happen. Igembe North MP Julius Taitumu is confident this will "fast-track infrastructure development." Infrastructure development. For training camps. While herders are still counting their losses. It’s a bold and strategic step, alright. A step towards more bureaucracy and less actual resolution.

Inspector General of Police Douglas Kanja chimes in that these schools in remote areas are for "adequate field simulation." Simulation. Because actual policing and preventing the loss of thousands of cattle is apparently too complicated. The real simulation here is how long it will take for these leaders to realize this isn't solving the problem, but rather repackaging it into a new development project.
