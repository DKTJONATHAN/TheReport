MPs Back IPOA Plan to Punish Police Chiefs For Juniors' Atrocities, Because Someone Has to Take the Fall

So, the honourable members of parliament, those paragons of virtue and protectors of the common mwananchi, are finally waking up to the fact that our police force has been running roughshod over us. IPOA, bless their well-intentioned hearts, wants to hold senior officers personally responsible for the excesses committed by their juniors. Apparently, the current system where only the poor sod who actually pulls the trigger or lands the beating gets hauled in, while the big bosses who ordered the deployment or looked the other way, walk free, is not working. Shocking, I know.

This whole drama is playing out because, surprise, surprise, cases of police brutality are on the rise. Peaceful protests turn into nightmares, and even just going about your business can land you on the wrong end of a police baton. The system's accountability mechanisms are about as effective as a screen door on a submarine, and when it comes to managing public order, well, let's just say chaos is the only constant. IPOA is tired of it, stating that the deaths, injuries, and destruction of property "have to stop." About time someone said it out loud in that hallowed chamber.

The proposed reforms are meant to shift the blame-or rather, the responsibility-onto police commanders, from the Inspector General all the way down to the OCS. They’re the ones making the deployment decisions, the tactical calls, and the rules of engagement. If their juniors go rogue, it's because the commanders either failed to prevent it or didn't have the guts to stop it. As IPOA Commissioner Kenwilliam Nyakomitah pointed out, right now, the law only requires them to report misconduct *after* it happens, leaving a "critical gap in prevention." It's like blaming the rain for a flooded street instead of the person who forgot to close the tap.

What's truly pathetic is that the Internal Affairs Unit (IAU), supposedly the internal watchdog, is apparently as functional as a broken down matatu. IPOA Director of Complaints, Dr. Ruth Kaguta, says it's "almost dysfunctional," plagued by leadership changes, lack of independence, and general public distrust. Apparently, complainants are telling them they don't trust the IAU to handle their cases because it’s too closely tied to the Inspector-General. So, the very unit meant to sort out police misconduct is, itself, part of the problem. It's a classic Kenyan bureaucratic merry-go-round, where problems are identified, debated, and then, somehow, nothing substantial changes. This level of dysfunction, where even basic oversight mechanisms are failing, is all too familiar, much like the constant struggle against cyber threats that seem to plague us daily, as seen in [Kenya’s password theft and spyware attacks skyrocketing 83%](/posts/kenya-s-password-theft-and-spyware-attacks-skyrocket-83----another-day--another-way-to-get-ripped-off).

The MPs seem to be on board, at least publicly, talking about "clearer lines of responsibility" and "mapping out the gaps." They’ll probably hold a few more meetings, write a few more reports, and then, who knows, maybe a new policy document will emerge. But let’s be real, expecting dramatic change from a system that has proven its talent for self-preservation is a fool's errand. The real test will be whether these "reforms" actually stick, or if they'll just be another footnote in the long, sad saga of police accountability in this country.
