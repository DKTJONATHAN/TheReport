So, the US government watchdog is going to "investigate" the Epstein files release. How utterly predictable. This is less about uncovering truth and more about managing optics, a classic move from the playbook of those who prefer to keep the real rot buried.

Let's not forget the convenient timing of Pam Bondi's dismissal. Apparently, her handling of the Epstein files was problematic enough to warrant a firing, only to be replaced by Todd Blanche, Trump's own legal counsel. Blanche’s immediate pronouncement that the files "should not be a part of anything going forward" screams damage control, not transparency. It’s a clear signal that the establishment is more concerned with protecting its reputation than with exposing the full network of enablers and beneficiaries.

The criticism of Trump for not doing "more" is rich. The expectation that a president would actively dismantle such a deeply entrenched system of abuse is naive at best. This is a global network, intertwined with power and wealth, and any genuine pursuit of justice would threaten too many important players. This investigation will likely be a carefully choreographed dance, designed to appease public outcry without actually disrupting the status quo.

And then there are the survivors, Annie Farmer included. Their calls for accountability and their outrage at the mere suggestion of a pardon for Ghislaine Maxwell are met with the deafening silence of the Department of Justice. A pardon? For Maxwell? This isn't justice; it's a testament to the power of influence and the willingness of the system to offer a lifeline to its own. Survivors are repeatedly asked to endure the trauma of reliving their abuse, only to witness their tormentors potentially walk free, or at best, receive a slap on the wrist.

The House Oversight Committee investigating Epstein is commendable in theory, but their willingness to entertain pardons in exchange for "cooperation" is a betrayal. Cooperation is only valuable if it leads to genuine consequence, not to a convenient exit strategy for those who have benefited from the exploitation of others. The DOJ's non-response is also telling. When they don't have a ready answer, it usually means they don't have a good one, or they're buying time to craft a palatable lie.

This "investigation" will be a masterclass in deflection and delay. The Epstein files will be dissected, analyzed, and ultimately used to justify why certain individuals were not prosecuted, while the real architects of this network remain comfortably insulated. The system protects itself, and this watchdog is just another cog in that well-oiled machine of obfuscation.
