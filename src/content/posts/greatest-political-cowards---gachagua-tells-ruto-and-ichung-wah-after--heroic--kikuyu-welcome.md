'COWARDS' GACHAGUA CLAIMS AS RUTO AND ICHUNG'WA GO LOW IN KIKUYU AFTER CLASHES

Ruto and Ichung'wah are "cowards" who use police to suppress dissent, claims Gachagua. Violence erupts in Kikuyu and along Southern Bypass.

Clashes and teargas as Gachagua pushes ahead with rally; Ichung'wah blames Gachagua for "organized political attacks."

Politics
Kenya, Politics, Corruption
Riot

FORMER DEPUTY PRESIDENT RIGATHI GACHAGUA HAS DECIDED TO SPICE THINGS UP, CALLING PRESIDENT WILLIAM RUTO AND MP KIMANI ICHUNG'WAH THE "GREATEST POLITICAL COWARDS" IN THE COUNTRY. Apparently, his grand entry into Kikuyu Town, complete with police roadblocks and teargas, was met with such "bravery" from residents that he felt compelled to issue a statement. Because nothing screams resilience like dodging teargas canisters while your political rivals are busy accusing you of orchestrating chaos. He even threw in a little dig about Ruto being a one-term president and Ichung'wah needing to follow him to Sugoi - because, you know, political discourse is just a fancy way of saying playground insults.

The whole kerfuffle kicked off after some "unrest" along the Southern Bypass and in Kikuyu Town. Ichung'wah, never one to miss a chance to point fingers, promptly accused Gachagua of mobilising "gangs of criminals" and "outsiders" to disrupt order. He even demanded that the government take action, claiming the chaos bore the hallmarks of "coordinated political attacks." It’s almost as if these politicians are auditioning for a bad action movie, complete with bonfires, debris, and attacked motorists. One wonders if Gachagua's "engagement" with residents involved handing out Molotov cocktails.

Gachagua, naturally, denies sponsoring the violence, claiming the government sent "goons and security" to stop him from meeting his people. This is classic Nairobi realism at its finest: everyone's a victim, everyone's pointing fingers, and no one has any actual proof, just accusations and the occasional blurry phone video. It’s a well-worn playbook, really. The streets of Nairobi have seen it all before, from the grand pronouncements to the petty squabbles. It makes you wonder if they ever pause to think about the actual problems facing Kenyans, or if it's just one long, drawn-out game of political chess where the pawns are the public.

Meanwhile, the Interior CS Kipchumba Murkomen is out here condemning "political violence" and urging courts to act firmly. He’s probably hoping for a dramatic courtroom showdown, but let’s be honest, these types of political skirmishes tend to end with a lot of noise and very little actual justice. It’s a cycle as predictable as the daily matatu madness on Uhuru Highway. And let's not forget the UDA politicians calling for respect for ODM - because when things get this messy, suddenly everyone remembers the importance of unity, until the next convenient opportunity to stab each other in the back arises. It's a performance worthy of any stage, though the actors are rather less talented. Perhaps they should take a leaf from the way some in power have treated vital resources, like those mineral supplies the US is trying to secure.
