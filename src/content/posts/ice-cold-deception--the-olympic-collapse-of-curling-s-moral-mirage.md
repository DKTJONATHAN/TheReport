The "Spirit of Curling" is officially dead, though any realist knows it was never more than a convenient fiction for a sport that had yet to grow up. The recent scandal at the 2026 Winter Olympics in Cortina d’Ampezzo, involving Canadian Marc Kennedy and Swedish player Oskar Eriksson, has stripped away the polite veneer of the "gentleman’s game" to reveal the same desperate, win-at-all-costs rot that infects every other professionalized pursuit. When the stakes are a gold medal, honor is the first casualty, quickly followed by the integrity of the governing bodies meant to protect it.

The incident began when Kennedy was caught "double-touching" the stone past the hog line - a subtle but effective way to manipulate the rock’s speed and trajectory. This is not a mistake; it is a calculated breach of physics performed by professionals who know exactly how much pressure their fingertips exert on twenty kilograms of granite. Kennedy’s response to being caught was not an apology but a vulgar outburst, proving that when a cheat is cornered, they don't retreat into sportsmanship; they weaponize aggression.

World Curling, the sport’s governing body, has displayed a masterclass in institutional spinelessness. After initially promising to assign officials to watch for these infractions, they folded the moment the new rules actually caught people. When Canadian skip Rachel Homan and Great Britain’s Bobby Lammie were flagged for the same violations, the blowback from powerful national federations was immediate. Instead of standing their ground, World Curling retreated, announcing that officials would only observe throws "at the request of the competing teams." This effectively hands the power back to the players to police themselves - a proven recipe for systemic abuse.

In both cases, the obsession with metrics and winning has outpaced the structures designed to maintain fair play.

The "Spirit of Curling" was a luxury of the amateur era. In the modern Olympic context, it is a liability. By refusing to use video replays to correct obvious fouls, World Curling is not "preserving tradition"; they are protecting a culture of gaslighting where the person who points out the cheating is labeled an "instigator" for breaking protocol. The message to athletes is clear: cheat if you can, curse if you’re caught, and wait for the governing body to apologize for inconveniencing you. The ice in Cortina isn't just slippery; it's transparently corrupt.
