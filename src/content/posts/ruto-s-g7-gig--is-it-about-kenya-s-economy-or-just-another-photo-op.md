So, President Ruto is off to the G7. Fancy. They'll probably talk about global economics, climate change, and all those lofty things that sound important while Nairobi traffic jams are still a national sport and your boda boda fare keeps going up faster than inflation. The "economic pressures fuelling domestic criticism" they mention? That's just the polite way of saying Kenyans are struggling to make ends meet, and they’re not exactly impressed with the grand pronouncements from afar.

The official line is that Kenya's role at the G7 is crucial. Crucial for what, exactly? For getting more photo opportunities and perhaps a few pats on the back? Because what we’re seeing on the ground is a different story. Every new authority that’s created, like that meteorology one he recently assented to, seems to just add another layer of bureaucracy and another drain on resources. It makes you wonder if all this international theatre is just a distraction from the mess at home.

And let’s not forget the tech world. Remember how we were all supposed to be rallying for "tech sovereignty" with Macron? It felt more like a gentle nudge towards European dependency, disguised as progress. These international gatherings often serve more to bolster the image of leaders than to actually solve the persistent economic woes that plague the everyday citizen. It’s all very well to discuss global strategies, but when your rent is due and the prices at the market are eye-watering, those discussions feel miles away.

The website you’re reading this on, by the way, is probably collecting data on you too, thanks to those "essential cookies" they claim are needed for functionality. They want to personalize your experience, sure, but more likely it’s about measuring advertising effectiveness. Just like these international summits are probably about measuring Ruto’s effectiveness on the global stage, not necessarily about improving your life here. You can choose to accept or reject some of their cookies, but the economic realities? Those you have to live with.

You can change your mind about the cookies, supposedly. You can even contact them about your consent. But can you change your mind about the economic policies that are making life a struggle? Not so easily. The G7 isn't going to magically fix the price of maize or the cost of fuel. It’s a convenient stage for leaders to appear engaged, while the real work, the tough, unglamorous work of fixing the economy, seems perpetually postponed. It’s a cycle, and frankly, it’s getting tiresome.
