So, our man Ruto is off to France for the G7 Summit, apparently to "represent Africa." Because that's what we need, another big speech about how the continent is on the cusp of greatness, while the same old issues fester. He'll be there to "champion Africa’s development agenda" and demand "reforms to the global economic order." Sounds inspiring, if you ignore the fact that we’re still begging for debt relief and struggling to keep the lights on.

He claims Kenya's "increasing presence in major international forums" shows our growing influence. Influence? Or just another excuse to fly first class and shake hands with people who have the real power? He talks about unlocking Africa's "potential" for "prosperity." Yes, the potential is undeniable - vast resources, a young population - but we’ve been talking about this for decades. It’s like a broken record, only this record is costing us a fortune in travel expenses.

Ruto’s defending his frequent foreign trips, saying we "must actively participate" to "accelerate economic growth." Let's see that economic growth. Meanwhile, the cost of living is still a punchline for most Kenyans. And while he’s busy with his "different paradigm for Africa," focusing on "opportunity, wealth creation and development," down here on the ground, people are worried about where their next meal is coming from. It’s a familiar story, a bit like the ongoing dirty global scramble for resources we’ve covered before. [Congo Cobalt Spill Shines Light on Dirty Global Scramble](/posts/congo-cobalt-spill-shines-light-on-dirty-global-scramble)

He’s dangling budget figures for agriculture and education like they’re some magic wand. Increased funding? Great. Will it actually reach the farmers or the schools, or will it disappear into the usual channels? He wants "relationships that benefit all sides," but let's be honest, most of these "mutual benefits" seem to disproportionately favor the ones already holding the purse strings.

So, while Ruto is hobnobbing with the G7 elite, pushing for "fairer trade arrangements" and "increased climate financing," the real agenda is likely just more promises and more photo opportunities. We'll be watching, and waiting, for actual results, not just more pronouncements from faraway lands.

He might be talking about a new dawn for Africa, but for many Kenyans, it feels more like the same old sunrise, only with a higher price tag.
