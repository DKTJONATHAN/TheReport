UGANDA CHOKES CIVIC FREEDOM WHILE KENYA PRETENDS TO UPHOLD IT

Uganda's latest move is a desperate, self-destructive grab for control. Kenya's regulations are a small win, but don't let them fool you.

Belinda Achieng comments on the contrasting actions of Uganda and Kenya regarding civil society and foreign funding.

Sovereignty, Control, Repression

History, as they say, rhymes. Or in this case, it’s a full-blown authoritarian cover band playing the same tired hits. Uganda, bless its misguided heart, is once again showing the world how to shoot itself in the foot with the Protection of Sovereignty Bill. They're so terrified of anyone with an opinion or a foreign bank account that they're essentially declaring war on their own people. Millions, including Ugandans who dare to associate with anything remotely outside the ruling party's tight grip, risk becoming criminals. It's a classic playbook, really - mimic Russia, China, and the rest of the authoritarian fan club, and watch your country wither.

This bill isn't just repressive; it's a masterclass in self-sabotage, disguised as patriotism. They're so eager to paint any outside influence as a threat that they'll happily disenfranchise their own diaspora, the very people sending billions back home. Imagine that - your own government making your hard-earned remittances a crime. Digital workers, entrepreneurs, anyone daring to collaborate with non-Ugandans could be thrown to the wolves. It’s a spectacular display of economic ignorance, as if the USD 2.5 billion in diaspora cash and USD 3.6 billion in foreign investment just magically appear. With health funding already in the gutter, this is less about sovereignty and more about a desperate, misguided attempt to cling to power.

Meanwhile, 700 kilometers south, Nairobi is humming with a different tune. Our own Interior Cabinet Secretary has unleashed the PBO Regulations, a move that, for once, feels less like a bureaucratic hurdle and more like a genuine attempt to acknowledge the work civil society does. They’re finally recognizing that human rights, anti-poverty work, and peacebuilding aren't dirty words. It's a recognition that, despite the usual political theatre, Kenya is choosing to keep its doors - and its mind - somewhat open. It's a stark contrast, and frankly, it's the kind of move that keeps places like this from becoming a total write-off, unlike the mess brewing north of us.

Of course, the real test is in the implementation. But for now, the PBO Act and its accompanying regulations are a global anomaly and a domestic relief. These organizations, far from being saboteurs, are pulling in billions - USD 1.9 billion, to be exact - and creating jobs for Kenyans. They're doing the heavy lifting in health, education, and poverty reduction, often with money that Uganda is now actively trying to scare away. It’s a reminder that embracing independent voices, rather than trying to silence them, is what actually builds a country, not whatever this Ugandan self-immolation is supposed to be. Maybe they should read up on how to run a country that isn’t a limited liability company.

The Ugandan Parliament should do the sensible thing - which, given their track record, is unlikely - and chuck this bill into the bin. It’s not about sovereignty; it's about a government terrified of its own people and the consequences of its own misrule. Kenya, for all its flaws, is at least showing a glimmer of understanding that economic prosperity and a functioning society require engagement, not isolation. Let’s hope this small win in Nairobi isn’t just a temporary pause before the next round of political games.
